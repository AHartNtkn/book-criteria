## Criteria Found

### Grammar Correctness
**What it measures**: Whether the prose follows standard English grammar rules (subject-verb agreement, pronoun-antecedent agreement, correct verb forms, proper modifier placement) with deviations only where clearly intentional for voice or effect.
**Assumed goal**: Traditional publishing editorial standards where mechanical polish signals professionalism — experimental fiction (e.e. cummings, Faulkner, Saramago) deliberately violates grammatical conventions for artistic effect.
**Score 0**: Frequent grammatical errors throughout (subject-verb disagreement, pronoun case errors, misused verb tenses) with no discernible artistic purpose. Reads as unedited.
**Score 5**: Grammar is flawless or near-flawless in narration. Any grammatical deviations (in dialogue, stream-of-consciousness, etc.) are clearly intentional, consistent, and serve characterization or artistic purpose. Professional editor would find no errors.
**Source**: https://bubblecow.com/blog/book-editing/copy-editing/what-are-the-5-cs-of-copyediting/
**Notes**: BubbleCow's "Correctness" C covers grammar, punctuation, spelling, and usage. The key is "maintaining accurate grammar while preserving authorial voice."

### Punctuation Accuracy
**What it measures**: Whether commas, periods, semicolons, colons, apostrophes, quotation marks, em dashes, en dashes, hyphens, and ellipses are used correctly and consistently throughout the manuscript.
**Assumed goal**: Chicago Manual of Style conventions for American fiction publishing, where punctuation serves both grammatical correctness and rhythmic/stylistic purposes — British publishing follows different conventions (single quotes, punctuation outside quotes) that are equally valid within their system.
**Score 0**: Widespread punctuation errors: missing commas, incorrect apostrophes (it's/its confusion), misused semicolons, hyphens where em dashes belong, inconsistent ellipsis formatting, unpaired quotation marks.
**Score 5**: Punctuation is correct and purposeful throughout. Commas, dashes, and semicolons are used for clarity and rhythm. Ellipses and em dashes follow a consistent style. Apostrophes and quotation marks are correct in every instance.
**Source**: https://www.writersdigest.com/write-better-fiction/copy-editing-proofreading-checklist-writers-need
**Notes**: Writer's Digest checklist specifically calls out quotation mark pairing, parentheses pairing, and ending punctuation verification as separate items. AJC style guide provides comprehensive rules for each mark.

### Dialogue Punctuation and Formatting
**What it measures**: Whether dialogue follows standard punctuation conventions — correct use of commas vs. periods with dialogue tags vs. action beats, proper capitalization after tags, correct placement of punctuation inside/outside quotation marks, proper handling of interrupted and trailing speech.
**Assumed goal**: Standard American fiction publishing conventions where dialogue tags use commas and lowercase continuation, action beats use periods and new sentences, em dashes mark interruption, and ellipses mark trailing off — these conventions are near-universal in commercial fiction and most literary fiction.
**Score 0**: Dialogue punctuation is inconsistent or incorrect: periods used with dialogue tags ("I love you." he said), action beats punctuated as tags ("I love you," she smiled), no distinction between interruption and trailing, smart/curly quotes inconsistently applied.
**Score 5**: Every instance of dialogue follows correct conventions. Tags and beats are properly distinguished. Interrupted dialogue uses em dashes correctly. Trailing dialogue uses ellipses correctly. Each speaker gets their own paragraph. Quotes within dialogue use single marks. Multi-paragraph dialogue has correct opening/closing quote placement.
**Source**: https://www.rabbitwitharedpen.com/blog/writing-dialogue-tags-action-beats-punctuation
**Notes**: Rabbit with a Red Pen provides the most comprehensive breakdown: tags (said, asked) use commas; beats (smiled, nodded) use periods; interrupted speech uses em dashes outside closing quotes; trailing speech uses ellipses. The distinction between tags and beats is one of the most common errors in unpublished manuscripts.

### Spelling and Typo Freedom
**What it measures**: Whether the text is free of misspellings, typographical errors, transposed letters, doubled words, missing words, and incorrect homophones.
**Assumed goal**: Professional publishing standards where zero tolerance for spelling/typo errors is expected in the final product — self-published works commonly have higher error rates but professional standards remain the benchmark.
**Score 0**: Multiple spelling errors, typos, wrong homophones (your/you're, their/there/they're, its/it's, affect/effect), doubled words, missing words per chapter. Reads as if spell-check was never run.
**Score 5**: Zero or near-zero spelling errors in the entire manuscript. Homophones are correct throughout. No doubled or missing words. Proper nouns and invented words are spelled consistently. Dialect spellings are intentional and consistent.
**Source**: https://bewrit.com/16-common-grammar-mistakes-to-watch-for-in-your-novel/
**Notes**: BeWrit identifies "silly typos" as the most common mistake in published work, including "extra space between words and numbers or letters that don't belong." Homophones are specifically flagged because spell-checkers miss them.

### Consistency of Style Choices
**What it measures**: Whether the manuscript maintains consistent editorial decisions throughout — Oxford comma usage, em dash formatting (spaced or unspaced), ellipsis style (three dots or Unicode character, spaced or unspaced), number formatting (spelled out or numerals), capitalization conventions, hyphenation patterns, British vs. American spelling, and all other style-guide decisions.
**Assumed goal**: Professional publishing standards where a single style sheet governs the entire manuscript — inconsistency signals that no copy editor reviewed the work, or that multiple authors/passes introduced conflicting conventions.
**Score 0**: Style choices vary randomly throughout the manuscript: Oxford comma present in some lists but absent in others, em dashes sometimes spaced and sometimes not, numbers sometimes spelled out and sometimes numerals within the same context, capitalization of terms inconsistent (e.g., "the Governor" vs. "the governor" in equivalent contexts).
**Score 5**: Every style choice is made once and applied uniformly throughout. A style sheet could be reconstructed from the manuscript with zero contradictions. The reader never notices formatting because it is invisible in its consistency.
**Source**: https://clearsightbooks.com/proofreading-oversights-you-dont-realize-are-giving-you-away-as-self-published/
**Notes**: Clear Sight Books specifically identifies mixed Oxford comma usage, mixed straight/curly quotes, alternating "OK"/"okay," mixed fraction formats, and inconsistent AM/PM formatting as tells of self-published work. RBC Editing emphasizes that "even a single inconsistency in hyphenation or capitalisation can undermine reader trust."

### Tense Consistency
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative maintains consistent verb tense throughout the main storyline, with deliberate shifts only at clear temporal boundaries (flashbacks, flash-forwards, embedded narratives).
**Assumed goal**: Standard fiction practice where one tense governs the main narrative and shifts are signaled by structural cues (scene breaks, chapter breaks, temporal markers) — some experimental works deliberately blur tense boundaries but this requires consistent internal logic.
**Score 0**: Tense shifts randomly between past and present within scenes, paragraphs, or even sentences. Dialogue tags alternate between "said" and "says" without pattern. Flashbacks are not differentiated by past perfect. The reader cannot tell whether shifts are intentional.
**Score 5**: The main narrative tense is maintained consistently. Flashbacks use past perfect tense (or equivalent temporal marker) to signal the shift. Any intentional tense changes occur at clear structural boundaries and follow a consistent rule. Dialogue tags maintain the same tense throughout.
**Source**: https://www.autocrit.com/editing/support/tense-consistency/
**Notes**: AutoCrit identifies tense consistency as a major editing concern. The Purdue OWL notes that the two most common places writers slip are dialogue tags (said vs. says) and action sequences that heat up (past sliding into present). Alyssa Matesic specifically identifies incorrect flashback tense as a common fiction error.

### Point of View Consistency
**What it measures**: Whether the chosen POV is maintained within scenes — no accidental head-hopping, no information the POV character couldn't know, no observations the POV character couldn't make.
**Assumed goal**: Modern commercial and literary fiction standards where limited POV is maintained within scenes and POV shifts occur only at clear structural breaks — omniscient POV is a valid choice but must be executed with consistent narrative distance and voice, not accidental slippage.
**Score 0**: The narration frequently reports thoughts, feelings, or knowledge that the POV character could not access. Multiple characters' internal states are revealed within a single scene without structural breaks. The reader cannot identify whose perspective governs any given paragraph.
**Score 5**: POV is rigorously maintained within every scene. The POV character's knowledge, perception, and emotional state are the sole lens. If multiple POVs are used, switches occur only at chapter or scene breaks with clear signaling. No information leaks from non-POV characters' heads.
**Source**: https://www.livewritethrive.com/2024/08/31/common-pov-violations-and-how-to-catch-them/
**Notes**: CS Lakin identifies POV violations as a common editing issue. Sandra Gerth's guide notes that POV consistency falls under technical execution, not just narrative craft — it is an editorial checkable item. Head-hopping is described as "a serious issue that would almost certainly lead to an instant rejection."

### Sentence Clarity
**What it measures**: Whether each sentence communicates its meaning on first reading — no ambiguous pronoun references, no garden-path constructions, no sentences requiring re-reading to parse, no buried subjects or actions.
**Assumed goal**: The school of thought that prose should be transparent to its content — the reader should experience the story, not the sentences. This conflicts with deliberately opaque prose styles (late Henry James, Thomas Bernhard) where syntactic difficulty is the point.
**Score 0**: Many sentences require re-reading to understand. Pronoun references are ambiguous ("Claire was worried about her test results, yet excited about going on the date with Josh. It made her toss and turn"). Modifiers are misplaced. Subjects are buried under qualifying clauses.
**Score 5**: Every sentence communicates its meaning immediately. Pronouns have clear antecedents. Modifiers are placed next to what they modify. The subject and action appear early in each sentence. Complex ideas are broken into digestible units.
**Source**: https://www.alyssamatesic.com/free-writing-resources/grammar-tips-for-novel-writing
**Notes**: Matesic identifies "The Undefined 'It'" as a common clarity error. BubbleCow's "Clarity" C specifies: "Replace vague pronouns with specific nouns. Break long sentences into single ideas. Use concrete nouns and strong verbs."

### Dangling and Misplaced Modifiers
**What it measures**: Whether modifying phrases are correctly attached to the words they are intended to modify, avoiding unintentional comic or confusing readings.
**Assumed goal**: Standard grammar where the modifier's target is the nearest appropriate noun — in fiction, misplaced modifiers are particularly damaging because they can create absurd mental images that break immersion.
**Score 0**: Multiple dangling or misplaced modifiers per chapter. Participial phrases attach to wrong subjects ("Running as fast as she could, the cape flapped in the wind"). Absolute phrases create impossible readings ("Opening the back door of a café, her purse fell to the ground").
**Score 5**: Every modifier clearly and correctly attaches to its intended target. No dangling participles, no misplaced adjectival phrases, no squinting modifiers. The reader never has to reconstruct intended meaning.
**Source**: https://theeditorsblog.net/2010/06/19/dangling-modifiers-a-common-writing-mistake/
**Notes**: The Editor's Blog, BeWrit, and Sandra Gerth all identify dangling modifiers as among the most common grammar errors in fiction manuscripts. Janice Hardy notes they "take the reader out of the fiction to instead focus on the writing."

### Participial Phrase Accuracy (Simultaneous Action)
**What it measures**: Whether present participial phrases (-ing constructions) are used only to describe actions that can logically occur simultaneously with the main clause action, not sequential actions misrepresented as concurrent.
**Assumed goal**: Standard grammar instruction where participial phrases denote simultaneous action — some fiction editors accept mild temporal looseness for flow, but physically impossible simultaneity is universally flagged.
**Score 0**: Frequent impossible simultaneous actions: "Unlocking the door, she went straight to bed." "Walking to the counter, she sat down and ordered." Characters perform physically incompatible actions in the same participial construction.
**Score 5**: Every participial phrase describes a logically simultaneous action. Sequential actions are expressed with sequential syntax. "After" or "having" constructions are used where appropriate. No physically impossible concurrent actions.
**Source**: https://sandragerth.com/participle-problems-in-fiction/
**Notes**: Sandra Gerth identifies four participle problems: dangling participles, simultaneous action violations, sequential action errors, and dialogue tag misuse. She advises using participles sparingly to avoid monotonous rhythm. The general principle: "the while is implied with present participles."

### Subject-Verb Agreement
**What it measures**: Whether subjects and verbs agree in number throughout, including tricky cases with collective nouns, compound subjects, intervening phrases, and indefinite pronouns.
**Assumed goal**: Standard English grammar rules — errors in subject-verb agreement are universally considered errors regardless of fiction genre or literary school.
**Score 0**: Frequent subject-verb disagreement, especially with collective nouns ("the group of students were" when acting as a unit), compound subjects ("neither the dog nor the cats was"), and sentences with intervening phrases ("the list of risks are long").
**Score 5**: Subject-verb agreement is correct throughout, even in complex constructions. Collective nouns are treated consistently (either singular or plural based on meaning). No agreement errors slip through intervening phrases.
**Source**: https://bewrit.com/16-common-grammar-mistakes-to-watch-for-in-your-novel/
**Notes**: BeWrit and Purdue OWL both identify subject-verb agreement as a common error that becomes more frequent with "stylized fiction with complex sentence structures."

### Apostrophe Correctness
**What it measures**: Whether apostrophes are used correctly for possessives, contractions, and plurals throughout — including tricky cases like "its" vs. "it's," singular possessives ending in 's' (James's vs. James'), and no apostrophes in plurals.
**Assumed goal**: Standard English grammar — apostrophe errors are universally considered errors. The possessive form of names ending in 's' has two acceptable conventions (James's and James') but must be consistent within a manuscript.
**Score 0**: Frequent apostrophe errors: "it's" used as possessive, "its" used as contraction, apostrophes in plurals ("the 1990's"), inconsistent possessive formation for names ending in 's'.
**Score 5**: Every apostrophe is correct. Possessives, contractions, and omissions are properly formed. Backward-facing apostrophes for dialect elisions ('cause, 'im) are correctly oriented. No greengrocers' apostrophes (plural nouns with apostrophes).
**Source**: https://bewrit.com/16-common-grammar-mistakes-to-watch-for-in-your-novel/
**Notes**: BeWrit and AJC style guide both address apostrophe rules. AJC provides detailed rules for descriptive phrases, individual vs. joint possession, and contractions in dialogue.

### Em Dash, En Dash, and Hyphen Distinction
**What it measures**: Whether the manuscript correctly distinguishes between hyphens (-), en dashes (–), and em dashes (—) and uses each in its proper context.
**Assumed goal**: Professional typesetting standards per Chicago Manual of Style — hyphens for compound words and word breaks, en dashes for ranges and compound adjectives with open compounds, em dashes for interruption/parenthetical insertion. Spaced or unspaced em dashes are both acceptable but must be consistent.
**Score 0**: Hyphens, en dashes, and em dashes are used interchangeably. Double hyphens (--) appear in place of em dashes. En dashes appear where hyphens belong. No consistent pattern is discernible.
**Score 5**: Each dash type is used correctly and consistently. Hyphens join compound words. En dashes mark ranges (1996–2006) and complex compounds. Em dashes mark interruptions and parenthetical asides. Spacing around dashes is consistent. No double hyphens remain.
**Source**: https://clearsightbooks.com/proofreading-oversights-you-dont-realize-are-giving-you-away-as-self-published/
**Notes**: Clear Sight Books specifically identifies "using the wrong mark, e.g., hyphen instead of en dash, is a proofreading oversight that gives your book away" as self-published. AJC style guide provides extensive rules for all three marks.

### Ellipsis Formatting Consistency
**What it measures**: Whether ellipses are formatted consistently throughout — whether using three spaced periods (. . .), three unspaced periods (...), or the Unicode ellipsis character (…), and whether spacing before/after ellipses is consistent.
**Assumed goal**: A single ellipsis convention maintained throughout the manuscript — CMOS recommends spaced periods with space before; many publishers use unspaced periods or the Unicode character. The choice matters less than consistency.
**Score 0**: Ellipses alternate between different formats within the manuscript. Some are three periods, some are Unicode characters. Spacing before and after varies. Ellipses are split across line breaks. Some have four dots (indicating a period plus ellipsis) and some don't when they should.
**Score 5**: One ellipsis format is used throughout. Spacing before and after is consistent. Ellipses at sentence ends correctly include a period where needed. Ellipses are never awkwardly split across lines.
**Source**: https://www.ajcollins.com.au/fiction-style-guide/
**Notes**: AJC style guide provides specific rules: "Space before ellipsis, after unless ending sentence." Different rules for mid-sentence pause, trailing off, trailing off then new sentence, and unheard sentence starts.

### Quotation Mark Consistency and Correctness
**What it measures**: Whether the manuscript uses consistent quotation mark style (double or single for primary dialogue, the opposite for quotes-within-quotes) and whether curly/smart quotes are used throughout without stray straight quotes.
**Assumed goal**: Professional publishing where curly (smart) quotes are the universal standard — straight quotes are a typewriter artifact. American convention uses double quotes for dialogue; British uses single. The choice must be consistent.
**Score 0**: Mixed straight and curly quotes throughout. Primary dialogue alternates between single and double marks. Quotes within quotes don't use the alternate style. Opening and closing marks face the wrong direction after apostrophes or elisions.
**Score 5**: One quote style used consistently throughout. All quotes are curly/smart. Quotes within quotes correctly use the alternate mark. Opening quotes face right, closing quotes face left. No straight quotes appear anywhere in the manuscript.
**Source**: https://practicaltypography.com/straight-and-curly-quotes.html
**Notes**: Butterick's Practical Typography calls straight quotes "one of the most grievous and inept typographic errors." Clear Sight Books identifies mixed straight/curly quotes as a self-publishing tell.

### Sentence Variety and Rhythm
**What it measures**: Whether the prose varies sentence length, structure, and opening patterns to create natural rhythm and avoid monotony — mixing short punchy sentences with longer flowing ones, varying sentence openers, and matching rhythm to emotional content.
**Assumed goal**: The craft tradition (from Strunk & White through contemporary writing guides) that varied sentence structure creates engaging prose — minimalist writers (Hemingway, Carver) use deliberately constrained variety, but even their prose has rhythmic purpose behind apparent uniformity.
**Score 0**: Sentences are monotonously uniform in length, structure, or opening pattern. Multiple consecutive sentences begin with the same word or pattern (e.g., "She... She... She..." or "He walked... He turned... He looked..."). Long passages lack any short sentences, or vice versa. No relationship between sentence rhythm and emotional content.
**Score 5**: Sentence length varies naturally. Short sentences appear at moments of impact, tension, or revelation. Longer sentences unfold during reflection or description. Sentence openers are varied (subjects, prepositional phrases, participial phrases, adverbial clauses). The rhythm matches the scene's emotional register.
**Source**: https://www.savannahgilbo.com/blog/line-editing-tips
**Notes**: Savannah Gilbo advises: "Short sentences add impact, tension, emotional weight. Long sentences provide space for reflection, complex thoughts. A mix creates a more natural, engaging rhythm." Sandra Gerth notes that "not only the beginnings of sentences and paragraphs, but also their lengths can become repetitive."

### Word Choice Precision
**What it measures**: Whether the prose uses specific, concrete, and precise words rather than vague, abstract, or generic ones — the right word rather than the almost-right word.
**Assumed goal**: The Mark Twain principle: "The difference between the almost right word and the right word is really a large matter — 'tis the difference between the lightning bug and the lightning." This school values specificity and concreteness over abstraction, though some literary traditions (Beckett, Kafka) deliberately use abstraction.
**Score 0**: Prose relies on vague, generic terms. "Things," "stuff," "nice," "good," "bad" appear frequently. Verbs are weak (was, had, went, got). Nouns are abstract when concrete alternatives exist. Words are imprecise or incorrect for their context.
**Score 5**: Every word earns its place. Nouns are specific and concrete. Verbs are dynamic and precise. Abstract language appears only when abstraction is the point. The reader sees, hears, and feels through precise diction rather than generic description.
**Source**: https://writers.com/word-choice-in-writing
**Notes**: Writers.com and multiple sources emphasize replacing vague language with specifics. "Fork" instead of "silverware," "piercing siren" instead of "loud sound." Mark Twain: "Substitute 'damn' every time you're inclined to write 'very'; your editor will delete it and the writing will be just as it should be."

### Concision and Filler Word Control
**What it measures**: Whether the prose is tight and efficient — free of unnecessary filler words, throat-clearing phrases, redundant modifiers, and padding that adds word count without meaning.
**Assumed goal**: The Strunk & White tradition ("Omit needless words") and the modern commercial fiction standard where every word earns its place — this conflicts with maximalist literary traditions (Proust, David Foster Wallace) where digressiveness and accretion are deliberate stylistic choices.
**Score 0**: Prose is padded with filler words (just, really, very, actually, basically, literally, definitely, certainly, probably, somewhat, somehow, quite, rather), throat-clearing phrases ("it is important to note," "in my opinion"), and redundant modifiers ("dark, pitch-black sky"). Sentences could lose 20-30% of their words without losing meaning.
**Score 5**: Every word contributes to meaning, rhythm, or voice. Filler words appear only in dialogue where they characterize the speaker. Narration is lean without being starved. Redundancy is absent. "Began to" and "started to" appear only when an action is interrupted.
**Source**: https://substack.dianaurban.com/p/words-you-should-cut-from-your-writing-immediately
**Notes**: Diana Urban lists 43 words to cut. Between the Lines Editorial lists 40 crutch words. BubbleCow's "Concision" C targets: "Filler words: very, really, actually, literally, basically. Throat clearing: 'it is important to note.' Redundant pairs: each and every. Nominalizations: 'make a decision' -> 'decide.'"

### Filter Word Control
**What it measures**: Whether the prose avoids unnecessary perception-reporting words (saw, felt, heard, noticed, watched, realized, wondered, thought, knew) that create distance between the reader and the character's experience.
**Assumed goal**: The deep-POV/immersive fiction tradition where filter words are considered a form of telling rather than showing — omniscient narration and distant third person naturally use more filter words, and their presence in those modes is not an error.
**Score 0**: Nearly every sensory detail or emotional moment is prefaced with a filter word: "She felt the cold wind," "He saw the door open," "She noticed the lights flickering," "He wondered what would happen." The reader is constantly told the character is perceiving rather than being placed in the perception.
**Score 5**: Sensory details and emotional states are rendered directly: "The cold wind bit her face," "The door swung open," "The lights flickered." Filter words appear only where the act of perceiving is itself the point (e.g., "She noticed him watching her" where the noticing is the action). Narrative distance is consistent with the chosen POV mode.
**Source**: https://www.instituteforwriters.com/less-filtering-more-show-dont-tell/
**Notes**: Institute for Writers provides the classic revision example: "She felt thick drops of rain pelting her bare head and neck" becomes "Thick drops of rain pelted her bare head and neck." Janice Hardy lists filter words: saw, felt, thought, wondered, looked, knew, guessed, sensed, experienced, seemed, heard, smelled, noticed, decided.

### Adverb Control
**What it measures**: Whether adverbs (particularly -ly adverbs) are used sparingly and purposefully, with stronger verbs and more specific description preferred over verb + adverb constructions.
**Assumed goal**: The Stephen King / Elmore Leonard tradition where adverbs in dialogue attribution are particularly discouraged and showing emotion through action is preferred to telling it through adverbs — this conflicts with some literary traditions that use adverbs deliberately (Tom Wolfe, Terry Pratchett).
**Score 0**: Adverbs appear frequently, especially in dialogue tags ("she said angrily," "he replied sarcastically"). Weak verb + adverb combinations dominate where a single strong verb would suffice ("walked slowly" instead of "ambled," "said loudly" instead of "shouted"). -Ly adverbs pad nearly every action.
**Score 5**: Adverbs appear rarely, and when they do, they earn their place by adding meaning no other construction could provide. Dialogue tags rely on "said" and "asked" with emotion conveyed through dialogue content, action beats, and context. Strong verbs replace verb + adverb combinations.
**Source**: https://www.themarginalian.org/2013/03/13/stephen-king-on-adverbs/
**Notes**: King: "The road to hell is paved with adverbs." His actual target was specifically adverbs in dialogue attribution. The key argument: "if you need an adverb to explain how dialogue is delivered, either the dialogue itself isn't doing its job, or you've chosen the wrong verb."

### Said Bookism Avoidance
**What it measures**: Whether dialogue attribution uses primarily invisible tags (said, asked) rather than conspicuous alternatives (exclaimed, declared, expostulated, hissed, breathed) that draw attention to themselves.
**Assumed goal**: Modern commercial fiction convention where "said" is invisible to readers — the counter-tradition holds that occasional alternatives add variety. The consensus is that invisible tags keep focus on dialogue content, while conspicuous tags distract.
**Score 0**: Nearly every dialogue tag uses a creative alternative to "said": characters exclaim, declare, retort, snort, bark, growl, muse, opine, and ejaculate their words. Adverbs further ornament tags. The reader notices the tags more than the dialogue.
**Score 5**: "Said" and "asked" dominate dialogue tags. Action beats replace tags where appropriate. Creative alternatives appear rarely and only when justified (a character can "whisper" or "shout" but not "smile" or "shrug" a line of dialogue). The reader experiences the dialogue, not the attribution.
**Source**: https://ladyknightediting.com/said-bookism/
**Notes**: Lady Knight Editing: "The main downfall of the said bookism is that it demonstrates laziness — if we can't tell that your character is yelling without being told, you haven't done a good enough job." LitReactor: words like "said/asked/replied" are "invisible cues" that don't intrude.

### Echo Word Avoidance
**What it measures**: Whether the same non-trivial word appears in close proximity (within a few sentences or a paragraph) without intentional rhetorical purpose.
**Assumed goal**: Standard editing practice where unintentional word repetition is considered a blemish — deliberate repetition for rhetorical effect (anaphora, epistrophe) is a different matter entirely.
**Score 0**: Non-trivial words (not articles/prepositions) repeat within the same paragraph or across consecutive sentences without rhetorical purpose. Character names, body parts ("eyes," "hands"), and action words ("looked," "turned") echo conspicuously. Unusual or distinctive words (e.g., "ancient," "chamber," "sigh") repeat within a page.
**Score 5**: Word echoes are absent or rare. When the same concept must be referenced repeatedly, varied phrasing is used naturally (not to the point of "elegant variation" or "said bookism" for nouns). Any repetition within close proximity serves deliberate emphasis.
**Source**: https://btleditorial.com/2024/04/30/how-to-limit-echo-words-in-your-novel/
**Notes**: Between the Lines Editorial: "Word echoes occur when we repeat the same word or short phrase in close proximity, page-wise." Autocrit specifically flags "repeated words" as an editing category. The key is distinguishing accidental echo from intentional rhetorical repetition.

### Redundancy Elimination
**What it measures**: Whether the prose avoids saying the same thing twice in different words, using redundant modifiers, or padding with phrases that add no new information.
**Assumed goal**: Lean prose tradition where every word earns its place — maximalist styles may use accretion and repetition deliberately, but redundancy (saying the same thing accidentally) is different from repetition (saying it deliberately for effect).
**Score 0**: Frequent redundant phrases: "dark, pitch-black sky," "nodded his head," "shrugged her shoulders," "stood up," "sat down," "each and every," "first and foremost." The same information is conveyed multiple times in different words within a paragraph. Dialogue restates what narration just showed.
**Score 5**: No accidental redundancy. Modifiers add new information rather than restating what's already implied. Body part actions don't specify the obvious body part. Narration and dialogue complement rather than duplicate each other.
**Source**: https://www.alyssamatesic.com/free-writing-resources/grammar-tips-for-novel-writing
**Notes**: Matesic: "Above, the stars were visible in the dark, pitch black sky" uses both "dark" and "pitch black" redundantly. BubbleCow targets "redundant pairs: each and every, first and foremost" and "nominalizations: 'make a decision' -> 'decide.'"

### Paragraph Structure and Breaks
**What it measures**: Whether paragraph breaks serve pacing, clarity, and reader orientation — new paragraphs for new speakers, new actors, new narrative modes, shifts in time/location, and appropriate paragraph length variation for pacing.
**Assumed goal**: Modern fiction formatting conventions where paragraphs are generally shorter than in 19th-century fiction, each speaker gets their own paragraph, and paragraph length varies to control pacing — literary fiction may use longer paragraphs than genre fiction.
**Score 0**: Multiple speakers share paragraphs. Wall-of-text paragraphs span entire pages without breaks. Paragraphs break in the middle of a single action or thought for no apparent reason. No variation in paragraph length — all are roughly the same size. Scene changes happen within paragraphs.
**Score 5**: Every speaker gets their own paragraph. Paragraph breaks mark shifts in speaker, actor, time, location, or narrative mode. Short paragraphs create urgency; longer ones create reflection. At least one break per page provides visual rest. Single-sentence paragraphs are used sparingly for emphasis.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/how-to-use-paragraph-breaks/
**Notes**: K.M. Weiland's three rules: new speaker = new paragraph, new actor = new paragraph, new narrative mode = new paragraph. "If you spend more than two sentences on any one narrative type, it's probably best to think about breaking for a new paragraph." She warns against overuse of single-sentence dramatic paragraphs.

### Passive Voice Control
**What it measures**: Whether passive voice is used deliberately and sparingly for specific effect (emphasis, mystery, pacing) rather than as a default construction that weakens prose.
**Assumed goal**: The general fiction editing standard that active voice is preferred for clarity and energy — but passive voice is a valid tool when the writer wants to emphasize the action over the actor, create mystery about the agent, or match a character's deflective voice.
**Score 0**: Passive voice dominates narration without purpose. "The door was opened" where "she opened the door" would be stronger. "The ball was thrown by the boy" constructions appear frequently. The prose feels distant and indirect because actors are routinely removed from their actions.
**Score 5**: Active voice dominates. Passive voice appears only where it serves a specific purpose: to obscure the agent for suspense, to emphasize the recipient of action, to match a character's evasive psychology, or where the actor is genuinely unknown or irrelevant. Every passive construction has a justifiable reason.
**Source**: https://wintersediting.com/passive-voice-fiction/
**Notes**: Amelia Winters: passive voice can "build suspense by not naming the perpetrator," "shift focus to the action or recipient," and "create mystery or distance." The consensus: "Using passive voice in writing is a stylistic choice, not a grammatical error."

### Comma Splice and Run-On Management
**What it measures**: Whether comma splices and run-on (fused) sentences are avoided in narration, and whether any deliberate comma splices in dialogue or stream-of-consciousness are clearly intentional.
**Assumed goal**: Standard grammar for narration with flexibility in dialogue — comma splices are acceptable in fiction for specific effects (rushed speech, racing thoughts, asyndeton) but must be clearly intentional rather than accidental.
**Score 0**: Comma splices and fused sentences appear frequently in narration with no apparent intent. Independent clauses are routinely joined with commas where periods, semicolons, or conjunctions are needed. No distinction between intentional and accidental splices.
**Score 5**: Narration is free of accidental comma splices and run-ons. Any comma splices that appear are clearly deliberate: in dialogue for breathless speech, in stream-of-consciousness for racing thoughts, or in short parallel clauses per Strunk & White's exception ("very short and alike in form"). The reader can always distinguish choice from error.
**Source**: https://cmosshoptalk.com/2019/04/16/comma-splices-and-run-on-sentences/
**Notes**: CMOS Shop Talk: "Comma splices are acceptable in fiction to emphasize the speed of a character's speech or thoughts." Strunk and White: "the comma is fine when the clauses are very short and alike in form." The key: "a deliberate comma splice in a short story is a choice; an accidental one in a research paper is an error."

### Sentence Fragment Control
**What it measures**: Whether sentence fragments are used deliberately for emphasis, pacing, voice, or dialogue naturalism — and whether accidental fragments (unfinished thoughts in narration, dependent clauses standing alone) are absent.
**Assumed goal**: Modern fiction convention that accepts deliberate fragments as a stylistic tool — the distinction between "fragment as craft" and "fragment as error" depends on whether the fragment is intentional and whether its meaning is clear.
**Score 0**: Fragments appear randomly without purpose. Dependent clauses stand alone in narration ("Because he was tired."). The reader cannot tell whether fragments are intentional. Fragments create confusion rather than emphasis.
**Score 5**: Fragments are used deliberately and sparingly for emphasis, urgency, or voice. Their meaning is clear from context. Deliberate fragments are distinct from the surrounding complete sentences, creating contrast. Accidental fragments are absent.
**Source**: https://wintersediting.com/sentence-fragments/
**Notes**: Amelia Winters: "An unintentional fragment looks sloppy; a deliberate fragment feels powerful." Purposes include emphasis, natural dialogue, tension/urgency, and mimicking thought patterns. "The intended meaning always needs to be clear."

### Number Formatting Consistency
**What it measures**: Whether numbers are formatted consistently throughout the manuscript — spelled out or numerals, with a consistent rule applied to the same categories of numbers.
**Assumed goal**: Chicago Manual of Style convention for fiction: spell out whole numbers zero through one hundred, use numerals for larger numbers, spell out numbers at the start of sentences, use numerals with units — the alternate rule (spell out single-digit only) is also acceptable if consistent.
**Score 0**: Numbers alternate randomly between spelled out and numerals in the same context. "Ten" in one paragraph and "10" in the next for the same type of quantity. Numbers beginning sentences are in numerals. No consistent rule is discernible.
**Score 5**: A single number convention is applied throughout. The same categories of numbers are always treated the same way. Dialogue uses spelled-out numbers consistently. Numbers beginning sentences are always spelled out. Time, dates, and measurements follow their own consistent sub-rules.
**Source**: https://cmosshoptalk.com/2020/01/31/numbers-in-creative-writing/
**Notes**: CMOS Shop Talk: "In writing, readability is more important than consistency. That's why flexibility in styling numbers is important." But within a manuscript, the same rule should apply consistently. Chicago advises avoiding "a thickly clustered group of spelled-out numbers."

### Italics Usage Consistency
**What it measures**: Whether italics are used consistently and correctly for their designated purposes — internal thoughts, emphasis, foreign words, titles of works, and any other manuscript-specific uses — without overuse or underuse.
**Assumed goal**: Modern fiction convention where italics serve specific functions and are used sparingly — overuse dilutes their impact, while inconsistent use confuses the reader about what italics signal in this particular manuscript.
**Score 0**: Italics usage is inconsistent: sometimes used for thoughts and sometimes not. Entire paragraphs are italicized for emphasis. Foreign words are sometimes italicized and sometimes not. Titles of works receive inconsistent treatment. Italics appear to serve no consistent function.
**Score 5**: Italics serve a consistent, identifiable function: direct thoughts in a specific tense/person, emphasis on specific words, foreign words on first use, titles of works. Usage is sparing (one or two instances per page maximum). The reader always knows what italics mean in this manuscript.
**Source**: https://cmosshoptalk.com/2020/08/18/formatting-thoughts-in-fiction/
**Notes**: CMOS Shop Talk provides specific rules for formatting thoughts. The AJC style guide adds: "Italicize foreign/unfamiliar words on first use only if repeated. Do not italicize thoughts with attribution tags." Key principle: "Use italics sparingly and consistently so they enhance meaning without distracting."

### Capitalization Consistency
**What it measures**: Whether capitalization conventions are applied consistently throughout — titles and honorifics, proper nouns, invented terms, generic vs. specific uses of titles, kinship terms, and fantasy/sci-fi species and races.
**Assumed goal**: Chicago Manual of Style conventions — capitalize proper nouns, lowercase generic uses, capitalize titles before names but not after. Fantasy and science fiction require additional consistency for invented terms.
**Score 0**: Capitalization is inconsistent: "the President" in one paragraph and "the president" in the next for the same character in the same context. Invented terms alternate between capitalized and lowercase. Kinship terms are randomly capitalized ("Come here, mother" vs. "Come here, Mother").
**Score 5**: Every capitalization choice follows a consistent rule. Titles are capitalized before names, lowercase when generic. Kinship terms are capitalized only when replacing a name. Invented terms maintain consistent capitalization throughout. Fantasy races/civilizations follow a consistent convention.
**Source**: https://www.ajcollins.com.au/fiction-style-guide/
**Notes**: AJC provides detailed rules: military titles capitalized when full title or direct address ("Captain Smith," "Yes, Captain") but lowercase for generic use ("the captain"). Nicknames and epithets capitalized. Kinship terms lowercase unless replacing a name. Fantasy races/civilizations capitalized.

### Scene Break and Chapter Break Formatting
**What it measures**: Whether scene breaks and chapter breaks are formatted consistently and professionally — using appropriate visual markers, consistent spacing, and following publishing conventions.
**Assumed goal**: Professional publishing standards where scene breaks use consistent markers (extra blank line, centered ornament, or centered asterisks), chapter breaks start new pages, and the first paragraph after any break is flush-left (not indented).
**Score 0**: Scene breaks are inconsistent — sometimes an extra line, sometimes asterisks, sometimes nothing. Chapter openings lack consistent formatting. First paragraphs after breaks are sometimes indented, sometimes not. The reader cannot always distinguish between scene breaks and formatting errors.
**Score 5**: Scene breaks use a consistent visual marker throughout. Chapter breaks are formatted identically. First paragraphs after all breaks are flush-left per convention. The reader always knows when a scene or chapter has changed.
**Source**: https://knowadays.com/blog/editing-tips-scene-breaks-in-books-and-manuscripts/
**Notes**: Shunn's "Proper Manuscript Format" provides industry-standard guidelines. Chicago Manual of Style: "the paragraph that comes after a break must be flushed and not indented." Different conventions for soft breaks (extra line) and hard breaks (centered ornament or asterisks).

### Widows and Orphans Control
**What it measures**: Whether the final typeset pages avoid isolated lines — widows (last line of a paragraph stranded at the top of a new page) and orphans (first line of a paragraph stranded at the bottom of a page).
**Assumed goal**: Professional book typesetting standards where widows and orphans are eliminated — this applies only to typeset/formatted pages, not raw manuscripts, and is primarily relevant for self-published works or final proofs.
**Score 0**: Widows and orphans appear frequently throughout the typeset pages. Single lines sit alone at page tops or bottoms. No effort to control pagination has been made.
**Score 5**: No widows or orphans appear in the typeset pages. A minimum of two lines (preferably three) appear at both the top and bottom of every page. Pagination is controlled through tracking, leading, or subtle rewriting.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Widows_and_orphans
**Notes**: Chicago Manual of Style: "an edited manuscript should have neither widows nor orphans." The professional standard is two lines minimum at top and bottom of each page. This is a typesetting concern rather than a writing concern, but self-published authors must address it.

### Hyphenation Consistency
**What it measures**: Whether compound words and phrasal adjectives are hyphenated consistently throughout — the same compound is always hyphenated or always open or always closed, following a single reference dictionary and style guide.
**Assumed goal**: Professional copyediting standards where compound-word hyphenation follows a reference dictionary (Merriam-Webster for American, Oxford for British) with a style sheet recording deviations — hyphenation conventions evolve over time and vary between dictionaries, so consistency within a manuscript is more important than any single "correct" form.
**Score 0**: The same compound word appears hyphenated, open, and closed in different locations ("ice cream," "ice-cream," "icecream"). Phrasal adjectives are inconsistently hyphenated ("well-known author" in one paragraph, "well known author" in the next). Age-related expressions use inconsistent patterns.
**Score 5**: Every compound word is treated the same way every time it appears. Phrasal adjectives before nouns are hyphenated; the same phrases after verbs are open, per convention. A single reference dictionary governs all ambiguous cases. A style sheet ensures consistency.
**Source**: https://bubblecow.com/blog/book-editing/copy-editing/what-are-the-5-cs-of-copyediting/
**Notes**: BubbleCow's "Consistency" C: "Hyphenation patterns (well-known author, but author is well known)." Clear Sight Books identifies "hyphenation present on some pages, absent on others" as a self-publishing tell.

### Line Editing Hierarchy: Clarity, Flow, Polish
**What it measures**: Whether the prose achieves all three levels of the line-editing hierarchy — first clarity (the reader understands what's happening), then flow (sentences and paragraphs connect naturally), then polish (language is refined and rhythm is shaped).
**Assumed goal**: The professional editing framework where clarity is non-negotiable, flow enables immersion, and polish distinguishes competent prose from excellent prose — this hierarchy implies that polished prose that lacks clarity has failed at the most fundamental level.
**Score 0**: Prose may have moments of beautiful language but the reader frequently loses track of what is happening, who is acting, or where the scene is set. Transitions between sentences and paragraphs are abrupt or missing. Flow is disrupted by unclear references and buried actions.
**Score 5**: The reader always understands physical and emotional events (clarity). Sentences and paragraphs flow into each other naturally, with smooth transitions and pacing that matches emotional content (flow). Language is refined — word choice is precise, rhythm is shaped, filler is eliminated (polish).
**Source**: https://www.savannahgilbo.com/blog/line-editing-tips
**Notes**: Savannah Gilbo's three-step hierarchy: "First, focus on clarity. Your reader should always understand what is happening physically and emotionally in a scene. If clarity is missing, no amount of polishing will fix the issue."

### Purple Prose Avoidance
**What it measures**: Whether descriptive prose serves the story rather than calling attention to its own ornateness — whether adjectives, metaphors, and elaborate phrasing enhance the reader's experience or obstruct it.
**Assumed goal**: The mainstream fiction convention that prose should be "transparent" — the reader experiences the story through the prose without being stopped by it. This conflicts with deliberately ornate literary traditions (Nabokov, Cormac McCarthy, Angela Carter) where prose density is the point.
**Score 0**: Description is so ornate it obscures meaning. Every noun has multiple adjectives. Metaphors are stacked and mixed. Simple actions are described in elaborate multi-clause sentences. The prose calls attention to itself at the expense of story, character, and pacing.
**Score 5**: Descriptive prose is vivid but proportionate. Adjectives are earned and specific rather than accumulated. Metaphors illuminate rather than decorate. The writing is invisible — the reader sees the story, not the prose.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Purple_prose
**Notes**: The term originates with Horace, who criticized "purple patches" stitched onto otherwise plain work. Writers.com: "characterized by excessive use of adjectives, adverbs, and metaphors." The key distinction is whether ornate prose serves the story or serves the author's ego.

### Correct Speaker Tag Verbs
**What it measures**: Whether dialogue tags use verbs that can actually be performed with speech — "said," "asked," "whispered," "shouted" — rather than verbs that describe physical actions that cannot produce speech — "smiled," "laughed," "shrugged," "sighed."
**Assumed goal**: Standard fiction convention where dialogue tag verbs must be speech-producing actions — "smiled" is an action beat, not a tag, and must be punctuated as a separate sentence. Some editors accept borderline cases like "laughed" and "breathed" as tags.
**Score 0**: Non-speech verbs are routinely used as dialogue tags: '"I love you," he smiled.' '"Let's go," she shrugged.' Physical actions are formatted as if they produce speech. The distinction between tags and beats is not understood.
**Score 5**: Dialogue tags use only speech-producing verbs. Physical actions are formatted as action beats (separate sentences with periods). Borderline cases are handled consistently. The reader always knows whether an action produces or accompanies speech.
**Source**: https://selfpublishingadvice.org/are-there-grammar-rules-for-writing-fiction-by-cs-lakin/
**Notes**: CS Lakin: "You cannot physically 'sigh speech' or 'cough speech.'" Rabbit with a Red Pen lists "gasped, breathed, laughed" as debated borderline cases. The distinction: tags are punctuated with commas; beats are punctuated with periods.

### Cross-Reference and Internal Consistency
**What it measures**: Whether all internal references are accurate — character names are spelled consistently, physical descriptions don't change without explanation, timeline events don't contradict, and all promises made by the text are fulfilled.
**Assumed goal**: Professional editing standards where factual consistency within the fictional world is maintained — a character's eye color doesn't change, distances between locations remain consistent, seasons progress correctly, and wounds don't heal without explanation.
**Score 0**: Characters' physical descriptions change without explanation (blue eyes become green). Timeline contradictions are present (events on Friday are referenced as happening on Thursday). Named places are spelled differently in different chapters. Injuries or conditions appear and disappear without explanation.
**Score 5**: Every internal fact is consistent. Character details match across every mention. The timeline holds up under scrutiny. Named places, objects, and terms are spelled identically throughout. Injuries heal at realistic rates. No promise made by the text goes unfulfilled.
**Source**: https://bubblecow.com/blog/book-editing/copy-editing/what-are-the-5-cs-of-copyediting/
**Notes**: BubbleCow's "Coherence and Completeness" C: "Character continuity: broken wrist does not heal invisibly; lost keys do not start cars." The DIY MFA checklist specifically targets character details, place name spellings, and series continuity.

### Readability of Complex Sentences
**What it measures**: Whether complex sentences with multiple clauses, modifiers, or embedded elements remain parseable on first reading — whether syntactic complexity serves the content or obscures it.
**Assumed goal**: The standard that complexity should match the complexity of the thought being expressed — simple ideas in simple sentences, complex ideas in carefully structured complex sentences. This conflicts with stream-of-consciousness traditions where syntactic overflow is the point.
**Score 0**: Complex sentences lose their subjects, pile up subordinate clauses without anchor points, or require multiple readings to parse. The reader loses the thread of the sentence before reaching its end. Syntactic complexity exists for its own sake rather than serving content.
**Score 5**: Complex sentences guide the reader through multiple ideas with clear structural cues. Subjects and verbs remain proximate. Subordinate clauses are positioned for clarity. The reader can follow even lengthy sentences without losing the thread. Complexity matches the complexity of the thought.
**Source**: https://www.wickedinkpublishing.com/the-red-pen-resources/the-art-of-line-editing-strengthening-your-prose-one-sentence-at-a-time
**Notes**: Wicked Ink: "Varying sentence length and structure for a natural, engaging rhythm." BubbleCow: "Put the subject and action near the start. Readers relax when they can see who's doing what."

### Fact-Checking for Fiction
**What it measures**: Whether real-world facts referenced in the fiction are accurate — historical dates, geographical details, scientific principles, cultural practices, and any other verifiable claims embedded in the narrative.
**Assumed goal**: Professional editing standards where factual accuracy in fiction supports verisimilitude — errors in verifiable facts break reader trust, even in genre fiction. This does not apply to deliberately counterfactual alternate history or magical realism.
**Score 0**: Real-world facts are incorrect: historical events are misdated, real locations are misdescribed, scientific principles are wrong, cultural practices are misrepresented. Contemporary objects appear in historical settings. The reader who knows the subject loses trust.
**Score 5**: Every verifiable real-world fact is accurate. Historical periods are correctly detailed. Geography is correct. Science follows actual principles (or the departure is acknowledged). Cultural practices are accurately represented. The knowledgeable reader finds no factual errors.
**Source**: https://diymfa.com/writing/copyediting-checklist/
**Notes**: DIY MFA: "Verify correct spellings of places and people names. Double-check dates. Verify statistics and definitions quickly." For fiction specifically: "checking timeline continuity and logic; for example, looking out for the mention of current everyday objects in historical works."

## Sentinels Found

### Em Dash Overuse
**Detection**: Count em dashes per 1000 words. If the rate exceeds 5-6 per 1000 words (roughly 20+ per average chapter), flag as potential autocomplete artifact.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs, particularly ChatGPT and instruction-tuned models, dramatically overuse em dashes as a structural crutch. The em dash becomes a one-size-fits-all tool for parenthetical asides, false nuance, and mansplaining. Plagiarism Today reports that "ChatGPT, Copilot and Deepseek all made heavy use of em dashes." The pattern is so characteristic it earned the nickname "the ChatGPT dash."
**Design space cost**: Legitimate fiction uses em dashes for interruption, parenthetical asides, and emphasis. Flagging em dash density risks penalizing authors like Emily Dickinson or contemporary literary fiction writers who use dashes heavily for stylistic reasons. The sentinel should track density, not mere presence.
**Source**: https://www.plagiarismtoday.com/2025/06/26/em-dashes-hyphens-and-spotting-ai-writing/

### "Serves As" / "Stands As" / Copula Avoidance
**Detection**: Search for the phrases "serves as," "stands as," "marks a," "represents a" used where simple "is" or "are" would suffice.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs systematically avoid basic copulas (is/are/was) in favor of pompous alternatives. Wikipedia's Signs of AI Writing documents this as a primary indicator: "substituting 'is/are' with 'serves as,' 'stands as,' 'marks,' 'represents'" and "using 'boasts/features/offers' instead of 'has.'" Tropes.fyi classifies this as "The 'Serves As' Dodge."
**Design space cost**: Fiction narrators sometimes use these constructions for rhythmic or formal effect. The sentinel should flag density and pattern, not isolated instances.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Signs_of_AI_writing

### Tricolon Abuse (Rule of Three Overuse)
**Detection**: Count instances where three parallel elements appear in sequence (whether words, phrases, clauses, or sentences). If tricolons appear more than 3-4 times per 1000 words, or if multiple tricolons cluster within a single paragraph, flag.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs overuse the rule-of-three pattern as a structural default. Tropes.fyi documents this: "A single tricolon is elegant; three back-to-back tricolons are a pattern recognition failure." The pattern is often extended to four or five items. It creates a "singsong" quality that signals AI composition.
**Design space cost**: Tricolons are a legitimate and ancient rhetorical device. Single instances are perfectly normal. The sentinel targets clustering and overuse, not the device itself.
**Source**: https://tropes.fyi/directory

### Negative Parallelism Pattern ("Not X — It's Y")
**Detection**: Search for the construction "It's not X — it's Y" or "Not X. Y." especially using em dashes. If this pattern appears more than once per 1000 words, flag.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Tropes.fyi identifies "Negative Parallelism" as the #1 AI writing trope: "The 'It's not X — it's Y' pattern using em dashes to create false profundity through reframing. Extremely common in AI output." Wikipedia similarly documents "Not X, but Y" as a primary AI writing indicator.
**Design space cost**: This construction exists in natural language and can be effective for genuine contrast. The sentinel targets repetitive, formulaic use rather than isolated rhetorical contrast.
**Source**: https://tropes.fyi/directory

### Self-Answering Rhetorical Questions ("The X? A Y.")
**Detection**: Search for the pattern of a short rhetorical question followed immediately by its one-word or one-phrase answer: "The result? Devastating." "The truth? Nobody cared."
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Tropes.fyi identifies this as a distinctive AI construction: "Self-posed rhetorical questions answered immediately, asking questions nobody asked for dramatic effect." This creates manufactured tension that reads as artificial.
**Design space cost**: Rhetorical questions answered in the text are a legitimate technique, but they should be rare and used for genuine emphasis. The sentinel targets the specific short-question-short-answer pattern, especially when repeated.
**Source**: https://tropes.fyi/directory

### AI Vocabulary Overuse ("Delve," "Tapestry," "Testament," "Vibrant," "Palpable")
**Detection**: Check for high-frequency co-occurrence of known AI-overused words: delve, tapestry, testament, vibrant, palpable, intricate, cacophony, nuanced, meticulous, pivotal, underscore, landscape, enduring, crucial, bolstered, fostering, showcasing. If 3+ of these words appear on the same page, or if any appears more than twice in the manuscript, flag.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: The PNAS study found instruction-tuned LLMs use specific words at 100-162x the human rate. GPT-4o uses "tapestry" at 155x the human rate, "camaraderie" at 162x, "amidst" at 100x. Wikipedia's Signs of AI Writing maintains an evolving list of era-specific AI vocabulary. Pangram Labs documents "serves as a testament" appearing 4,000x more frequently in AI text than human text.
**Design space cost**: These are real English words that human authors use. The sentinel should detect clustering and co-occurrence rather than penalizing isolated use. A single "delve" is not a tell; "delve" alongside "tapestry" and "palpable" is.
**Source**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11874169/

### Present Participial Clause Overuse
**Detection**: Count present participial phrases (opening -ing clauses) per 1000 words. If the rate exceeds approximately 8-10 per 1000 words (roughly 2-5x the human baseline), flag.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: The PNAS study found that instruction-tuned LLMs use present participial clauses at 2-5x the human rate. GPT-4o specifically uses them at 5.3x the human frequency (Cohen's d=1.38). This is one of the strongest statistical discriminators between LLM and human text.
**Design space cost**: Present participial clauses are a standard English construction. The sentinel targets unusual density, not presence. Some literary styles use participial openings heavily (e.g., Hemingway), but even those styles don't approach the LLM rate.
**Source**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11874169/

### Nominalization Overuse
**Detection**: Count nominalizations (nouns derived from verbs/adjectives: destruction, deforestation, pollution, implementation, utilization, consideration) per 1000 words. If the rate exceeds 1.5-2x the baseline for the genre, flag.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: The PNAS study found instruction-tuned LLMs use nominalizations at 1.5-2x the human rate. This creates an "informationally dense, noun-heavy style" that diverges from natural fiction prose. BubbleCow identifies nominalizations as a concision target: "'make a decision' -> 'decide,' 'conduct an analysis' -> 'analyze.'"
**Design space cost**: Academic and formal prose uses nominalizations legitimately. In fiction, the denser a passage's nominalization rate, the more it reads like non-fiction. The sentinel is most relevant for fiction that should have a conversational or narrative register.
**Source**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11874169/

### Low Burstiness (Uniform Sentence Length)
**Detection**: Compute the standard deviation of sentence lengths across a passage (e.g., 500 words). If the standard deviation is below a threshold (very uniform sentence lengths), flag.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: AI-generated text exhibits lower burstiness than human writing — sentence lengths cluster around a mean with little variation. Originality.AI: "Real people tend to write in bursts and lulls — we naturally switch things up. In contrast, AI models tend to be robotic and uniform." QuillBot: "A text with low burstiness might contain sentences that are all the same length and style."
**Design space cost**: Some fiction styles (e.g., early Hemingway, Cormac McCarthy's spare passages) deliberately use uniform short sentences. The sentinel should consider genre and style context. Low burstiness over extended passages (multiple pages) is more diagnostic than over a single paragraph.
**Source**: https://originality.ai/blog/perplexity-and-burstiness-in-writing

### "Here's the Kicker" / "Here's the Thing" Transition Pattern
**Detection**: Search for "Here's the kicker," "Here's the thing," "Here's where it gets interesting," "Here's what nobody talks about" and similar patterns.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Tropes.fyi classifies this as a distinctive AI trope: "False suspense transitions promising revelation but delivering unremarkable observations." These constructions are conversational-register attention-getters that LLMs use as structural transitions between paragraphs.
**Design space cost**: A first-person narrator with a conversational voice might legitimately use these phrases once or twice. The sentinel targets repeated use, especially in third-person narration where they break register.
**Source**: https://tropes.fyi/directory

### "Let's Break This Down" / Pedagogical Voice
**Detection**: Search for "Let's break this down," "Let's unpack," "Let's explore," "Let's dive in," "Think of it like," "Think of it as."
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Tropes.fyi: "Pedagogical hand-holding voice even for expert audiences." LLMs default to instructional/explanatory register even in creative contexts. In fiction, these phrases signal a narrator explaining to the reader rather than a character experiencing the world.
**Design space cost**: A teacher character or educational non-fiction voice might use these phrases. In most fiction narration, they are register-inappropriate and signal AI composition.
**Source**: https://tropes.fyi/directory

### Fractal Summaries (Redundant Intro/Conclusion)
**Detection**: Check whether chapters or sections begin by previewing their content and end by summarizing it, in addition to the actual content. "In this chapter, we'll explore..." followed by the content followed by "As we've seen..."
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Tropes.fyi: "Repetitive summarization at every document level: intro, section summaries, and conclusion." LLMs structure text like five-paragraph essays, with previews and summaries at every level. Fiction does not preview or summarize its own content.
**Design space cost**: Non-fiction legitimately uses this structure. In fiction, any instance is a strong tell.
**Source**: https://tropes.fyi/directory

### Bold-First Bullets / Markdown Formatting Artifacts
**Detection**: Check for markdown formatting that survives into prose: bold text used as inline headers, bullet points, numbered lists, Unicode arrows (→), or markdown syntax characters (* for emphasis, # for headers).
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Tropes.fyi: "Every bullet point starts with bolded text. Common in AI markdown but rare in human writing." Wikipedia identifies markdown syntax (asterisks for emphasis, brackets for links) as a direct AI artifact. Fiction manuscripts should contain no markdown.
**Design space cost**: Zero in fiction. Markdown formatting in a fiction manuscript is never intentional.
**Source**: https://tropes.fyi/directory

### "Quietly" / "Deeply" / "Fundamentally" — Magic Adverb Clustering
**Detection**: Check for co-occurrence of "quietly," "deeply," "fundamentally," "remarkably," "incredibly," "profoundly" within close proximity. If 3+ appear within a single page, flag.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Tropes.fyi: "Overuse of 'quietly,' 'deeply,' 'fundamentally,' 'remarkably' to artificially elevate mundane descriptions." These adverbs perform the work of making simple statements sound significant, which is a characteristic LLM pattern of inflating register.
**Design space cost**: Any single instance is fine. The sentinel targets clustering — multiple grandiose adverbs in proximity.
**Source**: https://tropes.fyi/directory

### Explicit Emotion Labeling (On-the-Nose Narration)
**Detection**: Search for direct emotional labels in narration: "She felt a wave of relief," "He was consumed by anger," "A sense of dread washed over her," "Joy filled his heart." Count instances per chapter.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: AI labels emotions rather than showing them through behavior, perception, and physical sensation. Future Fiction Academy: "Generic AI explains emotions explicitly." The pattern is: state the emotion by name rather than rendering the physical and behavioral correlates that allow the reader to infer it.
**Design space cost**: Even skilled human authors occasionally name emotions. The sentinel should target density — if every emotional moment is explicitly labeled rather than shown through behavior or sensation, that signals AI composition. Occasional labeling in omniscient narration is normal.
**Source**: https://futurefictionacademy.com/how-ai-writes-llm-for-authors/

### "Despite Its Challenges" / Formulaic Optimism
**Detection**: Search for "Despite [challenges/setbacks/difficulties]," "In the face of [adversity/challenges]," "Nevertheless, [subject] persevered/continued/thrived." Track occurrences per chapter.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Tropes.fyi: "Rigid formula acknowledging problems only to immediately dismiss them with optimism." Wikipedia: "Despite its [positive words], [subject] faces challenges..." This is RLHF-trained positivity bias manifesting as narrative structure — LLMs are trained to be helpful and optimistic, which bleeds into fiction.
**Design space cost**: Characters can be resilient, and narrators can acknowledge challenges. The sentinel targets the specific formulaic construction where challenges are acknowledged only to be immediately dismissed with positive framing.
**Source**: https://tropes.fyi/directory

### Vague Attribution ("Experts say," "It is often thought")
**Detection**: Search for "experts say," "experts argue," "observers note," "industry reports suggest," "it is often thought," "one could argue," "many believe." Any appearance in fiction narration (not dialogue) is a flag.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Tropes.fyi: "Invoking unnamed 'experts,' 'observers,' 'industry reports' without specificity." Wikipedia: "'Industry reports,' 'Observers have cited,' 'Experts argue.'" These constructions are from non-fiction AI output that leaks into fiction when the LLM shifts register.
**Design space cost**: A journalist character or non-fiction-adjacent narrator might use these. In most fiction narration, they are register-inappropriate and signal AI composition.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Signs_of_AI_writing

### "Newfound" / "Palpable" / "Interplay" — AI Signature Word Clustering
**Detection**: Check for co-occurrence of words that are individually unremarkable but collectively diagnostic: "newfound," "palpable," "interplay," "intricate," "fleeting," "unravel," "amidst," "cacophony." If 3+ appear within a single chapter, flag.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: The PNAS study documents these as dramatically overrepresented in LLM output. Pangram Labs: "'newfound sense of purpose' appears 4,000x more frequently in AI text." These words individually are legitimate but their co-occurrence at elevated rates is a statistical signature of LLM generation.
**Design space cost**: A literary author with a rich vocabulary might use several of these words. The sentinel targets co-occurrence density, not individual use.
**Source**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11874169/

### Repetitive Sentence Openings (Anaphora Without Purpose)
**Detection**: Count consecutive sentences beginning with the same word (typically "She," "He," "The," "It"). If 4+ consecutive sentences share the same opening word, flag. Also check paragraph openings — if 3+ consecutive paragraphs open with the same word, flag.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: While human authors occasionally fall into this pattern, LLMs do it systematically due to their token-by-token generation process. Sandra Gerth: "If more than two [paragraphs in a row] start with the same one, switch it up." The pattern is particularly characteristic when combined with uniform sentence length.
**Design space cost**: Deliberate anaphora is a powerful rhetorical device ("She couldn't move. She couldn't breathe. She couldn't think."). The sentinel should distinguish between intentional rhetorical repetition (usually 2-3 instances with dramatic purpose) and the monotonous 5-10 repetitions that signal uncontrolled generation.
**Source**: https://sandragerth.com/repetitive-sentence-and-paragraph-structure-in-your-writing/

### Invented Concept Labels ("The X Paradox," "The Y Trap")
**Detection**: Search for constructions like "the [noun] paradox," "the [noun] trap," "the [noun] divide," "the [noun] inversion," "the [noun] effect" that are presented as established concepts but appear to be coined in-text.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Tropes.fyi: "Inventing analytical-sounding compound terms presented as established concepts." LLMs generate these labels to give the appearance of analytical sophistication. In fiction, a narrator or character coining a concept-label is unusual; presenting an invented term as though it's well-known is a tell.
**Design space cost**: A character who is a scholar or analyst might coin terms. The sentinel targets terms presented as given knowledge rather than character invention.
**Source**: https://tropes.fyi/directory

### Grandiose Stakes Inflation
**Detection**: Check for phrases that inflate mundane events to world-historical significance: "fundamentally reshape," "change everything," "nothing would ever be the same," "a pivotal moment," "a turning point," "unlike anything before."
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Tropes.fyi: "Inflating every argument to world-historical significance." LLMs trained via RLHF tend to present everything as maximally important and significant. In fiction, this manifests as narration that tells the reader events are important rather than showing why they matter.
**Design space cost**: Fiction does have genuinely pivotal moments. The sentinel targets inflation of routine events, not genuine climactic moments. A story's actual climax may legitimately use this language; a character ordering coffee should not.
**Source**: https://tropes.fyi/directory

### Content Duplication (Repeated Passages)
**Detection**: Search for passages of 20+ words that appear verbatim or near-verbatim more than once in the manuscript. Exclude deliberate refrains, callbacks, or quoted speech.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Tropes.fyi: "Repeating entire sections verbatim when models lose track of previous content." LLMs generating long text can lose track of what they've already written and regenerate similar or identical passages. This is especially common across chapter boundaries.
**Design space cost**: Deliberate literary repetition (refrains, callbacks, motifs) exists. The sentinel should exclude clearly intentional repetition and focus on passages that appear to be independently generated descriptions of the same thing.
**Source**: https://tropes.fyi/directory

### Lack of Contractions in Dialogue
**Detection**: Check dialogue for absence of contractions where spoken English would naturally use them. If characters consistently say "I am," "do not," "cannot," "it is," "would not" rather than "I'm," "don't," "can't," "it's," "wouldn't," flag.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Early and safety-trained LLMs tend toward formal register even in dialogue, producing speech that sounds stiff and unnatural. While individual characters might speak formally for characterization reasons, universal formal dialogue across all characters signals AI generation.
**Design space cost**: Period fiction, formal characters, and non-native English speakers may speak without contractions. The sentinel should flag only when the pattern is universal across characters and scenes, with no variation.
**Source**: https://futurefictionacademy.com/how-ai-writes-llm-for-authors/

### Uniform Paragraph Length
**Detection**: Compute the standard deviation of paragraph lengths (in words) across a chapter. If paragraphs are uniformly similar in length (low standard deviation), flag.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Similar to uniform sentence length (low burstiness), uniform paragraph length signals automated generation. Human writers vary paragraph length instinctively — short paragraphs for tension, long paragraphs for reflection. LLMs tend to produce paragraphs of similar length.
**Design space cost**: Some literary styles maintain relatively uniform paragraph length. The sentinel should consider genre context and flag only when the uniformity persists across action, dialogue, and reflection scenes alike.
**Source**: https://originality.ai/blog/perplexity-and-burstiness-in-writing

### Listicle Structure in Prose ("The first... The second... The third...")
**Detection**: Search for sequential enumeration disguised as prose: "The first [thing]... The second... The third..." or "First,... Second,... Third,..." appearing in narration.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Tropes.fyi: "Lists disguised as prose using 'The first... The second... The third...' formatting." LLMs default to structured enumeration even in creative contexts. Fiction narration almost never explicitly numbers its points.
**Design space cost**: A character giving instructions or a narrator deliberately listing options might use this structure. The sentinel targets the pattern appearing in general narration where it signals non-fiction register.
**Source**: https://tropes.fyi/directory

### "It's Worth Noting" / Transitional Throat-Clearing
**Detection**: Search for "It's worth noting," "It bears mentioning," "Importantly," "Interestingly," "Notably," "Furthermore," "Moreover," "In addition" in narration.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Tropes.fyi: "Signaling filler transitions." Wikipedia identifies these as characteristic AI vocabulary. These are non-fiction transition phrases that LLMs use in all contexts. Fiction narration does not announce that something is "worth noting" — it simply narrates.
**Design space cost**: A narrator with a conversational or essayistic voice might use one of these occasionally. The sentinel targets multiple instances, especially in third-person narration.
**Source**: https://tropes.fyi/directory
