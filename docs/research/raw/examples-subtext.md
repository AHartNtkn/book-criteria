# Subtext and Show-Don't-Tell: Prose Exemplars

Three passages from published fiction demonstrating subtext, indirection, and emotional meaning conveyed through behavior and dialogue rather than narration.

---

## 1. Ernest Hemingway, "Hills Like White Elephants" (1927)

> "It's really an awfully simple operation, Jig," the man said. "It's not really an operation at all."
>
> The girl looked at the ground the table legs rested on.
>
> "I know you wouldn't mind it, Jig. It's really not anything. It's just to let the air in."
>
> The girl did not say anything.
>
> "I'll go with you and I'll stay with you all the time. They just let the air in and then it's all perfectly natural."
>
> "Then what will we do afterward?"
>
> "We'll be fine afterward. Just like we were before."
>
> "What makes you think so?"
>
> "That's the only thing that bothers us. It's the only thing that's made us unhappy."

**What makes the subtext work:** The word "abortion" never appears; the man's euphemisms ("simple operation," "just to let the air in") reveal his desperation to minimize what he's asking, while the girl's silence and her gaze at the ground carry all the resistance and grief she cannot or will not voice directly.

Source: Ernest Hemingway, "Hills Like White Elephants," *Men Without Women* (Scribner's, 1927). Full text available at [American Literature](https://americanliterature.com/author/ernest-hemingway/short-story/hills-like-white-elephants) and [University of Virginia Anthology](https://anthologydev.lib.virginia.edu/work/Hemingway/hemingway-hills).

## 2. Kazuo Ishiguro, *The Remains of the Day* (1989)

> "But that doesn't mean to say, of course, there aren't occasions now and then -- extremely desolate occasions -- when you think to yourself: 'What a terrible mistake I've made with my life.' And you get to thinking about a different life, a better life you might have had. For instance, I get to thinking about a life I may have had with you, Mr Stevens. And I suppose that's when I get angry over some trivial little thing and leave. But each time I do so, I realize before long -- my rightful place is with my husband."
>
> [Stevens narrates:] "Indeed -- why should I not admit it? -- at that moment, my heart was breaking."

**What makes the subtext work:** Miss Kenton confesses and then immediately retracts, burying the admission inside a qualification ("my rightful place is with my husband"). Stevens, who has spent the entire novel suppressing feeling behind professional duty, lets a single sentence of raw emotion escape -- then never acts on it. The gap between what both characters feel and what they permit themselves to say is the entire tragedy, and neither the narrator nor the text explains this; the reader must feel it.

Source: Kazuo Ishiguro, *The Remains of the Day* (Faber and Faber, 1989), "Day Six -- Evening / Weymouth" chapter. Passage confirmed via [SparkNotes analysis](https://www.sparknotes.com/lit/remains/section7/) and [SparkNotes quotes](https://www.sparknotes.com/lit/remains/quotes/page/5/).

## 3. Raymond Carver, "Cathedral" (1983)

> "Close your eyes now," the blind man said to me.
>
> I did it. I closed them just like he said.
>
> "Are they closed?" he said. "Don't fudge."
>
> "They're closed," I said.
>
> "Keep them that way," he said. He said, "Don't stop now. Draw."
>
> So we kept on with it. His fingers rode my fingers as my hand went over the paper. It was like nothing else in my life up to now.
>
> Then he said, "I think that's it. I think you got it," he said. "Take a look. What do you think?"
>
> But I had my eyes closed. I thought I'd keep them that way for a little longer. I thought it was something I ought to do.
>
> "Well?" he said. "Are you looking?"
>
> My eyes were still closed. I was in my house. I knew that. But I didn't feel like I was inside anything.
>
> "It's really something," I said.

**What makes the subtext work:** The narrator -- jealous, inarticulate, closed-off for the entire story -- chooses to keep his eyes shut after the exercise is done, voluntarily entering the blind man's world. He never names what he feels; "It's really something" is as far as his vocabulary stretches. But the physical choice (eyes staying closed) and the spatial dislocation ("I didn't feel like I was inside anything") convey a transformation that the narrator himself cannot articulate, forcing the reader to supply the emotional weight.

Source: Raymond Carver, "Cathedral," *Cathedral* (Knopf, 1983). Ending passage confirmed via [GradeSaver quotes](https://www.gradesaver.com/cathedral/study-guide/quotes), [Goodreads quotes](https://www.goodreads.com/work/quotes/346642-cathedral), and [SparkNotes analysis](https://www.sparknotes.com/short-stories/cathedral/section2/).
