## Criteria Found

### Prose Transparency (Windowpane Quality)
**What it measures**: Whether the prose functions as an invisible medium through which the reader perceives the story directly, rather than as an opaque surface calling attention to itself.
**Assumed goal**: Orwellian transparent prose tradition (Orwell, Hemingway, Leonard) where "good prose should be transparent, like a window pane" -- maximalist/lyrical traditions (Woolf, Faulkner, Morrison) deliberately treat prose as a stained-glass window with its own aesthetic value.
**Score 0**: Prose constantly calls attention to itself through ornate phrasing, conspicuous vocabulary, or elaborate constructions. The reader is aware of the author's presence on every page. Language obscures rather than reveals the story.
**Score 5**: Prose is invisible. The reader forgets they are reading words and experiences the story directly. Language serves as a perfectly clear medium, never drawing attention away from character, action, or meaning. The author achieves Leonard's standard: "If it sounds like writing, I rewrite it."
**Source**: [Orwellian versus stained-glass window prose - RLF](https://rlfconsultants.com/orwellian-versus-stained-glass-window-prose/); [Elmore Leonard: 10 Rules of Writing](https://fs.blog/elmore-leonard-10-rules-of-writing/)
**Notes**: Orwell's metaphor: "Good prose should be transparent, like a window pane." The reader shouldn't notice the prose any more than someone notices glass when looking through a window. Leonard's meta-rule: "If it sounds like writing, I rewrite it." The transparent tradition prioritizes story immersion over prose-as-art-object. This is a deliberate aesthetic choice, not mere absence of skill.

### Authorial Invisibility
**What it measures**: Whether the author remains invisible behind the narrative, or whether the author's presence, opinions, technique, or ego intrude upon the story.
**Assumed goal**: Flaubertian/Hemingway/Leonard tradition of the effaced author where the craftsperson hides behind the work -- metafictional and essayistic traditions (Barth, Calvino, Didion) deliberately foreground authorial presence as part of the aesthetic.
**Score 0**: The author is visibly present on every page -- through conspicuous technique, visible manipulation of characters, didactic commentary, self-indulgent description, or obvious thematic signposting. The reader constantly feels the puppeteer behind the story.
**Score 5**: The author has vanished entirely. Characters feel autonomous, events feel inevitable rather than engineered, and no sentence reveals the craftsperson's hand. The reader experiences the fictional world as self-existing rather than constructed.
**Source**: [12 Ways to Be an Invisible Writer - Storm Writing School](https://stormwritingschool.com/avoid-writerly-writing/)
**Notes**: Storm Writing School identifies 12 specific disruptions: cliches, pacing issues, setup/payoff problems, excessive portraiture, overplaying, telegraphing, info dumps, clumsy interiority, inconsistent tone, anachronism, didactic moments, and inconsistent character behavior. "Once the author becomes visible, the enchantment of the story dissolves."

### The Vivid and Continuous Fictional Dream
**What it measures**: Whether the prose maintains an uninterrupted dream-state in the reader's mind, or whether craft failures break the spell.
**Assumed goal**: John Gardner's framework (The Art of Fiction) where fiction creates "a vivid and continuous fictional dream" -- experimental and postmodern traditions (Barthelme, Pynchon) deliberately disrupt the fictional dream as an aesthetic strategy.
**Score 0**: The fictional dream is constantly interrupted. Clumsy prose, factual errors, inconsistent diction, accidental rhymes, overloaded sentences, jarring shifts in psychic distance, and technical errors repeatedly break immersion. The reader is pulled out of the story on every page.
**Score 5**: The reader enters a sustained dream-state, losing awareness of the act of reading. Prose vividness comes from concrete sensory detail; continuity comes from the absence of anything that distracts. The dream persists unbroken from beginning to end.
**Source**: [The Vivid and Continuous Dream - Barry Donaldson](https://medium.com/the-craft-of-fiction/the-vivid-and-continuous-dream-ffefb617fbb4)
**Notes**: Gardner identified three components: vivid (powerful feelings or strong, clear images), continuous (uninterrupted by errors or technical breaks), and the dream (absorption so deep the reader loses track of time). Dream-breakers include: POV shifts, factual errors, font/format inconsistencies, typos, inconsistencies, accidental rhymes, and overloaded sentences. "Failing to achieve the vivid and continuous dream makes fiction fundamentally bad" in Gardner's framework.

### Iceberg Principle (Strategic Omission)
**What it measures**: Whether the prose achieves depth through deliberate omission of information the author knows, creating subtext and emotional resonance beneath a sparse surface.
**Assumed goal**: Hemingway's theory of omission / iceberg theory where "the dignity of movement of an ice-berg is due to only one-eighth of it being above water" -- maximalist traditions (Dostoevsky, Proust, David Foster Wallace) achieve depth through exhaustive inclusion rather than strategic omission.
**Score 0**: Everything is on the surface. The author states every emotion, explains every motivation, provides every backstory detail, and leaves nothing for the reader to infer. Alternatively, information is omitted not from knowledge but from ignorance, creating "hollow places" rather than resonant depth.
**Score 5**: The visible story is the tip of a vast iceberg. The author knows everything about the characters, world, and themes but has strategically withheld most of it. The reader feels the weight of the unstated -- emotions, histories, and meanings -- operating powerfully beneath the surface. Omission comes from knowledge, not ignorance, and strengthens rather than weakens the work.
**Source**: [Iceberg theory - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iceberg_theory); [Hemingway's Iceberg Theory - Hunting the Muse](https://huntingthemuse.net/library/hemingways-iceberg-theory)
**Notes**: Hemingway in Death in the Afternoon: "If a writer of prose knows enough of what he is writing about he may omit things that he knows and the reader, if the writer is writing truly enough, will have a feeling of those things as strongly as though the writer had stated them." Carlos Baker noted Hemingway learned "how to get the most from the least, how to prune language and avoid waste motion, how to multiply intensities, and how to tell nothing but the truth in a way that allowed for telling more than the truth." Jenna Blum: "the knowledge that you have about your character that never makes it into the story acts as the bulk of the iceberg. And that is what gives your story weight and gravitas."

### Word Economy and Compression
**What it measures**: Whether every word in the prose earns its place, with no unnecessary words, phrases, or sentences padding the text.
**Assumed goal**: Strunk and White / Hemingway / Carver tradition where "a sentence should contain no unnecessary words, a paragraph no unnecessary sentences, for the same reason that a drawing should have no unnecessary lines and a machine no unnecessary parts" -- expansive literary traditions (Melville, Woolf) use deliberate digression and accumulation as aesthetic strategies.
**Score 0**: Prose is bloated with unnecessary words, redundant phrases, padding, and filler. Sentences could be cut in half without losing meaning. Paragraphs meander without purpose. The reader encounters "thick paragraphs of prose you can see have too many words in them" (Leonard).
**Score 5**: Every word serves a purpose. Prose has been compressed to its essential form without sacrificing clarity, rhythm, or emotional impact. The writing demonstrates "ruthless efficiency" -- meticulously pruned of anything superfluous, creating lean, muscular narrative where each sentence is packed with meaning.
**Source**: [The Elements of Style - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Elements_of_Style); [Compression as Craft - Good River Review](https://www.goodriverreview.com/post/compression-as-craft-what-fiction-writers-can-learn-from-poets)
**Notes**: Strunk's principle: "Omit needless words." Compression as described by Good River Review means "the deliberate distillation of language so that a single word, image, or syntactic choice carries more than one burden." Writing under compression "forces each word to earn its place" and "pressure sharpens resonance and clarifies intention." Vonnegut: "If a sentence, no matter how excellent, does not illuminate your subject in some new and useful way, scratch it out."

### Strong Verbs and Precise Nouns
**What it measures**: Whether the prose relies on specific, active verbs and precise nouns to convey meaning, rather than leaning on adjectives, adverbs, and modifiers as crutches.
**Assumed goal**: Strunk and White / King / Hemingway verb-centric prose tradition where strong verbs and precise nouns carry the weight -- lyrical/impressionistic traditions may legitimately foreground adjectival density for atmosphere and sensory immersion.
**Score 0**: Prose is dominated by weak verbs (was, had, seemed, appeared) modified by adverbs, and generic nouns propped up by adjective chains. "He walked slowly" instead of "he ambled." "The big, scary, dark house" instead of "the ruin." Modifiers do work that verbs and nouns should handle.
**Score 5**: Verbs are specific and active, conveying action and emotion in a single word. Nouns are precise enough to stand alone without modifiers. Adverbs are rare; adjectives are deployed only when no stronger noun exists. The prose achieves King's ideal: "The adverb is not your friend."
**Source**: [9 Tips For Writing Perfect Prose - Jericho Writers](https://jerichowriters.com/prose-style/); [Strong Verbs - Fictionary](https://fictionary.co/journal/strong-verbs/)
**Notes**: Jericho Writers: "Every sentence needs nouns and verbs, while adjectives are definitely optional." Instead of "ran quickly," use "sprinted." Instead of "said loudly," use "called." King in On Writing: counsels against -ly adverbs especially in dialogue attribution. Orwell's Rule 3: "If it is possible to cut a word out, always cut it out."

### Active Voice Dominance
**What it measures**: Whether the prose predominantly uses active voice constructions that create clarity, directness, and forward momentum, deploying passive voice only when it serves a specific purpose.
**Assumed goal**: Orwell / Strunk and White active-voice tradition prioritizing clarity and directness -- certain literary effects (mystery, bureaucratic voice, deliberate distancing) legitimately employ passive constructions.
**Score 0**: Passive voice dominates the prose, creating vagueness, removing agency from characters, and producing a sluggish, indirect reading experience. "The door was opened by him" instead of "He opened the door." Subjects receive actions rather than performing them.
**Score 5**: Active voice predominates at roughly 80-20 ratio. Characters act rather than being acted upon. Passive constructions appear only when deliberately chosen for emphasis, tone, or narrative strategy. The prose has directness and drive.
**Source**: [Fiction University: The Real Problem With Passive Voice](http://blog.janicehardy.com/2009/05/passive-aggression.html); [Active vs Passive Voice - The Novelry](https://www.thenovelry.com/blog/active-voice-vs-passive-voice)
**Notes**: Orwell's Rule 4: "Never use the passive where you can use the active." Fiction University notes the real problem isn't grammar but clarity: "Active voice sentences tend to be simpler and more direct." However, passive voice can serve emphasis -- "the last phrase of a sentence has the strongest emphasis" so changing order via passive can highlight the most important information.

### Sentence Rhythm and Variation
**What it measures**: Whether the prose varies sentence length and structure to create a pleasing, functional rhythm that controls pacing, emphasis, and reader engagement.
**Assumed goal**: Le Guin / Woolf rhythmic prose tradition where "style is a very simple matter; it is all rhythm. Once you get that, you can't use the wrong words" -- deliberately monotone or flat-affect prose (some Carver, Beckett) may use rhythmic uniformity as an aesthetic strategy.
**Score 0**: Sentences are monotonously uniform in length and structure. Either all short and choppy (creating a hammering effect) or all long and complex (creating a droning effect). No variation creates a deadening reading experience. The prose stutters rather than flows.
**Score 5**: Sentence lengths vary purposefully. Short sentences punctuate for impact; longer sentences develop ideas and create breathing room. The prose has a musical quality -- ebb and flow, tension and release. Rhythm matches emotional content: terse in crisis, expansive in reflection. The reader moves through prose "smoothly step by step."
**Source**: [Incorporating Rhythm in Prose Style - Story in Literary Fiction](https://www.storyinliteraryfiction.com/essays-on-writing/incorporating-rhythm-in-prose-style/); [9 Tips For Writing Perfect Prose - Jericho Writers](https://jerichowriters.com/prose-style/)
**Notes**: Virginia Woolf: "Style is a very simple matter; it is all rhythm. Once you get that, you can't use the wrong words." Le Guin: "The chief duty of a narrative sentence is to lead to the next sentence." Rhythm is controlled primarily by "hearing the prose -- by listening to it." Five signs of failed rhythm: reading interrupted by stuttering effect, comprehension difficulty despite clear sentences, loss of smooth progression, discordant images, and non-sequiturs.

### Paratactic Clarity
**What it measures**: Whether the prose effectively uses parataxis (short, independent clauses placed side by side without subordinating conjunctions) to create immediacy, objectivity, and emotional resonance through juxtaposition.
**Assumed goal**: Hemingway / hard-boiled tradition using parataxis for stark immediacy and subtext -- hypotactic traditions (James, Proust) achieve depth through subordination and syntactic complexity.
**Score 0**: Prose is either monotonously paratactic (becoming a grocery list) or overwhelmed by subordination (burying meaning in nested clauses). If paratactic, it parodies Hemingway rather than achieving his effect. If hypotactic, it loses the reader in syntactic tangles.
**Score 5**: Paratactic constructions create genuine power. Short declarative sentences build through juxtaposition to create emotional intensity that feels objective rather than manipulative. The reader infers connections between sentences, creating "mimetic response" where meaning arises from the gaps. Equal-weight sentences convey subtext through omission.
**Source**: [Hemingway and Parataxis - TSS Publishing](https://theshortstory.co.uk/hemingway-and-parataxis-by-peter-jordan/)
**Notes**: Peter Jordan: Parataxis "mirrors rapid, unsettled thought patterns, forcing readers to interpret meaning unconsciously rather than being told directly." The power: "when readers unconsciously interpret text and emotion rises, the reader is not entirely sure why they feel the way they do. This is incredibly powerful." Richard Lanham: "It is easy to write a laundry list, but not so easy to write like Hemingway without falling into parody."

### Concrete Specificity
**What it measures**: Whether the prose uses specific, concrete, sensory details rather than abstract generalities to create vivid experiences and convey meaning.
**Assumed goal**: Chekhov / Hemingway / Gardner concrete-detail tradition where "fiction occurs when the particular penetrates the general" -- philosophical and essayistic fiction (Musil, Kundera) may operate primarily at the level of abstraction.
**Score 0**: Prose relies on abstract generalizations. "She was sad." "The house was threatening." "He felt afraid." Emotions are named rather than evoked. Settings are described in generic terms. The reader must supply all concrete detail from imagination because the author provides none.
**Score 5**: Every abstraction is grounded in concrete, sensory detail. Emotions emerge from specific physical actions and environmental details. "No reader is ever scared by the word 'afraid'" -- instead, the prose provides enough concrete detail to make the reader conclude the character is afraid. Specificity creates both vividness and universality.
**Source**: [Big 'A' Little 'a' - LitReactor](https://litreactor.com/columns/big-a-little-a-writing-between-the-concrete-and-abstract)
**Notes**: Jon Gingerich: "Fiction occurs when the particular penetrates the general." Saying a character is afraid "attempts to evoke the abstraction by naming it" but fails because "no reader is ever scared by the word 'afraid.'" The writer should provide concrete details making the reader conclude the character is afraid. The grand theme should exist "like a ghost, something perhaps tangentially acknowledged but otherwise a silent concept." Working general-to-specific (announcing theme first) reads as "amateurish and grossly disengaging."

### Showing vs. Telling (Appropriate Balance)
**What it measures**: Whether the prose appropriately balances dramatized scenes (showing) with efficient summary (telling), using each where it serves the story best.
**Assumed goal**: Chekhov / Hemingway / modern creative writing tradition where dramatized action conveys character and theme -- some cultural storytelling traditions and genre conventions legitimately rely more heavily on direct narration and telling.
**Score 0**: Either the prose tells everything (naming emotions, explaining motivations, summarizing events that should be dramatized) or it shows everything (dramatizing every minor transition, bogging the story in unnecessary scenes, exhausting the reader with constant immersion).
**Score 5**: Important moments are dramatized through action, dialogue, and sensory detail. Transitions and background are told efficiently. The balance serves pacing: showing for emotional peaks, telling for necessary connective tissue. The writer knows "sometimes a writer tells as a shortcut, to move quickly to the meaty part of the story" (James Scott Bell).
**Source**: [Show, don't tell - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Show,_don't_tell)
**Notes**: Chekhov: "In descriptions of Nature one must seize on small details, grouping them so that when the reader closes his eyes he gets a picture." Palahniuk's extension: avoid "thought verbs" (thinks, knows, understands, realizes, believes, wants, remembers) in favor of "specific sensory detail: action, smell, taste, sound, and feeling." Bell's nuance: "If you try to do it constantly, the parts that are supposed to stand out won't, and your readers will get exhausted."

### Dialogue Invisibility (Said-Tag Discipline)
**What it measures**: Whether dialogue attribution is invisible and unobtrusive, allowing the dialogue itself to carry tone and character, rather than attribution verbs and adverbs doing the emotional work.
**Assumed goal**: Leonard / King minimalist dialogue-tag tradition where "said" is invisible and dialogue carries its own weight -- some literary traditions (19th century, certain genre fiction) use varied attribution as part of the narrative voice.
**Score 0**: Every dialogue tag is a creative verb (grumbled, exclaimed, interjected, hissed, barked) modified by adverbs ("she said gravely," "he admonished sternly"). Attribution tells the reader how to interpret dialogue the writer didn't trust to convey its own tone. Tags draw constant attention to themselves.
**Score 5**: "Said" and "asked" predominate, functioning as invisible markers. Many exchanges use action beats or no attribution at all. Dialogue conveys its own tone through word choice, rhythm, and context. The reader hears characters speaking; the machinery of attribution vanishes.
**Source**: [Elmore Leonard: 10 Rules of Writing](https://fs.blog/elmore-leonard-10-rules-of-writing/); [Should You Use Dialogue Tags Other Than Said? - Storm Writing School](https://stormwritingschool.com/synonyms-for-said/)
**Notes**: Leonard's Rule 3: "Never use a verb other than 'said' to carry dialogue." Rule 4: "Never use adverbs to modify 'said'" -- calling this "a mortal sin." "Said" is invisible because readers process it as a function word, not a content word. Alternatives like "spluttered," "scoffed," "demanded" are noticeable and "tell the reader what to think." Using neutral tags "forces the work to happen in the dialogue itself."

### Emotional Restraint and Understatement
**What it measures**: Whether the prose achieves emotional power through restraint and understatement rather than through overwrought emotional display and explicit sentiment.
**Assumed goal**: Hemingway / Chekhov / Ishiguro restraint tradition where "understatement compresses emotional force rather than suppressing it" -- romantic and expressionist traditions legitimately amplify emotional display for intensity.
**Score 0**: Every emotional moment is overplayed. Characters weep, tremble, and gasp at every turn. The narrator tells the reader what to feel. Sentimentality substitutes for genuine emotion. The prose tries to make the reader cry through force rather than craft.
**Score 5**: Emotional power comes from what is not said. Characters process grief through mundane action. Devastating revelations arrive in simple language. The reader feels emotion rising from below the surface precisely because the prose refuses to push it. "A little bit of restraint twists the heart strings and is more accessible to the human condition."
**Source**: [The Power of Understatement in Fiction Writing - Carmel Bird](https://janefriedman.com/understatement/); [The Art of Restraint: Writing Lessons from Ishiguro](https://www.gilliamwritersgroup.com/blog/the-art-of-restraint-writing-lessons-from-kazuo-ishiguro)
**Notes**: Carmel Bird: "You tell the reader less so that the reader knows more." Strunk and White: "It is seldom advisable to tell all." Ishiguro's narrators speak "in an understated, almost formal manner, even when confronting jarring emotional truths." The Swedish Academy on Ishiguro: his work uncovers "the abyss beneath our illusory sense of connection with the world." Chekhov: "suffer together with your heroes, but I consider one must do this so that the reader does not notice it. The more objective, the stronger will be the effect."

### Chekhov's Objectivity
**What it measures**: Whether the prose presents characters and situations with objective neutrality, allowing readers to form their own judgments rather than having the author's judgments imposed.
**Assumed goal**: Chekhov's total objectivity principle where the writer "accused nobody, justified nobody" -- morally committed traditions (Dickens, Hugo, much contemporary literary fiction) use authorial judgment as a feature, not a bug.
**Score 0**: The author's moral judgments pervade the text. Characters are transparently heroes or villains by authorial fiat. The narrator editorializes about character behavior. Reader interpretation is foreclosed by authorial authority.
**Score 5**: The author presents situations with scientific neutrality. Characters are neither condemned nor excused. Actions speak for themselves. The reader must evaluate moral dimensions independently. The prose achieves Chekhov's standard: "only an objective observer" who shows rather than judges.
**Source**: [Anton Chekhov's 6 Rules for a Great Story - The Marginalian](https://www.themarginalian.org/2018/10/03/chekhov-6-rules/); [Analysis of Anton Chekhov's Stories - Literariness](https://literariness.org/2019/09/28/analysis-of-anton-chekhovs-stories/)
**Notes**: Chekhov's six rules include "total objectivity" and "truthful descriptions of persons and objects." He insisted on "an almost scientific objectivity in his depiction of character and events." Chekhov: "The more objective, the stronger will be the effect." Yet he also required "compassion" -- objectivity paired with empathy, not cold detachment.

### Trusting the Reader (Anti-Overexplanation)
**What it measures**: Whether the prose respects reader intelligence by allowing them to infer meaning from action and context, rather than spelling out every implication, emotion, and connection.
**Assumed goal**: Hemingway / Carver / modern literary fiction tradition where readers are "active collaborators" -- genre fiction targeting younger or broader audiences may legitimately require more explicit guidance.
**Score 0**: The prose explains everything. After showing a character slam a door, the narrator adds "She was angry." After establishing subtext through dialogue, the next paragraph explains what the subtext meant. Every metaphor is decoded. Every implication is stated. The reader is treated as incapable of inference.
**Score 5**: The prose trusts readers completely. Subtext remains subtext. Implications are left for discovery. "The moment you explain the subtext, it stops being subtext." Actions and dialogue convey emotional states without narratorial commentary. The reader feels smart because they are allowed to participate in meaning-making.
**Source**: [Most Common Writing Mistakes: Overexplaining](https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/most-common-mistakes-surefire-sign/); [Trust the Reader - Seth Harwood](https://writewithseth.com/trust-the-reader-writing/)
**Notes**: Weiland: "Overexplaining is often symptomatic of authorial insecurity." Harwood: "Fiction works through participation -- the reader is an active collaborator. When you leave room for that collaboration, readers lean forward; when you fill every gap with explanation, readers lean back." Hemingway in Hunting the Muse: "The writer must keep his mouth shut when the story is being told."

### Vocabulary Simplicity and Precision
**What it measures**: Whether the prose uses simple, common words chosen for their precision rather than elaborate vocabulary chosen for its impressiveness.
**Assumed goal**: Orwell / Hemingway / Vonnegut plain-diction tradition where short words are preferred and vocabulary serves meaning not display -- baroque prose traditions (Nabokov, Updike) treat vocabulary richness as an aesthetic pleasure in itself.
**Score 0**: Prose is stuffed with Latinate vocabulary, jargon, and conspicuously impressive words. "Utilize" instead of "use." "Ameliorate" instead of "improve." The author dresses up vocabulary to beautify prose, producing what King calls "stuffy and awkward" writing. Words are chosen for their sound-of-intelligence rather than their fit.
**Score 5**: The first word that comes to mind is used if it is appropriate and accurate. Short, common words predominate. When a complex word is the most precise choice, it appears naturally and without self-consciousness. The prose achieves Vonnegut's standard: "simplicity of language is not only reputable, but perhaps even sacred."
**Source**: [How to Write with Style: Kurt Vonnegut](https://www.themarginalian.org/2013/01/14/how-to-write-with-style-kurt-vonnegut/); [Politics and the English Language - Orwell Foundation](https://www.orwellfoundation.com/the-orwell-foundation/orwell/essays-and-other-works/politics-and-the-english-language/)
**Notes**: Orwell's Rule 2: "Never use a long word where a short one will do." Rule 5: "Never use a foreign phrase, a scientific word, or a jargon word if you can think of an everyday English equivalent." King: "Use the first word that comes to your mind, as long as it is appropriate and colorful." Vonnegut: Shakespeare and Joyce "wrote sentences which were almost childlike when their subjects were most profound." "The Bible opens with a sentence well within the writing skills of a lively fourteen-year-old."

### Metaphor and Figurative Language Freshness
**What it measures**: Whether figurative language is original and necessary rather than cliched, decorative, or reflexive.
**Assumed goal**: Orwell / Hemingway anti-cliche tradition where every metaphor must be freshly minted and earn its place -- poetic prose traditions (McCarthy, Morrison) deploy figurative language more densely as an essential texture.
**Score 0**: Prose is littered with dead metaphors ("ring the changes on," "stand shoulder to shoulder with"), mixed metaphors that signal the writer "is not interested in what he is saying" (Orwell), and ornamental similes that add nothing to meaning. Figurative language is reflexive decoration rather than illumination.
**Score 5**: Every metaphor or simile is original, precise, and necessary. Figurative language illuminates something that literal language cannot. Dead metaphors have been replaced with fresh observations. The prose uses figurative language sparingly enough that when it appears, it resonates.
**Source**: [Politics and the English Language - Orwell Foundation](https://www.orwellfoundation.com/the-orwell-foundation/orwell/essays-and-other-works/politics-and-the-english-language/); [9 Tips For Writing Perfect Prose - Jericho Writers](https://jerichowriters.com/prose-style/)
**Notes**: Orwell's Rule 1: "Never use a metaphor, simile, or other figure of speech which you are used to seeing in print." Orwell's diagnosis: dying metaphors "have lost all evocative power and are merely used because they save people the trouble of inventing phrases for themselves." Jericho Writers example of failed accuracy: "She lay in the early morning light listening to the roar of traffic softly rising like mist" -- traffic that roars cannot rise softly, the words contradict each other.

### Psychic Distance Control
**What it measures**: Whether the prose manages the reader's felt distance from the characters and events with deliberate control, moving smoothly between close interiority and distant overview as the story requires.
**Assumed goal**: Gardner's psychic distance framework where smooth transitions between levels create immersive fiction -- fixed-distance experimental prose (some Robbe-Grillet, camera-eye narration) deliberately maintains a single psychic distance as an aesthetic strategy.
**Score 0**: Psychic distance lurches unpredictably. The prose jumps from omniscient overview to deep interiority without transition. Or it remains stuck at a single distance regardless of scene requirements -- distant when intimacy is needed, or breathlessly close when overview would serve better.
**Score 5**: Psychic distance shifts smoothly and purposefully. Close distance for emotional peaks, moderate distance for most scenes, overview distance for transitions and context. Transitions between levels feel natural, moving "one level at a time" so the process is "smooth and gradual." The reader is always at the right distance from the action.
**Source**: [Psychic Distance in Creative Writing - UNR](https://www.unr.edu/writing-speaking-center/writing-speaking-resources/psychic-distance-in-creative-writing); [Narrating Deep or Shallow - Storm Writing School](https://stormwritingschool.com/psychic-distance/)
**Notes**: Gardner introduced the concept of psychic distance as "the distance that the reader feels between himself and the events of the story." Five levels: from most distant ("It was winter of the year 1853. A large man stepped out of a doorway.") to closest (stream-of-consciousness interior monologue). Most modern fiction "spends most of its time sliding around in the middle distances." Shifts in psychic distance that are too abrupt disrupt Gardner's fictional dream.

### Sentence-Level Pacing
**What it measures**: Whether sentence construction (length, structure, word choice) matches and reinforces the emotional and narrative pace of the scene.
**Assumed goal**: Prose craft tradition (Le Guin, King, Hemingway) where sentence construction is a pacing instrument -- some experimental prose deliberately decouples sentence rhythm from narrative pace for ironic or defamiliarizing effect.
**Score 0**: Sentence construction works against the scene. Action sequences are described in long, winding sentences that drain urgency. Quiet, reflective moments are rendered in short, clipped sentences that create false tension. The prose rhythm contradicts the emotional content.
**Score 5**: Short sentences in action sequences create urgency and impact. Longer sentences in reflective passages create contemplation and breathing room. Monosyllabic words with hard consonants accelerate pace; polysyllabic words with soft sounds decelerate. The prose's rhythm is a precision instrument matching and reinforcing the story's emotional dynamics.
**Source**: [Pacing in Writing - bibisco](https://bibisco.com/blog/pacing-in-writing-a-guide-for-fiction-authors/); [Incorporating Rhythm in Prose Style](https://www.storyinliteraryfiction.com/essays-on-writing/incorporating-rhythm-in-prose-style/)
**Notes**: bibisco: "Shorter sentences and paragraphs tend to create a faster pace, while longer ones slow it down." Word-level: "Monosyllabic words with hard consonant sounds feel faster and more violent, while polysyllabic words with softer sounds feel slower and more thoughtful." The relationship between sentence construction and pacing creates what one source calls "pressure -- the art of controlling the reader's emotional experience."

### Dialogue Naturalness
**What it measures**: Whether dialogue sounds like real people speaking in compressed, heightened form -- naturalistic enough to be believable but crafted enough to serve the narrative.
**Assumed goal**: Leonard / Hemingway / Carver naturalistic dialogue tradition where speech patterns reveal character -- stylized dialogue traditions (Mamet, Coward, some literary fiction) deliberately heighten speech beyond naturalism for aesthetic effect.
**Score 0**: Dialogue is either completely artificial (no human speaks this way, characters deliver exposition in complete paragraphs) or slavishly realistic (every um, uh, and false start of actual speech, becoming tedious). Characters all sound the same regardless of background or personality.
**Score 5**: Each character has a distinctive voice with recognizable verbal tics, vocabulary level, and speech rhythms. Dialogue sounds natural while serving narrative purpose. Exposition is woven invisibly into conversation rather than delivered as information dumps. The writer achieves "dialogue that is realistic enough that it makes characters feel believable but doesn't distract the reader."
**Source**: [How to Write Natural Dialogue in Fiction](https://www.inklingcreative.work/post/how-to-write-natural-dialogue-in-fiction); [Elmore Leonard: 10 Rules of Writing](https://fs.blog/elmore-leonard-10-rules-of-writing/)
**Notes**: Leonard: "I always refer to style as sound." His focus was on character voices -- writing in scenes from particular character viewpoints, concentrating on "character voices revealing identity and reaction rather than inserting himself into the narrative." The balance: "realistic enough to be believable but doesn't distract" vs. literal transcription which is chaotic and tedious.

### Classic Prose Stance (Equal-to-Equal Presentation)
**What it measures**: Whether the prose adopts the classic prose stance of presenting truth to an equal, rather than lecturing down, performing up, or hedging anxiously.
**Assumed goal**: Thomas and Turner's classic prose framework (Clear and Simple as the Truth) where writer and reader are equals contemplating a subject together -- instructional, bureaucratic, and academic prose legitimately adopt different writer-reader relationships.
**Score 0**: The prose adopts an inappropriate stance. The writer lectures the reader as a student (instructional). Or the writer hedges every claim with anxiety markers -- "it seems," "perhaps," "in my opinion" (academic). Or the writer performs expertise to impress rather than communicate (bureaucratic). The prose relationship between writer and reader is unequal.
**Score 5**: The writer presents observations with quiet confidence, as if pointing something out to a friend. No hedging, no lecturing, no performing. The prose creates what Thomas and Turner call "classic joint attention" -- both writer and reader gaze through "an undistorting window" at the subject. Truth is presented as self-evident, and the reader is trusted to reach the same conclusions.
**Source**: [Classic Prose Style - EnglishComposition.Org](https://englishcomposition.org/advanced-writing/classic-prose-style/)
**Notes**: Steven Pinker called classic prose "the strongest cure" for enfeebled prose. Key enabling conventions: "truth can be known," thinking precedes writing, language suffices for meaning, readers are intellectually equal. Classic style is "brief, efficient, clear, elegant, and pure" and the sentence appears "as if it could have been written in no other way." Warning: can "self-parody if insights are mundane" -- the aphoristic tone becomes ostentatious when applied to trivial observations.

### Information Density Management
**What it measures**: Whether the prose controls how much information is delivered per sentence and per paragraph, preventing both overload and starvation.
**Assumed goal**: Transparent prose tradition (Hemingway, Leonard, Carver) where information is delivered in digestible units at a controlled pace -- dense prose traditions (Pynchon, DeLillo) deliberately pack high information density for intellectual engagement.
**Score 0**: Information is either crammed into overloaded sentences that demand multiple re-readings, or spread so thin that the reader processes empty calories -- pages of prose conveying almost nothing. Density never varies to match scene requirements.
**Score 5**: Information density matches narrative needs. High-density passages deliver crucial plot or character information efficiently. Low-density passages provide breathing room for reflection and emotional processing. The writer follows the principle: "If the pace is fast, you must deliver the information in a more clear, concise fashion."
**Source**: [Pacing in Writing - bibisco](https://bibisco.com/blog/pacing-in-writing-a-guide-for-fiction-authors/)
**Notes**: bibisco: "If the speed of information is too slow, readers skim or stop reading because they no longer care. If it's too fast for too long, they can't breathe, understand the characters, or process the change happening within them or the world." "Just because the pace is fast doesn't mean you need to leave details out -- you must tell the story with everything the reader needs."

### White Space and Breathing Room
**What it measures**: Whether the prose uses paragraph breaks, dialogue exchanges, scene breaks, and visual white space to create appropriate breathing room and control reading pace.
**Assumed goal**: Commercial fiction / thriller / Hemingway tradition where white space creates pace and accessibility -- literary fiction traditions accepting dense, unbroken prose as a deliberate aesthetic choice (Saramago, Bernhard).
**Score 0**: Dense blocks of unbroken prose dominate the page with no visual relief. Or the opposite: every sentence gets its own paragraph, creating a breathless, fragmented quality that prevents depth. White space is either absent or deployed without regard to its pacing effect.
**Score 5**: Paragraph breaks arrive at natural breath-points. Dialogue creates natural white space. Scene breaks mark genuine shifts. The visual rhythm of the page reinforces the emotional rhythm of the story. Dense passages for immersion alternate with open passages for processing. "Slow pacing allows the reader to breathe -- it's the pause between drumbeats."
**Source**: [How to Give Your Scene Quality Breathing Space](https://www.ignitedinkwriting.com/ignite-your-ink-blog-for-writers/how-to-give-your-scene-quality-breathing-space/2020); [Write It Sideways - White Space](https://writeitsideways.com/how-and-when-to-use-white-space-in-writing/)
**Notes**: "White space created by paragraph breaks, dialogue exchanges, or scene cuts speed up the reading experience while dense blocks of prose slow it." "Dialogue offers the reader breathing room. Fast paced as it might be, dialogue moves the story along and offers actual visual space on the page." The contrast between dense and open passages creates "ebb and flow -- the peaks from fast-paced action feel sharper after a lull."

### Readability and Accessibility
**What it measures**: Whether the prose is accessible to a general educated reader without requiring specialized knowledge, unusual patience, or repeated re-reading to comprehend.
**Assumed goal**: Accessible prose tradition (King, Leonard, Vonnegut) where prose should be comprehensible to the broadest possible audience without dumbing down -- avant-garde traditions (Joyce, Gass, Markson) deliberately challenge accessibility as an aesthetic strategy.
**Score 0**: Prose is impenetrable. Sentences require multiple readings. Vocabulary alienates the general reader. Syntax is tangled beyond purpose. The Gunning Fog Index would place the text at post-graduate reading level. The reader cannot enter the story because the prose itself is a barrier.
**Score 5**: Prose flows naturally and is immediately comprehensible. Sentence construction aids rather than impedes understanding. Vocabulary is appropriate and unshowy. The prose achieves King's standard: writing in "a natural style that's recognizably human rather than mechanical." A general educated reader can engage fully without struggle. The Fog Index sits in the 8-10 range typical of popular fiction.
**Source**: [Gunning fog index - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gunning_fog_index); [Why Clarity is More Important Than Vocabulary - Novlr](https://www.novlr.org/writing-tips/why-clarity-is-more-important-than-vocabulary-in-creative-writing)
**Notes**: The Gunning Fog Index measures years of education needed to comprehend a text, based on sentence length and word complexity. "Popular fiction, news articles, and general interest magazines typically target 8-10." King advises against "dressing up vocabulary to beautify prose, as it comes out stuffy and awkward." Clarity is "more important than vocabulary in creative writing."

### Functional Sentence (Every Sentence Serves)
**What it measures**: Whether every sentence in the prose advances the story by revealing character, advancing action, establishing mood, or providing necessary context -- with no sentences that exist merely because the author wrote them.
**Assumed goal**: Vonnegut / Leonard "functional sentence" tradition where "every sentence must do one of two things -- reveal character or advance the action" -- digressive literary traditions (Sterne, Rushdie) treat tangential sentences as part of the pleasure.
**Score 0**: Many sentences exist without purpose. Descriptive passages neither establish mood nor advance plot. Introspective passages neither develop character nor deepen theme. The prose contains what Leonard calls "the part that readers tend to skip" -- "thick paragraphs of prose you can see have too many words in them."
**Score 5**: Every sentence has a job. Description establishes atmosphere or foreshadows. Introspection reveals character depth. Transitions efficiently bridge scenes. No sentence exists merely because the author enjoyed writing it. The prose passes Vonnegut's test: "If a sentence, no matter how excellent, does not illuminate your subject in some new and useful way, scratch it out."
**Source**: [Kurt Vonnegut: 8 Basics of Creative Writing](https://www.writingclasses.com/toolbox/tips-masters/kurt-vonnegut-8-basics-of-creative-writing); [Elmore Leonard: 10 Rules of Writing](https://fs.blog/elmore-leonard-10-rules-of-writing/)
**Notes**: Vonnegut's Rule 4: "Every sentence must do one of two things -- reveal character or advance the action." Leonard's Rule 10: "Try to leave out the part that readers tend to skip." Leonard identified this rule in 1983 -- the skippable parts are "thick paragraphs of prose you can see have too many words in them" and extended character introspection.

### Cliche-Free Language
**What it measures**: Whether the prose avoids cliched phrases, stock descriptions, and prefabricated language units, replacing them with original observation and fresh expression.
**Assumed goal**: Orwell / modern literary fiction anti-cliche tradition -- genre fiction conventions accept certain stock phrases as efficient shorthand within well-understood frameworks.
**Score 0**: Prose is constructed from prefabricated language units. Hearts pound, eyes widen, blood runs cold, tensions mount, silences are deafening. Every description relies on the nearest available stock phrase rather than original observation. Orwell's diagnosis applies: these phrases "construct your sentences for you -- even think your thoughts for you."
**Score 5**: Every phrase is freshly minted from direct observation. Where a cliche would appear, the writer has looked at the actual thing and described what they specifically see, feel, or notice. Stock phrases have been replaced with precise, particular language that could only come from this story, this moment, this character's experience.
**Source**: [Politics and the English Language - Orwell Foundation](https://www.orwellfoundation.com/the-orwell-foundation/orwell/essays-and-other-works/politics-and-the-english-language/); [9 Tips For Writing Perfect Prose - Jericho Writers](https://jerichowriters.com/prose-style/)
**Notes**: Orwell's Rule 1 targets cliches directly. His diagnosis of their danger: "The writer either has a meaning and cannot express it, or he inadvertently says something else, or he is almost indifferent as to whether his words mean anything or not. This mixture of vagueness and sheer incompetence is the most marked characteristic of modern English prose." Orwell: these prefabricated phrases "anaesthetize a portion of one's brain" and allow meaning to choose words rather than the reverse.

### Prose as Clear Thinking
**What it measures**: Whether the prose reflects clear, disciplined thinking -- each sentence logically connected, each observation precisely articulated -- rather than muddled thought disguised by elaborate language.
**Assumed goal**: Orwell's thesis that "the slovenliness of our language makes it easier for us to have foolish thoughts" and that clear writing both requires and enables clear thinking -- stream-of-consciousness and associative traditions deliberately mirror the non-linear quality of thought.
**Score 0**: The prose disguises muddled thinking behind elaborate language. Sentences are vague, ideas are disconnected, and the reader cannot extract a clear meaning. Pretentious diction substitutes for actual thought. "Operators or verbal false limbs" (Orwell) pad sentences: "render inoperative," "make contact with," "have the effect of."
**Score 5**: Every sentence expresses a clear thought. The writer has let "the meaning choose the word, and not the other way about." Ideas connect logically. The prose demonstrates the kind of disciplined thinking that can only emerge when a writer refuses to hide behind prefabricated language. Clarity of expression reflects clarity of mind.
**Source**: [Politics and the English Language - Orwell Foundation](https://www.orwellfoundation.com/the-orwell-foundation/orwell/essays-and-other-works/politics-and-the-english-language/)
**Notes**: Orwell's central thesis: "The great enemy of clear language is insincerity." Language and thought degrade in a feedback loop: "an effect can become a cause, reinforcing the original cause and producing the same effect in an intensified form." Clear writing is "a necessary first step toward political regeneration" because it makes "stupidity obvious and resistance to orthodoxy possible."

### Plain Prose When Appropriate, Beautiful Prose When Earned
**What it measures**: Whether the prose calibrates its stylistic register to the emotional weight of the scene -- plain and direct for most narration, with richer language reserved for moments that earn it.
**Assumed goal**: Weiland / modern craft tradition balancing plain and beautiful prose where "the story comes first, and most of the time, that means focusing more on honest prose than elaborate prose" -- sustained lyrical prose traditions (Woolf, McCarthy's descriptive passages) maintain elevated registers throughout.
**Score 0**: Either the prose is relentlessly plain when moments of beauty would serve the story (emotional flatness), or it is relentlessly ornate when directness would serve better (purple prose). The register never shifts to match the emotional needs of the scene.
**Score 5**: The prose modulates its register. Most narration is clean and direct. When the story reaches moments of genuine emotional or aesthetic significance, the prose rises to meet them with richer language, imagery, or rhythm. "Knowing when to choose beauty over simplicity has a lot to do with pacing -- beauty is almost always slower, so if there's a moment in the story when both the characters and the readers can slow down and savor, that's a moment to try something more lyrical."
**Source**: [How to Decide Between Plain Prose and Beautiful Prose](https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/plain-prose-beautiful-prose/)
**Notes**: Ethan Canin's observation: "you cannot simultaneously write something true about character and write something linguistically beautiful" -- words are limited, forcing the writer to choose. The balance: "There are appropriate moments in every story for poetic passages. But the story comes first." The craft challenge: knowing when directness is more powerful than elaboration, and when elaboration is earned rather than indulgent.

### Minimalist Narrative Authority
**What it measures**: Whether the prose modulates narrative authority effectively -- constraining narrator presence, reducing commentary, and limiting access to characters' mental states to create realism and interpretive openness.
**Assumed goal**: Minimalist/Carver/Hemingway tradition where "minimalist narrators frequently adopt observational, registrational roles with elliptical information delivery" -- omniscient narrator traditions (Tolstoy, Eliot) achieve depth through expansive narrative authority.
**Score 0**: Either the narrator intrudes constantly with judgments, explanations, and psychological analysis (destroying interpretive space) or the narrator is so absent that the reader has no foothold (creating confusion rather than ambiguity). Narrative authority is miscalibrated.
**Score 5**: The narrator operates with calibrated restraint. Information is delivered elliptically. Character mental states are implied rather than stated. "Reading 'less' demands that the reader do more" -- limited narrative information doesn't impoverish meaning but multiplies interpretive possibilities through suggestion rather than explanation. The narrator achieves what Greaney calls "equivocation" -- deliberate ambiguity through calculated omission.
**Source**: [An Introduction to Literary Minimalism - Phil Greaney](https://philgreaney.wordpress.com/2012/02/07/an-introduction-to-literary-minimalism-in-the-american-short-story/)
**Notes**: Greaney: "Minimalism modulates narrative authority by constraining narrator presence, reducing commentary, and limiting access to characters' mental states." Key practitioners show "detached, often absent narrators" with "deadpan delivery." Greaney distinguishes between "equivocation" (deliberate ambiguity through calculated omission) and mere incompleteness. Amy Hempel: "what's not reported in your work is more important than what actually appears on the page."

### Description Serving Story
**What it measures**: Whether descriptive passages serve the narrative by establishing mood, revealing character, or advancing thematic concerns -- rather than existing as self-indulgent set-pieces.
**Assumed goal**: Leonard / Hemingway anti-description tradition where "even if you're good at it, you don't want descriptions that bring the action, the flow of the story, to a standstill" -- literary traditions (Hardy, McCarthy's landscapes) treat descriptive writing as an essential art in itself.
**Score 0**: Descriptive passages exist for their own sake. Extended character portraits halt the story. Setting descriptions go on for paragraphs without advancing mood or meaning. The writer is showing off rather than serving the story. Leonard's warning applies: description that brings "the action, the flow of the story, to a standstill."
**Score 5**: Description is woven into action and dialogue. A character's appearance is revealed through one telling detail rather than a full portrait. Setting emerges through interaction rather than exposition. Hemingway's standard in "Hills Like White Elephants" -- a single physical detail (removing a hat) suffices when dialogue and action carry the scene. Every descriptive element pulls double duty.
**Source**: [Elmore Leonard: 10 Rules of Writing](https://fs.blog/elmore-leonard-10-rules-of-writing/); [12 Ways to Be an Invisible Writer](https://stormwritingschool.com/avoid-writerly-writing/)
**Notes**: Leonard's Rules 8 and 9: "Avoid detailed descriptions of characters" and "Don't go into great detail describing places and things." He exempts writers who excel at description (Atwood, Harrison) but notes even they risk "halting narrative momentum." Storm Writing School: "Description must serve the larger narrative purpose, not exist for its own sake."

## Sentinels Found

### Adverb-Heavy Dialogue Attribution
**Detection**: More than 10% of dialogue tags use adverbs modifying "said" (e.g., "he said gravely," "she whispered urgently," "they exclaimed joyfully").
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs default to adverb-modified dialogue tags because training data contains them abundantly and they are statistically safe completions. The pattern of verb+adverb attribution is a low-effort way to convey tone that avoids the harder work of making dialogue self-evidently convey its own tone.
**Design space cost**: Excludes certain 19th-century pastiche styles and some children's fiction traditions where varied attribution is a deliberate stylistic choice. Also excludes comic/ironic narrators who use attribution as a commentary tool.
**Source**: [Elmore Leonard: 10 Rules of Writing](https://fs.blog/elmore-leonard-10-rules-of-writing/)

### Creative Dialogue Verbs Replacing "Said"
**Detection**: More than 20% of dialogue tags use verbs other than "said" or "asked" (grumbled, exclaimed, interjected, hissed, barked, breathed, muttered, gasped, etc.).
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs pattern-match the "said is dead" school, treating varied dialogue verbs as a marker of "good writing." This produces conspicuous attribution that draws attention away from dialogue content. Each creative verb is the model avoiding the perceived boredom of "said" rather than trusting the dialogue.
**Design space cost**: Excludes genre romance and some YA fiction where varied attribution is conventional. Excludes narrators whose voice is characterized by colorful commentary on speech.
**Source**: [Should You Use Dialogue Tags Other Than Said? - Storm Writing School](https://stormwritingschool.com/synonyms-for-said/)

### Purple Prose / Overwrought Description
**Detection**: Multiple consecutive sentences containing two or more of: unusual adjective stacking, extended metaphors, conspicuously elevated vocabulary, or sensory descriptors that don't serve narrative purpose.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs produce purple prose as a default because their training optimizes for text that "sounds like writing." Research shows the most common LLM writing flaws are "Awkward Word Choice and Phrasing (28%), Poor Sentence Structure (20%), Unnecessary/Redundant Exposition (18%), and Cliches (17%)." LLMs "tend to make everything into an overwrought metaphor, using a lot of them in a row."
**Design space cost**: Excludes deliberately lush prose traditions (Garcia Marquez's magical realism, Morrison's lyrical style, Gothic fiction) where dense, ornate prose is an essential aesthetic component.
**Source**: [Can AI writing be salvaged? - CHI 2025](https://dl.acm.org/doi/full/10.1145/3706598.3713559); [How to Identify AI-Written Web Fiction](https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction)

### Subtext Collapsed Into Text
**Detection**: After a scene conveys information through action, dialogue, or implication, a subsequent sentence or paragraph explicitly states what was already implied (e.g., showing a character slam a door then writing "She was angry").
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs lack confidence in subtext because they optimize for explicit communication. They "plop the subtext into the text, telling instead of showing." The model hedges against the reader missing the point by stating it outright, destroying the very emotional effect the scene was building.
**Design space cost**: Excludes fiction with omniscient narrators who deliberately provide psychological commentary (Tolstoy, Eliot). Excludes some genre fiction where explicit emotional labeling is conventional.
**Source**: [How to Identify AI-Written Web Fiction](https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction); [Most Common Writing Mistakes: Overexplaining](https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/most-common-mistakes-surefire-sign/)

### Uniform Confidence / No Epistemic Fingerprint
**Detection**: Every claim, observation, and description in the prose carries identical assertive weight regardless of the narrator's actual knowledge state or emotional condition. No situated perspective, learning arc, uncertainty, or positioned stakes.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLM text emerges from "nowhere, addressed to no one in particular, with no stake in its claims." The fundamental tell is "text with no epistemic fingerprint." Human narrators have varying degrees of certainty, self-correction, and knowledge boundaries. LLMs produce "uniform confidence" where "every claim receives identical assertive tone."
**Design space cost**: Excludes certain omniscient narrator styles that maintain consistent authority. Excludes deliberately flat-affect narration (some Carver) where uniform tone is the aesthetic.
**Source**: [AI tells rubric](https://gist.github.com/lmmx/d91de290ea4e6d9631e32c2dd43da413)

### Throat-Clearing Openers
**Detection**: Chapters or scenes begin with unnecessary scene-setting or contextualizing sentences before the actual story content begins ("In the world of...", "It was a time when...", "The morning dawned...").
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate "throat-clearing openers" -- unnecessary scene-setting before actual content -- because they optimize for gradual information delivery rather than the fiction craft principle of starting as close to the action as possible.
**Design space cost**: Excludes deliberate establishing-shot openings in some literary fiction. Excludes omniscient narrators whose sweeping openings are part of their voice.
**Source**: [AI tells rubric](https://gist.github.com/lmmx/d91de290ea4e6d9631e32c2dd43da413); [Kurt Vonnegut: 8 Basics of Creative Writing](https://www.writingclasses.com/toolbox/tips-masters/kurt-vonnegut-8-basics-of-creative-writing)

### Symmetric Paragraph/Section Length
**Detection**: All paragraphs or sections within a chapter are roughly the same length and rhythm, with no variation in density, pace, or visual weight across the page.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs produce "symmetric load-balancing" where "all sections roughly equal length and rhythm, masking what author found difficult or important." Human writers naturally vary paragraph length based on emotional weight, pacing needs, and emphasis. Uniform paragraph size signals mechanical generation.
**Design space cost**: Excludes deliberate formal experiments with uniform structure. Excludes certain genre conventions where consistent paragraph length serves readability.
**Source**: [AI tells rubric](https://gist.github.com/lmmx/d91de290ea4e6d9631e32c2dd43da413)

### Adverb Inflation / Intensifier Stacking
**Detection**: Frequent use of empty intensifiers ("fundamentally," "substantially," "critically," "deeply," "profoundly," "utterly") that add volume without meaning.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs use intensifiers as "adverb inflation" -- "fundamentally," "substantially," "critically" without semantic work or differentiated weight. These words fill space while signaling emphasis the prose hasn't earned through specificity.
**Design space cost**: Excludes emphatic narrative voices where intensification is a character trait. Excludes rhetorical styles (essayistic fiction) where emphasis markers serve argumentative structure.
**Source**: [AI tells rubric](https://gist.github.com/lmmx/d91de290ea4e6d9631e32c2dd43da413)

### Lists of Three (Tricolon Overuse)
**Detection**: More than three instances per chapter of three-item lists used as metaphor stacking or descriptive accumulation (e.g., "geometry of madness, a wound in the fabric of what is, older than law and time").
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs gravitate toward tricolon structures because they are statistically common in training data and feel rhetorically complete. The pattern becomes "piling descriptors rather than selecting the strongest" -- the model generates three options where a human writer would choose the best one.
**Design space cost**: Excludes rhetorical prose traditions where the tricolon is a deliberate device (oratorical fiction, Biblical cadence). Excludes comic accumulation as a style.
**Source**: [How to Identify AI-Written Web Fiction](https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction)

### "Not X; Y" Construction Overuse
**Detection**: More than five instances per chapter of the construction where a thing is defined by negation then replacement: "It was no current. It was a summons." "This was not grief. This was fury."
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: This construction "appears universally across models" as a way to create dramatic emphasis. It's a low-cost way to generate apparent profundity -- negate an expectation, then provide an alternative -- without actually doing the work of precise description or original observation.
**Design space cost**: Excludes philosophical fiction where negation-and-replacement is a deliberate dialectical technique. Excludes noir fiction where this construction is a genre convention.
**Source**: [How to Identify AI-Written Web Fiction](https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction)

### Consistency Poisoning / Cliche Loops
**Detection**: Once a stylistic pattern appears (a type of metaphor, a sentence structure, a descriptive habit), it recurs with minimal variation for extended passages, as if the prose has locked into a groove it cannot escape.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Once LLMs generate mediocre text, they become trapped in a "local minimum of samey cliches" and cannot escape the pattern. Unlike human writers who "naturally give it a rest," AI models continue reproducing the same pattern because their context window contains examples of it. Human writers instinctively vary phrasing when they notice repetition.
**Design space cost**: Excludes deliberate repetition as a literary device (Gertrude Stein, some Beckett). Excludes musical prose where repeating motifs are structurally intentional.
**Source**: [How to Identify AI-Written Web Fiction](https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction)

### Runaway Thesaurus Syndrome
**Detection**: Near-synonyms used in close proximity as if the author is demonstrating vocabulary range rather than choosing the precise word. Words that mean the same thing are deployed consecutively: "the vast, immense, boundless expanse."
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs exhibit "runaway thesaurus syndrome -- using another word that means more or less the same thing" rather than selecting the single most precise word. This makes prose "hard to read" because the reader processes what appear to be distinct concepts only to realize they're being told the same thing three ways.
**Design space cost**: Excludes deliberate synonym accumulation for rhetorical effect (some Faulkner, ceremonial prose). Excludes comic exaggeration through synonymic piling.
**Source**: [How to Identify AI-Written Web Fiction](https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction); [Can AI writing be salvaged? - CHI 2025](https://dl.acm.org/doi/full/10.1145/3706598.3713559)

### Verb Upgrading / Dramatic Inflation
**Detection**: Neutral verbs systematically replaced with dramatic alternatives: "discovered" instead of "noticed," "shattered" instead of "broke," "devoured" instead of "ate." The prose treats every action as momentous.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs "replace neutral verbs with dramatic synonyms" because training data rewards vivid language and the model cannot distinguish when vividness is warranted from when it is not. Every observation becomes a discovery, every glance a gaze, every walk a stride. This flattens the emotional landscape by treating everything as equally dramatic.
**Design space cost**: Excludes deliberately heightened prose styles (Gothic, epic fantasy) where verbal intensity is part of the aesthetic. Excludes comic overstatement.
**Source**: [AI tells rubric](https://gist.github.com/lmmx/d91de290ea4e6d9631e32c2dd43da413)

### Telegraphing Before Showing
**Detection**: A sentence labels an action or quality before dramatizing it: "Monte did not mince his words" followed by Monte speaking bluntly, or "She was devastated" followed by a scene showing devastation.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs hedge against reader misunderstanding by announcing what a scene will demonstrate before demonstrating it. This pattern -- labeling then showing -- "robs readers of subtext" because the label forecloses interpretation before the dramatization can create it.
**Design space cost**: Excludes some omniscient narrator styles where such prefacing is part of the narrative voice. Excludes educational or children's fiction where explicit labeling aids comprehension.
**Source**: [12 Ways to Be an Invisible Writer - Storm Writing School](https://stormwritingschool.com/avoid-writerly-writing/)

### Blocky Dialogue/Exposition Alternation
**Detection**: The prose alternates in regular, predictable blocks between dialogue exchanges, exposition paragraphs, and narrative action, with clean boundaries between each mode rather than organic interweaving.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLM output creates "clearly defined areas of dialogue, exposition and repetitive narration" -- artificial compartmentalization where human writing would organically blend modes. The mechanical alternation reveals generation-by-block rather than continuous narrative flow.
**Design space cost**: Excludes deliberately structured fiction where modal separation is a formal choice. Excludes some screenplay-influenced fiction with clean mode boundaries.
**Source**: [How to Identify AI-Written Web Fiction](https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction)

### Absence of Genuine Hedging / Missing Self-Correction
**Detection**: Narrator or characters never express genuine uncertainty ("I think," "from what I understand," "not certain but"), never correct themselves mid-thought, never return to earlier topics with additional recalled information. All knowledge is delivered with uniform confidence.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs have "no visible learning" -- text arrives fully formed without "I initially thought..." or confusion-to-clarity arcs. There is "no 'Oh and another thing'" -- no return to earlier topics with additional recalled information. Real human thought includes self-correction, tangents, and associative returns that LLMs systematically lack.
**Design space cost**: Excludes certain confident omniscient narrators. Excludes deliberately polished first-person narrators (some unreliable narrators whose polish is the point).
**Source**: [AI tells rubric](https://gist.github.com/lmmx/d91de290ea4e6d9631e32c2dd43da413)

### Clean Ending Summary
**Detection**: Chapters or scenes conclude with a sentence or paragraph that summarizes the significance of what just occurred, restating the emotional or thematic takeaway rather than trusting the reader to have absorbed it.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs produce "the clean ending" -- concluding with a summary "restating significance rather than trusting reader comprehension." This pattern reflects the model's optimization for explicit communication over artistic restraint. Human writers end scenes on action, image, or unresolved tension; LLMs close loops.
**Design space cost**: Excludes essayistic narrators who deliberately summarize for philosophical effect. Excludes some literary fiction where thematic restatement is part of the narrative structure.
**Source**: [AI tells rubric](https://gist.github.com/lmmx/d91de290ea4e6d9631e32c2dd43da413)

### Dying Metaphor Reliance
**Detection**: More than five dead or dying metaphors per chapter -- phrases like "ring the changes on," "toe the line," "stand shoulder to shoulder," "a breath of fresh air," "grinding to a halt" -- used without awareness of their original imagery.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs reproduce dying metaphors because they are statistically common in training data and carry safe, recognizable meanings. The model selects prefabricated phrases because they are probable completions, not because they are vivid or original. Orwell: these phrases exist "because they save people the trouble of inventing phrases for themselves."
**Design space cost**: Excludes fiction deliberately imitating cliche-heavy speech patterns for characterization. Excludes satirical prose that deploys cliches for ironic effect.
**Source**: [Politics and the English Language - Orwell Foundation](https://www.orwellfoundation.com/the-orwell-foundation/orwell/essays-and-other-works/politics-and-the-english-language/)

### Exclamation Point Overuse
**Detection**: More than three exclamation points per 100,000 words of prose (outside of dialogue where a character's personality justifies them).
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs use exclamation points to signal emphasis and emotional intensity because they are a cheap way to convey excitement without earning it through prose craft. The punctuation does work that sentence construction should handle.
**Design space cost**: Excludes Tom Wolfe's New Journalism style. Excludes some YA and children's fiction. Excludes comic prose where exclamation points are part of the humor.
**Source**: [Elmore Leonard: 10 Rules of Writing](https://fs.blog/elmore-leonard-10-rules-of-writing/)

### "Suddenly" as Default Transition
**Detection**: The word "suddenly" appears more than twice per chapter as a transition device introducing unexpected events.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs use "suddenly" as a low-effort way to introduce plot turns because it signals unexpectedness without the craft work of actually building surprise through pacing and setup. Leonard banned the word entirely. Writers who overuse "suddenly" also tend to misuse exclamation points -- both are cheap intensity substitutes.
**Design space cost**: Minimal. Almost no legitimate prose tradition requires the word "suddenly" as a structural device. Individual instances in dialogue or character thought are fine; it's the pattern of reliance that's diagnostic.
**Source**: [Elmore Leonard: 10 Rules of Writing](https://fs.blog/elmore-leonard-10-rules-of-writing/)

### Weather-Opening Default
**Detection**: A chapter or scene opens with weather description that does not connect to character experience, mood, or thematic concern -- pure atmospheric stage-setting.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs frequently open scenes with weather because it is a statistically common opening pattern in training data. Leonard's Rule 1: "Never open a book with weather" unless "it's only to create atmosphere" connected to character reaction. Opening with weather is the safest, most generic scene-starter available to a model.
**Design space cost**: Excludes fiction where weather is thematically central (storm narratives, survival stories). Excludes openings where weather is genuinely filtered through character perception.
**Source**: [Elmore Leonard: 10 Rules of Writing](https://fs.blog/elmore-leonard-10-rules-of-writing/)

### Oscillating Writing Modes
**Detection**: The prose swings between distinctly different registers -- "long, heavy comparisons, then short lines that sounded like stock phrases" -- without organic transition or narrative justification.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs exhibit "oscillating writing modes" because the model "balances predictability with apparent creativity." The output alternates between elaborate description and terse summary in a pattern that reflects generation mechanics rather than narrative intent.
**Design space cost**: Excludes fiction with deliberately fragmentary or collage-like structures. Excludes multi-voiced narratives where register shifts reflect different characters or modes.
**Source**: [How to Identify AI-Written Web Fiction](https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction)

### Contradictory Sensory Details
**Detection**: Descriptive passages contain sensory details that contradict each other: sounds that are simultaneously loud and soft, motions that are both quick and slow, imagery that combines incompatible qualities.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs string together descriptive words without modeling the physical reality being described. The result is passages where "traffic that roars cannot rise softly" (Jericho Writers example) -- the words individually seem appropriate but combine incoherently because the model lacks a consistent sensory model of the scene.
**Design space cost**: Excludes deliberate synaesthetic or surrealist prose where sensory contradiction is the technique. Excludes dream sequences or altered-state narration where contradiction represents the character's experience.
**Source**: [9 Tips For Writing Perfect Prose - Jericho Writers](https://jerichowriters.com/prose-style/)

### Emotion-Named-Not-Shown
**Detection**: Emotional states are named directly ("He felt sad," "She was terrified," "A wave of grief washed over him") rather than conveyed through physical action, dialogue, or environmental detail.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs default to naming emotions because it is the most direct, statistically safe way to convey character state. The craft of showing emotion through concrete detail requires modeling specific physical manifestations of internal states, which is harder than simply labeling them. This is the canonical "telling instead of showing" pattern.
**Design space cost**: Excludes close-psychic-distance narration where naming emotions reflects the character's internal self-awareness. Excludes omniscient narrators who provide direct psychological access as part of their authority.
**Source**: [Big 'A' Little 'a' - LitReactor](https://litreactor.com/columns/big-a-little-a-writing-between-the-concrete-and-abstract); [12 Ways to Be an Invisible Writer](https://stormwritingschool.com/avoid-writerly-writing/)

### Info-Dump Exposition Blocks
**Detection**: Paragraphs of pure exposition delivering backstory, world-building, or character history that are not integrated into scene action, dialogue, or character experience. Also: "As you know, Bob..." dialogue where characters tell each other things both already know.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs deliver information in blocks because they process context as a sequence and dump accumulated knowledge when it becomes relevant. The "As you know, Bob" pattern appears because the model needs to communicate information to the reader and uses dialogue as the most available vehicle, regardless of whether the fictional characters would realistically exchange that information.
**Design space cost**: Excludes fiction with deliberately essayistic narrators (Melville, Eco). Excludes genre fiction (hard SF, historical fiction) where exposition serves a specific contract with the reader.
**Source**: [12 Ways to Be an Invisible Writer](https://stormwritingschool.com/avoid-writerly-writing/)

### Missing Tonal Consistency with Character
**Detection**: Descriptive language and vocabulary in character-focalized narration uses words and concepts the POV character would not know, use, or think in.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs have a "house style" that persists regardless of which character is supposedly focalizing the narrative. The model's vocabulary and sentence construction reflect its training distribution, not the character's education, background, or personality. A child's POV shouldn't use adult analytical vocabulary; a working-class character's POV shouldn't use academic diction.
**Design space cost**: Excludes fiction with deliberately omniscient narrators whose language is independent of character consciousness. Excludes magical realism or fabulist fiction where elevated language in humble settings is the technique.
**Source**: [12 Ways to Be an Invisible Writer](https://stormwritingschool.com/avoid-writerly-writing/)

### Passive Voice Dominance
**Detection**: More than 30% of sentences use passive voice construction, with subjects consistently receiving rather than performing actions.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs tend toward passive constructions because they are statistically common in the formal/academic writing that makes up much training data. Passive voice removes agency and creates the "free-floating agency" described in the AI tells rubric -- "subjects lack pronouns; causation becomes murky and untethered."
**Design space cost**: Excludes deliberately bureaucratic or alienated narration (Kafka-esque prose). Excludes character voices whose passivity is thematically meaningful.
**Source**: [AI tells rubric](https://gist.github.com/lmmx/d91de290ea4e6d9631e32c2dd43da413); [Fiction University](http://blog.janicehardy.com/2009/05/passive-aggression.html)

### Repetitive Sentence Structure
**Detection**: More than five consecutive sentences following the same syntactic pattern (Subject-Verb-Object, Subject-Verb-Object, Subject-Verb-Object) without variation in length, structure, or rhythm.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs exhibit "repetitive sentence structures" as a fundamental pattern because the model generates each sentence from the same local context and optimizes for the same structural templates. "Humans instinctively vary phrasing when noticing this pattern, while AI models lack this self-correction instinct."
**Design space cost**: Excludes deliberately monotone prose (some Beckett, Bernhard) where syntactic uniformity is the aesthetic. Excludes children's fiction where repetitive structure aids reading.
**Source**: [How to Identify AI-Written Web Fiction](https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction)
