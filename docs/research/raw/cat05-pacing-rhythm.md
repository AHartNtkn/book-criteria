## Criteria Found

### Macro-Pacing Variation (Tempo Modulation Across the Narrative Arc)
**What it measures**: Whether the story deliberately alternates between fast-paced and slow-paced sections across chapters and acts, creating a rhythm of intensity and relief.
**Assumed goal**: Commercial fiction craft tradition (Writer's Digest school, Donald Maass) holding that pacing must oscillate like a heartbeat to sustain engagement -- literary fiction practitioners like Woolf or Sebald may deliberately sustain a single slow tempo for immersive or meditative effect.
**Score 0**: The entire narrative runs at a single unchanging tempo -- either relentlessly fast (exhausting) or uniformly slow (soporific) -- with no deliberate variation.
**Score 5**: Fast and slow sections alternate with clear intentionality; high-intensity scenes feel sharper because of preceding lulls, and reflective passages feel earned after action. The reader's experience mirrors the "peaks and valleys" pattern described by multiple craft sources.
**Source**: https://www.writersdigest.com/pacing-for-emotional-impact-in-fiction-building-tension-and-release-in-a-novel
**Notes**: Writer's Digest states: "The peaks from fast-paced action feel sharper after a lull. The valleys of slower moments feel more poignant after a rush of adrenaline." MyStoryDoctor emphasizes "relentless speed exhausts readers; unbroken slowness loses them."

### Scene-to-Summary Ratio
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative appropriately balances fully dramatized scenes (real-time, moment-by-moment) against narrative summary (compressed time), and whether the ratio serves the story's genre and emotional needs.
**Assumed goal**: Workshop-tradition fiction craft (Center for Fiction, Jane Friedman's editorial model) where the standard recommendation is ~70% scene to 30% summary -- maximalist literary fiction (e.g., Proust, Woolf) may be almost entirely scene, while saga/epic fiction may use much more summary.
**Score 0**: The narrative is almost entirely summary with no dramatized scenes, or almost entirely scene with no compression, creating either distance or exhaustion. The ratio is clearly unintentional.
**Score 5**: Crucial moments (conflict, decision, revelation, change) are fully dramatized scenes; transitional and less important periods are compressed into summary. The ratio feels deliberate and genre-appropriate. Summary has its own through-line and precise imagery rather than reading as a list of events.
**Source**: https://janefriedman.com/scene-summary-postcard-three-types-of-scenes-used-in-commercial-upmarket-and-literary-fiction/
**Notes**: Jane Friedman distinguishes three scene types: Classic Scene (real-time with inciting incident through resolution), Summary Scene (compressed time with a through-line), and Postcard Scene (deepens character without plot advancement). Commercial fiction favors straightforward classic scenes; literary fiction mixes all three.

### Genette's Duration Spectrum (Narrative Speed Control)
**What it measures**: Whether the author deploys the full spectrum of narrative speeds -- ellipsis (time skipped), summary (compressed), scene (real-time equivalent), and pause/description (time stops while prose continues) -- with purposeful variation.
**Assumed goal**: Narratological tradition (Genette's Narrative Discourse) applied to fiction evaluation -- this is a structural-analytical framework rather than a prescriptive one, so it applies across all fiction but is most valued in academic literary criticism and developmental editing.
**Score 0**: The narrative operates at a single speed throughout, typically unvarying scene-level pacing with no ellipsis, no summary, and no descriptive pause.
**Score 5**: All four speeds appear where appropriate. Ellipsis skips unimportant time; summary compresses necessary but non-dramatic periods; scenes dramatize key moments; descriptive pauses create atmosphere or emphasis. Speed changes feel motivated by story needs.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pace_(narrative)
**Notes**: Genette defines pace as the ratio between time narrated and pages used to narrate it. The four speeds are: ellipsis (infinitely rapid), summary (relatively rapid), scene (relatively slow), descriptive pause (no story progress). Wikipedia also notes the "Surf Dracula" problem where setup for unimportant elements consumes excessive time.

### Sentence-Level Rhythm Variation
**What it measures**: Whether sentence length and structure vary enough to create a felt rhythm, avoiding both monotonous uniformity and random chaos.
**Assumed goal**: Gary Provost's "Write Music" school of prose craft, endorsed broadly across commercial and literary fiction -- the principle that prose should have audible rhythm when read aloud. Experimental prose (Beckett, Bernhard) may deliberately use extreme uniformity or extreme run-on as a stylistic choice.
**Score 0**: Sentences are uniformly the same length and structure throughout (all medium-length declarative sentences, or all short fragments, or all long complex sentences), creating a metronomic, flat reading experience.
**Score 5**: Short, medium, and long sentences alternate with purposeful variation. Short sentences deliver punch and urgency; long sentences create flow and immersion; the mix creates what Provost calls "music" -- a rhythm that pleases the reader's ear and matches the emotional register of the content.
**Source**: https://www.aerogrammestudio.com/2014/08/05/this-sentence-has-five-words/
**Notes**: Gary Provost's famous passage: "This sentence has five words. Here are five more words. Five-word sentences are fine. But several together become monotonous... I vary the sentence length, and I create music. Music. The writing sings. It has a pleasant rhythm, a lilt, a harmony."

### Sentence Structure Variation (Beyond Length)
**What it measures**: Whether the grammatical construction of sentences varies -- not just length but syntactic pattern: subject-first vs. inverted, simple vs. compound vs. complex, declarative vs. interrogative vs. imperative, active vs. passive.
**Assumed goal**: Novela Studio's prose rhythm framework and Le Guin's Steering the Craft tradition, which holds that when every sentence follows subject-verb-object regardless of length, prose flattens out. This principle applies broadly but is especially critical in literary fiction and narrative nonfiction.
**Score 0**: Every sentence follows the same grammatical pattern (e.g., "He did X. She did Y. They went Z.") regardless of length variation, creating syntactic monotony.
**Score 5**: Sentences vary in construction: some lead with dependent clauses, some with sensory details, some with subjects; questions, fragments, and imperatives appear where emotionally appropriate. The diagnostic (examining five consecutive sentences for identical openings) passes.
**Source**: https://novela.so/en/blog/how-to-use-prose-rhythm
**Notes**: Novela Studio provides a diagnostic: "Examine five consecutive sentences; if three or more share identical openings, restructure." Le Guin: "pace and movement depend above all on rhythm, and the primary way you feel and control the rhythm of your prose is by hearing it."

### Paragraph Length Variation
**What it measures**: Whether paragraph length varies purposefully to control reading pace, emphasis, and visual experience, rather than running at a uniform length.
**Assumed goal**: Gilliam Writers Group microstructures framework -- the principle that paragraph architecture (shape, length, density) controls how readers breathe, pause, and absorb emotionally. This applies across all prose fiction but is especially visible in literary fiction influenced by Proust (long) and Hemingway (short).
**Score 0**: Every paragraph is approximately the same length (e.g., all 4-5 sentences, or all single sentences), creating visual and rhythmic monotony. One-sentence paragraphs are overused to the point of losing dramatic impact, or dense blocks are unbroken throughout.
**Score 5**: Long paragraphs create immersion and complexity for introspection and description; short paragraphs create urgency and punch for action and emphasis; single-sentence paragraphs are reserved for maximum dramatic impact and used sparingly. The visual shape of the page reflects the story's emotional dynamics.
**Source**: https://www.gilliamwritersgroup.com/blog/microstructures-how-paragraph-shape-affects-narrative-pace
**Notes**: Gilliam Writers Group: "Dense blocks may daunt readers or signal authority. White space invites quick reading or provokes unease." Common pitfall: monotonous paragraph length flattens tension; random breaks feel arbitrary.

### White Space and Breath Units
**What it measures**: Whether the text uses visual white space (paragraph breaks, scene breaks, chapter breaks, dialogue formatting) purposefully to create reading rhythm, emphasis, and rest.
**Assumed goal**: C.S. Lakin's editorial framework and modern commercial fiction conventions where white space functions as punctuation -- the principle that visual presentation affects reading pace and cognitive processing. Dense literary fiction (Saramago, Bernhard) deliberately minimizes white space as a stylistic choice.
**Score 0**: The page is either a solid wall of unbroken text providing no visual rest, or is so fragmented with breaks that no sustained reading flow is possible.
**Score 5**: White space functions at multiple levels: line breaks highlight phrases, paragraph breaks create breath pauses, scene breaks provide substantial rest, chapter endings create major pauses. Important moments are surrounded by white space that "shines a light" on them. Dialogue formatting creates natural acceleration through vertical reading.
**Source**: https://www.livewritethrive.com/2025/01/06/the-power-of-white-space-on-the-page/
**Notes**: Peter Roy Clark: "White space is the most powerful form of punctuation." Lakin emphasizes that white space reduces eye strain, aids processing, and helps readers track location in the narrative.

### Recovery Beats / Emotional Processing Scenes
**What it measures**: Whether the story provides adequate breathing room after intense sequences, allowing both characters and readers to process emotional experiences before the next escalation.
**Assumed goal**: Swain/Bickham scene-sequel model where every action scene (goal-conflict-setback) requires a reaction scene (emotion-dilemma-decision) -- this is a commercial fiction craft standard that literary fiction may handle through entirely different means (e.g., temporal dilation, stream-of-consciousness).
**Score 0**: Action sequences follow one another without any processing time. Characters never react emotionally to events. The reader has no space to internalize what happened before the next crisis arrives.
**Score 5**: After major action or emotional intensity, the narrative provides proportionate recovery: characters process, reflect, and make decisions. These sequel/reaction scenes control pacing by their length -- short sequels maintain urgency, long sequels create contemplation. Recovery beats make subsequent intense scenes hit harder through contrast.
**Source**: https://www.advancedfictionwriting.com/blog/2009/05/25/scenes-sequels-and-chapters/
**Notes**: Ingermanson (on Swain): "Scenes read fast because they're active. Sequels read slower because they're contemplative." The sequel structure is Reaction-Dilemma-Decision. Sequel length directly controls overall pacing tempo.

### Chapter/Scene Length Variation
**What it measures**: Whether chapter and scene lengths vary deliberately to control pacing and reading rhythm, rather than defaulting to a uniform length.
**Assumed goal**: Commercial page-turner tradition (Dan Brown's Da Vinci Code at ~1,300 words/chapter) and its contrast with literary/epic fiction (Game of Thrones at ~4,000 words/chapter) -- the principle that chapter length itself communicates pace and creates psychological effects on readers.
**Score 0**: Every chapter is approximately the same length regardless of content, creating predictable rhythmic uniformity and preventing the pacing effects that length variation enables.
**Score 5**: Chapter lengths vary based on content needs: short chapters for rapid action and momentum, longer chapters for deep character work and immersion. The variation creates a reading rhythm where short chapters after long ones feel accelerating, and long chapters after short ones provide welcome depth. Strategic variation prevents both exhaustion and boredom.
**Source**: https://www.literatureandlatte.com/blog/how-chapter-and-paragraph-length-affects-the-pacing-of-novels
**Notes**: Literature & Latte: "Short chapters can give readers the impression that a lot is happening, and quickly, whereas longer chapters can provide a more contemplative story." Most novels require a blend to reel readers in and maintain interest.

### Hook Placement and Density
**What it measures**: Whether chapters and scenes end with forward-momentum elements (hooks) that compel continued reading, and whether hook types vary to prevent predictability.
**Assumed goal**: Commercial fiction editorial tradition (River Editor, Kill Zone Blog) where chapter endings must create "the promise that the next chapter will be worth reading" -- literary fiction may deliberately avoid hooks or use subtle thematic hooks rather than plot-driven ones.
**Score 0**: Chapters end at natural stopping points with no forward momentum, giving readers permission to put the book down. Or: every chapter ends with the same type of hook (always cliffhangers, always reveals), becoming predictable.
**Score 5**: Hook types vary across chapters (question, revelation, tension escalation, emotional shift, decision, arrival, interruption, threat). Hook density increases in the second half. Each chapter ending provides both scene satisfaction (something happened) and forward momentum (something compels continuation). The strongest hooks appear where reader commitment might flag (middle sections).
**Source**: https://rivereditor.com/guides/how-to-write-compelling-chapter-hooks-keep-readers-reading-2026
**Notes**: River Editor genre guidelines: thrillers need 80%+ chapters with strong hooks; literary fiction ~50% strong/50% moderate. Pattern strategy: "Strong -> Moderate -> Strong -> Strong -> Light -> Strong" prevents predictability. Critical balance: scene satisfaction + hook = great chapter ending.

### Information Density Management
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative controls how much new information (worldbuilding, backstory, character detail, plot data) is delivered per unit of prose, avoiding both overload and emptiness.
**Assumed goal**: William Sloane's density framework (via Toby Litt and Marie Brennan) where "every sentence should be doing at least two things -- three is better" -- this is valued across all literary fiction and upmarket commercial fiction. Spare minimalism (Carver) achieves density through implication rather than stacking.
**Score 0**: Information arrives in undifferentiated dumps (paragraphs of pure exposition, worldbuilding lectures) or is spread so thin that pages pass without the reader learning anything new. The "one-thing-at-a-time scene" dominates.
**Score 5**: Every passage does multiple things simultaneously: advancing plot while revealing character while building world while creating mood. Information is woven into action and dialogue rather than delivered in blocks. Dense passages alternate with sparser ones to prevent cognitive overload.
**Source**: https://www.swantower.com/essays/writing-craft/information-density/
**Notes**: William Sloane: "The enemy of fictional density is the one-thing-at-a-time scene." Toby Litt: "If it's only doing one thing (conveying information, for example), it's insufficiently dense." Brennan notes the particular challenge of conveying society-wide changes that "fit very badly" in narrative form.

### Temporal Compression and Expansion
**What it measures**: Whether the author controls the ratio of narrative time to story time purposefully -- expanding important moments and compressing unimportant ones -- rather than giving equal weight to all time periods.
**Assumed goal**: Lee Martin's short fiction craft tradition and broader narratological practice -- the principle that "time in fiction is not measured by clocks but by how much attention is given to it." This applies universally but is especially critical in short fiction where compression is the primary structural tool.
**Score 0**: All time periods receive equal attention regardless of narrative importance. Trivial passages of time are given as much space as pivotal moments, or pivotal moments are compressed to the same density as routine events.
**Score 5**: Key moments are expanded with sensory detail and emotional depth; routine periods are compressed or elided entirely. The compression-expansion pattern creates the story's distinctive temporal signature. As Lee Martin notes, the story "condenses human experience while also expressing the depth of that experience."
**Source**: https://leemartinauthor.com/2017/08/28/compression-expansion-short-story/
**Notes**: Lee Martin: "The short story often depends on compression for its power... it must find a way to let the tensions of the plot and the revelations of characters come to a point where something closes while also opening up." Compression can provide relief after tense scenes; expansion can heighten suspense by lingering.

### Action-to-Reflection Ratio
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative balances external action (events happening, characters doing things) with internal reflection (characters processing, thinking, feeling) in proportions appropriate to the genre and story.
**Assumed goal**: Swain/Bickham scene-sequel model as adapted by general craft tradition -- action without reflection creates shallow exhaustion; reflection without action creates stasis. The ideal ratio varies by genre: thrillers skew toward action; literary fiction toward reflection.
**Score 0**: The story is all action with no reflection (events happen but nobody processes them, creating emotional emptiness) or all reflection with no action (characters think endlessly but nothing happens).
**Score 5**: Action sequences drive plot forward creating excitement and conflict; reflective scenes provide depth, emotional resonance, and character development. The ratio matches genre expectations while ensuring that reflection follows action naturally and action emerges from decisions made during reflection.
**Source**: https://www.wordprocess.com/story-pacing-balance/
**Notes**: WordProcess: "Action and reflection are the yin and yang of storytelling." Reflection is "most important because it helps readers identify the reasoning of the story, helping them align with the main character's thoughts, aiding in immersion."

### Dialogue Pacing Variation
**What it measures**: Whether dialogue exchanges vary in pace -- from rapid-fire stichomythia to longer speeches -- matching the emotional and dramatic needs of each scene, rather than defaulting to a single dialogue rhythm.
**Assumed goal**: Mythcreants dialogue craft framework -- the principle that dialogue pace is a rhythm tool: rapid exchanges create urgency and tension, while longer speeches create intimacy or gravitas. This applies across all dialogue-heavy fiction.
**Score 0**: All dialogue operates at the same pace throughout the story. Every exchange is either all rapid-fire or all extended monologue, regardless of dramatic context.
**Score 5**: Rapid exchanges accelerate confrontational and high-stakes scenes; longer speeches slow for emotional moments and exposition. Dialogue beats (action tags, internal reactions) control micro-rhythm within conversations. The pacing of dialogue matches the emotional register of the scene.
**Source**: https://mythcreants.com/blog/pacing-your-dialogue/
**Notes**: Mythcreants: "Short exchanges equal speed while longer, introspective lines create a slow burn." C.S. Lakin adds that dialogue beats "provide texture, grounding dialogue in the physical and emotional world."

### Scene Transition Craft
**What it measures**: Whether transitions between scenes are handled with deliberate craft -- smooth enough to maintain reading flow but clear enough to orient the reader -- rather than being jarring, confusing, or absent.
**Assumed goal**: Commercial fiction editorial tradition (Plot to Punctuation, September C. Fawkes) where transitions should orient the reader in time, place, and POV within the first few sentences of a new scene -- experimental fiction (Burroughs, Pynchon) may deliberately disorient through cut-up or non-sequitur transitions.
**Score 0**: Transitions are either absent (the reader is suddenly in a new time/place with no orientation) or so heavy-handed (pages of transitional material) that they kill momentum. POV changes occur mid-paragraph without signals.
**Score 5**: Transitions use bridging techniques (imagery or thematic echoes between scenes), sensory grounding in the new setting, and clear temporal/spatial orientation. The transition method matches the desired pacing effect: sharp cuts for urgency, gradual transitions for contemplation. Each new scene is established quickly.
**Source**: https://www.plottopunctuation.com/article-scenecraft-2-how-to-create-smooth-scene-transitions.html
**Notes**: Plot to Punctuation recommends the "bridging technique" where something at the end of one scene is mirrored at the beginning of the next. Transitions should end one scene on a compelling hook and establish the next scene quickly.

### Micro-Pacing (Line-Level Tempo Control)
**What it measures**: Whether the author controls moment-to-moment reading speed through word choice, sound, and syntax at the individual line level.
**Assumed goal**: Ursula K. Le Guin's Steering the Craft framework -- the principle that prose rhythm is physical ("the noise words make, the sounds and silences") and that the "mind's ear" must hear the prose while writing. This is valued most in literary fiction and poetic prose but applies wherever prose quality matters.
**Score 0**: Word choice shows no awareness of sound or rhythm. Monosyllabic hard-consonant words appear in contemplative passages; polysyllabic soft words appear in action sequences. The prose has no audible rhythm when read aloud.
**Score 5**: Monosyllabic words with hard consonants (crack, slam, jab) accelerate action; polysyllabic words with softer sounds (meandering, luminous, solitude) decelerate for reflection. Word sounds match emotional content. Reading aloud reveals a felt rhythm that embodies the characters' emotional states.
**Source**: https://lithub.com/a-writing-lesson-from-ursula-k-leguin/
**Notes**: Le Guin: "The basic elements of language are physical: the noise words make, the sounds and silences that make the rhythms marking their relationships." Also: "changing sentence rhythms embody and express the emotions of the characters." Writer's Digest adds: use crisp punchy verbs with onomatopoeia (crash, lunge, sweep) and harsh consonant sounds for action; trim unnecessary words.

### Emotional Pacing Congruence
**What it measures**: Whether the prose's formal properties (sentence length, paragraph density, word choice, pace) match the emotional content being described -- anxiety feels fast and fragmented, calm feels flowing and spacious, grief feels weighted and suspended.
**Assumed goal**: Novela Studio's character emotional state alignment model -- the principle that form and content should be congruent: "anxious/panicked: short, fractured sentences; calm/content: flowing, unhurried constructions; confused/overwhelmed: irregular, unpredictable mixing."
**Score 0**: The formal properties of the prose contradict the emotional content. A panic scene is written in long, flowing sentences; a peaceful scene is written in staccato fragments. There is no congruence between what is felt and how it reads.
**Score 5**: The prose's rhythm physically embodies the emotion being described. Readers feel the panic through the fractured sentences, the peace through the flowing prose. The Word Codex describes this as micro-pacing setting "the temperature and pressure of the prose."
**Source**: https://novela.so/en/blog/how-to-use-prose-rhythm
**Notes**: Novela Studio: "Match sentence construction to internal experience." Word Codex: "micro-pacing is how the temperature and pressure of the prose are set by the author and felt by the reader." Dostoyevsky's "I am a sick man... I am a spiteful man" exemplifies fractured rhythm embodying psychological turbulence.

### Expectation-Silence-Surprise Pattern
**What it measures**: Whether the prose deploys a rhythmic trinity of establishing patterns (expectation), inserting breath and pause (silence), and breaking patterns strategically (surprise) at both the sentence and scene level.
**Assumed goal**: Katherine Catmull's framework (via Ze Frank's concept, published at Yellow Bird Editors) -- a specific model for prose-level rhythm control. This applies primarily to literary and upmarket commercial fiction where prose craft is foregrounded.
**Score 0**: The prose is either all surprise (no patterns established, so nothing can be disrupted) or all expectation (patterns never broken, creating monotony). Silence (deliberate pause) is absent.
**Score 5**: Patterns are established through consistent rhythms (sustained dialogue, similar sentence lengths, consecutive short paragraphs), then paused through lingering description or longer sentences, then broken through isolated short sentences, perspective shifts, or unexpected information. All three elements work in balance.
**Source**: https://www.yellowbirdeditors.com/blog/2016/8/19/make-your-prose-pacey-how-to-engage-with-expectation-silence-and-surprise
**Notes**: Catmull: "When it's all surprise, it stops being a surprise. The craft of it is in the matter of all three." Specific techniques: isolate a single short sentence in its own paragraph for emphasis; abruptly zoom into minute detail or shift to wide perspective.

### Escalating Stakes Across Scenes
**What it measures**: Whether each successive scene raises the stakes, consequences, or complexity compared to the previous one, creating a progressive escalation toward the climax.
**Assumed goal**: Story Grid and commercial fiction structural tradition where "a well-structured story raises the stakes scene by scene and act by act" -- picaresque fiction, slice-of-life literary fiction, and episodic structures may deliberately avoid escalation for other effects.
**Score 0**: Stakes remain flat throughout or actually decrease. Early scenes are more intense than later ones. There is no sense of progressive building. Or: stakes start too high and have nowhere to escalate.
**Score 5**: Each scene raises emotional or physical stakes. Consequences of failure become more significant with each decision. The escalation feels organic (emerging from character choices) rather than mechanical (external threats arbitrarily increasing). The climax represents the culmination of accumulated stakes.
**Source**: https://storygrid.com/mastering-the-clock/
**Notes**: Story Grid: "Start small and then escalate, with each scene raising the emotional or physical stakes." Fiction University: "The root problem of starting with too-high stakes is that they can't escalate." Escalation creates tension; flat stakes create boredom.

### Tension Layering (Multiple Simultaneous Tensions)
**What it measures**: Whether scenes carry multiple sources of potential conflict simultaneously (overarching plot tension, interpersonal tension, internal tension) rather than relying on a single conflict source.
**Assumed goal**: September C. Fawkes' pacing framework -- the principle that "a single conflict per scene is rarely enough to maintain reader engagement" and that tension (the anticipation of conflict) is distinct from conflict itself.
**Score 0**: Each scene contains only one source of tension or conflict. When that tension resolves, there is nothing else maintaining engagement. Scenes feel thin and one-dimensional.
**Score 5**: Scenes layer multiple tension sources: a protagonist rescuing someone from danger (overarching) while disagreeing with a companion (interpersonal) while doubting their own judgment (internal). When one tension resolves, others persist. Reader engagement is maintained through continuous anticipation.
**Source**: https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2018/06/8-common-pacing-problems.html
**Notes**: Fawkes: "Tension is the anticipation or potential for conflict to happen. Conflict is the actual problem happening." She recommends brainstorming multiple potential conflicts, plus intrigue, intellectual stimulation, and emotional appeals.

### Ticking Clock / Narrative Urgency
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative creates a sense of urgency through deadlines, countdowns, or deteriorating situations that pressure characters to act now rather than later.
**Assumed goal**: Thriller/suspense craft tradition (Fiction University, Story Grid) where ticking clocks are fundamental pacing accelerators -- literary fiction may create urgency through emotional or existential pressure rather than literal deadlines.
**Score 0**: Characters face no time pressure. There is no reason events must happen now rather than later. The narrative lacks any sense of urgency.
**Score 5**: The story creates proportionate urgency -- literal deadlines, deteriorating situations, or emotional windows closing -- that pressures characters into action and accelerates reader engagement. The urgency is organic (arising from story logic) rather than arbitrary.
**Source**: http://blog.janicehardy.com/2015/09/why-every-plot-needs-ticking-clock.html
**Notes**: Fiction University: "You want a certain amount of pressure on your protagonist to act to resolve the novel's core conflict, which not only keeps tensions high, but it speeds up the pacing." Urgency can be emotional, not just temporal: "something important might be lost -- not necessarily in minutes or hours, but in emotion, opportunity, or identity."

### Subtext as Pacing Lubricant
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative employs subtext (meaning implied but not stated) to maintain intellectual engagement and reading momentum, rather than making everything explicit.
**Assumed goal**: September C. Fawkes' pacing framework -- the principle that subtext engages readers intellectually by requiring interpretation, which accelerates perceived pacing even in structurally slower scenes. This is valued most in literary fiction and character-driven commercial fiction.
**Score 0**: Everything is stated explicitly. Characters say exactly what they mean. The narrator explains every implication. Readers have nothing to interpret, resulting in perceived slowness even during action.
**Score 5**: Subtext runs beneath dialogue and narration, engaging readers intellectually. Characters say one thing and mean another. Implications are left for the reader to assemble. This intellectual engagement creates a sense of pace even in contemplative scenes.
**Source**: https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2018/06/8-common-pacing-problems.html
**Notes**: Fawkes: "Without subtext, emotion becomes too direct and readers lack intellectual investment, making pacing feel slow despite correct beats and plotting." She describes editing a manuscript with "all the right beats and emotional draws" that still felt slow due to absent subtext.

### Weight Distribution (Word Count Proportional to Narrative Importance)
**What it measures**: Whether the number of words devoted to an event or topic is proportional to its actual importance in the story.
**Assumed goal**: General fiction craft principle from multiple sources (Wikipedia on narrative pace, September C. Fawkes) -- the principle that "the number of words devoted to an event depends on its narrative importance, not actual duration." Beginning writers tend to weight all moments equally.
**Score 0**: Trivial events receive extensive coverage while important events are rushed. Extended descriptions of minor characters appear while plot revelations are compressed into single lines. The story's emphasis is misaligned with its actual priorities.
**Score 5**: Word count allocation matches narrative importance. Major revelations, turning points, and emotional climaxes receive expansive treatment. Routine transitions, minor events, and setup receive proportionate (smaller) attention. As Fawkes notes: "The more words you spend talking about something, the more the weight of the story shifts in that direction."
**Source**: https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2018/06/8-common-pacing-problems.html
**Notes**: Also from Wikipedia on Pace (narrative): "The number of words devoted to an event depends on its narrative importance, not actual duration." Beginning writers "weight all moments equally" where experienced authors "selectively expand important scenes while compressing trivial ones."

### Flashback Integration Without Momentum Loss
**What it measures**: Whether flashbacks are integrated into the narrative without destroying present-timeline momentum, and whether they earn their space by illuminating the present.
**Assumed goal**: Commercial fiction editorial tradition (Moore Attuned, Alyssa Matesic) where flashbacks must justify their momentum cost -- modernist and postmodernist fiction (Faulkner, Morrison) may use non-linear temporality as a primary structural principle.
**Score 0**: Flashbacks interrupt tense present-action scenes, causing readers to skim forward. They run for pages without illuminating the present. Re-entry to the present timeline is disorienting.
**Score 5**: Flashbacks are positioned during natural lulls rather than mid-action. They are brief (a few lines when possible), increase present-scene tension or provide essential context, and end with a clear return to the present. Longer flashbacks are placed at scene or chapter boundaries.
**Source**: https://mooreattuned.com/5-things-that-slow-pacing-of-your-novel/
**Notes**: Moore Attuned: "Flashbacks, by definition, pause the forward momentum of the story." Matesic: "If the flashback goes on for more than a few pages, the reader will likely lose interest or begin to forget what was happening in the present narrative."

### Opening Pace and Inciting Incident Timing
**What it measures**: Whether the story's opening establishes engagement quickly enough for its genre, with the inciting incident arriving at an appropriate point rather than being delayed excessively or rushed.
**Assumed goal**: Agent-editor submission tradition (Helping Writers Become Authors, Story Grid) where "agents these days are asking for the inciting incident to happen within the first ten pages" -- literary fiction permits a slower opening that establishes character and atmosphere before the inciting event. Genre determines expectations.
**Score 0**: The inciting incident is buried deep in the manuscript (past 20% of the story) with no hooks to sustain interest during the preamble. Or: the inciting incident occurs on page one before the reader has any reason to care.
**Score 5**: The hook arrives almost immediately (within 1% of the narrative). The inciting incident is placed appropriately for genre -- thrillers within the first few pages, literary fiction within the first 10%, epic fantasy perhaps somewhat later but with strong hooks maintaining engagement. The reader's baseline reality is established efficiently before being disrupted.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/your-books-inciting-event-its-not-what-you-think-it-is/
**Notes**: Story Grid: "The Hook occurs ideally at the 1% mark." The inciting incident can go at 1% (immediate disruption), 10% (after character establishment), or before the story begins (in medias res). Genre determines which is appropriate.

### Sagging Middle Prevention
**What it measures**: Whether the story's middle section (approximately Act 2) maintains momentum through meaningful change, escalation, and a strong midpoint, rather than collapsing into repetitive filler.
**Assumed goal**: Three-act structure tradition (Helping Writers Become Authors, Inkshift) where the midpoint shift divides Act 2 into two purposeful halves -- episodic and picaresque fiction may structure the middle differently but still requires scene-level engagement.
**Score 0**: The middle section drags. The same types of conflicts repeat without escalation. Thematic conflict becomes murky. The protagonist stops making significant decisions. Chapters blur together. Readers report that "the beginning hooked me and the ending delivered, but the middle dragged."
**Score 5**: A strong midpoint event divides the middle into two distinct halves. The protagonist transitions from reactive to proactive. Each scene in the middle section adds new information, raises stakes, or forces character growth. The middle feels like purposeful escalation, not padding.
**Source**: https://inkshift.io/resources/saggy-middle
**Notes**: Inkshift identifies five distinct saggy middle problems, with repetitive conflict being the most common: "each individual scene might be well-written. The repetition only becomes apparent when you read across chapters." The fix: meaningful change at the midpoint.

### Climax Pacing (Sufficient Buildup and Space)
**What it measures**: Whether the climactic sequence receives adequate space and buildup to feel earned and impactful, rather than being rushed or anticlimactic.
**Assumed goal**: General fiction craft principle (Writing Mastery, Fiction University, Donald Maass) -- the climax is the payoff of accumulated tension, and rushing it wastes everything the story has built. This applies across all genres.
**Score 0**: The climax resolves in a few paragraphs after hundreds of pages of buildup. Conflicts are resolved too easily. The emotional payoff falls flat because the resolution was too quick. Or: the climax is overextended past its natural breaking point.
**Score 5**: The climax receives proportionate space. Pacing slows briefly before the climax (descriptive details, inner thought) to heighten suspense, then accelerates during the climactic action (short sentences, rapid events). The contrast between buildup and release creates maximum impact.
**Source**: https://www.writingmastery.com/blog/what-is-an-anticlimax-tips-to-avoid-an-anticlimactic-ending
**Notes**: Writing Mastery: "A hasty climax can leave readers dissatisfied and confused." Beemgee: "If you've rushed the ending, you'll leave readers feeling unsatisfied." The fix: give your climax ample space; slow down before to heighten, speed up during to deliver.

### Subplot Pacing as Counterpoint
**What it measures**: Whether subplots function as pacing relief valves and tonal counterpoints to the main plot, providing variety and preventing monotony.
**Assumed goal**: Commercial fiction structural tradition (Writers.com, Savannah Gilbo) where subplots "vary the pace and tone" -- single-plot minimalist fiction (some short stories, novellas) may intentionally avoid subplots.
**Score 0**: Subplots either don't exist (the main plot runs at a single unvarying intensity) or exist but compete with the main plot for attention without providing contrast or relief.
**Score 5**: When the main plot is high-intensity, cutting to a slower-paced subplot provides tension release. Subplots offer tonal counterpoint (humor in a thriller, gravity in a comedy). The interplay between main plot and subplots creates "musical counterpoint" -- richer rhythm than either alone.
**Source**: https://writers.com/subplots-in-fiction
**Notes**: Writers.com: "Subplots are your chance to vary the pace and tone. When the main A-plot is a high-stakes thriller, you can cut to a slower, more emotional B-plot. This provides a necessary release of tension, making the return to the A-plot even more impactful."

### Scene Variety (Avoiding Repetitive Scene Structure)
**What it measures**: Whether scenes vary in their internal structure, openings, and resolutions, rather than following the same pattern every time.
**Assumed goal**: Helping Writers Become Authors editorial framework -- the principle that "if every scene starts and ends the same way, readers will pick up on the pattern and zone out." This applies across all fiction but is especially critical in genre fiction with naturally similar scene types (investigations in mysteries, battles in fantasy).
**Score 0**: Every scene follows an identical structural pattern. All scenes open the same way, develop the same way, and close the same way. In a cop story, every investigation scene is structurally interchangeable. Readers detect the formula and disengage.
**Score 5**: Scenes vary in opening (action, dialogue, description, flashback), development (escalation patterns, revelation timing), and closing (hook types, resolution levels). Even structurally similar scenes (multiple investigation scenes) each bring something new in structure, not just content.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/6-questions-to-help-you-avoid-repetitive-scenes/
**Notes**: The solution to repetitive scenes is usually to "delete the scene you don't need and/or combine its important bits with a more comprehensive and important scene." Experiment with different openings: "a shocking revelation, a flashback, or an in-the-moment action."

### Emotional Pacing for Heavy Content
**What it measures**: Whether narratives dealing with grief, trauma, or other emotionally heavy content manage reader overwhelm through tonal variation, narrative distance shifts, and breathing room.
**Assumed goal**: Trauma narrative craft tradition (Brevity journal, Aggie Stewart's emotional pacing framework) -- the principle that "readability in a grief novel often comes from voice, pacing, and tonal variation rather than plot twists." This applies to any fiction handling emotionally intense material.
**Score 0**: Heavy emotional content is delivered at unrelenting intensity with no variation in narrative distance, tone, or pace. The reader is either numbed by constant darkness or emotionally cornered by explicit emotional detail with no room for their own response.
**Score 5**: Narrative distance shifts around emotional events (sometimes close, sometimes pulled back). Dark humor, observational detail, and scenes of ordinary life give readers breathing room. The author "pulls back so the reader doesn't have to," allowing the reader's own emotional response to be "more powerful, intimate, and immediate."
**Source**: https://brevitymag.com/craft-essays/emotional-pacing/
**Notes**: Brevity: "Emotional pacing relies on shifts in narrative distance around emotional events. Techniques can be subtle (inserting a poetic device, creating a juxtaposition) or overt (changing POV, verb tense, or genre, or inserting scene/chapter breaks)." Jami Gold adds: "If you're too explicit with emotional details, you corner the reader."

### Show/Tell Balance for Pacing Control
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative strategically uses both showing (dramatized, expanded) and telling (summarized, compressed) to control pace, rather than defaulting exclusively to one mode.
**Assumed goal**: Fiction craft tradition that treats showing and telling as complementary pacing tools rather than a binary rule -- the "show don't tell" maxim oversimplifies what is actually a pacing-control spectrum. This nuanced view is held by developmental editors and experienced craft teachers.
**Score 0**: The narrative is either all showing (every moment dramatized at equal expansion, creating exhausting length) or all telling (everything summarized, creating emotional distance). There is no strategic toggling between modes.
**Score 5**: Significant events are shown (dramatized with sensory detail, dialogue, and unfolding action). Context and transitions are told (summarized efficiently). The author recognizes that showing slows pace and creates richness while telling speeds pace and provides efficiency, and toggles deliberately between them.
**Source**: https://indieauthormagazine.com/time-for-show-and-tell/
**Notes**: Indie Author Magazine: "Showing slows the story but also imparts a richness that readers crave, while telling speeds up the pace." SuperSummary adds: "A rule of thumb is to always show significant events, while using narrative summary for information that is important for context but doesn't need dramatising."

### Audience Expectation Alignment
**What it measures**: Whether the story's pacing delivers what its genre and opening promise, rather than surprising readers with content they didn't sign up for.
**Assumed goal**: Genre fiction commercial tradition (September C. Fawkes) where misalignment between reader expectations and delivered content creates perceived pacing problems regardless of actual structural quality -- literary fiction has wider latitude because readers expect the unexpected.
**Score 0**: The story promises one type of experience (thriller, romance, horror) and delivers pacing appropriate to a different type. A thriller spends most of its time in philosophical reflection. A literary novel delivers relentless action with no depth. Readers who picked up the book for its genre feel cheated.
**Score 5**: Pacing matches genre promises. A thriller delivers fast pacing with brief reflective interludes. A literary novel delivers contemplative pacing with strategic dramatic moments. The content readers came for appears with appropriate frequency and weight.
**Source**: https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2018/06/8-common-pacing-problems.html
**Notes**: Fawkes: "Imagine a Jurassic movie where the main plot centered on two characters working at the theme park falling in love, with no dino terror until the end." Readers have genre expectations; delivering different content creates perceived sluggishness regardless of craft quality.

### Mood Transition Smoothness
**What it measures**: Whether shifts between different emotional tones (comedy to tragedy, action to intimacy, horror to calm) are handled with transitional craft rather than jarring abruptness.
**Assumed goal**: Beemgee editorial diagnostic framework -- the principle that "abrupt tonal changes disorient readers" and that foreshadowing and gradual transitions maintain coherence. This applies across all fiction but is especially important in stories that mix tones.
**Score 0**: Mood shifts are abrupt and disorienting. A comedic scene immediately follows a death scene with no transitional element. The emotional whiplash confuses rather than stimulates.
**Score 5**: Mood transitions are prepared through foreshadowing, gradual tempo changes, and transitional passages that allow the reader to adjust. The transitions maintain coherence while the tonal variety provides richness.
**Source**: https://www.beemgee.com/blog/eight-signs-that-the-pacing-of-your-novel-is-off-how-to-fix-it/
**Notes**: Beemgee identifies "mood shifts jarringly between scenes" as a sign of too-fast pacing. Fix: "Add foreshadowing signaling mood changes; slow transitions to maintain reader investment and narrative coherence."

### Time Skip Clarity and Justification
**What it measures**: Whether jumps in time (ellipsis) are clearly signaled to the reader and justified by the narrative, rather than being confusing or arbitrary.
**Assumed goal**: Commercial fiction craft tradition (Writer's Edit, Plot to Punctuation) where readers must never be confused about temporal location -- experimental fiction (Vonnegut, Mitchell) may use disorienting time structure as a deliberate technique.
**Score 0**: Time passes without the reader realizing it, causing confusion about when events are happening. Or: every time skip is accompanied by heavy-handed timestamps that interrupt flow. Or: time skips occur at inappropriate moments (mid-scene).
**Score 5**: Time skips are clearly signaled through white space, scene breaks, chapter breaks, or brief orienting phrases. They skip only unimportant time periods. Re-entry after a skip quickly establishes when and where we are. The character connections are strong enough that readers care about what happens on the other side.
**Source**: https://writersedit.com/fiction-writing/5-ways-present-flow-time-story/
**Notes**: Writer's Edit: "Don't make readers guess that time has passed." Fonda Lee advises that "timeskips in themselves are pretty jarring, no matter how they're handled, so use them only when absolutely necessary." Character connection must be strong enough to survive the discontinuity.

### Showing vs. Telling for Info Dumps
**What it measures**: Whether backstory, worldbuilding, and expository information is woven into the narrative through action and dialogue rather than delivered in static blocks that halt momentum.
**Assumed goal**: Broadly shared anti-infodump principle across commercial and literary fiction -- the principle that "info dumping immediately feels like a lecture instead of an experience." Epic fantasy (Tolkien, Sanderson) may use more explicit exposition but still faces the pacing cost.
**Score 0**: Backstory arrives in multi-paragraph blocks of pure exposition. Characters deliver speeches of information the reader needs but no character would naturally say. The narrative halts for worldbuilding lectures.
**Score 5**: Information is woven into scenes through character action, dialogue that reveals naturally, and sensory detail. The reader learns about the world by watching characters interact with it. Necessary exposition is broken into small pieces distributed across scenes.
**Source**: https://medium.com/new-writers-welcome/7-common-mistakes-fiction-writers-make-with-pacing-tension-5d1b79d31383
**Notes**: Medium/Winifred Cyril: "Info dumping immediately feels like a lecture instead of an experience. A story makes the reader feel, imagine, and contemplate." Also Kristen Corrects: "Action scenes that are glossed over with summarization fail to immerse readers."

### Redundancy Elimination (Show AND Tell Problem)
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative avoids the specific redundancy of showing something through action/dialogue and then immediately explaining what was just shown through narration.
**Assumed goal**: September C. Fawkes' editorial diagnostic -- the principle that showing something and then telling the reader the same information wastes words and slows pacing through redundancy. This applies universally.
**Score 0**: The narrative consistently shows a character's emotion through action, then explains in narration what the reader just witnessed. "She slammed her fist on the table. She was angry." Every implication is over-explained.
**Score 5**: When something is shown effectively, the narration trusts the reader to understand it. Telling only restates shown material when it adds genuinely new information, a different perspective, or deeper significance beyond what the action conveyed.
**Source**: https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2018/06/8-common-pacing-problems.html
**Notes**: Fawkes: "Writers show something, then restate with explanatory paragraphs what readers already comprehended, creating redundancy that slows pacing." The fix: "Trust audience comprehension. Only repeat information if the telling adds new meaning, value, or information beyond what was shown."

### Reader Ahead of Character Problem
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative avoids situations where the reader has already figured out something obvious but the character hasn't, creating false mystery that drags pacing.
**Assumed goal**: September C. Fawkes' pacing diagnostic -- distinguishing between genuine suspense (the audience knows and anticipates) and false mystery (the audience knows but the character is pointlessly confused). This is a common problem in genre fiction with trope-savvy readers.
**Score 0**: The reader figures out obvious conclusions long before characters do, and the narrative treats the character's ignorance as mystery or suspense. Pages are spent with the character confused about something the reader solved chapters ago.
**Score 5**: When readers can clearly predict an outcome, the story either subverts expectations or moves forward quickly. True suspense (readers anticipating how plans will play out) is used instead of false mystery (readers waiting for characters to catch up). The distinction between reader-ahead-as-suspense and reader-ahead-as-boredom is maintained.
**Source**: https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2018/06/8-common-pacing-problems.html
**Notes**: Fawkes: "This differs from true suspense, where audiences anticipate how known plans will play out." The fix: "Avoid prolonging obviously resolved mysteries. Either subvert reader expectations or move plot forward once conclusions become apparent."

### Prose Stress Pattern Awareness
**What it measures**: Whether the author demonstrates awareness of syllabic stress patterns in prose, using natural English stress to create rhythm that supports the narrative's emotional register.
**Assumed goal**: Virginia Woolf's "Style is a very simple matter; it is all rhythm" school and the broader poetic-prose tradition (Le Guin, Morrison, McCarthy) -- this is primarily valued in literary fiction and poetic prose, less so in plot-driven commercial fiction.
**Score 0**: The prose's stress patterns fight against the content. Trochaic (stressed-unstressed) patterns appear where iambic (unstressed-stressed) would flow better. Sentences feel choppy or awkward when read aloud due to clashing stress.
**Score 5**: Natural English stress patterns are used to create rhythm that supports content. Two-syllable feet (iambs, trochees) read briskly; anapests slow things down. The prose has a cadence that feels "right" when heard, even in silent reading through the "mind's ear."
**Source**: https://www.storyinliteraryfiction.com/essays-on-writing/incorporating-rhythm-in-prose-style/
**Notes**: Virginia Woolf: "Style is a very simple matter; it is all rhythm." The essay identifies five indicators of weak rhythm: reading interruption, reduced comprehension ease, absence of smooth progression, discordant imagery, and non-sequiturs. Caveat: "attention to rhythm is certainly not necessary for every writer of fiction to achieve greatness."

### POV Switch Pacing as Hook
**What it measures**: Whether POV switches at chapter or scene breaks are used as a pacing tool, creating natural hooks by forcing readers to leave one storyline before returning.
**Assumed goal**: Multi-POV commercial fiction tradition (George R.R. Martin, Gillian Flynn) where POV switches function as built-in hooks -- single-POV fiction does not apply.
**Score 0**: POV switches occur at arbitrary points with no regard for hook potential. Readers switch to a new POV with no tension about the storyline they just left. Or: the hooked storyline isn't returned to for so many chapters that readers forget or stop caring.
**Score 5**: POV switches occur at moments of unresolved tension in the departing storyline, creating a natural hook. The hooked storyline is returned to within 1-2 chapters. Each POV's internal pacing works independently while the switching pattern creates inter-storyline tension.
**Source**: https://rivereditor.com/guides/how-to-write-compelling-chapter-hooks-keep-readers-reading-2026
**Notes**: River Editor: "POV switches at chapter breaks can function as hooks themselves -- reader wants to know what happens in one storyline but must follow another character first. However, return to hooked storylines within 1-2 chapters maximum to maintain reader interest and prevent frustration."


## Sentinels Found

### Metronomic Sentence Length
**Detection**: Measure the word count of 10+ consecutive sentences. If the coefficient of variation (standard deviation / mean) is below 0.15, the sentences are suspiciously uniform.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs optimize for consistent clarity and grammatical correctness, producing sentences that avoid extremes of very short or very long. The result is text with low burstiness -- perfectly even sentence variation that feels "smooth but oddly mechanical."
**Design space cost**: Loss of rhythmic musicality. Human prose achieves its emotional range through dramatic variation in sentence length -- Gary Provost's "the writing sings" comes from mixing five-word punches with thirty-word crescendos.
**Source**: https://medium.com/@VerifyHQ/how-token-prediction-shapes-ai-writing-style-deep-dive-for-detection-and-mastery-b0f259491a65

### Uniform Paragraph Length
**Detection**: Measure the line/word count of 10+ consecutive paragraphs. If all paragraphs are within 20% of the median length, the variation is insufficient.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: AI maintains near-identical paragraph lengths across entire pieces, contrasting with human writers who vary paragraph architecture based on emotional and pacing needs. Detection tools flag this as a strong AI signal.
**Design space cost**: Loss of visual rhythm on the page. Paragraph length variation is the primary tool for controlling how readers "breathe, pause, and emotionally absorb." Uniform paragraphs flatten both the visual and emotional experience.
**Source**: https://www.pangram.com/blog/comprehensive-guide-to-spotting-ai-writing-patterns

### Subject-Verb-Object Sentence Pattern Lock
**Detection**: Check whether 5+ consecutive sentences follow the same grammatical pattern (e.g., all subject-first declaratives: "He did X. She did Y. They went Z.").
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs default to the most common English sentence structure (SVO declarative) and rarely vary into inverted constructions, leading questions, fragments, or imperative forms. The syntactic uniformity persists even when sentence length varies.
**Design space cost**: Loss of syntactic musicality. Human prose derives much of its rhythm from structural variation -- dependent-clause openers, sensory-detail openers, interrogatives, fragments. Locking into SVO creates what multiple sources call "prose that flattens out no matter how varied the length."
**Source**: https://novela.so/en/blog/how-to-use-prose-rhythm

### Perfectly Smooth Transitions
**Detection**: Check whether every paragraph transition uses formulaic connective phrases ("However," "Moreover," "Additionally," "Furthermore," "In contrast"). Count the frequency of such transitions per page.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: AI generates "clear topic sentences, perfect transitions, tidy conclusions" that create "rigid, unnatural scaffolding." Human writing has rougher, more organic transitions that sometimes surprise or disorient slightly.
**Design space cost**: Loss of natural voice. Perfect transitions eliminate the "natural messiness" that readers recognize as authentic human communication. The prose sounds processed rather than lived-in.
**Source**: https://medium.com/startup-insider-edge/ais-writing-quirks-too-much-dash-too-little-soul-f832903f267e

### Em-Dash Overuse for Artificial Pause
**Detection**: Count em-dashes per 1,000 words. If the frequency exceeds 5 per 1,000 words, especially when semicolons and parentheses are rare, this indicates AI-generated prose.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: AI "relies heavily on em dashes to create artificial explanatory pauses instead of varying punctuation naturally." The em-dash becomes the sole pause mechanism, replacing the variety of punctuation (semicolons, parentheses, ellipses, periods) that human writers use.
**Design space cost**: Loss of punctuation variety. Each punctuation mark creates a different quality of pause -- semicolons link related thoughts, parentheses add asides, ellipses trail off. Em-dash monoculture reduces the micro-rhythm palette.
**Source**: https://www.pangram.com/blog/comprehensive-guide-to-spotting-ai-writing-patterns

### Three-Item List Pattern
**Detection**: Check for recurring structures where descriptions or examples come in lists of exactly three. Count occurrences per chapter.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: AI defaults to the rhetorical "rule of three" from training data, producing "lists of exactly three items" as a tic rather than a deliberate stylistic choice. Human writers vary list lengths based on content needs.
**Design space cost**: Predictability at the micro-level. When every description has exactly three elements, the reader's ear detects the pattern and the prose feels formulaic rather than responsive to its content.
**Source**: https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction

### Dialogue-Exposition-Narration Block Structure
**Detection**: Check whether the text alternates in rigid blocks: a block of dialogue, then a block of exposition, then a block of narration, then dialogue again, never mixing these modes within passages.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: AI organizes text into "distinct dialogue, exposition, and narration zones" creating "blocky" structure. Human writers weave these modes together within paragraphs and passages.
**Design space cost**: Loss of textural complexity. Human prose gains rhythm from interleaving dialogue with action, thought, and description within the same passage, creating a fluid experience rather than a zone-by-zone march.
**Source**: https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction

### Stacked Sensory Details
**Detection**: Check whether multiple sensory modalities (sight, sound, smell, touch, taste) are invoked in rapid succession within single passages, rather than selecting one or two effective sensory details.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: AI over-applies the workshop advice "use sensory details to immerse the reader" by stacking multiple senses simultaneously. Human writers usually emphasize one sensory modality per moment.
**Design space cost**: Loss of sensory focus. Stacking senses creates overload rather than immersion. A single precisely chosen sensory detail is more evocative than a checklist of all five senses.
**Source**: https://www.rachelneumeier.com/2026/03/13/what-makes-fiction-look-generated/

### Repetitive Scene Beat Pattern
**Detection**: Map the sequence of beats across 5+ scenes (e.g., Description -> Theme -> Small action -> Description -> Foreshadowing). If the same beat sequence repeats across scenes, the structure is formulaic.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: AI applies beat sheet patterns mechanically, creating "the same beats happening over and over in predictable sequences." Human writers vary beat patterns scene to scene based on content needs.
**Design space cost**: Loss of scene-level surprise and variety. When every scene follows the same internal rhythm, readers detect the formula and disengage. The prose feels generated by template rather than emerging from the story's needs.
**Source**: https://www.rachelneumeier.com/2026/03/13/what-makes-fiction-look-generated/

### Generic Emotional Reactions
**Detection**: Check whether characters' emotional responses to events are predictable, appropriate-seeming, and interchangeable (any character would respond this way). No character has a surprising or distinctively personal emotional reaction.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: AI generates "generic, appropriate emotions rather than authentic reactions" because it predicts the most probable emotional response from training data. Human characters have idiosyncratic responses shaped by their specific history and personality.
**Design space cost**: Loss of character distinctiveness and emotional authenticity. When every character responds with the "correct" emotion (sad at death, happy at reunion), the prose feels like a surface-level simulation of human experience.
**Source**: https://www.rachelneumeier.com/2026/03/13/what-makes-fiction-look-generated/

### Absence of Contractions in Prose
**Detection**: Check the ratio of contracted forms ("it's," "don't," "can't") to uncontracted forms ("it is," "do not," "cannot") in prose narration and especially in dialogue. If contractions are rare or absent, the prose reads as stilted.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs "rarely use contractions, creating a stilted cadence compared to conversational human writing." This is particularly noticeable in dialogue, where the absence of contractions makes characters sound robotic.
**Design space cost**: Loss of conversational naturalness and voice. Contractions are a fundamental rhythm tool -- "don't" is one syllable where "do not" is two, and the choice affects micro-pacing at every occurrence.
**Source**: https://www.pangram.com/blog/comprehensive-guide-to-spotting-ai-writing-patterns

### No Recovery Beats Between Action Sequences
**Detection**: Check whether consecutive high-intensity scenes occur without any intervening reflection, processing, or lower-intensity scene. Three or more consecutive action scenes with no sequel/reaction is a red flag.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: AI trained on action-heavy fiction may generate continuous escalation without understanding the psychological need for processing. Human writers instinctively provide recovery because they themselves need to process the emotion.
**Design space cost**: Loss of emotional impact through contrast. Without recovery, action becomes numbing. The Swain/Bickham model exists precisely because constant action without reaction creates shallow, exhausting reading.
**Source**: https://www.advancedfictionwriting.com/blog/2009/05/25/scenes-sequels-and-chapters/

### "Not X; Y" Rhetorical Tic
**Detection**: Count instances of the "not X, but Y" or "not X; Y" construction per chapter. If this pattern appears more than 3 times per 1,000 words, it indicates AI prose.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: This is an LLM-specific rhetorical tic -- "It's not just X, it's also Y" and "not X; Y" constructions appear "ubiquitously" across AI-generated text as a substitution for more varied rhetorical structures.
**Design space cost**: Loss of rhetorical variety. Human writers achieve contrast through dozens of different constructions. The "not X; Y" monoculture sounds like a single prose voice speaking across all contexts.
**Source**: https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction

### Consistent Escalation Without Plateaus
**Detection**: Map the tension level of each scene across the full narrative. If tension increases monotonically with no drops, plateaus, or deliberate regressions, the escalation is mechanical.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: AI may learn "escalate stakes each scene" as a rule and apply it mechanically, producing relentless escalation without the deliberate valleys that human storytellers use for contrast. Real stories breathe.
**Design space cost**: Loss of the tension-release cycle that makes peaks feel sharp. Monotonic escalation numbs rather than thrills. The "roller coaster" metaphor requires both ascents and descents.
**Source**: https://www.writersdigest.com/pacing-for-emotional-impact-in-fiction-building-tension-and-release-in-a-novel

### Every Chapter Ends with a Cliffhanger
**Detection**: Check whether every chapter ending uses the same hook type (especially unresolved danger or withheld information). If more than 80% of chapters in non-thriller fiction end with cliffhangers, the hook strategy is monotonous.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: AI may over-apply the "chapter must end with a hook" rule by defaulting to cliffhangers rather than varying hook types. Human writers cycle through question, revelation, emotional shift, decision, and other hook types.
**Design space cost**: Loss of hook variety and authenticity. "Page-turner doesn't mean manipulative cliffhanger on every chapter. That exhausts readers and ultimately feels cheap." Over-hooking creates reader cynicism rather than genuine engagement.
**Source**: https://rivereditor.com/guides/how-to-write-compelling-chapter-hooks-keep-readers-reading-2026

### Symmetrical Dialogue Exchanges
**Detection**: In dialogue scenes, check whether speaker turns alternate perfectly (A-B-A-B) with each turn being roughly the same length. No interruptions, overlaps, trailing off, or monologues.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: AI generates "extremely curt exchanges" with "artificial 'cliffhanger' endings on dialogue" and an even back-and-forth pattern. Human dialogue is messier -- people interrupt, monologue, trail off, talk past each other.
**Design space cost**: Loss of dialogue authenticity and the pacing variety that comes from asymmetric exchanges. Real conversation has variable turn lengths and irregular rhythm.
**Source**: https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction

### Describe-Speak-Act Cycle in Dialogue
**Detection**: Check whether dialogue follows a repeating three-part cycle: (1) character speaks, (2) performs an action with emotional description, (3) next character responds with the same structure. If this pattern repeats more than 3 times consecutively, it's formulaic.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: AI follows "a repeating three-part cycle where a character speaks, performs an action with emotional description, and then the next character responds with the same structure." Human writers vary dialogue tagging, sometimes using bare dialogue, sometimes action beats, sometimes internal thought.
**Design space cost**: Loss of dialogue scene rhythm. Monotonous dialogue formatting creates the same reading experience regardless of the scene's emotional content. A tense confrontation should feel different from a casual conversation at the structural level.
**Source**: https://www.rachelneumeier.com/2026/03/13/what-makes-fiction-look-generated/

### Deteriorating Quality in Later Chapters
**Detection**: Compare prose quality metrics (sentence variation, vocabulary diversity, structural novelty) between the first 20% and the last 20% of the text. If the later sections show measurably reduced variation, the text may have been generated with accumulating context pressure.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: AI shows "inconsistency over time: early chapters pass scrutiny; later sections deteriorate as the model perpetuates its own errors." Once the model enters a local minimum of clichés, "it won't escape."
**Design space cost**: Loss of sustained craft. Human writers may write uneven drafts but typically revise toward consistency. AI's degradation pattern is distinctive because it's one-directional (gets worse, doesn't recover).
**Source**: https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction

### Identical Narrative Arc Across Stories
**Detection**: Compare the broad story arc against a known AI default pattern. If the story features a protagonist returning to a small town and resolving a minor conflict through community reconnection, it matches the most common AI-generated plot structure.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Cross-model analysis found that "AI-generated stories overwhelmingly conform to a single narrative plot structure: a protagonist lives in or returns home to a small town and resolves a minor conflict by reconnecting with tradition and organizing community events."
**Design space cost**: Loss of narrative originality. This default arc is not inherently bad, but its overwhelming prevalence in AI output makes it a strong signal of generated content.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2603.13545v1

### No Subtext in Dialogue
**Detection**: Check whether characters always say exactly what they mean, with no gap between surface statement and underlying intention. If dialogue is entirely on-the-nose with no implied meaning, subtext is absent.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: AI generates dialogue that is functional and clear but lacks the indirection and implication that creates tension and depth. Subtext requires modeling the gap between what characters say and what they want, which LLMs handle poorly.
**Design space cost**: Loss of intellectual engagement. Subtext engages readers by requiring interpretation; its absence makes dialogue feel flat and pacing feel slow even in fast-moving scenes. As Fawkes notes, "emotion becomes too direct and readers lack intellectual investment."
**Source**: https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2018/06/8-common-pacing-problems.html

### Overexplicit Emotional Narration
**Detection**: Check whether the narrator explicitly labels characters' emotions after showing them through action ("She slammed the door. She was furious."). If emotional labels follow demonstrated emotions more than 50% of the time, the prose is over-explaining.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: AI hedges against reader misunderstanding by making emotion explicit through both showing and telling, creating the "show AND tell" redundancy that Fawkes identifies as a specific pacing problem. Human writers trust the reader.
**Design space cost**: Loss of reader agency and pacing efficiency. Every redundant emotional label wastes words and slows pace while insulting the reader's intelligence. The over-explanation pattern is distinctive because AI cannot judge whether the showing was sufficient.
**Source**: https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2018/06/8-common-pacing-problems.html

### Oxford Comma Consistency as Mechanical Tell
**Detection**: Check whether the Oxford comma is used with 100% consistency throughout. If present in every single list without exception, the consistency is atypical of human writing.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: AI "uses Oxford commas consistently, contrasting with human inconsistency." Human writers tend to have preferred usage but occasionally deviate based on rhythm, emphasis, or inattention. Perfect consistency is a statistical anomaly.
**Design space cost**: Minimal -- the Oxford comma itself is fine. But its perfect consistency is a data point contributing to the overall "mechanical consistency" pattern that makes AI prose detectable. It signals that no human revision pass occurred.
**Source**: https://www.pangram.com/blog/comprehensive-guide-to-spotting-ai-writing-patterns
