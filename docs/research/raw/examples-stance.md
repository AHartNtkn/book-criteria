# Classic Prose Stance: Fiction Excerpts

Reference examples of the classic prose stance as defined by Francis-Noel Thomas
and Mark Turner in *Clear and Simple as the Truth*: the writer presents
observations with quiet confidence, as if pointing something out to an equal. No
hedging, no lecturing, no performing expertise. The motive is truth, the purpose
is presentation, the reader and writer are intellectual equals, and the occasion
is informal.

---

## 1. George Orwell, *Nineteen Eighty-Four* (1949)

> It was a bright cold day in April, and the clocks were striking thirteen.
> Winston Smith, his chin nuzzled into his breast in an effort to escape the vile
> wind, slipped quickly through the glass doors of Victory Mansions, though not
> quickly enough to prevent a swirl of gritty dust from entering along with him.
> The hallway smelt of boiled cabbage and old rag mats.

**What makes it work:** Every sentence states what is, with zero qualification.
The narrator does not wonder, suppose, or explain --- it shows you a man walking
through a door and tells you what the hallway smells like. The single anomalous
detail ("the clocks were striking thirteen") lands hard precisely because it is
delivered with the same flat confidence as everything else; the prose does not
pause to underline its own strangeness.

---

## 2. John Cheever, "The Swimmer" (1964)

> It was one of those midsummer Sundays when everyone sits around saying, "I
> drank too much last night." You might have heard it whispered by the
> parishioners leaving church, heard it from the lips of the priest himself,
> struggling with his cassock in the vestiarium, heard it from the golf links and
> the tennis courts, heard it from the wildlife preserve where the leader of the
> Audubon group was suffering from a terrible hangover. "I drank too much," said
> Donald Westerhazy. "We all drank too much," said Lucinda Merrill.

**What makes it work:** The narrator addresses "you" as a companion who already
knows this world --- not explaining suburban Sunday mornings but pointing across
the landscape at what you can both already see. The escalating catalog (church,
golf links, wildlife preserve) is delivered as shared observation, not satirical
commentary; the comedy arises from the facts themselves, presented without
editorial wink.

---

## 3. Kazuo Ishiguro, *Never Let Me Go* (2005)

> My name is Kathy H. I'm thirty-one years old, and I've been a carer now for
> over eleven years. That sounds long enough, I know, but actually they want me
> to go on for another eight months, until the end of this year. That'll make it
> almost exactly twelve years.

**What makes it work:** Kathy speaks to the reader as someone who already
understands the world she inhabits --- "carer," "they," the institutional
rhythms are presented as plain fact, not exposited. The conversational asides
("I know," "actually") treat the reader as an equal who might raise the same
mild objection she is preemptively answering. The horror of her situation is
never flagged; the stance itself --- calm, matter-of-fact, trusting the reader
to notice --- is what makes the novel devastating.

---

## Common Pattern

All three passages share the structural signature of classic prose: the narrator
occupies the position of someone who has seen something clearly and is now
showing it to a companion. There is no meta-commentary on the act of writing, no
hedging ("it seemed," "perhaps," "one might say"), no bid for the reader's
sympathy or admiration. The prose gets out of its own way. Truth is treated as
something that can be known and stated plainly; the reader is trusted to receive
it without assistance.
