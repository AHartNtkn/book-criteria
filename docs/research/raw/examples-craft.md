# Exemplar Passages: Line-Level Prose Craft

Three short excerpts from published fiction demonstrating concision, strong verbs, precise nouns, and economy of language -- passages where every word earns its place and there is zero filler.

---

## 1. Physical World Through Pure Observation

**Ernest Hemingway, "Big Two-Hearted River: Part I" (1925), from *In Our Time***

> The train went on up the track out of sight, around one of the hills of burnt timber. Nick sat down on the bundle of canvas and bedding the baggage man had pitched out of the door of the baggage car. There was no town, nothing but the rails and the burned-over country. The thirteen saloons that had lined the one street of Seney had not left a trace. The foundations of the Mansion House hotel stuck up above the ground. The stone was chipped and split by the fire. It was all that was left of the town of Seney.

**What makes this work:** Every sentence is a plain declarative statement reporting what Nick sees. There are no qualifiers, no emotional adjectives, no authorial commentary. The verbs do the structural work: "pitched," "stuck up," "chipped and split." The negatives -- "no town," "nothing but," "not left a trace" -- accumulate to build devastation through absence rather than description. Hemingway does not say the town was destroyed; he inventories what is gone ("thirteen saloons," "the Mansion House hotel") and lets the reader construct the loss. "The stone was chipped and split by the fire" is a single factual observation that carries the force of catastrophe because the preceding sentences have established that this stone is the only thing that survived. The emotional weight of the passage -- a returning soldier confronting annihilation -- is never stated; it exists entirely in the gap between the flat delivery and the enormity of what is described.

## 2. Landscape as Extinction Event

**Cormac McCarthy, *The Road* (2006)**

> When he woke in the woods in the dark and the cold of the night he'd reach out to touch the child sleeping beside him. Nights dark beyond darkness and the days more gray each one than what had gone before. Like the onset of some cold glaucoma dimming away the world.

**What makes this work:** Three sentences, forty-nine words, and the entire novel's premise is established: a father, a child, an annihilated world, habitual vigilance, progressive deterioration. The first sentence is a single flowing action anchored by concrete sensory nouns -- "woods," "dark," "cold," "child." The second sentence drops all articles and pronouns, compressing into a biblical fragment that enacts the linguistic impoverishment of the world it describes. "Dark beyond darkness" uses a single word against itself to convey a condition language cannot quite reach. The third sentence delivers one simile -- "cold glaucoma" -- that is simultaneously medical, tactile, and metaphysical: it names a specific disease (precision), invokes temperature (sensory), and describes the world going literally blind (thematic). McCarthy wastes nothing. The passage contains zero adverbs, one adjective used as a noun ("the dark"), and one simile that does triple duty.

## 3. Sensory Compression and Narrative Velocity

**Denis Johnson, "Car Crash While Hitchhiking" (1992), from *Jesus' Son***

> A salesman who shared his liquor and steered while sleeping... A Cherokee filled with bourbon... A VW no more than a bubble of hashish fumes, captained by a college student...
>
> And a family from Marshalltown who headonned and killed forever a man driving west out of Bethany, Missouri...
>
> I rose up sopping wet from sleeping under the pouring rain, and something less than conscious, thanks to the first three of the people I've already named -- the salesman and the Indian and the student -- all of whom had given me drugs.

**What makes this work:** Johnson compresses an entire night of hitchhiking into a catalog of three rides, each identified by a single concrete detail: shared liquor, bourbon, hashish fumes. The nouns do the characterization -- no physical descriptions, no names, no backstory. "Headonned" is a noun forced into verb duty, violent and grammatically wrong in exactly the way the collision is wrong. "Killed forever" is redundant on purpose -- it insists on the permanence in a narrative voice too altered to trust its own understanding of death. "Something less than conscious" is an exact clinical self-assessment delivered in the same flat tone as the rest. The passage moves from fragmentary catalog to complete sentence as the narrator assembles coherence, enacting the drug-hazed reconstruction of memory at the level of syntax. Every image is concrete, every verb is active, and the total word count for establishing character, setting, stakes, and narrative voice is under a hundred words.
