## Criteria Found

### Internal Consistency of World Rules
**What it measures**: Whether the fictional world follows its own established rules without contradiction throughout the narrative.
**Assumed goal**: The Tolkienian subcreation tradition where the secondary world must feel as internally consistent as the primary world, producing what Tolkien called "secondary belief" rather than mere "willing suspension of disbelief" -- stories that do not aspire to complete secondary worlds (e.g., surrealist fiction, magical realism, fabulism) may deliberately violate internal consistency for artistic effect.
**Score 0**: The world contradicts its own established rules repeatedly. Magic works one way in Chapter 3 and another in Chapter 10 with no explanation. Characters teleport across distances that previously took weeks. Physical laws, social rules, or system mechanics shift to serve plot convenience.
**Score 5**: Every established rule is followed or, when broken, the violation is itself significant and explained within the world's logic. Readers can predict how world systems will behave in new situations because the rules are coherent. The world functions as a reliable system that generates organic plot consequences.
**Source**: https://fantasy-hive.co.uk/2021/04/the-four-cs-of-fantasy-worldbuilding-worldbuilding-by-the-numbers/
**Notes**: The Four Cs framework rates "Consistent" as the C most likely to break immersion -- survey respondents ranked internal inconsistencies as "number one way to break immersion." "Audiences consider themselves experts of the real world, using it to gauge created world veracity."

### Completeness and Illusion of Depth (Iceberg Principle)
**What it measures**: Whether the world feels like it extends beyond what is shown on the page -- that it existed before the story began and contains unexplored regions, histories, and cultures.
**Assumed goal**: The Hemingway/Tolkien iceberg tradition where the writer knows 100% of the world but reveals only 10%, creating depth through implication -- minimalist fiction or fabulism may deliberately keep the world thin or dreamlike as an aesthetic choice.
**Score 0**: The world feels like a flat cardboard cutout that exists only for the immediate plot. There is no sense of history, no offhand references to places or events beyond the story, no implication that life continues outside the protagonist's view. The world begins and ends with what the story directly addresses.
**Score 5**: Casual references to events, places, and histories not central to the plot create a sense of a vast, lived-in world. The reader infers complex systems and deep history from breadcrumbs. The author clearly knows far more than is shown, and this knowledge permeates the writing with authority and specificity. Unexplored corners invite the reader's imagination.
**Source**: https://jpenberth.substack.com/p/the-professional-worldbuilding-method
**Notes**: The professional worldbuilding method describes three layers: Surface (10%) is what readers directly experience; Middle (30%) is hinted at briefly; Deep (60%) is background information informing the writing but never appearing directly. "Worldbuilding breadcrumbs" are small hints suggesting larger systems without explicit explanation. Star Wars's offhand reference to "clone wars" before they were ever depicted is a canonical example.

### Worldbuilding Delivery (Content vs. Exposition)
**What it measures**: How world information is conveyed to the reader -- whether it is integrated naturally through character experience or delivered through exposition dumps.
**Assumed goal**: The "show, don't tell" craft tradition where worldbuilding is woven into narrative action rather than presented as exposition -- epistolary fiction, omniscient narration traditions, or certain literary SF (e.g., Kim Stanley Robinson) may deliberately use extended exposition as a stylistic choice.
**Score 0**: World information arrives in large blocks of exposition, "As You Know Bob" dialogue, prologues that are history lectures, or narrator asides that halt the story. Characters explain things they already know for the reader's benefit. The world is told to the reader rather than experienced.
**Score 5**: World rules, history, and culture emerge organically through character action, sensory detail, dialogue that serves character purposes, and environmental storytelling. The reader learns about the world by watching characters live in it. Information arrives precisely when the reader needs it and in a form that serves the scene.
**Source**: https://btleditorial.com/2023/02/28/info-dumps-worldbuilding/
**Notes**: "Even if you have 18 countries you've deeply developed, if your characters aren't engaging with those countries...you probably don't need to bring them into the story." Info dumps occur when writers "dump substantial amounts of world or character information simultaneously, typically through telling rather than showing." Janet Forbes states: "Worldbuilding gets a bad rap because authors don't know how to handle it."

### Setting-Character Integration
**What it measures**: Whether setting, character, and plot are integrated into a cohesive whole rather than existing as separate layers.
**Assumed goal**: The organic worldbuilding school (K.M. Weiland, Writing Excuses) where worldbuilding must serve story through character -- picaresque or travelogue fiction may foreground setting exploration over tight character-setting integration.
**Score 0**: The world exists as decoration that has no bearing on character behavior, motivation, or development. Characters act as though they could be in any world. The setting is a backdrop painted behind the story rather than a force shaping it. World details could be swapped out without changing the plot.
**Score 5**: The world actively shapes who characters are, what they want, what obstacles they face, and how they change. Characters' behaviors, speech patterns, values, and assumptions are products of their world. The plot could not happen in any other setting. Removing the worldbuilding would destroy the character arcs because they are inseparable.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/how-to-make-your-fantasy-world-feel-real/
**Notes**: "Your choices for your fantasy world should never be arbitrary." Every setting element must support the story arc and thematic metaphor. The "bob and weave" method "recognizes the integral symbiosis of all story parts." The world's unique elements must affect every societal aspect logically and consistently. Also: "the story doesn't serve the magic system; the magic system serves the story."

### Setting as Active Force (World-as-Character)
**What it measures**: Whether the setting functions as an active narrative force with its own personality, history, and capacity to shape events -- rather than serving merely as passive scenery.
**Assumed goal**: The literary fiction sense-of-place tradition (Eudora Welty, Toni Morrison, Cormac McCarthy) where place is inseparable from meaning -- fiction set in deliberately anonymous or interchangeable spaces (Kafka, Beckett) may intentionally suppress place-as-character.
**Score 0**: The setting is inert background. It does not create obstacles, reveal character, generate mood, or change over the course of the story. Locations are functional containers (a room, a forest, a city) with no personality or narrative weight. The story could happen anywhere.
**Score 5**: The setting creates conflict, reflects character interiority, establishes and shifts mood, and transforms across the story's arc. It has a backstory, a personality, and a relationship with the characters that evolves. The setting "speaks to readers, revealing secrets and emotions and feelings in ways the living characters may not be able to share." Readers remember the setting as vividly as they remember the characters.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/make-your-setting-a-character-in-its-own-right/
**Notes**: Setting achieves character status when it possesses "narrative, memory and imagination, with history." Culture within setting context equals personality. The setting must enable, obstacle, explain, or symbolize the Inciting Event. K.M. Weiland recommends creating a composite "typical resident" profile. Also from Scribophile: stories must feel as though they "could not have happened anywhere else."

### Sensory Specificity and Immersion
**What it measures**: Whether the setting is conveyed through specific, multi-sensory detail that creates an embodied reading experience rather than abstract or purely visual description.
**Assumed goal**: The immersive realism tradition where settings should be experienced sensorily through deep POV -- deliberately abstract, impressionistic, or idea-driven fiction (Borges, Calvino) may suppress sensory detail in favor of conceptual engagement.
**Score 0**: Setting description is absent or purely visual and generic. Descriptions are abstract ("it was a beautiful city") or limited to sight alone. There is no texture, sound, smell, taste, or temperature. The reader cannot imagine being physically present in the world.
**Score 5**: The world is conveyed through all relevant senses -- sight, sound, smell, taste, touch, temperature, proprioception. Descriptions are specific and concrete rather than generic. Sensory details are filtered through character perception and reveal character as well as setting. The reader feels physically transported.
**Source**: https://www.livewritethrive.com/2022/10/31/use-sensory-detail-to-bring-setting-to-life/
**Notes**: "There's more to crafting powerful settings than just plucking out a few details and slapping them together; it's about selecting ones that create a sensory experience." While most writers default to visual description, there are "actually more than twenty specific senses humans experience." A masterful writer considers the whole range. From Writer's Digest: "Don't let your setting be just pretty scenery."

### Cultural Depth and Specificity
**What it measures**: Whether the fictional cultures feel like authentic, multi-dimensional societies with internal diversity, history, values, customs, and contradictions -- rather than monolithic stereotypes or shallow window dressing.
**Assumed goal**: The cultural anthropology-informed worldbuilding tradition (Le Guin, Jemisin, Octavia Butler) where cultures should be as complex and internally contradictory as real ones -- allegory or fable may deliberately simplify culture to a single symbolic dimension.
**Score 0**: Cultures are monolithic (the "Planet of Hats" problem) -- all members of a group think, look, and act the same. Cultural details are limited to surface markers (funny hats, unusual food) without deeper values, contradictions, or internal diversity. Or cultures are generic real-world analogs without transformation or specificity.
**Score 5**: Cultures contain internal factions, dissenters, and diversity. They have coherent worldviews that inform everything from daily customs to power structures to language patterns. Cultural practices emerge from history, geography, and belief systems in ways that feel inevitable rather than arbitrary. Individuals within cultures have their own relationships to cultural norms (compliance, rebellion, ambivalence).
**Source**: https://www.sfwa.org/2020/01/31/a-worldbuilding-guide-to-crafting-diverse-cultures/
**Notes**: Edward T. Hall's Cultural Iceberg Model distinguishes external culture (visible: dress, food, customs) from internal culture (invisible: values, beliefs, worldview). "The worldview of a culture is the axis on which a culture turns, and without an awareness of what that axis is and how it moves the culture, you might end up with a culture that feels off-kilter." N.K. Jemisin asks: "How would powerful people act in this society? How would less powerful people act?"

### Magic/Technology System Design
**What it measures**: Whether the world's magic, technology, or other speculative systems are designed with coherent rules, meaningful limitations, narrative costs, and logical consequences.
**Assumed goal**: The Sanderson hard-magic tradition where systems should have clear rules proportional to their plot importance -- soft magic traditions (Tolkien, Le Guin) deliberately keep systems mysterious and rules implicit, which is equally valid when magic is not used to resolve plot problems.
**Score 0**: Magic or technology has no rules, no limitations, and no costs. It works however the plot needs it to. Conflicts are resolved by powers that appear without foreshadowing. The speculative system is a black box that generates whatever the author needs when they need it.
**Score 5**: The system has clear rules, meaningful limitations, and narrative costs. Limitations generate conflict rather than resolving it. The system is explored in depth rather than breadth -- a few powers considered thoroughly rather than many powers used superficially. Consequences ripple logically through society, culture, and daily life.
**Source**: https://www.brandonsanderson.com/blogs/blog/guide-to-sandersons-laws-of-magic-lecture-notes
**Notes**: Sanderson's First Law: "An author's ability to solve conflict with magic is DIRECTLY PROPORTIONAL to how well the reader understands said magic." Second Law: "Limitations are more important than abilities." What makes Superman interesting? "Two things: his code of ethics and his weakness to kryptonite." Third Law: "Expand what you already have before you add something new." A brilliant system is "more often one with relatively few powers that the author has considered in depth."

### Second-Order Consequences and Ripple Effects
**What it measures**: Whether the world's speculative elements (magic, technology, altered physics, etc.) have been traced through their logical consequences on society, culture, economics, daily life, and power structures.
**Assumed goal**: The speculative sociology tradition (Le Guin, Kim Stanley Robinson, N.K. Jemisin) where a world's premise must be followed to its social and structural conclusions -- adventure fantasy or space opera may deliberately bracket societal consequences to focus on individual-scale action.
**Score 0**: Speculative elements exist in isolation. A world has teleportation but cities still look medieval. A society has telepathy but privacy norms are unchanged. Magic exists but has no economic implications. Technology is advanced but society is functionally identical to the present day ("Earth with hats").
**Score 5**: Every major speculative element has been traced through its second-, third-, and fourth-order effects. If personal teleportation exists, cities have changed form. If magic is real, institutions have formed around it. The world's premise reshapes everything it logically would: economics, politics, social norms, architecture, daily routines, language, art. The world feels like a system rather than a collection of cool ideas.
**Source**: https://literarycompass.com/a-7-step-guide-to-building-believable-sci-fi-societies/
**Notes**: "Go beyond the cool gadgets and consider the second- and third-order effects of your world's tech. A ubiquitous neural interface might eliminate language barriers, but it could also destroy the concept of privacy." From K.M. Weiland: avoid "Earth-with-hats" syndrome -- modern society with fantasy decoration. The professional worldbuilding method warns: "Unique world elements should ripple through society, shaping economics, power structures, relationships, and daily life."

### Economics and Infrastructure Believability
**What it measures**: Whether the world's economic systems, trade networks, infrastructure, and material logistics feel plausible and internally consistent.
**Assumed goal**: The materialist worldbuilding tradition (often associated with hard SF, epic fantasy, and historically-grounded fiction) where economies should follow recognizable supply-and-demand logic -- fairy tales, myth-based fantasy, or surrealist fiction may deliberately ignore economics without loss.
**Score 0**: Gold coins appear whenever convenient and obey no known rules of economics. Characters travel freely without consideration of cost, supply lines, or infrastructure. Cities exist without visible means of feeding their populations. Armies march without logistics. Trade routes, agriculture, and industry are completely absent.
**Score 5**: The economy feels organic and consequential. Trade exists because regions specialize based on geography. Currency or exchange systems reflect what the culture values. Class structures have economic roots. Infrastructure (roads, ports, communication networks) exists and affects plot logistics. Characters' economic circumstances constrain and enable their choices.
**Source**: https://authorspathway.com/crafting-your-story/world-building/how-to-create-a-believable-economy-in-your-fictional-world/
**Notes**: "The underlying substrate of any believable world is economics." Speculative fiction often employs "a deliberate vagueness when it comes to the economics of fictional universes." From Newbie to Novelist: "building grand worlds without infrastructure is like [building on] a foundation of straw." Different economic models beyond capitalism should be considered: "Gift economies, barter economies, mutual aid economies, honor economies."

### Geography and Physical World Plausibility
**What it measures**: Whether the physical world -- terrain, climate, ecology, distances, travel times -- is internally plausible and consistent.
**Assumed goal**: The naturalistic worldbuilding tradition where geography should follow recognizable physical laws unless explicitly altered by speculative elements -- dream-logic or mythic fiction may deliberately use impossible geography (rivers that flow uphill, lands that shift).
**Score 0**: Rivers flow the wrong way without explanation. Climate zones make no geographical sense. Travel times are wildly inconsistent -- journeys that take weeks in one chapter take hours in another. Ecology is impossible (a desert next to a rainforest with no transition). Maps contradict the narrative.
**Score 5**: Geography follows real-world principles or clearly explained speculative ones. Climate zones, biomes, and ecology are internally consistent. Travel times are plausible and consistent throughout. The physical world shapes culture, trade, and conflict in recognizable ways. When geography violates real-world rules, there is an in-world explanation that creates rather than destroys interest.
**Source**: https://mythcreants.com/blog/crafting-plausible-maps/
**Notes**: "Be prepared to explain the existence of any anomaly on your map." If rivers flow uphill or tundra forms in jungles, "you should think through why something is breaking natural laws." Distances matter because "time and distance affect logistics: how fast armies move, how long it takes news to spread, how isolated a village is from the capital." A healthy person walks 3-4 mph; wagons during the Oregon Trail covered 10-20 miles per day.

### Travel Time and Distance Consistency
**What it measures**: Whether distances, travel times, and spatial relationships remain consistent throughout the narrative.
**Assumed goal**: The continuity-focused epic fantasy tradition where spatial logic must hold across multi-chapter and multi-book narratives -- single-location stories or stories with deliberately warped spacetime (magical realism, dream sequences) may not need this.
**Score 0**: A journey that takes two weeks in one part of the story is completed in a day elsewhere with no explanation. Armies or messages arrive impossibly fast or slow depending on plot needs. The spatial relationships between locations change implicitly. No sense of consistent scale exists.
**Score 5**: Travel times and distances are consistent with the world's established transportation methods and geography. When characters travel, the time taken correlates with distance and mode of transport. If rapid travel exists (teleportation, fast ships), its availability and limitations are consistent. The reader can build a reliable mental map of spatial relationships.
**Source**: https://myjourneyasafantasywriter.wordpress.com/2020/11/22/worldbuilding-realistic-travel-times/
**Notes**: "If you don't consider how long it takes for the character(s) in one subplot to make their trip, then the reader may perceive time passing at different rates inconsistently." Also from Newbie to Novelist: "timelines should be consistent, realistic, and supported."

### Atmosphere, Mood, and Tone Integration
**What it measures**: Whether the setting's physical details, weather, architecture, and environment work together to create a coherent emotional atmosphere that serves the narrative.
**Assumed goal**: The Gothic and literary fiction traditions where atmosphere is a primary vehicle of meaning -- hardboiled or minimalist prose traditions may deliberately strip setting of atmospheric weight in favor of action or dialogue.
**Score 0**: Setting descriptions create no mood or create mood that clashes with the scene's emotional content. Physical details are chosen randomly rather than for atmospheric effect. The world feels emotionally neutral -- it neither supports nor contrasts the story's emotional register.
**Score 5**: Every setting detail contributes to a coherent emotional atmosphere. Weather, lighting, architecture, landscape, and environmental sounds work together to establish and shift mood throughout the narrative. Atmosphere deepens theme and reveals character interiority. The world's emotional texture is inseparable from the story's meaning.
**Source**: https://kingdompen.org/writing-tone-and-atmosphere/
**Notes**: "Tone is the author's voice, atmosphere is the stage, and mood is the feeling that fills the room." "The physical details of your setting are some of your most powerful tools when it comes to creating atmosphere." "Short, staccato sentences can make readers feel anxious, or exhilarated. Longer sentences, with more punctuation and descriptive language, can have a calming lull."

### Sense of Wonder and the Numinous
**What it measures**: Whether the world evokes genuine wonder, awe, or the feeling of encountering something truly strange and new -- rather than merely presenting a catalog of invented elements.
**Assumed goal**: The Tolkienian/mythopoeic tradition where fantasy should evoke the numinous and genuinely strange -- gritty realism or noir fantasy may deliberately suppress wonder in favor of disillusionment, which is an equally valid aesthetic.
**Score 0**: The world's speculative elements feel mundane, derivative, or exhaustively explained until all mystery is drained. There is no sense of encountering something genuinely new or strange. The world reads like a bureaucratic catalog of invented systems rather than a place of imaginative power.
**Score 5**: The world contains elements that genuinely surprise, enchant, or unsettle. Mystery coexists with logic. Not everything is fully explained, and the unexplained spaces invite wonder rather than frustration. The world feels larger and stranger than any single character's understanding of it. As David Farland states: "It's not magic that the novel needs, it is wonder!"
**Source**: https://mystorydoctor.com/creating-a-sense-of-wonder-in-fantasy/
**Notes**: Rudolf Otto's three components of the numinous: mysterium (outside ordinary life), tremendum (awe at overwhelming power), fascinans (positive attraction). "A numinous experience is a state of mind that depends on the reader as much as the author." Soft magic systems enhance wonder by leaving mechanics mysterious; hard magic systems create a different kind of wonder through systematic exploration of implications.

### Social Structures and Power Dynamics
**What it measures**: Whether the world's social hierarchies, governance systems, and power structures are coherent, consequential, and reflective of the world's other elements.
**Assumed goal**: The politically-engaged SFF tradition (Le Guin, Jemisin, Mieville) where power structures are not decoration but the substance of the world's meaning -- adventure fiction or quest narratives may legitimately simplify governance to keep focus on individual-scale action.
**Score 0**: Governance and social structures are absent, contradictory, or purely decorative. A kingdom has no visible means of enforcing its authority. Social hierarchies are stated but never affect character behavior or plot. Power structures are generic ("the king rules") with no specificity about how power actually operates.
**Score 5**: Governance, class, and social hierarchies shape every aspect of daily life, character motivation, and plot conflict. Power structures have visible mechanisms of enforcement and resistance. Social structures interact logically with economics, religion, geography, and technology. Characters at different social levels experience the same world differently.
**Source**: https://www.firstdraftpro.com/blog/how-to-develop-a-social-structure-for-your-fictional-world
**Notes**: N.K. Jemisin asks: "How would powerful people act in this society? How would less powerful people act? These things will affect the character that you ultimately decide to tell the story through." From Mythcreants: "Many manuscripts simply have no authority figures at all -- kingdoms lack armies, modern nations lack police."

### Religion and Belief Systems
**What it measures**: Whether the world's religious and belief systems feel organic, influential, and integrated into culture rather than existing as decoration or absent entirely when they should be present.
**Assumed goal**: The cultural anthropology worldbuilding tradition where religion shapes values, customs, taboos, and power structures -- secular or post-religious settings may deliberately omit religion, and that absence itself should be explained.
**Score 0**: Religion is absent from a world where it logically should exist, or it exists as pure decoration with no influence on character values, social norms, or plot. Belief systems are reduced to the existence of temples and priests without affecting how people actually think or behave.
**Score 5**: Belief systems shape character worldviews, social taboos, calendar systems, art, architecture, interpersonal relationships, and political power. Religious diversity creates tension and alliances. Characters have individual relationships with their belief systems (devout, skeptical, lapsed, converted). Religion interacts with the world's speculative elements (magic, technology) in consequential ways.
**Source**: https://mythcreants.com/blog/creating-religions-belief-systems/
**Notes**: "A religion is best when it's woven into the fabric of the story's world, influencing daily lives, shaping political landscapes, and motivating characters." Core elements include object of worship, creation myth, moral code, afterlife beliefs, rituals, and religious hierarchy. "Focus on what's relevant to your story -- you don't need to map out every ritual or name every deity."

### Language and Naming Consistency
**What it measures**: Whether names, linguistic patterns, and language use within the world feel consistent and reflective of the cultures that produced them.
**Assumed goal**: The Tolkienian linguistic worldbuilding tradition where naming conventions should reflect underlying cultural and linguistic patterns -- modernist or postmodern fiction may deliberately use anachronistic or inconsistent naming for artistic effect.
**Score 0**: Names from wildly different linguistic traditions coexist within a single culture with no explanation. Naming conventions shift randomly. Characters from the same region have names that sound like they come from completely different languages. Place names bear no phonological relationship to each other despite geographic proximity.
**Score 5**: Naming conventions within each culture share recognizable phonological patterns. Different cultures have distinct naming traditions that reflect their linguistic backgrounds. Names carry cultural information (class, region, era, profession) that readers can learn to decode. Language barriers and linguistic differences are acknowledged when they would realistically exist.
**Source**: https://ryanlanz.com/2019/11/18/worldbuilding-naming-techniques-and-philosophies/
**Notes**: "A naming language is a simple type of constructed language (conlang) that makes invented names feel more consistent and real." From A.K. Larkwood: "choose a subset of letters and sounds for your naming language and stick to those so they look consistent." From Caro C. Smith: "Consistent naming patterns -- such as name endings following patterns based on gender or social hierarchy -- creates subtle but very effective worldbuilding."

### Daily Life Texture and Material Culture
**What it measures**: Whether the world includes the texture of everyday life -- food, clothing, work, shelter, tools, customs, entertainment -- that makes it feel inhabited rather than a stage set.
**Assumed goal**: The "lived-in world" tradition (George R.R. Martin, Robin Hobb, Susanna Clarke) where mundane detail creates authenticity -- fabulist or allegorical fiction may deliberately strip away material specificity to achieve universality.
**Score 0**: Characters exist in a world without visible daily life. Nobody eats specific food, wears described clothing, does recognizable work, or engages in mundane activities. The world is a series of action set-pieces connected by blank space. There is no sense of what ordinary life looks like between dramatic events.
**Score 5**: The world has specific food, clothing, customs, entertainments, work patterns, and daily routines that arise logically from its geography, climate, technology, and culture. Material culture reflects and reinforces other worldbuilding elements (a maritime culture eats fish; a cold climate produces heavy textiles). Mundane details ground the fantastic and make the world feel inhabited by real people living real lives.
**Source**: https://www.atlasobscura.com/articles/food-in-fantasy-worldbuilding
**Notes**: "Once food is seen as something that shapes identities, lifestyles, social structures, and relationships, it becomes really important in worldbuilding." From Writer's Digest: "engage your reader at the human level with objects that are familiar and character-centered." Clothing should "make sense for the weather, the materials available, and culture." "Wear tells the truth through scuffs, pill, crease memory."

### History Informing Present
**What it measures**: Whether the world's past visibly shapes its present -- through ruins, traditions, grudges, institutions, legends, and scars on the landscape -- rather than history being absent or decorative.
**Assumed goal**: The epic fantasy and historical fiction tradition where past events create present conflicts -- contemporary or near-future fiction set in the real world may rely on actual history rather than invented history.
**Score 0**: The world has no visible past. There are no ruins, no old grudges, no institutions shaped by historical events, no cultural memories or traditions with roots in earlier eras. Characters behave as though history began with them. Or history is mentioned but never affects anything.
**Score 5**: Historical events created the present political map, cultural tensions, institutional structures, and character worldviews. History is visible in the landscape (ruins, monuments, scarred terrain), in language (idioms referring to past events), in customs (traditions whose original meaning has been half-forgotten), and in ongoing conflicts (ancient grudges, territorial disputes, religious schisms). Characters are products of their history even when they don't know it.
**Source**: https://newbietonovelist.com/2022/10/02/11-most-common-world-building-mistakes-how-to-avoid-them/
**Notes**: Having no history is "pretty much the same as creating a character without a backstory." "Cultures Never Evolve" is a major worldbuilding mistake -- "people are always learning, growing, and developing." A race that endures for millennia must experience vast changes. From K.M. Weiland: setting achieves character status when readers can see "defining epochs: critical historical events that mark community identity."

### Creativity and Originality of Conceits
**What it measures**: Whether the world's speculative departures from reality (fantasy conceits, SF innovations) are original and explored to their fullest rather than derivative or superficial.
**Assumed goal**: The New Weird and innovative SFF tradition (Mieville, Vandermeer, Leckie) where originality of premise is a primary virtue -- genre fiction operating within established subgenre conventions (cozy fantasy, military SF) may legitimately use familiar conceits while innovating within them.
**Score 0**: The world's speculative elements are direct copies of existing popular fiction (generic Tolkien elves, Star Wars-clone technology, Harry Potter-style magic school) with no transformation, subversion, or original development. The reader has seen this world many times before.
**Score 5**: The world's speculative elements are genuinely novel, or familiar elements are combined, subverted, or explored in ways that feel fresh. Conceits are explored to their fullest implications rather than used as surface decoration. The world makes the reader think "I've never seen this before" or "I never thought about that possibility."
**Source**: https://fantasy-hive.co.uk/2021/04/the-four-cs-of-fantasy-worldbuilding-worldbuilding-by-the-numbers/
**Notes**: The "Creative" C measures "how often and to what extent the constructed world deviates from the real world." "All fantasy conceits should be explored to their fullest." Avoid "nominal changes -- worldbuilding window dressing without logical foundation." From David Farland: "Research every magic system that I could find before I devised my runic magic system." Mieville advocates for "culture shock, which is about not understanding, rather than understanding."

### Worldbuilding-Story Balance
**What it measures**: Whether worldbuilding serves the story or overwhelms it -- whether there is an appropriate balance between setting detail and narrative momentum.
**Assumed goal**: The mainstream narrative fiction tradition where worldbuilding should enhance rather than replace story -- some readers and traditions (Tolkien appendices, hard SF info-dumps) genuinely value worldbuilding as an end in itself, and the balance point is genre-dependent.
**Score 0**: Either the world is so underdeveloped that the story floats in a void, or worldbuilding has swallowed the story entirely. In the latter case, pages of world detail pass without advancing plot, developing character, or creating emotional engagement. The author has "Worldbuilder's Disease" -- the world is more developed than the narrative.
**Score 5**: Worldbuilding and story are inseparable. Every piece of world information serves character development, plot advancement, or thematic deepening. The world is richly detailed but the story never stalls for a world tour. As Writer's Digest advises: "You put your novel into the world, not the world into your novel."
**Source**: https://writersinthestormblog.com/2022/11/worldbuilding-the-enemy-of-good-writing/
**Notes**: "Worldbuilding gets a bad rap because authors don't know how to handle it." Common failure: exposition that "lacks motion or emotional connection to characters." Worldbuilder's Disease: "when your world-building has effectively swallowed your story, and you're more focused on the backstory than the story itself." Also from Institute for Writers: "Chapter one isn't the place for world building, it should be centered on The Hook."

### Secondary Belief vs. Suspension of Disbelief
**What it measures**: Whether the world achieves what Tolkien called "secondary belief" -- genuine immersion where the reader believes in the world on its own terms -- rather than merely asking the reader to overlook impossibilities.
**Assumed goal**: Tolkien's subcreation tradition where the writer's goal is to make the reader genuinely believe in the secondary world rather than merely tolerate it -- metafictional or self-aware narratives may deliberately break secondary belief for ironic or critical effect.
**Score 0**: The world requires constant forgiveness from the reader. Implausible elements are not justified by the world's own logic but must be accepted because "it's just fantasy." The reader is repeatedly pulled out of the story by elements that don't feel true even within the fiction's own rules.
**Score 5**: The world commands genuine belief on its own terms. "He makes a Secondary World which your mind can enter. Inside it, what he relates is 'true': it accords with the laws of that world." When disbelief arises, "the spell is broken; the magic, or rather art, has failed." The reader forgets they are reading fiction and inhabits the world directly.
**Source**: https://onthecobblestoneroad.com/fantasy-worldbuilding/
**Notes**: Tolkien disliked the phrase "willing suspension of disbelief" -- he thought successful subcreation produces genuine belief, not mere tolerance of impossibility. "To make a Secondary World...commanding Secondary Belief will probably require labour and thought, and will certainly demand a special skill, a kind of Elvish Craft." The key is maintaining consistency so that disbelief never arises.

### Avoidance of Monocultures (Planet of Hats)
**What it measures**: Whether civilizations, races, and societies contain internal diversity rather than being defined by a single characteristic.
**Assumed goal**: The realistic diversity tradition where societies should be as internally complex as real ones -- allegory or parable may deliberately simplify cultures to single traits for symbolic purposes.
**Score 0**: Every member of a race, nation, or culture shares identical traits, values, and behaviors. "All dwarves are miners, all elves are archers, all Klingons are warriors." Entire planets or civilizations are defined by a single characteristic with no internal dissent, subcultures, or individuality.
**Score 5**: Cultures contain internal factions, dissenting voices, regional variations, class differences, generational gaps, and individual exceptions. Members of the same culture disagree about their own values and traditions. Social complexity reflects the fact that Earth's single species has "almost two hundred sovereign states with an estimated 5,000 ethnicities and 7,000 living languages."
**Source**: https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/PlanetOfHats
**Notes**: "Reducing the infinite complexity that can emerge within a group of sentient beings to a single marketable trait weakens them as a force in the narrative and reduces realism." The trope "treads dangerously close to stereotyping races of people with single negative traits." From Newbie to Novelist: "Avoid creating nations...that all think, look, and act the same."

### Technology Level Consistency
**What it measures**: Whether the world's technological capabilities are consistent across domains and logical given the world's history, resources, and speculative elements.
**Assumed goal**: The hard SF and technology-focused worldbuilding tradition where technological development should be coherent and integrated -- science fantasy (Star Wars) or soft SF may deliberately mix technology levels for aesthetic purposes.
**Score 0**: Technology is wildly inconsistent across domains with no explanation. Characters have advanced medicine but primitive transportation, or vice versa, without any speculative justification. The technology level shifts to serve plot convenience. Magic and technology coexist without any consideration of how each would affect the other.
**Score 5**: Technology is consistent across related domains, or inconsistencies are explained by world-specific factors (a resource that enables one technology but not another, cultural taboos, etc.). The interaction between magic/speculative elements and technology has been thought through. Technological capabilities and limitations constrain the plot consistently.
**Source**: https://www.firstdraftpro.com/blog/how-to-create-a-believable-technological-system
**Notes**: "A society that hasn't developed electricity yet, for instance, is not going to have MRI machines." "If magic is widespread and common, it would likely replace other technologies." Define the "overall technological stage of your world, ranging from pre-industrial societies to futuristic civilizations." Technology should logically affect "work, social interactions, and education."

### The Compelling C (Reader Engagement with World)
**What it measures**: Whether the world captures and holds reader interest through wonder, mystery, specificity, or sheer imaginative force -- independent of whether it is technically well-constructed.
**Assumed goal**: The commercial and literary fiction shared standard that readers must be engaged -- the specific mechanism of engagement (wonder, horror, intellectual curiosity, aesthetic pleasure) varies by genre and tradition.
**Score 0**: The world is technically consistent but boring. It generates no curiosity, no desire to explore, no emotional response. The reader does not care about the world and does not wish to spend more time in it. World elements are functional but uninspiring.
**Score 5**: The world commands attention and generates genuine desire to explore. The reader wants to know more about the world's history, visit its locations, understand its systems. The world creates emotional responses -- wonder, dread, longing, fascination. As the Four Cs framework notes, this is measured by "how long the audience will maintain their willing suspension of disbelief."
**Source**: https://fantasy-hive.co.uk/2021/04/the-four-cs-of-fantasy-worldbuilding-worldbuilding-by-the-numbers/
**Notes**: "Most personal and subjective of the four Cs." "Audiences remain permeable during approximately the first act. After initial goodwill expires, audiences become critical." Strong entertainment through "story twists, character reveals, and sense of wonder" can override consistency issues. This criterion interacts with all others but is not reducible to any of them.

### World Anchoring Through Familiar Elements
**What it measures**: Whether the fantastical world is grounded in enough recognizable human experience (food, family, fear, weather, bodily sensation) to remain emotionally accessible.
**Assumed goal**: The reader-accessibility tradition where fantastical worlds need footholds of familiarity -- maximally alien or estranging fiction (Lem, Wolfe, the New Weird) may deliberately withhold familiar anchors as an artistic strategy.
**Score 0**: The world is pure invention with no recognizable human touchstones. Readers cannot connect emotionally because nothing in the world relates to their experience. Or conversely, the world is so generic that it provides no specific anchor at all -- it is a featureless "fantasy world" or "sci-fi setting" without identity.
**Score 5**: Fantastical elements are grounded in universal human experiences -- the texture of food, the feeling of weather, the dynamics of family and community, the fear of the unknown. The world is strange enough to be interesting and familiar enough to be emotionally legible. As Writer's Digest advises: "Engage your reader at the human level with objects that are familiar and character-centered."
**Source**: https://www.writersdigest.com/write-better-fiction/3-dos-and-donts-of-world-building-for-writers
**Notes**: "Omitting familiar, grounded elements makes your world feel distant and unfamiliar." "Pure uniqueness without relatable anchors disconnects readers from characters." The successful secondary world needs "at least some familiarity to connect with before they can be broadened to accept fictional elements."

### Worldbuilding Through Absence and Negative Space
**What it measures**: Whether the world uses what is absent, unspoken, or implied as effectively as what is explicitly present -- whether strategic omission creates depth.
**Assumed goal**: The Le Guin/Harrison school where restraint and mystery are more powerful than completeness -- maximalist worldbuilding traditions (Tolkien appendices, hard SF encyclopedism) may value comprehensive disclosure, though even Tolkien's published fiction practices selective disclosure.
**Score 0**: Everything in the world is exhaustively explained. There are no mysteries, no gaps, no elements left to the reader's imagination. The world feels like an encyclopedia entry rather than a living place. Or alternatively, omissions are accidental rather than strategic, creating confusion rather than mystery.
**Score 5**: Strategic absences create depth and mystery. Not everything is explained, and the unexplained elements invite reader engagement rather than frustrating it. As Le Guin demonstrated: "by leaving every detail of her creation unarticulated, she successfully made the world feel bigger and more real -- more lived-in -- because real worlds can never be fully knowable." The world acknowledges its own unknowns.
**Source**: https://www.sfwa.org/2026/03/24/worldbuilding-through-absence-how-to-let-whats-missing-shape-your-story/
**Notes**: Hemingway's iceberg principle: "If a writer knows enough of what he is writing about, he may omit things that he knows and the reader...will have a feeling of those things as strongly as though the writer had stated them." Mieville argues for "culture shock, which is about not understanding, rather than understanding." Harrison praises M. John Harrison's refusal to exhaustively explain all worldbuilding elements.

### Worldconjuring vs. Worldbuilding (Artistic Integration)
**What it measures**: Whether the world serves as art -- evoking emotion, meaning, and aesthetic experience -- rather than functioning merely as a rationalized encyclopedia of invented facts.
**Assumed goal**: The Harrison/Michel literary worldbuilding critique where worlds should serve poetic, emotional, and philosophical purposes -- the rationalist Sanderson/hard-SF tradition values systematic completeness as its own form of aesthetic pleasure, and neither approach is inherently superior.
**Score 0**: The world is a dry catalog of invented facts with no emotional, thematic, or aesthetic dimension. It answers every question about how the world works but never makes the reader feel anything about it. The world is technically complete but artistically empty.
**Score 5**: The world evokes emotion, embodies theme, and creates aesthetic experiences that cannot be separated from its factual construction. It functions as what Lincoln Michel calls "worldconjuring" -- using hints and literary technique where readers fill gaps collaboratively. As M. John Harrison argued, the best speculative fiction represents "the triumph of writing over worldbuilding." The world is both believable and beautiful.
**Source**: https://electricliterature.com/against-worldbuilding/
**Notes**: Harrison argues worldbuilding "actually undercuts the best and most exciting aspects of fantastic fiction, subordinating the uncontrolled, the intuitive & the authentically imaginative to the explicable." Temple counters that worldbuilding can encompass "complexity, nuance, a tactile sense of place, internal logic, a story that fulfills its own promise." Michel argues worldbuilding expectations "narrow the paths available to writers and to readers."

### Environmental Change and Dynamism
**What it measures**: Whether the world changes over the course of the story -- through weather, seasons, consequences of plot events, ecological shifts, or political changes -- rather than remaining a static backdrop.
**Assumed goal**: The dynamic world tradition where settings should be affected by and respond to narrative events -- bottle episodes or single-setting stories may legitimately maintain a static environment as part of their aesthetic.
**Score 0**: The world is unchanging regardless of what happens in the story. Battles leave no scars. Seasons don't change. Political upheavals don't alter daily life. The world is a painted backdrop that remains the same from beginning to end.
**Score 5**: The world responds to narrative events and changes over time. Weather shifts, seasons turn, landscapes are scarred by conflict, political changes alter daily life, and characters return to find familiar places transformed. "Changing weather can help settings that must be revisited feel different and real."
**Source**: https://authorspathway.com/crafting-your-story/world-building/how-to-make-fictional-worlds-feel-lived-in/
**Notes**: "Consider how the environment changes over the course of the story." "A story starting in the gloom of winter and ending in a refreshing spring garden feels quite different from a story starting in the sweltering heat of summer that descends into the rot and decay of autumn." From K.M. Weiland: "The setting itself transforms between Act One (Normal World) and Act Two (Adventure World)."

### Perspective-Filtered Worldbuilding
**What it measures**: Whether world information is filtered through specific character perspectives -- revealing character psychology through what they notice, ignore, value, or misunderstand about their world -- rather than delivered from an omniscient camera.
**Assumed goal**: The deep POV tradition where worldbuilding should be mediated by character consciousness -- omniscient narration traditions (Tolkien, Pratchett) may legitimately present world information from outside any character's perspective.
**Score 0**: World description comes from a neutral, omniscient camera that notices everything with equal weight. Characters' unique perspectives do not filter what is described. A peasant farmer and a merchant prince notice the same things about the same city in the same way.
**Score 5**: Each character notices, values, and interprets the world differently based on their background, training, class, and personality. A soldier notices defensible positions; a merchant notices trade goods; a child notices hiding places. Worldbuilding reveals character as much as it reveals setting. What characters miss or misunderstand about their own world is as revealing as what they understand.
**Source**: https://btleditorial.com/2023/02/28/info-dumps-worldbuilding/
**Notes**: "By filtering the world through character perspective, you naturally prioritize what matters to them (and therefore what matters to the story)." From Writer's Digest: "Allow your reader to experience the world through your character's POV." From the AI tells rubric: authentic worldbuilding requires "bounded knowledge" -- characters don't know everything about their own world.

## Sentinels Found

### Generic Pseudo-Medieval European Setting
**Detection**: The world contains castles, knights, taverns, kings, and vaguely feudal social structures with no specific cultural, geographic, or historical identity beyond "medieval Europe."
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs are trained on vast amounts of fantasy fiction dominated by Tolkien-derived medieval European settings. When generating fantasy without specific constraints, they default to the statistically most common fantasy setting: generic medieval Europe with unexamined conventions.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates the possibility of culturally specific, geographically grounded, or historically textured settings. Reduces worldbuilding to a set of interchangeable stock props rather than a distinctive place.
**Source**: https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/MedievalEuropeanFantasy

### Monoculture Races/Species (Planet of Hats)
**Detection**: Every member of a non-human race or foreign culture shares identical personality traits, values, and behaviors (all dwarves are gruff miners, all elves are wise and ancient, all orcs are violent).
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs compress cultural complexity into statistical archetypes. When generating races or cultures, they reproduce the most common associations from training data rather than creating internally diverse societies. Real societies contain dissenters, subcultures, and individual variation that LLMs rarely generate unprompted.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates internal cultural complexity, reduces narrative potential for within-culture conflict, and can reproduce racial stereotyping through fantasy coding.
**Source**: https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/PlanetOfHats

### Magic System Without Limitations or Costs
**Detection**: Magic can do anything the plot requires with no established rules, costs, or consequences. Characters solve problems with magic that appears to have no constraints.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate plot-convenient solutions without tracking systemic constraints across a narrative. Without a human author's deliberate system design, magic becomes an arbitrary problem-solving device. LLMs lack the persistent state needed to enforce limitations they haven't been explicitly reminded of.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates tension, makes conflict resolution arbitrary, and prevents the reader from predicting or understanding how the world works. Violates Sanderson's First Law.
**Source**: https://www.brandonsanderson.com/blogs/blog/guide-to-sandersons-laws-of-magic-lecture-notes

### Info-Dump Opening / Encyclopedic Prologue
**Detection**: The first chapter or prologue consists primarily of world exposition -- history, geography, political structure, magic system rules -- before any character action or conflict begins.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs trained on worldbuilding guides and fantasy wikis may front-load world information because that is how reference material is structured. They reproduce the structure of their training data (encyclopedia entries, worldbuilding documents) rather than the structure of effective fiction (character-first, world-through-action).
**Design space cost**: Kills reader engagement before the story begins. Creates the false impression that the world is more important than the characters. Institute for Writers: "Chapter one isn't the place for world building, it should be centered on The Hook."
**Source**: https://www.instituteforwriters.com/nine-worldbuilding-mistakes-to-avoid/

### "As You Know, Bob" Dialogue
**Detection**: Characters explain to each other things they both already know, clearly for the reader's benefit. E.g., "As you know, our kingdom has been at war with the Northlands for thirty years."
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs need to convey world information and default to the most direct method: having characters state it in dialogue. They lack the craft awareness to recognize that characters would not explain shared knowledge to each other. This is information delivery disguised as dialogue.
**Design space cost**: Breaks character voice, destroys verisimilitude, and signals that the world exists for the reader rather than for the characters. Readers recognize this as artificial.
**Source**: https://btleditorial.com/2023/02/28/info-dumps-worldbuilding/

### Uniform Confidence in World Description
**Detection**: Every piece of world information is presented with identical certainty. There are no mysteries, no "nobody knows why," no contested histories, no unreliable cultural narratives. The world is presented as completely known.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate text with uniform confidence because they predict the most likely next token. They do not naturally generate epistemic uncertainty, contested knowledge, or acknowledged gaps in understanding. Real worlds are full of things that "nobody really knows" or "scholars disagree about."
**Design space cost**: Eliminates mystery, reduces the world's depth, and makes it feel like an encyclopedia rather than a place where knowledge is partial, contested, and socially constructed.
**Source**: https://gist.github.com/lmmx/d91de290ea4e6d9631e32c2dd43da413

### Symmetric Load-Balancing of World Elements
**Detection**: Every aspect of worldbuilding (magic, geography, politics, religion, economy) receives exactly equal narrative space regardless of its importance to the story. Three paragraphs on magic, three on geography, three on politics -- artificial uniformity.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs tend to distribute attention evenly across topics because they are trained on structured content (listicles, wikis, worldbuilding guides) where balanced coverage is the norm. Human authors weight worldbuilding detail by narrative relevance -- some aspects get pages, others get a sentence.
**Design space cost**: Buries story-critical world information in equal-weight noise. Prevents the reader from understanding which aspects of the world matter most. Creates the impression of a world built from a checklist rather than from narrative necessity.
**Source**: https://gist.github.com/lmmx/d91de290ea4e6d9631e32c2dd43da413

### View-From-Nowhere Setting Description
**Detection**: Setting is described from a disembodied, omniscient perspective with no character viewpoint filtering the information. The narration reads like a guidebook or documentary voiceover rather than someone's lived experience of a place.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs default to an institutional, encyclopedia-like voice when describing settings. They describe places as collections of facts rather than as experienced through a particular consciousness. The "AI tells" rubric identifies this as "View from Nowhere" -- narrative lacking a situated narrator with stake in events.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates the characterization that comes from perspective-filtered description. Prevents the world from revealing character. Creates emotional distance between reader and setting.
**Source**: https://gist.github.com/lmmx/d91de290ea4e6d9631e32c2dd43da413

### Speculative Elements Without Second-Order Effects
**Detection**: The world has magic, advanced technology, or other speculative elements, but society, daily life, economics, and culture are identical to a non-speculative world. Magic exists but hasn't changed anything.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate speculative elements as surface features without tracing their systemic implications. They add magic or technology as a new layer without modifying the existing layers because they lack the causal reasoning needed to propagate consequences through a system. This produces "Earth-with-hats."
**Design space cost**: Destroys believability for any reader who asks "but what would actually happen if...?" Wastes the most interesting aspect of speculative fiction -- the exploration of how different premises create different worlds.
**Source**: https://jpenberth.substack.com/p/the-professional-worldbuilding-method

### Inconsistent Travel Times / Rubber-Band Geography
**Detection**: Characters travel between the same two points in wildly different amounts of time depending on plot needs. A journey that took two weeks earlier is completed in a day later, or vice versa, with no explanation.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs lack persistent spatial models. They generate travel descriptions based on narrative convenience rather than maintaining a consistent spatial relationship between locations. Each scene is generated in relative isolation without checking against previously established distances.
**Design space cost**: Destroys the reader's mental map of the world. Makes military strategy, communication, trade, and logistics impossible to reason about. Signals that the world is not a coherent space but a collection of disconnected stage sets.
**Source**: https://myjourneyasafantasywriter.wordpress.com/2020/11/22/worldbuilding-realistic-travel-times/

### Contradictory World Rules Across Scenes
**Detection**: A rule established in one scene (magic requires rare ingredients; technology has specific limitations; social customs dictate behavior) is violated in a later scene without acknowledgment or explanation.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs have limited context windows and no persistent world state. Rules established early in a narrative may fall outside the model's attention span by the time later scenes are generated. Without explicit tracking, constraints established in one passage are forgotten or contradicted in another.
**Design space cost**: Immediately breaks secondary belief. "Nothing pulls a reader out of a story faster than a world that doesn't follow its own rules." Once the reader catches one contradiction, they begin hunting for others, destroying immersion.
**Source**: https://www.writribe.com/post/internal-consistency-avoiding-contradictions-in-storytelling

### Missing Authority Structures
**Detection**: A world presented as having organized society (kingdoms, nations, federations) but no visible mechanisms of authority, enforcement, or governance. No armies, police, bureaucracy, courts, or institutions.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate the surface markers of organized society (kings, councils, governments) without generating the infrastructure that makes governance function. They reproduce the nouns of political organization without the verbs of political operation.
**Design space cost**: Makes the world's social structures feel like window dressing. Prevents political intrigue, law enforcement, military strategy, or any plot that depends on institutional power. From Mythcreants: "Many manuscripts simply have no authority figures at all."
**Source**: https://mythcreants.com/blog/five-common-worldbuilding-mistakes-in-new-manuscripts/

### Adverb-Inflated Setting Description
**Detection**: Setting descriptions rely heavily on intensifying adverbs ("truly ancient," "fundamentally transformed," "deeply mystical," "incredibly vast") without concrete sensory detail to justify the intensification.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs substitute intensity markers for specificity. When they cannot generate specific, concrete sensory detail, they compensate with adverbs that assert impressiveness without demonstrating it. This is the textual equivalent of telling rather than showing.
**Design space cost**: Produces setting descriptions that feel hollow and unconvincing. The reader is told the world is impressive but not shown why. Specific sensory detail (the smell of iron in the air, the sound of wind through stone arches) is replaced by generic assertions of grandeur.
**Source**: https://gist.github.com/lmmx/d91de290ea4e6d9631e32c2dd43da413

### Throat-Clearing World Introduction
**Detection**: The narrative opens with a general statement about the world before getting to anything specific. "In a world where magic is real..." or "In the kingdom of [Name], there existed..."
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs reproduce the most common opening structures from their training data. "In a world where..." is among the most statistically frequent openings for fantasy and sci-fi content. The model generates the most probable opening tokens rather than a distinctive, character-grounded entry point.
**Design space cost**: Wastes the most valuable real estate in the narrative (the opening lines) on generic orientation rather than hook, character, or conflict. Signals to experienced readers that the author (or AI) is defaulting to formula.
**Source**: https://gist.github.com/lmmx/d91de290ea4e6d9631e32c2dd43da413

### No Knowledge Gaps in Characters About Their Own World
**Detection**: Characters demonstrate perfect, encyclopedic knowledge of their world's magic, politics, history, and geography. No character is confused, uncertain, or wrong about how anything works.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate characters who share the model's own omniscient perspective on the generated world. Because the LLM constructed the world, it does not naturally generate characters who have partial or incorrect knowledge of it. Real inhabitants of real worlds are constantly uncertain, misinformed, or ignorant about aspects of their own world.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates discovery, learning, misunderstanding, and the dramatic irony that comes from characters being wrong about their own world. Also eliminates the reader's experience of learning alongside the character.
**Source**: https://gist.github.com/lmmx/d91de290ea4e6d9631e32c2dd43da413

### Visually-Dominant Description Without Other Senses
**Detection**: All setting description is visual. There are no sounds, smells, tastes, temperatures, textures, or bodily sensations. The world reads like a painting rather than a place you could stand in.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs are trained primarily on text that describes visual appearances (the most commonly described sense in prose). They significantly undergenerate non-visual sensory information because visual description is statistically dominant in training data. The model defaults to what has been described most often.
**Design space cost**: Produces thin, unconvincing settings that fail to immerse the reader. Multi-sensory description is one of the primary mechanisms for creating the felt experience of being in a place. Vision alone cannot create embodied immersion.
**Source**: https://www.livewritethrive.com/2022/10/31/use-sensory-detail-to-bring-setting-to-life/

### World Exists Only Where the Plot Needs It
**Detection**: Locations, institutions, and world elements appear only when the plot requires them and are never referenced before or after. The world has no existence beyond the protagonist's immediate needs.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate world elements on demand as the plot requires them, without establishing them in advance or maintaining them afterward. They lack the forward-planning and backward-referencing that creates a world that exists independently of the current scene. Each scene's setting is generated in isolation.
**Design space cost**: Destroys the illusion that the world exists independently of the story. The reader senses that locations and institutions are being invented on the fly rather than being part of a pre-existing world. This is the opposite of the iceberg principle -- there is nothing beneath the waterline.
**Source**: https://jpenberth.substack.com/p/the-professional-worldbuilding-method

### Cliche Reaching in Setting Description
**Detection**: Setting descriptions rely on stock phrases: "sits at the intersection of," "the heart of the kingdom," "a land of contrasts," "ancient and mysterious," "war-torn landscape." These are borrowed phrasings that don't reflect character voice or world specificity.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate the most statistically common phrases associated with setting description. These stock phrases appear frequently in travel writing, fantasy wikis, and marketing copy, making them high-probability outputs. They are generic by definition -- they could describe any setting.
**Design space cost**: Prevents the setting from having a distinctive voice or identity. Generic phrases create generic settings. Specific, unusual phrasing is what makes a setting memorable and distinct from every other fantasy kingdom.
**Source**: https://gist.github.com/lmmx/d91de290ea4e6d9631e32c2dd43da413

### Cultures as Aesthetic Skins Over Default Society
**Detection**: Different "cultures" in the world differ only in surface aesthetics (clothing, food, architecture) while sharing identical values, social structures, family patterns, power dynamics, and ways of thinking. They are the same society wearing different costumes.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs can generate surface-level cultural markers (clothes, food, building styles) but struggle to generate genuinely different value systems, epistemologies, or social logics. Cultural variation requires modeling how different foundational assumptions produce different social structures -- a form of causal reasoning that LLMs perform weakly.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates genuine cultural encounter, cross-cultural conflict, and the reader's experience of encountering a truly different way of being human. Reduces "cultural diversity" to a costume change.
**Source**: https://www.sfwa.org/2020/01/31/a-worldbuilding-guide-to-crafting-diverse-cultures/

### Perfect Internal Consistency With No Acknowledged Contradictions
**Detection**: The world is presented as perfectly self-consistent with no mysteries, paradoxes, or unresolved questions. Every system is fully explained. No character acknowledges uncertainty about how the world works.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate consistent answers because they are trained to be helpful and complete. They do not naturally generate acknowledged contradictions, mysteries that characters puzzle over, or aspects of the world that "nobody really understands." Real worlds are full of unresolved questions and contested explanations.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates the intellectual engagement that comes from a world with genuine mysteries. A world where everything is explained is a world where there is nothing to discover. Also removes the realistic texture of partial knowledge and contested understanding.
**Source**: https://gist.github.com/lmmx/d91de290ea4e6d9631e32c2dd43da413

### Economy Operates on Convenient Vagueness
**Detection**: Characters always have enough money (or none, for drama) without any visible economic system. Gold appears when needed. Prices are never mentioned or are wildly inconsistent. No one has a visible source of income.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs do not track economic state persistently. They generate wealth or poverty as the scene requires without maintaining a coherent economic model. Money appears as a plot device rather than as part of a functioning system.
**Design space cost**: Removes a major source of realistic constraint and motivation. Economic circumstances should limit what characters can do, explain why they make certain choices, and create class-based conflicts. Vague economics produces characters who exist outside material reality.
**Source**: https://mythcreants.com/blog/four-fictional-economies-that-dont-make-sense/

### Food/Clothing/Material Culture Absent or Generic
**Detection**: Characters eat "food," wear "clothes," and live in "houses" with no specificity. Or all food is "roasted meat and ale," all clothing is "tunics and cloaks," regardless of the world's climate, culture, or geography.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs default to the most common fantasy food/clothing associations from training data (medieval European defaults: stew, bread, ale, tunics, cloaks). They do not derive material culture from the world's geography, climate, and available resources because that requires causal reasoning the model doesn't perform reliably.
**Design space cost**: Removes one of the most effective grounding mechanisms for worldbuilding. Specific material culture (what people eat for breakfast, what fabric their clothes are made from, what their tools look like) is what makes a world feel lived-in rather than abstract.
**Source**: https://www.atlasobscura.com/articles/food-in-fantasy-worldbuilding
