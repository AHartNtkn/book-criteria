## Criteria Found

### Conceptual Originality (Premise Freshness)
**What it measures**: Whether the story's central premise, situation, or "what if" offers something the reader hasn't encountered in that exact form before, or combines familiar elements in a genuinely surprising way.
**Assumed goal**: Literary and commercial fiction traditions that value novelty of premise as a hook for reader engagement — genre fiction communities (romance, cozy mystery, LitRPG) may explicitly value familiar premises executed with variation, where conceptual familiarity is a feature rather than a bug, and the reader's pleasure comes from seeing how convention is navigated rather than whether convention is present.
**Score 0**: The premise is indistinguishable from dozens of existing works — "chosen one discovers powers," "detective solves murder of wealthy patriarch," "couple meets-cute through misunderstanding" — with no twist, recontextualization, or combinatorial surprise that differentiates it. A reader could predict the entire arc from the first paragraph.
**Score 5**: The premise makes the reader think "I've never seen this before" or "I've never seen these elements combined this way." Even if individual components are familiar, their combination, recontextualization, or the specific "what if" produces genuine novelty. The concept alone generates curiosity about execution.
**Source**: [The Novelry - High Concept Fiction](https://www.thenovelry.com/blog/high-concept-fiction), [Writer's Digest - 7 Qualities of High-Concept Stories](https://www.writersdigest.com/write-better-fiction/write-better-the-7-qualities-of-high-concept-stories), [Writers In The Storm - Originality in Fiction](https://writersinthestormblog.com/2018/10/originality-in-fiction/)
**Notes**: The Writers In The Storm article by Kathryn Craft argues that originality flows from "a stew of our own trademark mix of interests and desires, then filtered through our worldview." She demonstrates with doll houses appearing in three different contemporary works (The Miniaturist, Hereditary, Sharp Objects) each using the motif in "completely original ways" despite identical objects. The Writer's Digest high-concept article emphasizes that high-concept ideas are often posed with a "what if" scenario. The Novelry distinguishes between originality (approach and fresh perspective) and uniqueness (being one-of-a-kind and incomparable).

### Originality of Execution
**What it measures**: Whether the story finds fresh, non-default ways to handle scenes, transitions, character introductions, dialogue, revelations, and other narrative mechanics — distinct from whether the premise itself is original.
**Assumed goal**: Craft-focused fiction traditions (MFA literary fiction, "writer's writer" traditions) that hold execution originality as the primary measure of artistry — commercial genre fiction may prioritize efficient, reader-serving execution of familiar beats, where originality of execution can actively interfere with reader expectations (e.g., a romance reader wants the meet-cute to be satisfying, not experimentally deconstructed).
**Score 0**: Every scene plays out in the most default, expected way. Character introductions use stock patterns (mirror description, "Let me tell you about myself" internal monologue). Revelations are delivered through exposition dumps. Transitions follow the most obvious sequence. A writing workshop instructor would call it "competent but unsurprising."
**Score 5**: Scenes consistently surprise in how they unfold while remaining coherent. Character introductions reveal personality through unexpected means (action, dialogue, what they notice). Revelations emerge through dramatic situations rather than being stated. Transitions create unexpected juxtapositions. The reader repeatedly encounters moments where the execution itself is the pleasure.
**Source**: [Writing Is Hard Work - Originality vs Execution](https://writingishardwork.wordpress.com/2015/02/18/originality-vs-execution-which-is-more-important/), [Helping Writers Become Authors - The Single Best Trick for Originality](https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/originality-in-your-fiction/), [Fiction University - Execution or Idea](http://blog.janicehardy.com/2011/02/execution-or-idea-which-comes-first.html)
**Notes**: Roger Colby argues "the way it is told, the way the action is described" creates uniqueness, not plot originality, and that "in the current age, execution of prose is much more important than being original." K.M. Weiland's "single best trick" is asking "What would be unexpected?" at every scene juncture, noting the importance of questioning your first automatic choice. She cites Grosse Pointe Blank as a film where the protagonist telling people the truth about being an assassin, visiting a psychiatrist, and his former love interest kissing him immediately all subvert expectations while remaining coherent.

### Defamiliarization (Making the Familiar Strange)
**What it measures**: Whether the writing causes the reader to perceive familiar things, situations, or experiences in a new way — the degree to which the prose fights "automatized" perception and re-vivifies ordinary experience.
**Assumed goal**: Literary fiction tradition rooted in Russian Formalism (Shklovsky, 1917) where the purpose of art is to counteract "over-automatization" of perception — genre fiction traditions that emphasize immersive transparency and invisible prose (e.g., commercial thriller, where the prose should not call attention to itself) may consider defamiliarization a disruption of the reader's dream rather than an enhancement of it.
**Score 0**: The prose describes familiar things in familiar ways. A sunset is beautiful, a city is bustling, grief is heavy. The reader's perception is never challenged or renewed. Objects and experiences pass through the prose unchanged, as automatic as they are in daily life. The writing confirms existing perception rather than transforming it.
**Score 5**: The prose consistently causes the reader to see familiar things as if for the first time. Descriptions of ordinary objects, emotions, or situations produce the sensation of strangeness and renewed perception. The reader's habitual way of understanding the world is disrupted and enriched by the writing. Techniques may include unusual perspective (Tolstoy's horse narrator in "Kholstomer"), unexpected sensory channels (describing a sunrise through touch and sound rather than sight), cross-sensory description, or language that makes forms "difficult" and perception "lengthy."
**Source**: [Defamiliarization - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Defamiliarization), [Gilliam Writers Group - Defamiliarization: The Art of Making Strange](https://www.gilliamwritersgroup.com/blog/defamiliarization-the-art-of-making-strange), [Literariness.org - Defamiliarization](https://literariness.org/2016/03/17/defamiliarization/)
**Notes**: Shklovsky's original 1917 argument: "The purpose of art is to impart the sensation of things as they are perceived and not as they are known." He argued that habitual perception, what he called "automatization," deadens the senses, causing objects and experiences to become overly familiar and lose their sensory impact. The Wikipedia article notes that poetic language is deliberately difficult, contrasting with prose as "ordinary speech — economical, easy, proper." The Gilliam Writers Group article gives specific techniques: describing familiar scenes using unexpected sensory channels, cross-sensory blending ("the salty look of the wall"), magnified focus on mundane details, non-linear storytelling, and genre blending. Anais Nin: "It is the function of art to renew our perception."

### Specificity of Detail
**What it measures**: Whether the fiction deploys concrete, particular, non-interchangeable details rather than generic, category-level descriptions — whether the details could belong only to this story and these characters, or could be swapped into any story without loss.
**Assumed goal**: Realist and literary fiction traditions following John Gardner's principle that verisimilitude is "the backbone of good writing" and John Hodgman's dictum that "Specificity is the soul of narrative" — minimalist traditions (e.g., Carver, early Hemingway) may deliberately withhold specific detail to create ambiguity and reader projection, and speculative fiction may blur specificity when the world itself is meant to feel dreamlike or abstract.
**Score 0**: Descriptions use category-level language: "a tree," "a house," "a beautiful woman," "a delicious meal." Characters notice generic things. Settings could be any city, any forest, any room. Physical details, when present, are drawn from the most common stock images (rain on a windowpane, crackling fire, cobblestone streets). A reader could not reconstruct any unique visual from the prose.
**Score 5**: Details are precise, concrete, and non-interchangeable: a specific species of tree with a specific quality of light through its specific leaves, a meal described through particular tastes and textures that reveal character and culture. Physical descriptions include the kind of detail that signals observed reality — asymmetries, imperfections, idiosyncrasies. James Wood's "thisness": details that "draw abstraction towards itself and seem to kill that abstraction with a puff of palpability."
**Source**: [Kristyn J. Miller - The Power of Specificity in Fiction](https://kristynjmiller.com/2021/05/22/the-power-of-specificity-in-fiction/), [Blue Pencil Agency - Why Specificity is so Important](https://bluepencilagency.com/why-specificity-is-so-important/), [Fiction Writers Review - How Fiction Works Discussion](https://fictionwritersreview.com/review/how-fiction-works-discussion-review-2/), [World Builders - A Swim in a Pond in the Rain](https://www.worldbuilders.ai/p/swim-pond-rain)
**Notes**: John Hodgman: "Specificity is the soul of narrative." James Wood coins "thisness" for the quality of detail that "draws abstraction towards itself and seems to kill that abstraction with a puff of palpability" — examples include a baby's arms so fat they seem tied up with string (Anna Karenina) and shoes full of a speared man's blood (Heart of Darkness). George Saunders argues that "the more specificity you add, the more interesting your story becomes, and the more interesting your story, the more humane its characters." He describes "ritual banality avoidance" — denying yourself the banal version by removing a character in hopes a better story comes along. Saunders: "Good writerly habit might consist of continually revising towards specificity, so that specificity can then produce plot."

### Freshness of Figurative Language
**What it measures**: Whether metaphors, similes, and other figures of speech are original and vivid rather than drawn from the common stock of "dying metaphors" — whether the figurative language produces genuine perceptual surprise or merely confirms automatic associations.
**Assumed goal**: Fiction traditions that value prose style as a vehicle for perception (literary fiction, prose poetry, "writer's writer" traditions) following Orwell's rule to "never use a metaphor, simile, or other figure of speech which you are used to seeing in print" — genre fiction with transparent prose conventions (thriller, commercial romance) may deliberately use familiar figurative language because unfamiliar metaphors slow reading pace and break immersion.
**Score 0**: The prose relies entirely on dead metaphors and stock figures: hearts pound, stomachs churn, eyes are windows to the soul, silence is deafening, darkness is oppressive. Comparisons are so familiar they produce no perceptual effect. Metaphors are mixed without awareness (a sign the writer "is not interested in what he is saying," per Orwell). The figurative language could be generated by autocomplete.
**Score 5**: Figurative language is fresh, precise, and produces genuine surprise while remaining apt. Metaphors create new perceptual connections rather than confirming existing ones. Comparisons reveal something about both the tenor and vehicle that the reader hadn't seen before. There are zero dead metaphors, and figures of speech create the sensation of seeing something for the first time.
**Source**: [George Orwell - Politics and the English Language](https://www.orwellfoundation.com/the-orwell-foundation/orwell/essays-and-other-works/politics-and-the-english-language/), [Writer's Digest - 10 Tips to Bypass Cliche and Melodrama](https://www.writersdigest.com/whats-new/10-tips-to-bypass-cliche-and-melodrama), [Countercraft - MFA vs. LLM](https://countercraft.substack.com/p/mfa-vs-llm-is-openais-metafiction)
**Notes**: Orwell describes "a huge dump of worn-out metaphors which have lost all evocative power and are merely used because they save people the trouble of inventing phrases for themselves." His examples include "ring the changes on, toe the line, ride roughshod over, stand shoulder to shoulder with, Achilles' heel, swan song, hotbed." He argues that incompatible metaphors mixed together are "a sure sign that the writer is not interested in what he is saying." The Countercraft analysis of OpenAI fiction identifies "Womb of Oblivion" lines — "incredibly easy to write because, well, they don't mean anything. You can swap around the words at will" — and five-metaphor pile-ups mixing construction, tailoring, and magic imagery that confuse rather than illuminate.

### Distinctive Voice
**What it measures**: Whether the prose has a recognizable, individual quality that could not have been written by a different author or by a statistical average of all authors — whether reading a random paragraph would allow identification of the author or at minimum would not be attributable to "anyone."
**Assumed goal**: Fiction traditions that value authorial voice as a primary marker of literary achievement — transparent-prose traditions (commercial thriller, hard SF exposition) may deliberately suppress distinctive voice in favor of invisible narration that does not call attention to itself, treating voice distinctiveness as self-indulgent distraction from story.
**Score 0**: The prose sounds like it was written by no one in particular. Sentence structures follow default patterns. Word choices are drawn from the most statistically common options. The rhythm is even and unremarkable. There is no sense of a particular human consciousness shaping the language. The writing could be attributed to any competent but undistinguished author — or to a language model producing "the average voice of its training data."
**Score 5**: The prose has a voice so distinctive it functions "as distinctive as fingerprints." There is a cadence, rhythm, selection of words and phrases, set of obsessions, and way of seeing that belongs to one consciousness. The voice creates "sympathetic vibrations in readers" and gives "a sense of connection to another live human presence." Reading a random paragraph produces the sensation of encountering a specific mind at work.
**Source**: [Nathan Bransford - How to craft a great voice](https://nathanbransford.com/blog/2010/05/how-to-craft-great-voice), [Eva Langston - What is "Voice"?](https://evalangston.com/2017/11/05/writing-voice/), [Future Problem Solving - How is Creative Writing evaluated?](https://resources.futureproblemsolving.org/article/how-evaluated-creative-writing/), [Medium / Writers Notes - Idiosyncratic Writing](https://medium.com/writers-notes/idiosyncratic-writing-can-it-be-done-successfully-fb201fa8b704)
**Notes**: Nathan Bransford defines voice as "what writing feels like" and argues it creates a "real and complex moment of communication." The Future Problem Solving evaluation criteria describe strong style/voice as including "clear, distinctive personal touch, use of literary techniques that makes text lively and engaging, unique stylistic nuances and elements." The idiosyncratic writing article notes that "you know that an author has a specific writing style when you can identify the author merely by reading a random paragraph." Francine Prose argues there are no universal rules for creating character or voice, and that "whatever we may think we know about the best way to create a character, literature shows us that it differs from writer to writer, sometimes from book to book."

### Prose Density (Layered Meaning)
**What it measures**: Whether each sentence and paragraph is doing multiple things simultaneously — characterization, world-building, narrative momentum, thematic resonance, foreshadowing — or whether prose is "thin" (single-purpose sentences conveying only information).
**Assumed goal**: Literary fiction traditions following William Sloane's principle that density means "the optimum number of things going on at once, some of them overtly, others by implication" — fast-paced genre fiction (thriller, action) may deliberately thin prose for velocity, where multi-layered sentences slow the reader when speed is the aesthetic priority.
**Score 0**: Each sentence does exactly one thing: conveys a fact, describes a setting, states an emotion, or advances the plot. Prose is purely functional. No sentence simultaneously characterizes while advancing plot while establishing mood while foreshadowing. Reading produces no resonance, no sense of layers, no backward or forward connections.
**Score 5**: Every sentence does at least two things, and the best do three or more. A character's observation simultaneously reveals their psychology, establishes the world, creates mood, and foreshadows a later development. The prose connects backward (to the reader's accumulated memory of the text) and forward (as "invisible prophecy of what is coming"). Reading produces the sensation of depth and richness — "the illusion of reality" created by density matching the actual world, which is "absolutely filled with stuff."
**Source**: [Theodora Goss - Writing with Density](https://theodoragoss.com/2016/05/07/writing-with-density/), [Gail Gaymer Martin - Texture In Writing](http://writingright-martin.blogspot.com/2008/04/texture-in-writing-what-is-it.html), [World Builders - A Swim in a Pond in the Rain](https://www.worldbuilders.ai/p/swim-pond-rain)
**Notes**: William Sloane (cited by Theodora Goss): density means "richness, substance — it is the core of knowing your materials." The test: "every sentence in your story should be doing at least two things, three is better." Thin writing relies on single-purpose sentences conveying only information. Dense writing has backward and forward reference: prose connects to what's been read while foreshadowing what's coming. Saunders' "bouncer test": each line must justify its presence, answering "What are you doing here?" Martin defines texture as "layering and deepening the color of the story, using vivid language, strong emotion, dynamic characters and plot lines that build one conflict or point on another."

### Non-Default Narrative Choices
**What it measures**: Whether the writer makes deliberate, surprising choices at each decision point in the narrative (structure, POV, scene selection, character actions, resolutions) rather than defaulting to the most statistically common or expected option.
**Assumed goal**: Fiction traditions that reward "second-order thinking" — choosing the unexpected option after considering and rejecting the obvious one — applicable across both literary and genre fiction, though the inventory of "default" choices differs by genre. A romance novel's defaults differ from a literary novel's defaults, but both can be evaluated for whether the writer transcends their genre's autopilot.
**Score 0**: Every structural choice, character action, and plot resolution follows the most predictable path. If a writing workshop instructor could guess the next scene from the current one, the story is on autopilot. POV is third-person limited past tense without justification. Conflict resolves through the most obvious mechanism. Characters make the decision the reader expects. The story never deviates from the center of its genre's probability distribution.
**Score 5**: The story consistently surprises at the level of craft choices. K.M. Weiland's test: the writer has examined and rejected the first automatic instinct at every juncture. POV, tense, structure, scene selection, and character decisions all show evidence of deliberate choice rather than default. The narrative is "a perfectly valid, rational story told in parallel to a consistently surprising one." Choices feel purposeful rather than arbitrary — surprise with coherence.
**Source**: [Helping Writers Become Authors - The Single Best Trick for Originality](https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/originality-in-your-fiction/), [Writer's Digest - 5 Ways to Surprise Your Reader](https://www.writersdigest.com/write-better-fiction/5-ways-to-surprise-your-reader-without-it-feeling-like-a-trick), [LitReactor - 10 Stories We Never Need to See in Workshops Again](https://litreactor.com/columns/10-stories-we-never-need-to-see-in-workshops-again)
**Notes**: K.M. Weiland defines originality as "the unexpected — something new, something readers haven't already experienced" and argues for applying this at "every single scene" and "every juncture." Writer's Digest distinguishes surprise from manipulation: "Readers love to be surprised, but they're not fans of being tricked or manipulated." The LitReactor workshop piece catalogs ten story types that are exhausted from workshop overuse, including "white kid goes to Europe," "everyone has cancer," "existential crisis," and "the dead baby" — all representing default choices that hundreds of writers have already made. The lechmazur writing_styles research documents LLM default patterns: past tense dominates universally (~100%), linear chronology (82-99%), single-POV (93-100%), reliable narration near-universal, high register default, "near-uniformly earnest" stance (~99.8%), and positive endings for every model.

### Trope Awareness and Subversion
**What it measures**: Whether the writer demonstrates awareness of the conventions, tropes, and reader expectations of their genre or mode, and either deploys them with fresh execution or deliberately subverts them — as opposed to deploying tropes unconsciously or without variation.
**Assumed goal**: Genre fiction traditions where trope awareness is central to the writer-reader contract — genre readers want "the same, but different" and evaluate writers on how skillfully they navigate convention. Literary fiction traditions may evaluate by a different standard where trope awareness matters less than originality of perception, and some experimental fiction deliberately ignores genre conventions entirely.
**Score 0**: Tropes are deployed unconsciously — the writer appears unaware they are using a stock character (the gruff mentor with a heart of gold), a stock plot (the prophecy that comes true), or a stock scene (the training montage). There is no variation, subversion, or fresh perspective on the convention. The tropes feel inherited rather than chosen.
**Score 5**: The writer demonstrates clear awareness of genre conventions and either executes them with fresh specificity (the trope is recognizable but feels new because of concrete, non-interchangeable detail) or subverts them deliberately (the setup of the trope creates an expectation that is paid off with something unexpected). Subversion has Weiland's "two mandatory segments: first, the expectation is set up that something familiar is coming, then that set-up is paid off with something else entirely."
**Source**: [Writer's Digest - Using Story Tropes to Subvert Reader Expectations](https://www.writersdigest.com/write-better-fiction/using-story-tropes-to-subvert-reader-expectations), [DIY MFA - Understanding Tropes and Genre Conventions](https://diymfa.com/writing/understanding-tropes-and-genre-conventions/), [Kenneth W. Myers - Blending and Subverting Familiar Tropes](https://myersfiction.com/2024/07/16/blending-and-subverting-familiar-tropes-in-new-ways/), [Atmosphere Press - Writing Tropes: Subversion & Breaking the Mold](https://atmospherepress.com/subverting-writing-tropes/)
**Notes**: The DIY MFA article articulates the "same but different" reader expectation: genre readers "don't want an exact repeat of something they've read before, but they want something that follows similar conventions and gives those conventions a new spin." Kenneth W. Myers identifies three subversion techniques: Inversion and Reversal (flipping expectations by reversing roles or outcomes), Deconstruction and Realism (analyzing tropes realistically), and Genre Hybridization and Context Shifting (using tropes in new settings). The Writer's Digest article notes: "subversion works best when you first establish the trope, then break the reader's expectation, so the payoff feels deliberate and satisfying."

### Moral and Psychological Complexity
**What it measures**: Whether characters face genuinely ambiguous moral situations and exhibit psychological depth — whether the fiction allows for complexity, contradiction, and uncertainty rather than reducing characters and situations to simple moral categories.
**Assumed goal**: Literary fiction tradition and morally ambitious genre fiction (noir, literary thriller, complex fantasy) where human complexity is the subject — children's fiction, fables, heroic fantasy, and moral tales may legitimately operate with simpler moral frameworks where clear good/evil distinctions serve the narrative's purpose and audience.
**Score 0**: Characters are flatly good or evil. Moral situations have obvious correct answers. The protagonist is infallibly right, the antagonist infallibly wrong. There is no ambiguity, no competing legitimate values, no moment where the reader is genuinely uncertain about who is right. The fiction operates as moral instruction rather than moral exploration.
**Score 5**: Characters face situations where competing values produce genuine uncertainty. The protagonist has real flaws that matter to the plot, not cosmetic "likeable flaws." The antagonist has comprehensible, perhaps even sympathetic, motivations. StoryScope's finding applies: human stories "frame protagonist choices as more morally ambiguous" compared to AI stories. Readers are invited to bring their own ethical framework rather than being told what to think.
**Source**: [book-editing.com - Not So Black and White: Moral Ambiguity in Character Development](https://www.book-editing.com/morality-character-development/), [StoryScope - arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.03136), [npmartin.com - Shades of Gray: The Allure of Morally Ambiguous Characters](https://npmartin.com/morally-gray-characters/)
**Notes**: The book-editing.com article argues: "Moral absolutes — being totally pure, or totally evil — make for predictable, cardboard-cutout, one-dimensional characters." StoryScope research found that AI stories over-explain themes and favor tidy, single-track plots while human stories frame protagonist choices as more morally ambiguous with increased temporal complexity. The research demonstrates this is one of the most reliable narrative-level features distinguishing AI from human fiction (93.2% macro-F1 detection using narrative features alone).

### Novelistic Discovery
**What it measures**: Whether the fiction reveals something about human experience that the reader did not already know — whether it functions as an act of exploration and discovery rather than merely confirming existing understanding.
**Assumed goal**: The Kunderian literary fiction tradition where the novel's purpose is to discover "some unknown aspect of existence" and the "imperative of originality applied to every detail" — entertainment-focused genre fiction may not aspire to revelatory discovery, treating fiction primarily as pleasure delivery rather than knowledge generation.
**Score 0**: The fiction tells the reader nothing they didn't already know about human experience. Emotions are described in expected terms. Character insights are platitudes. The story confirms received wisdom without examining it. A reader finishes the work with exactly the same understanding of human nature they had before starting.
**Score 5**: The fiction functions as an instrument of discovery. It reveals something about human experience — a psychological dynamic, a moral complexity, an emotional truth — that the reader recognizes as true but had not previously articulated. Milan Kundera's standard: "the novelist is an explorer feeling his way in an effort to reveal some unknown aspect of existence." The reader finishes the work with an expanded understanding of what it means to be human.
**Source**: [Milan Kundera - The Art of the Novel (various)](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/28637.The_Art_of_the_Novel), [Spectrum Culture - A Swim in a Pond in the Rain review](https://spectrumculture.com/2021/04/11/a-swim-in-a-pond-in-the-rain-by-george-saunders-review/), [Ezra Pound - Make It New](https://imagejournal.org/article/making-it-new/)
**Notes**: Kundera defines the novel as "the great prose form in which an author thoroughly explores, by means of experimental selves (characters), some great themes of existence." He argues that "the novel DISCOVERS something new" and that "the imperative of originality applied to every detail" is essential. Saunders emphasizes that "knowledge breeds compassion" — increasing specification of character creates both originality and empathy. Pound's "Make it new" embodies the modernist imperative: art that merely restates the already-known fails its function.

### Emotional Authenticity vs. Sentimentality
**What it measures**: Whether the fiction's emotional content arises organically from specific situations, characters, and details, or whether it deploys generic emotional buttons (illness, death, children in peril) as manufactured shortcuts to feeling.
**Assumed goal**: Fiction traditions that value earned emotion — the Writer's Digest distinction between genuine drama and melodrama — applicable across literary and genre fiction, though the threshold for "earned" varies. Literary fiction tends to demand more groundwork before emotional payoff; genre fiction may tolerate faster emotional setups if they serve pacing.
**Score 0**: Emotional effects are manufactured through sensational subject matter deployed without specificity: terminal illness used for guaranteed sympathy, dead children used for automatic grief, abuse narratives used for instant moral clarity. Characters display emotions through stock physical reactions (tears streaming, fists clenching, hearts pounding). The emotional manipulation is visible — the writer is reaching for the reader's feelings through the shortest path rather than the truest one.
**Score 5**: Emotional effects are earned through specific characters in specific situations, arrived at through the accumulation of concrete detail. The Writer's Digest "authenticity test" is passed: the material was "come by honestly, through personal experience or genuine imagination." Emotions arise from the reader's investment in characters built through specificity, not from inherently dramatic subject matter. Even moments of high emotion feel restrained, precise, and particular rather than histrionic.
**Source**: [Writer's Digest - 10 Tips to Bypass Cliche and Melodrama](https://www.writersdigest.com/whats-new/10-tips-to-bypass-cliche-and-melodrama), [Writers Helping Writers - How to Differentiate Stories from AI Slop](https://writershelpingwriters.net/2025/09/how-to-differentiate-stories-from-ai-slop/), [Good Story Company - Avoid Cliches](https://www.goodstorycompany.com/blog/avoid-cliches)
**Notes**: The Writer's Digest article argues that sensational subject matter (murder, abuse, mental illness) carries "built-in audience expectations" and that "convenience in plot, characters, and coincidences almost always spells doom." It identifies melodrama's hallmarks: "protagonists as obvious heroes/victims; antagonists as clear villains; over-the-top emotional displays (seizures, screams, floods of tears); gratuitous physical violence; violence becoming the story itself rather than serving it." The antidote: "elevate the ordinary — move hackneyed elements to background; foreground earthier dramas of ordinary existence." Writers Helping Writers identifies AI fiction's emotional authenticity problem: "AI can't dig into what situations feel like and what they mean to our characters."

### Cliche Avoidance at Sentence Level
**What it measures**: Whether the prose at the phrase and sentence level avoids stock constructions, tired imagery, and formulaic transitions — whether the writer has taken Orwell's rule seriously that "the concrete melts into the abstract" when topics are raised in default language.
**Assumed goal**: Prose-focused fiction traditions from Flaubert's war against "received ideas" through Orwell's "never use a metaphor you are used to seeing in print" to contemporary MFA workshop norms — genre fiction with rapid-reading conventions may accept more stock phrasing because the prose is meant to be transparent and forgettable, functioning as delivery mechanism for plot rather than as aesthetic object.
**Score 0**: Nearly every sentence contains at least one stock phrase, dead metaphor, or formulaic construction: "her heart raced," "he let out a breath he didn't know he was holding," "the silence was deafening," "a wave of emotion washed over her." Transitions use "Meanwhile," "Furthermore," or "In that moment." The prose reads as if assembled from a finite inventory of pre-existing phrases.
**Score 5**: The prose is free of dead metaphors and stock constructions. Every phrase shows evidence of having been examined and found adequate by this specific writer for this specific moment. Even functional sentences avoid autopilot. Orwell's test is passed: no metaphor, simile, or figure of speech "which you are used to seeing in print." Flaubert's test is passed: no "received idea" — no automatic thought or platitude — survives in the final prose.
**Source**: [George Orwell - Politics and the English Language](https://www.orwellfoundation.com/the-orwell-foundation/orwell/essays-and-other-works/politics-and-the-english-language/), [Flaubert - Dictionary of Received Ideas (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dictionary_of_Received_Ideas), [Good Story Company - Avoid Cliches](https://www.goodstorycompany.com/blog/avoid-cliches), [Francine Prose - Reading Like a Writer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reading_Like_a_Writer)
**Notes**: Orwell identifies "staleness of imagery" and "lack of precision" as the two main problems plaguing modern prose. He lists "a huge dump of worn-out metaphors which have lost all evocative power." Flaubert's Dictionary of Received Ideas catalogs the "automatic thoughts and platitudes, self-contradictory and insipid" that populate bourgeois conversation — his entire literary project was a war against this automatism. Francine Prose argues that gestures performed by fictional characters should not be "physical cliches" but "illuminations that move the narrative." The Good Story Company identifies specific fiction cliches: characters waking up at story opening, staring in mirrors, going to sleep at chapter end, "a girl who is quiet and loves to read books."

### Cliche Avoidance at Story Level
**What it measures**: Whether the story avoids exhausted premises, character types, and plot patterns that have been done to the point of creative depletion — whether the story-level choices show awareness of what has already been written many times before.
**Assumed goal**: Workshop and editorial fiction traditions where slush-pile readers have seen the same story types hundreds of times — genre fiction with strong convention expectations operates by a different standard where familiar story-level elements are expected and evaluated on execution quality, not novelty.
**Score 0**: The story deploys one or more exhausted story types without any fresh element: the "chosen one" prophecy, the love triangle resolved by one party conveniently dying, the twist where "it was all a dream," the redemption through sacrifice of the mentor figure, the detective who is also a drunk with a dark past. The LitReactor "10 stories we never need to see in workshops again" list: white kid goes to Europe, everyone has cancer, existential crisis without stakes, the dead baby, abusive relationship redemption, romanticized history without nuance.
**Score 5**: The story either avoids exhausted territory entirely or engages with familiar elements in ways that justify their presence — fresh combination, genuine subversion, or execution so specific that the familiar story type is transformed by its particular details. The editor's "what else is there?" question is answered: even if the basic shape is recognizable, the specific content makes the story feel necessary rather than repetitive.
**Source**: [LitReactor - 10 Stories We Never Need to See in Workshops Again](https://litreactor.com/columns/10-stories-we-never-need-to-see-in-workshops-again), [Writer's Digest - 10 Tips to Bypass Cliche and Melodrama](https://www.writersdigest.com/whats-new/10-tips-to-bypass-cliche-and-melodrama), [SparkPress - Formulaic Books: Faulty or Fabulous?](https://gosparkpress.com/formulaic-books-faulty-or-fabulous/)
**Notes**: The LitReactor article identifies ten story types exhausted by workshop overuse, each representing a "deepest basin in the corpus landscape" — the story a beginning writer gravitates toward when they don't yet have enough material to tell something specific and personal. The Writer's Digest article argues that "sensational settings (mental hospitals, abortion clinics) carry built-in audience expectations" and that meeting those expectations head-on results in cliche. The SparkPress article notes the publishing industry paradox: "editors say they want something different, but what they mean is they want the safe and same with a minuscule twist."

### Vivid and Continuous Dream
**What it measures**: Whether the prose creates and sustains what John Gardner called "a vivid and continuous fictional dream" — an immersive experience where the reader is regularly presented with "proofs, in the form of closely observed details, that what is said to be happening is really happening."
**Assumed goal**: The Gardner/realist fiction tradition where verisimilitude through concrete detail is the inner strategy of all major genres — metafictional traditions (Barth, Borges, Calvino) that deliberately break the fictional dream for intellectual effect, and experimental traditions that prioritize language over immersion, operate by different standards.
**Score 0**: The prose does not create any sense of immersive experience. Descriptions are abstract or absent. The reader is told what happens but never sees, hears, smells, or feels it. Gardner's "proofs" are missing — there are no closely observed details to make the fiction feel real. The reading experience is purely intellectual, with no sensory engagement.
**Score 5**: The prose creates a sustained sensory experience. Gardner's two principles are met: the writing is vivid (the reader can see, hear, smell, taste, and feel the fictional world) and continuous (nothing bumps the reader out of the dream through clumsy writing, logical inconsistency, or inappropriate authorial intrusion). The reader has the experience of "being there."
**Source**: [Barry Donaldson - The Vivid and Continuous Dream](https://medium.com/the-craft-of-fiction/the-vivid-and-continuous-dream-ffefb617fbb4), [John Gardner - The Art of Fiction (various reviews)](https://www.kirkusreviews.com/book-reviews/john-gardner/the-art-of-fiction/)
**Notes**: Gardner argues that "if writing is to be any good, it must create a vivid and continuous fictional dream." The reader is "regularly presented with proofs — in the form of closely observed details — that what is said to be happening is really happening." Good description is the writer's means of "reaching down into his subconscious mind, finding clues to what questions his fiction must ask, and, with luck, hints about the answers." The two guiding principles: vividness (describing things so the reader can see them in their mind's eye) and continuity (not introducing interruptions that bump the reader out of the dream).

### Creative Problem-Solving Within the Story
**What it measures**: Whether characters solve problems, overcome obstacles, and navigate conflicts through creative, non-obvious means that arise from their specific abilities, knowledge, and circumstances — or whether solutions are generic, convenient, or imported from outside the story's established logic.
**Assumed goal**: Fiction traditions that value character agency and earned resolutions (mainstream literary and genre fiction) — some literary fiction may deliberately feature characters who fail to solve problems or who are overwhelmed by circumstances, where the lack of creative resolution is the point (naturalism, absurdism).
**Score 0**: Problems are solved through the most convenient mechanism: a previously unmentioned skill appears, information arrives by coincidence, the antagonist makes an inexplicable mistake, or a deus ex machina intervenes. The solutions demonstrate no creativity and could be predicted by any reader familiar with the genre. Characters' specific knowledge, personality, and established capabilities play no role in the resolution.
**Score 5**: Problems are solved in ways that feel simultaneously surprising and inevitable. Solutions arise from the intersection of specific character traits, knowledge, and circumstances that have been established earlier in the narrative. The reader thinks "I didn't see that coming, but of course — that's exactly what this character would do given what they know and who they are." The creative problem-solving within the story mirrors the creative problem-solving of the author.
**Source**: [Fiction University - Decisions, Decisions: Creating Character Choices That Matter](http://blog.janicehardy.com/2011/02/decisions-decisions-character-choices.html), [Medium / Tom Farr - How to Create and Solve Problems to Write Better Stories](https://medium.com/@farrtom/creating-and-solving-problems-to-write-great-stories-be10efccaa75)
**Notes**: Fiction University emphasizes that "the protagonist is the engine that drives the plot forward — they act on purpose because they want something." Readers want characters to "overcome their problems and solve them in a satisfying way through their own resolve and intelligence," emphasizing that character agency is central to compelling narrative choices. The Tom Farr article argues that writers should show the character's thought process as they work through problems and brainstorm multiple solutions.

## Sentinels Found

### "A Tapestry of" / "A Testament to" / "A Dance of" Construction
**Detection**: The text contains phrases matching the pattern "a [abstract noun] of [abstract noun]" using the specific vocabulary cluster: tapestry, testament, dance, symphony, kaleidoscope, mosaic, crucible, beacon, whirlwind, or similar ornamental abstract-to-abstract constructions that add no concrete meaning.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs over-index on these phrases because they are statistically common in training data, particularly in book reviews, dust jackets, and promotional copy. The 2025 Helsinki study and multiple AI-detection resources identify "tapestry," "testament," and similar words as appearing far more frequently in post-2022 AI-generated text than in pre-2022 human text. These phrases are semantically empty — "a tapestry of emotions" communicates nothing a specific description wouldn't communicate better.
**Design space cost**: Flagging this pattern could penalize a writer who deliberately uses one of these constructions ironically, in dialogue reflecting a character's pomposity, or in a single instance as legitimate figurative language. The cost is low because any skilled writer would use these constructions sparingly if at all.
**Source**: [Wikipedia - Signs of AI writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Signs_of_AI_writing), [Hunting the Muse - How to tell if writing is AI](https://huntingthemuse.net/library/how-to-tell-if-writing-is-ai), [Reuters Institute - How AI-generated prose diverges from human writing](https://reutersinstitute.politics.ox.ac.uk/news/how-ai-generated-prose-diverges-human-writing-and-why-it-matters)

### Exposed Subtext (Telling the Theme)
**Detection**: Characters or narration explicitly state the thematic meaning of events, emotions, or relationships that should be conveyed through action, dialogue, and detail. Examples: "She realized that love was about acceptance, not perfection," "In that moment, he understood the true meaning of sacrifice," "The garden was a metaphor for their relationship — growing, but requiring constant tending."
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Alexander Wales identified "exposed subtext" as a primary LLM failure mode: the model "plops the subtext into the text, telling instead of showing." When given outlines containing character motivations or thematic goals, LLMs embed these as literal text rather than dramatizing them. The StoryScope research confirms: AI stories "over-explain themes." This occurs because LLMs optimize for clarity of meaning delivery rather than for the indirect communication that creates literary depth.
**Design space cost**: Penalizes fiction that deliberately makes theme explicit (fables, didactic fiction, some YA fiction that uses direct moral statement as a genre convention). Also penalizes some postmodern fiction that exposes its own mechanics as a technique.
**Source**: [Alexander Wales - Adventures in AI Text Generation pt 2](https://alexanderwales.com/adventures-in-ai-text-generation-pt-2-of/), [StoryScope - arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.03136), [Writers Helping Writers - How to Differentiate Stories from AI Slop](https://writershelpingwriters.net/2025/09/how-to-differentiate-stories-from-ai-slop/)

### Tidy Resolution / Positive Ending Default
**Detection**: The story resolves all conflicts neatly, characters achieve understanding or reconciliation, and the ending is unambiguously positive with no residual tension, ambiguity, or cost. All threads are "wrapped up with a bow."
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: The lechmazur writing_styles research documents that positive endings dominate for every LLM model tested, with near-uniform earnest stance (~99.8%). The StoryScope research found that AI stories "favor tidy, single-track plots" and systematically avoid narrative tension, "sanitising real-world conflicts" and "downplaying narrative tension in favour of nostalgia and reconciliation." Human fiction exhibits significantly more ending-valence diversity and ambiguity.
**Design space cost**: Penalizes genre fiction where a tidy positive resolution is a genre requirement (romance's HEA — Happily Ever After — is definitional, not optional). Also penalizes children's fiction and certain commercial fiction forms where resolution is part of the reader contract. Strongest as a sentinel in literary fiction contexts.
**Source**: [GitHub - lechmazur/writing_styles](https://github.com/lechmazur/writing_styles), [StoryScope - arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.03136), [LessWrong - Ok, AI Can Write Pretty Good Fiction Now](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/b6sTwHF3hmheFvvpu/ok-ai-can-write-pretty-good-fiction-now)

### AI Vocabulary Clustering
**Detection**: High density of words from the established AI-vocabulary list within a short span: "delve," "tapestry," "testament," "pivotal," "crucial," "vibrant," "nestled," "embark," "landscape," "foster," "underscore," "nuanced," "meticulous," "intricate," "enduring," "bolstered," "garner," "showcase," "interplay," "enhance." Multiple words from this list co-occurring in close proximity.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: The Wikipedia "Signs of AI writing" article documents that these words "started appearing far more frequently in text produced after 2022, when LLM chatbots became widely accessible" and that they "often co-occur in LLM output: where there is one, there are likely others." The 2025 Helsinki study found surge in these specific words. Individual instances are not diagnostic (human writers use these words too), but clustering of multiple words from the list is highly indicative.
**Design space cost**: False positives for writers with naturally ornate or academic prose styles. Individual words from the list are perfectly legitimate in isolation. The sentinel should only fire on clustering (3+ words from the list in a short span), not on individual instances.
**Source**: [Wikipedia - Signs of AI writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Signs_of_AI_writing), [Hunting the Muse - How to tell if writing is AI](https://huntingthemuse.net/library/how-to-tell-if-writing-is-ai), [Blake Stockton - Don't Write Like AI: Red Flag Words](https://www.blakestockton.com/red-flag-words/), [Grammarly - Common Words and Phrases in AI-Generated Content](https://www.grammarly.com/blog/ai/common-ai-words/)

### Purple Prose / Ornamental Metaphor Overload
**Detection**: Consecutive sentences each contain a different metaphor, simile, or ornamental comparison. Metaphors are mixed (incompatible vehicles in close proximity). Descriptive language is consistently elevated above what the scene requires. Words are chosen for sonic impressiveness rather than precision ("utilize" for "use," "commence" for "begin").
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: The LAMP corpus study identified "purple prose" as one of seven major categories of AI writing problems. The Countercraft MFA vs. LLM analysis found that AI fiction's "Womb of Oblivion" lines are "incredibly easy to write because, well, they don't mean anything — you can swap around the words at will." Alexander Wales identified purple prose as a primary LLM failure mode: the model tends toward "overwrought metaphors and florid descriptions used excessively." EQ-Bench explicitly penalizes "excessive vocabulary flourishes that don't contribute to writing quality" and "relentlessly poetic prose that borders on incoherence."
**Design space cost**: Penalizes deliberately ornate prose styles (baroque fiction, magical realism with heightened language, prose poetry). Some writers (Nabokov, Faulkner, McCarthy) deploy dense figurative language as a deliberate aesthetic choice. The sentinel should focus on incoherent or mixed metaphors and metaphors that add no information, not on density of figurative language per se.
**Source**: [arXiv - Can AI writing be salvaged?](https://arxiv.org/html/2409.14509v3), [Countercraft - MFA vs. LLM](https://countercraft.substack.com/p/mfa-vs-llm-is-openais-metafiction), [Alexander Wales - Adventures in AI Text Generation pt 2](https://alexanderwales.com/adventures-in-ai-text-generation-pt-2-of/), [EQ-Bench Creative Writing](https://eqbench.com/creative_writing.html)

### "A Sense of [Abstract Noun]" Construction
**Detection**: The phrase "a sense of" followed by an abstract noun (unease, wonder, foreboding, loss, belonging, dread, hope, longing) used as a primary emotional descriptor, especially when repeated.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: The LAMP corpus study found that "a sense of" appeared disproportionately in LLM outputs compared to human seed paragraphs, with "unspoken" appearing in roughly 15% of LLM responses. This construction is a distancing mechanism — it tells the reader about an emotional atmosphere rather than dramatizing it through specific detail. It is the emotional equivalent of "telling not showing."
**Design space cost**: "A sense of" is legitimate English prose and appears in published human fiction. The sentinel should flag repeated use or use as the primary emotional register, not isolated instances. A single "a sense of unease hung over the proceedings" is fine; five such constructions in a chapter signals autopilot.
**Source**: [arXiv - Can AI writing be salvaged?](https://arxiv.org/html/2409.14509v3), [Alexander Wales - Adventures in AI Text Generation pt 2](https://alexanderwales.com/adventures-in-ai-text-generation-pt-2-of/)

### Uniform Narrative Stance (Earnest, Single-POV, Past Tense, Reliable, High Register)
**Detection**: The story uses the default LLM configuration: third-person limited, past tense, single-POV, reliable narrator, high register, earnest tone, with no variation, experiment, or justification for these choices. All five defaults are present simultaneously.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: The lechmazur writing_styles analysis of 400 stories per LLM documents these as near-universal defaults: past tense (~100%), linear chronology (82-99%), single-POV (93-100%), reliable narration (near-universal), high register (default), "near-uniformly earnest" stance (~99.8%). Human fiction exhibits dramatically more variation across all these dimensions. The simultaneous presence of all five defaults, with no deviation, is the equivalent of the prose choosing the most statistically probable option at every structural decision point.
**Design space cost**: Many perfectly good human-written stories use exactly this configuration — it is the most common configuration for a reason. The sentinel should only fire when combined with other indicators. It is a necessary-but-not-sufficient condition, indicating the absence of deliberate structural choice.
**Source**: [GitHub - lechmazur/writing_styles](https://github.com/lechmazur/writing_styles)

### Flat Event Escalation
**Detection**: Story events progress at a uniform intensity level without meaningful escalation, de-escalation, or variation in stakes. Each scene operates at roughly the same emotional temperature. There is no narrative staircase — no sense that later events build meaningfully on earlier ones.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: StoryScope identified "flat event escalation" as a model-specific fingerprint (particularly for Claude). Saunders argues that stories must function as "escalation" systems where "each narrative moment should represent meaningful change, creating a staircase effect where readers move to progressively new positions." LLMs struggle with long-range narrative arc management because they optimize locally (each paragraph sounds good) rather than globally (each paragraph advances the whole).
**Design space cost**: Some literary fiction deliberately operates at a uniform emotional register (Chekhov, Carver). Slice-of-life fiction may resist escalation as a narrative principle. The sentinel is strongest for fiction that appears to intend a dramatic arc but fails to execute one.
**Source**: [StoryScope - arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.03136), [World Builders - A Swim in a Pond in the Rain](https://www.worldbuilders.ai/p/swim-pond-rain)

### Stock Emotional Physicality
**Detection**: Characters express emotions exclusively through a fixed inventory of stock physical reactions: hearts pounding, stomachs churning, breaths catching, fists clenching, jaws tightening, tears streaming, hands trembling, eyes widening, blood running cold, shivers running down spines.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: These are the most statistically common physical-emotional associations in fiction training data. LLMs default to them because they are the highest-probability completions for emotional scenes. Human writers who are paying attention to their craft find character-specific, situation-specific physical expressions of emotion — a character who expresses anxiety by reorganizing their desk, or grief by forgetting to eat. The stock reactions convey generic emotion; specific reactions convey character.
**Design space cost**: Individual instances of stock physicality appear in excellent fiction. The sentinel should flag systematic reliance — a pattern where emotions are always expressed through the same small inventory of physical cliches, with no character-specific variation.
**Source**: [Good Story Company - Avoid Cliches](https://www.goodstorycompany.com/blog/avoid-cliches), [Francine Prose - Reading Like a Writer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reading_Like_a_Writer), [arXiv - Can AI writing be salvaged?](https://arxiv.org/html/2409.14509v3)

### "The Weight of [Abstract Noun]" / "The Air Was Thick With [Abstract Noun]"
**Detection**: Phrases using "the weight of" or "the air was thick with" followed by an abstract emotional or thematic noun (silence, grief, expectation, tension, history, memory, unspoken words).
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: The LAMP corpus study specifically identified "weight of" as a syntactic template appearing disproportionately in LLM outputs. "The air was thick" was similarly identified by Alexander Wales. These constructions attempt to create atmosphere through formula rather than through specific sensory detail. They are the literary equivalent of using a filter rather than composing the photograph.
**Design space cost**: Both constructions appear occasionally in published human fiction. The sentinel should flag repeated use or use as the primary atmospheric technique, not isolated instances.
**Source**: [arXiv - Can AI writing be salvaged?](https://arxiv.org/html/2409.14509v3), [Alexander Wales - Adventures in AI Text Generation pt 2](https://alexanderwales.com/adventures-in-ai-text-generation-pt-2-of/)

### Narrative Clustering (All Stories Feel the Same)
**Detection**: Across multiple outputs or chapters, the narrative choices (structure, conflict type, resolution pattern, character dynamics, thematic emphasis) fall within a narrow range — the fiction exhibits low diversity across its own instances. Different stories by the same process feel interchangeable in their narrative DNA.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: StoryScope's central finding: "AI-generated stories cluster in a shared region of narrative space, while human-authored stories exhibit greater diversity." The lechmazur writing_styles project quantified within-model diversity and found the spread is modest (0.047 range across all models tested). Human fiction exhibits dramatically more structural, tonal, and stylistic diversity. When multiple stories from the same source feel interchangeable at the narrative level despite different surface content, this signals statistical averaging rather than individual creative decision-making.
**Design space cost**: An author with a strong, consistent style (Hemingway, Murakami) may produce stories that cluster in narrative space while being individually excellent. The sentinel is strongest when evaluating a body of work for evidence of process rather than evaluating a single piece.
**Source**: [StoryScope - arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.03136), [GitHub - lechmazur/writing_styles](https://github.com/lechmazur/writing_styles), [Nature - Stylometric comparisons of human versus AI-generated creative writing](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05986-3)

### Imposing Conventional Interpretive Frameworks
**Detection**: The narrative imposes standard interpretive frameworks on ambiguous situations — assuming heteronormative relationships where the text doesn't specify, imposing "redemption arcs" where the author intended ambiguity, or reading conventional moral lessons into situations designed to resist them.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: The "Reading Subtext" research found that when summarizing human fiction, all tested LLMs "impose conventional interpretive frameworks" — one summary "assumed a heteronormative reading of a relationship that the text did not support" and another "imposed a 'redemption arc' narrative where the writer intended ambiguity." This occurs because LLMs pattern-match to the most common narrative templates in training data, defaulting to conventional readings of ambiguous situations. In fiction generation (not just summarization), this manifests as stories that collapse moral ambiguity into conventional moral lessons.
**Design space cost**: Some fiction deliberately deploys conventional frameworks (fairy tales, moral fables, inspirational fiction). The sentinel is strongest for fiction that appears to intend complexity but resolves into convention.
**Source**: [arXiv - Reading Subtext: Evaluating LLMs on Short Story Summarization with Writers](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.01061), [Resultsense - When AI can't read between the lines](https://www.resultsense.com/insights/2026-04-01-llm-narrative-summarisation-subtext-writer-evaluation)

### Rule-of-Three Default
**Detection**: Description, exposition, or emotional content is consistently delivered in three-item lists: "adjective, adjective, adjective" or "short phrase, short phrase, and short phrase." The triadic structure appears in nearly every descriptive passage.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: The Wikipedia "Signs of AI writing" article identifies "Rule of Three" as a structural tell: "adjective, adjective, adjective" patterns and "short phrase, short phrase, and short phrase" used to "make superficial analysis appear comprehensive." Hunting the Muse identifies "Rule of three applied unnecessarily" as one of six key AI writing tells. Human prose uses varied list lengths (two items, four items, single items expanded) and varies rhythm; LLM prose defaults to three because it is the most statistically common rhetorical list length in training data.
**Design space cost**: The rule of three is a legitimate and ancient rhetorical device. Individual instances are not diagnostic. The sentinel should flag systematic, unreflective deployment — when nearly every description or emotional beat falls into triadic structure.
**Source**: [Wikipedia - Signs of AI writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Signs_of_AI_writing), [Hunting the Muse - How to tell if writing is AI](https://huntingthemuse.net/library/how-to-tell-if-writing-is-ai)

### Elegant Variation / Synonym Chains
**Detection**: The text systematically avoids repeating key terms by substituting synonyms, creating unnatural chains: a character is referred to as "the detective," "the investigator," "the sleuth," "the keen-eyed officer" within a short passage. A specific noun is never used twice, replaced each time with a different near-synonym.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: The Wikipedia "Signs of AI writing" article identifies "elegant variation" as a structural tell: "excessive synonym substitution (protagonist, key player, eponymous character)" creating "unnatural synonym chains." This pattern emerges because LLMs are trained on data that includes editorial advice to "vary your word choice" but apply this advice mechanically, without understanding that in fiction, clarity often requires repeating the exact right word rather than substituting a near-synonym that introduces ambiguity.
**Design space cost**: Some writers genuinely avoid repetition as a stylistic choice. The sentinel should flag cases where the variation creates confusion or where synonyms are not true synonyms (referring to a sword alternately as "the blade," "the weapon," and "the steel" is fine; calling it "the implement" is suspicious).
**Source**: [Wikipedia - Signs of AI writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Signs_of_AI_writing)

### Vocabulary Homogeneity Across Diverse Characters
**Detection**: All characters in the fiction use the same vocabulary level, sentence complexity, and register. A child speaks with the same diction as a professor. A blue-collar worker uses the same abstract language as a philosopher. There is no vocabulary variation tied to character identity.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Stylometric research found that human writers demonstrate "cognitive complexity in their word choices" informed by "semantic memory, lived experiences, and an awareness of audience expectations." LLMs produce prose that follows "a narrow and uniform pattern" because all output flows from the same statistical model. When all characters sound the same, it indicates that character voice is being generated by the same process rather than being differentiated by the writer's understanding of how different people speak and think.
**Design space cost**: First-person narrators may impose their own vocabulary on all reported speech. Some fiction deliberately uses uniform voice as a stylistic choice (Hemingway's characters often sound like Hemingway). The sentinel is strongest in third-person fiction with multiple speaking characters.
**Source**: [Nature - Stylometric comparisons of human versus AI-generated creative writing](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05986-3), [Countercraft - MFA vs. LLM](https://countercraft.substack.com/p/mfa-vs-llm-is-openais-metafiction)

### Dream Sequences and Flashbacks as Default
**Detection**: The story includes dream sequences or flashbacks as the primary mechanism for conveying backstory, emotional state, or thematic content, particularly when these could be conveyed through present-tense action and dialogue.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: StoryScope identified model-specific patterns: "GPT over-indexes on dream sequences." Dream sequences and flashbacks are statistically convenient narrative devices because they allow the model to deliver exposition and emotional content without the constraint of maintaining scene-level realism and continuity. They are the narrative equivalent of a cutscene in a video game — a way to deliver information without solving the harder problem of embedding it in dramatic action.
**Design space cost**: Dream sequences and flashbacks are legitimate literary devices with a long tradition. The sentinel should flag their use as a default delivery mechanism for backstory, not their presence as a deliberate artistic choice.
**Source**: [StoryScope - arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.03136)

### Absence of Subtext
**Detection**: Every piece of information in the story is stated explicitly. Characters say exactly what they mean. Narration explains every motivation. There are no scenes where the surface meaning diverges from the actual meaning, no moments where what a character says and what they feel are different, no conversations that are "about" something other than their literal content.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: The "Reading Subtext" research found that all tested LLMs "make faithfulness mistakes in over 50% of summaries and struggle with specificity and interpretation of difficult subtext." Unreliable narrators "break models consistently." The LAMP corpus study documented that "unnecessary/redundant exposition" — stating the obvious rather than showing — accounts for 18% of all edits needed in LLM prose. LLMs generate text by predicting the most probable next token, which biases toward explicitness (the training data rewards clarity over indirection).
**Design space cost**: Some genres operate with minimal subtext by convention (middle-grade fiction, certain thriller subgenres). Young readers may need more explicit emotional communication. The sentinel is strongest for fiction that appears to aim for literary or psychological depth.
**Source**: [arXiv - Reading Subtext: Evaluating LLMs on Short Story Summarization with Writers](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.01061), [arXiv - Can AI writing be salvaged?](https://arxiv.org/html/2409.14509v3), [Writers Helping Writers - How to Differentiate Stories from AI Slop](https://writershelpingwriters.net/2025/09/how-to-differentiate-stories-from-ai-slop/)

### Negative Parallelism Default ("Not Just X, But Also Y")
**Detection**: The prose systematically uses the construction "not just X, but also Y" or "not merely X, but Y" or "it wasn't X — it was Y" as a primary rhetorical structure for emphasis and revelation.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: The Wikipedia "Signs of AI writing" article identifies two forms of negative parallelism as structural tells: "Not only...but..." constructions that clear up "imaginary misconceptions" and "It's not..., it's..." constructions that explicitly deny a first characteristic. These patterns appear because LLMs have been trained on persuasive and explanatory text that uses this structure to create emphasis. In fiction, the construction reads as authorial editorializing rather than narrative showing.
**Design space cost**: Negative parallelism is a legitimate rhetorical device. Individual instances are not diagnostic. The sentinel should flag systematic use as a default emphasis structure.
**Source**: [Wikipedia - Signs of AI writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Signs_of_AI_writing)

### Generic Opening Situation (Mirror, Waking Up, Weather)
**Detection**: The story opens with a character waking up, looking in a mirror and describing their appearance, observing the weather, or engaging in routine activity with internal monologue about their situation. No element of the opening is specific to this character or this story.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: The Good Story Company identifies these as the most common fiction cliches: "Character waking up at story or chapter opening," "Character staring in mirror analyzing appearance," "Character going to sleep at chapter end." These openings are defaults because they are low-entropy narrative situations — the model needs to start somewhere, and these situations require minimal contextual setup. They are the narrative equivalent of "Once upon a time" without the genre convention that justifies it.
**Design space cost**: Some stories legitimately begin with waking, mirrors, or weather when these are thematically significant (Metamorphosis begins with waking up because the waking is the story). The sentinel should flag generic use, not thematically justified use.
**Source**: [Good Story Company - Avoid Cliches](https://www.goodstorycompany.com/blog/avoid-cliches), [LitReactor - 10 Stories We Never Need to See in Workshops Again](https://litreactor.com/columns/10-stories-we-never-need-to-see-in-workshops-again)
