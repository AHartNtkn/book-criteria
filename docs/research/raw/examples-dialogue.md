# Dialogue Craft -- Published Prose Excerpts

Three excerpts from well-regarded novels demonstrating invisible attribution, action
beats replacing tags, conflict within exchanges, and dialogue doing multiple things
simultaneously.

---

## 1. Ernest Hemingway, "Hills Like White Elephants" (1927)

> "It's really an awfully simple operation, Jig," the man said. "It's not really an
> operation at all."
>
> The girl looked at the ground the table legs rested on.
>
> "I know you wouldn't mind it, Jig. It's really not anything. It's just to let the
> air in."
>
> The girl did not say anything.
>
> "I'll go with you and I'll stay with you all the time. They just let the air in and
> then it's all perfectly natural."
>
> "Then what will we do afterward?"
>
> "We'll be fine afterward. Just like we were before."
>
> "What makes you think so?"
>
> "That's the only thing that bothers us. It's the only thing that's made us unhappy."
>
> The girl looked at the bead curtain, put her hand out and took hold of two of the
> strings of beads.
>
> "And you think then we'll be all right and be happy."
>
> "I know we will. You don't have to be afraid. I've known lots of people that have
> done it."
>
> "So have I," said the girl. "And afterward they were all so happy."

**Source:** University of Virginia, Literature in Context: An Open Anthology
(https://anthologydev.lib.virginia.edu/work/Hemingway/hemingway-hills)

**What makes the dialogue craft work:** Neither character ever names the abortion they
are discussing; the man's relentless euphemisms ("simple operation," "just to let
the air in," "perfectly natural") do triple duty -- advancing the argument, exposing
his manipulative character, and generating tension through the gap between what is
said and what is meant. The girl's short, flat replies and her physical deflections
(looking at the ground, gripping the bead curtain) replace dialogue tags entirely
and carry the emotional weight the man's words are designed to suppress.

## 2. Elmore Leonard, *Out of Sight* (1996)

> "Well, does it make sense to you?"
>
> He said, "It doesn't have to, it's something that happens. It's like seeing a person
> you never saw before -- you could be passing on the street -- and you look at each
> other..."
>
> Karen was nodding. "You make eye contact without meaning to."
>
> "And for a few moments," Foley said, "there's a kind of recognition. You look at
> each other and you know something."
>
> "That no one else knows," Karen said. "You see it in their eyes."
>
> "And the next moment the person's gone," Foley said, "and it's too late to do
> anything about it, but you remember it because it was right there and you let it go,
> and you think, What if I had stopped and said something? It might happen only a few
> times in your life."
>
> "Or once," Karen said.

**Source:** Goodreads, *Out of Sight* quotes page
(https://www.goodreads.com/work/quotes/1817274-out-of-sight)

**What makes the dialogue craft work:** A bank robber and a U.S. Marshal, locked in
a car trunk together during an escape, build a shared sentence across alternating
lines -- each completing the other's thought -- so the dialogue itself enacts the
connection they are describing. Leonard uses almost no tags ("Foley said" and "Karen
said" appear just often enough to anchor the rhythm) and zero adverbs; the intimacy
is carried entirely by the characters' willingness to finish each other's sentences,
which simultaneously advances the romance plot and establishes the doomed chemistry
that drives the rest of the novel.

## 3. Richard Price, *Lush Life* (2008)

> "Just . . ." Billy reading his mind. "You're him, OK? Now . . . The guy shot your
> friend, knows you're the only eyewitness. Wouldn't you be worried that that guy
> might be coming back to tie up loose ends? Wouldn't you be in fear for your life?
> Wouldn't you get the hell out of Dodge until the cops catch this guy? But this Cash,
> correct me if I'm wrong, he doesn't do that."
>
> "Billy . . ."
>
> "As far as I know, he still lives where he lives, works where he works, goes about
> his business like there's nothing, nobody out there to fear. Why is that?"
>
> "Don't do this to yourself," Matty said.

**Source:** The Mookse and the Gripes, review of *Lush Life*
(https://mookseandgripes.com/reviews/2009/05/01/richard-prices-lush-life/)

**What makes the dialogue craft work:** Billy Marcus, the murder victim's father, has
worked out that the supposed eyewitness Eric Cash is actually the killer -- and he
lays out the logic in front of the detective, Matty, in a cascade of rhetorical
questions that simultaneously reveal Billy's intelligence, his obsessive grief, his
growing certainty, and the detective's helplessness. The single action beat ("Billy
reading his mind") replaces any attribution tag and does the work of an entire
paragraph of interior monologue. Matty's responses -- a trailing-off name, a deflection
-- confirm through their inadequacy that Billy is right, without the narrative ever
saying so.
