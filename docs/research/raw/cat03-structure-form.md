## Criteria Found

### Three-Act Proportional Balance
**What it measures**: Whether the novel's act structure follows sound proportional distribution (approximately 25% setup, 50% confrontation, 25% resolution)
**Assumed goal**: Aristotelian dramatic structure as codified by Syd Field's screenplay paradigm and adapted for novels — assumes stories benefit from a beginning/middle/end with proportional weight
**Score 0**: No discernible act structure; story weight distributed randomly; setup may consume 50% or resolution may be crammed into 5%; no identifiable structural movement from setup through confrontation to resolution
**Score 5**: Each act receives appropriate proportional weight; setup efficiently establishes characters and conflict without overstaying; the middle section has sufficient space for complication and development; resolution is neither rushed nor padded; structural transitions between acts are clear and motivated
**Source**: https://reedsy.com/blog/guide/story-structure/three-act-structure/
**Notes**: Aristotle's Poetics traces this to one of five key elements of tragedy. The 25/50/25 ratio is a guideline, not a rigid formula, but significant deviation creates specific problems (bloated setup, sagging middle, rushed ending).

### Structural Beat Placement
**What it measures**: Whether major structural turning points occur at approximately the expected positions in the narrative
**Assumed goal**: Plot-point screenwriting tradition (Field, Snyder, Fawkes) — assumes stories have identifiable turning points at predictable intervals; less relevant to literary fiction, slice-of-life, or episodic structures
**Score 0**: Major beats are absent, redundant, or wildly misplaced; inciting incident occurs at 40%; midpoint is indistinguishable; climax arrives with no preceding escalation; beats bunch up in one section while other sections contain no significant turns
**Score 5**: Turning points land at approximately their expected positions (inciting incident ~10-12%, first plot point ~25%, midpoint ~50%, third plot point ~75%, climax ~88-95%); each beat genuinely changes the story's trajectory; turns are spaced to create rhythm without mechanical rigidity
**Source**: https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2023/07/the-12-rule-of-story-structure-SCF.html
**Notes**: September C. Fawkes' 12% rule identifies nine beats at ~1%, 12%, 25%, 37%, 50%, 62%, 75%, 87%, and 89-100%. The principle is that successful stories have significant turning points every ~12%, with major turns at quarter marks and medium turns between them.

### Midpoint Effectiveness
**What it measures**: Whether the story's midpoint functions as a genuine pivot that reorients the narrative
**Assumed goal**: Western plot-driven fiction in the tradition of K.M. Weiland and Blake Snyder, where a central pivot reorients the protagonist's approach — does not apply to picaresque, episodic, or conflict-free narrative forms
**Score 0**: No identifiable midpoint event; the 50% mark passes without any shift in character understanding, stakes, or approach; protagonist remains in the same reactive mode throughout; the middle section sags with repetitive complications
**Score 5**: At approximately the 50% mark, a major event occurs that constitutes a Moment of Truth, shifting the protagonist from reaction to action; the midpoint functions as a set-piece scene that is one of the story's most visual/dramatic moments; it reveals new information that recontextualizes the conflict; it may include mirror symbolism where the protagonist confronts themselves
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/the-midpoint-as-the-swivel-point-of-your-storys-linked-structure/
**Notes**: The midpoint stands alone at the base of the circular structure with no paired beat. It is the swivel point around which first-half and second-half beats mirror each other. Sam Peckinpah called it the "centerpiece" — everything hangs upon it.

### Five Turning Points Presence
**What it measures**: Whether the novel contains the five major turning points that sustain narrative momentum
**Assumed goal**: C. S. Lakin's structural completeness model — assumes novels need five distinct trajectory-changing events to sustain a full-length narrative arc; less applicable to novellas, short fiction, or meditative literary works
**Score 0**: Fewer than three identifiable turning points; story proceeds as a sequence of events without trajectory-changing moments; no clear inciting incident, midpoint shift, dark moment, or climax
**Score 5**: All five turning points are present and functional: (1) Opportunity/Inciting Incident at ~10% that creates desire, (2) Goal Lock-In that makes the objective concrete, (3) Midpoint/Mirror Moment at ~50% with full commitment, (4) Dark Night of the Soul at ~75% with apparent hopelessness, (5) Climax where all conflict converges
**Source**: https://www.livewritethrive.com/2021/03/29/the-5-turning-points-in-your-novel/
**Notes**: C. S. Lakin argues "almost every great story has five turning points." Each serves distinct structural functions. A missing turning point doesn't just create a gap—it undermines the turns adjacent to it.

### Scene-Sequel Rhythm
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative alternates between active conflict scenes and reflective sequel sequences to create pacing rhythm
**Assumed goal**: Dwight V. Swain's "Techniques of the Selling Writer" framework — assumes fiction operates through alternating units of conflict and transition; foundational to genre fiction craft but less descriptive of stream-of-consciousness or experimental prose
**Score 0**: All action with no reflection (exhausting), or all reflection with no action (boring); scenes lack clear goal-conflict-disaster structure; no identifiable moments where characters process events and make decisions; pacing is monotone
**Score 5**: Scenes follow goal-conflict-disaster structure and are followed by reaction-dilemma-decision sequels; the ratio of scene to sequel varies to control tempo; peaks (scenes) and valleys (sequels) create visual rhythm in the story's pacing; the pattern is flexible rather than mechanical
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scene_and_sequel
**Notes**: Dwight V. Swain defined a scene as "a unit of conflict" and a sequel as "a unit of transition that links two scenes." Jack Bickham's sequel variant uses emotion, thinking, and decision. The key insight is that pacing is controlled through the proportional ratio of scenes to sequels.

### Scene Purpose and Necessity
**What it measures**: Whether every scene advances the story through clear goal, friction, and change
**Assumed goal**: Developmental editing tradition (BubbleCow, Sol Stein) where every scene must justify its existence through causal contribution — contrasts with literary traditions that value digression, atmosphere, or thematic meditation as ends in themselves
**Score 0**: Multiple scenes could be removed without affecting the story; scenes lack identifiable goals, conflicts, or changes; scenes repeat the same information or emotional beat; the story contains scenes that exist only for atmosphere or information dumps
**Score 5**: Every scene has a clear goal (what someone wants), friction (an obstacle), and change (a win, loss, or new knowledge that shifts the next move); removing any scene would create a gap in causality or understanding; scenes advance both plot and character simultaneously
**Source**: https://bubblecow.com/blog/developmental-editing-checklist
**Notes**: BubbleCow's developmental editing checklist recommends filling G-F-C (Goal-Friction-Change) boxes for each scene in margins. Blank boxes signal cuts or rewrites.

### Scene Entrance and Exit Craft
**What it measures**: Whether scenes begin and end at the dramatically optimal moments
**Assumed goal**: William Goldman's screenwriting-derived "arrive late, leave early" principle applied to prose fiction — assumes narrative economy over immersive pacing; less relevant to literary fiction that uses slow openings and extended exits for atmospheric or psychological effect
**Score 0**: Scenes begin with arrivals, small talk, and setup exposition before anything happens; scenes end with characters wrapping up conversations, departing, and returning to neutral states; extensive preamble and wind-down surround the actual dramatic content
**Score 5**: Scenes begin mid-action, mid-thought, or mid-argument at the latest possible entry point; scenes end shortly after their dramatic peak, before full resolution; no time is spent on arrivals, departures, or "settling in" unless those moments are themselves dramatic; the principle of "arrive late, leave early" is consistently applied
**Source**: https://bluepencilagency.com/arrive-late-leave-early-how-to-write-fast-paced-fiction/
**Notes**: William Goldman: "You always attack a movie scene as late as you possibly can." Hitchcock: "Drama is life with the dull parts cut out." This applies to fiction with slightly more latitude than screenwriting.

### Chapter Architecture
**What it measures**: Whether chapters function as effective structural units with hooks, development, and purposeful endings
**Assumed goal**: Commercial and genre fiction where chapter structure functions as a pacing and retention mechanism — less applicable to novels with minimal chapter divisions or experimental structures (e.g., Saramago, Bolaño)
**Score 0**: Chapters are arbitrary divisions with no internal arc; chapter endings land at random points with no hook or sense of completion; chapters begin without establishing what's at stake; no variation in chapter function or intensity
**Score 5**: Each chapter has its own mini-arc; chapter openings hook the reader into the new section; chapter endings look both backward (providing some satisfaction) and forward (creating anticipation); the balance of chapter ending types follows approximately 60-70% moderate-to-high tension hooks, 20-30% medium tension, and 10-20% resolution/calmer moments
**Source**: https://rivereditor.com/guides/how-to-end-chapters-make-readers-keep-reading-hooks-cliffhangers-2026
**Notes**: The Editor's Blog emphasizes that good chapter endings "look both backward and forward, and both satisfy and entice." The best chapter endings are not always cliffhangers — a mix of ending types creates sustainable reading momentum.

### Chapter Length Variation
**What it measures**: Whether chapter lengths vary purposefully to serve the story's pacing needs
**Assumed goal**: Craft tradition (Literature & Latte, James Patterson) that treats chapter length as a pacing instrument — assumes varied chapter lengths signal intentional design; some literary traditions (e.g., Murakami) use uniform length as a deliberate stylistic choice
**Score 0**: All chapters are approximately the same length regardless of content; no relationship between chapter length and dramatic intensity; long chapters are used for action sequences that need speed; short chapters are used for reflective passages that need space
**Score 5**: Chapter length varies in response to content; shorter chapters create urgency and acceleration during tense sequences; longer chapters slow pace for deeper character development or complex scenes; variation is purposeful rather than random; after action-heavy short chapters, longer reflective chapters provide respite; medium chapters serve transitions and momentum-building
**Source**: https://www.literatureandlatte.com/blog/how-chapter-and-paragraph-length-affects-the-pacing-of-novels
**Notes**: Short chapters have "a kind of jump-cut, fast-edited quality." Literary fiction tends toward longer chapters for deeper introspection. The key is that variation serves function — if a chapter is shorter or longer than the pattern, there should be a discernible reason.

### Subplot Integration Quality
**What it measures**: Whether subplots are woven into the main narrative as structural reinforcement rather than mere filler
**Assumed goal**: Novel-length fiction in the tradition of David Farland and Robert McKee, where subplots serve as structural reinforcement for the main throughline — does not apply to novellas, single-thread narratives, or fix-up novels
**Score 0**: Subplots are disconnected from the main plot and could be removed without impact; subplots appear and disappear irregularly with no structural logic; subplot resolutions happen offscreen or are abandoned; the story feels like multiple unrelated narratives
**Score 5**: Every subplot pressures or illuminates the central story; subplots are woven in at regular intervals creating a rhythmic pattern; bridge characters connect subplot worlds to main plot worlds; subplot climaxes coincide with or amplify main plot turning points; each subplot could stand alone as a compelling story while also serving the whole
**Source**: https://www.writersdigest.com/write-better-fiction/7-ways-to-add-great-subplots-to-your-novel
**Notes**: David Farland emphasizes "It's not the thread, it's the weave" — the quality of subplot integration matters more than the quality of individual subplots in isolation. Subplots should add depth, create conflict, reveal background, and tie back to the main story.

### Subplot as Thematic Mirror
**What it measures**: Whether subplots function as thematic echoes, mirrors, or foils to the main plot
**Assumed goal**: Thematic-unity school of craft (McKee, NotMFA) where subplots function as mirror/foil arguments about the central theme — contrasts with panoramic novels (Dickens, Tolstoy) where subplots may explore independent social themes
**Score 0**: Subplots explore entirely different themes from the main plot; no thematic resonance between storylines; subplots contradict the story's thematic argument without apparent intention; secondary characters face challenges unrelated to the protagonist's central struggle
**Score 5**: Mirror subplots reflect the main plot's thematic question with different outcomes; foil subplots present alternative responses to the same dilemma; parallel character arcs illuminate different aspects of the same universal struggle; subplots run counterpoint in pace, tone, and color while reinforcing the same thematic core
**Source**: https://www.notmfa.com/craft/form-not-formula-mirrors-and-foils
**Notes**: Mirror characters deepen the protagonist's internal need (emotional resonance). Foil characters complicate the protagonist's external want (thematic resistance). These are architectural roles, not aesthetic decoration.

### Temporal Ordering Justification
**What it measures**: Whether any non-linear temporal ordering serves a clear narrative purpose
**Assumed goal**: Fiction that uses non-chronological narration (flashbacks, parallel timelines, in medias res) — assumes temporal reordering must be justified by narrative effect rather than used as default; relevant to any story departing from linear chronology
**Score 0**: Non-linear elements are used without clear purpose; flashbacks interrupt momentum without providing necessary information; timeline jumps confuse rather than intrigue; the chronological scrambling seems arbitrary rather than meaningful; or conversely, linear structure is used when the story would clearly benefit from temporal manipulation
**Score 5**: If non-linear: every departure from chronology serves a specific narrative purpose (juxtaposition of thematically similar moments, delayed revelation for suspense, structural mirroring); the reader can follow the timeline without confusion; temporal shifts highlight "particularly poignant images or important plot turns." If linear: the chronological approach is the best way to tell this particular story
**Source**: https://litreactor.com/columns/out-of-order-a-discussion-of-nonlinear-narrative-structure
**Notes**: Non-linear techniques include flashbacks, flash-forwards, in medias res, frame narratives, reverse chronology, and parallel timelines. The key criterion is not whether non-linearity is present but whether it is justified by the story's needs.

### In Medias Res Execution
**What it measures**: When a story opens in medias res, whether it balances immediate engagement with necessary context
**Assumed goal**: Action-forward opening strategy rooted in Homeric epic tradition and modern thriller craft — assumes the value of immediate immersion; does not apply to stories that deliberately build from quiet beginnings (e.g., pastoral, literary realism)
**Score 0**: Opening plunges into action with no character grounding, making readers unable to care about what's happening; or the in medias res opening is the most exciting thing in the story and everything after is anticlimactic; or the story delays essential context so long that confusion overwhelms intrigue
**Score 5**: The mid-action opening creates immediate engagement while still orienting the reader to character and stakes; context is woven in naturally rather than through exposition dumps; the opening's promise is fulfilled and exceeded by later events; the thin line between "dangling enough unknowns to entice" and "failing to explain critical background" is navigated successfully
**Source**: https://kindlepreneur.com/in-medias-res/
**Notes**: In medias res began in ancient times with Homer's Iliad. The key risk is the "cheap opening" where writers make their opener the most exciting thing they can think of, and the rest doesn't measure up.

### Dual/Parallel Timeline Balance
**What it measures**: Whether stories with multiple timelines maintain equal engagement and structural integrity across all threads
**Assumed goal**: Dual-timeline literary and commercial fiction (e.g., Kate Morton, Anthony Doerr) where parallel chronologies must each sustain independent reader interest — only applicable to stories with two or more distinct timelines
**Score 0**: One timeline is significantly more engaging than the other(s), causing reader frustration during switches; timelines have no meaningful connection to each other; one timeline is padded with filler to match the other's length; transitions are confusing or unmarked; timelines never converge or their convergence is unsatisfying
**Score 5**: Both timelines are equally compelling with their own plot points at approximately 25%, 50%, and 75%; transitions are clearly marked with dates, characters, and sensory cues; the timelines inform and enhance each other meaningfully; the earlier timeline concludes just before the later timeline's climax; pacing alternation is consistent and logical
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/6-ways-to-pull-off-dual-timelines-in/
**Notes**: This criterion only applies to stories using dual or parallel timelines. The key structural requirements are: each timeline needs its own complete arc, transitions must orient readers immediately, and convergence should be architecturally planned.

### Macro Pacing Variation
**What it measures**: Whether the story's pacing varies across its length to create rhythm, rather than proceeding at a uniform pace
**Assumed goal**: Darling Axe / September C. Fawkes pacing school — assumes narratives need peaks-and-valleys tempo variation for sustained reading; contrasts with deliberately flat-affect prose (e.g., Camus, Beckett) that uses monotone pacing as a stylistic choice
**Score 0**: The entire story proceeds at the same pace; every chapter has the same intensity level; no contrast between tense and quiet sections; the story is either all sprint (exhausting) or all walk (boring); no sense of acceleration toward the climax
**Score 5**: Pacing follows a "peaks and valleys" pattern with alternating high-intensity and quieter moments; quiet sections provide emotional processing without losing tension entirely; intensity generally escalates toward the climax; the opening features a dramatic peak to hook readers; post-climax deceleration is deliberate and necessary; the pattern of "three quick beats, then one longer scene to absorb impact" creates sustainable rhythm
**Source**: https://darlingaxe.com/blogs/news/pacing
**Notes**: "Quiet doesn't mean no tension" — background conflict can simmer during reflective sections. The key failure mode is monotone pacing, where every scene operates at the same intensity regardless of content.

### Scene-to-Summary Ratio
**What it measures**: Whether the balance between dramatized scenes and narrative summary is appropriate
**Assumed goal**: Scene-dominant fiction in the showing-over-telling tradition — assumes ~90% dramatized scenes as the modern novel default; less applicable to 19th-century omniscient narration, fabulist fiction, or compressed literary novellas that rely heavily on summary
**Score 0**: Everything is dramatized including mundane transitions, marches, and routine activities, creating tedious pacing; or everything is summarized including emotionally crucial moments, creating distance and disengagement; no discernible principle governs what gets shown vs. told
**Score 5**: Approximately 90% of the novel is written in scene (dramatized); summary is used selectively for time passage, routine activities, and transitions; the rule "write the slow parts fast and the fast parts slow" is followed — mundane events are compressed while significant changes are expanded into full scenes; the choice of what to dramatize versus summarize is principled and consistent
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pace_(narrative)
**Notes**: The 90% scene recommendation comes from multiple craft sources. The principle: anything that shows significant change, action, emotion, or things you want readers to feel immersed in should be a scene. Anything mundane with no significant change should be summarized.

### Escalation and Rising Stakes
**What it measures**: Whether conflicts, stakes, and consequences progressively escalate toward the climax
**Assumed goal**: Fichtean Curve and thriller-craft tradition where progressive escalation drives forward momentum — central to genre fiction (thriller, fantasy, adventure); less applicable to character studies, literary fiction, or narratives structured around decline rather than escalation
**Score 0**: Stakes remain flat throughout; the danger in act one is equivalent to the danger in act three; obstacles are repetitive rather than escalating; the protagonist faces the same type and magnitude of challenge repeatedly; consequences don't compound
**Score 5**: Each turning point raises consequences; choices cost more than prior ones; personal, social, and public stakes climb together; the Fichtean Curve pattern of multiple escalating crises creates a staircase of intensity; reversals stem from character choices rather than random events; the climax represents the highest possible stakes in the story
**Source**: https://www.novlr.org/the-reading-room/the-fichtean-curve-how-to-escalate-tension-and-conflict/
**Notes**: The Fichtean Curve emphasizes multiple escalating crises rather than a single climactic peak. Each confrontation builds upon the previous one, creating a staircase effect. The critical requirement is that escalation is progressive — later crises must genuinely exceed earlier ones.

### Second Act Structural Integrity
**What it measures**: Whether the middle section of the novel avoids the "sagging middle" through sufficient turning points and forward momentum
**Assumed goal**: Three-act novel structure where the middle 50% must sustain momentum without the novelty of the opening or the resolution pull of the ending — a problem specific to novel-length works; short fiction and novellas rarely have this structural challenge
**Score 0**: The middle section feels aimless; the protagonist stops making significant decisions; obstacles repeat without progression; the novelty of the setup has worn off and nothing replaces it; the middle falls into one of two failure modes: repetition (same obstacles with no progression) or stalling (characters processing rather than acting)
**Score 5**: The middle section contains a clear midpoint turn and multiple subsidiary turning points; each cluster of chapters builds toward a moment that reorients the story; smaller reversals maintain anticipation; the middle simultaneously builds tension (things get worse) and develops character (people change); compression is applied to redundant beats; decisions carry escalating weight
**Source**: https://inkshift.io/resources/saggy-middle
**Notes**: Act 2 is typically 50% of the manuscript and is "a dreaded swamp if you don't plan enough turning points." The specific challenge is that it must do two things simultaneously that work against each other: build tension AND develop character. Solutions include proper turning point spacing and compression of redundant beats.

### Revelation Scheduling
**What it measures**: Whether information is revealed to the reader at the optimal time for maximum narrative effect
**Assumed goal**: Mystery, thriller, and suspense fiction where strategic information withholding is a primary engagement mechanism — rooted in Jane Friedman's slow-reveal framework; less central to literary fiction that foregrounds theme over surprise
**Score 0**: All important information is front-loaded in exposition dumps; there is nothing left to discover after the first quarter; or critical information is withheld so long that readers lose interest or feel cheated; revelations have no dramatic impact because they weren't set up; no layered discovery
**Score 5**: Information is released strategically using the tease-hint-reveal pattern; each revelation raises new questions, creating nested mysteries; unreliable narrators or limited perspectives create natural information asymmetry; the four-step slow reveal structure (present consistent aspect, introduce contradictory behavior, recontextualize, disclose truth) is employed effectively; ambiguity techniques create productive uncertainty; the timing of revelations is "just right" — neither too soon nor too late
**Source**: https://janefriedman.com/suspense-slow-reveal/
**Notes**: The Rule of Three (tease, hint, reveal) primes readers to anticipate, builds tension through repetition, then delivers satisfaction. One effective approach is to "nest mysteries within mysteries, so that as one revelation comes to light, it immediately raises new questions."

### Foreshadowing and Payoff Structural Integrity
**What it measures**: Whether setups planted early in the narrative are paid off, and whether payoffs are earned through prior setup
**Assumed goal**: Chekhov's principle of narrative economy — every element introduced must serve a purpose; foundational to tightly plotted fiction but potentially constraining for maximalist, picaresque, or associative narrative styles where not every element resolves
**Score 0**: Major plot developments arrive without any prior setup; "Chekhov's guns" are hung on the wall and never fired; foreshadowing is so heavy-handed it spoils the surprise; or the resolution depends on elements never previously introduced; setups and payoffs are disconnected
**Score 5**: Every significant element introduced early serves a purpose later; payoffs feel both surprising and inevitable; foreshadowing is subtle enough to miss on first read but obvious on reread; no important element goes unused; the balance between setup and payoff creates reader trust — every detail the author includes creates a promise, and those promises are kept
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chekhov's_gun
**Notes**: Chekhov: "If you say in the first act that there is a rifle hanging on the wall, in the second or third act it absolutely must go off." The converse also applies: if a rifle goes off in act three, it should have been visible in act one. This applies to objects, skills, character traits, and information.

### Promise-Progress-Payoff Architecture
**What it measures**: Whether the story makes clear promises in its beginning, shows meaningful progress in its middle, and delivers satisfying payoffs in its end
**Assumed goal**: Brandon Sanderson's Promise-Progress-Payoff framework — assumes stories operate as implicit contracts between author and reader; central to commercial speculative fiction; less descriptive of avant-garde or anti-narrative works that deliberately frustrate expectation
**Score 0**: The beginning makes no clear promises about what the story will deliver; the middle wanders without visible progress toward any goal; the ending resolves problems never established; or promises are made but never fulfilled; the perfect payoff's balance of surprise and inevitability is absent
**Score 5**: The beginning clearly promises what kind of experience the story will deliver (tone, genre, stakes, central question); the middle shows visible progress with complications; the ending delivers payoffs that are both unexpected and inevitable; this architecture operates at every level — whole story, act, and individual scene; each payoff both fulfills an established promise and reframes the story's meaning
**Source**: https://www.brandonsanderson.com/blogs/blog/brandon-sandersons-2025-guide-to-plot-lecture-2
**Notes**: Brandon Sanderson's framework. The perfect payoff balances expectation and surprise — "not about delivering exactly what readers predict, but about giving them something that feels both unexpected and inevitable." This principle applies recursively: each scene, act, and the full story should exhibit promise, progress, and payoff.

### Climax Effectiveness and Earned Resolution
**What it measures**: Whether the climax resolves the central conflict in a way that feels earned rather than arbitrary
**Assumed goal**: Plot-driven fiction where the climax must resolve the central conflict through previously established means — the anti-deus-ex-machina tradition from Aristotle through modern craft; does not apply to absurdist, surrealist, or deliberately anticlimactic literary traditions
**Score 0**: The climax resolves through coincidence, deus ex machina, or previously unestablished abilities; the climax doesn't address the central conflict; the resolution feels anticlimactic relative to the buildup; the protagonist doesn't drive the resolution through their own choices and growth; the ending feels either rushed or padded
**Score 5**: The climax converges all internal and external conflict; the resolution is driven by the protagonist's choices and transformation; the outcome feels both surprising and inevitable because it was earned through foreshadowing; the climactic moment is commensurate with the buildup; all prior plot setups pay off; the resolution satisfies the core promise made in the opening
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/storys-climax-4/
**Notes**: A false climax (faux resolution followed by further complication) can be effective if used intentionally. An accidental anticlimax is the result of bad plotting. What matters most is that the climax "feels earned and resonates with the overall themes and tone of the story."

### Denouement Proportionality
**What it measures**: Whether the post-climax resolution is appropriately proportioned — long enough to provide satisfaction but short enough to maintain energy
**Assumed goal**: September C. Fawkes' proportional denouement model — assumes a post-climax resolution segment is necessary but must be brief (~2-3%); contrasts with novels that end at the climax (e.g., thriller tradition) or that use extended epilogues (e.g., Tolkien)
**Score 0**: The story ends immediately after the climax with no falling action, leaving readers disoriented; or the denouement drags on for many chapters after the central tension has resolved, losing reader interest; loose ends are either ignored or addressed at tedious length; the denouement re-introduces new conflicts
**Score 5**: The denouement occupies approximately 2-3% of the story (1-3 chapters for a novel); remaining conflict and tension are addressed and dissipated; important loose ends are tied up; character change is validated; the tone shifts from tension to resolution without abruptness; the ending earns its emotional weight without overstaying
**Source**: https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2025/06/how-long-should-denouement-be-short-vs.html
**Notes**: The specific danger is that "skipping the falling action can make your ending feel abrupt" while making it too long means the reader has already emotionally departed. The denouement addresses what needs addressing — it's not about ending quickly but about ending purposefully.

### Inciting Incident Timing
**What it measures**: Whether the inciting incident occurs early enough to engage readers but late enough for character grounding
**Assumed goal**: Savannah Gilbo's inciting-incident-placement model — assumes the first disruption must occur early enough to establish direction (~10-15%) but late enough for character grounding; primarily relevant to commercial fiction pacing expectations
**Score 0**: The inciting incident occurs so early (first page) that readers have no connection to the character it affects; or it occurs so late (past 25%) that readers have already disengaged from waiting; the inciting incident is unclear or too subtle to register as a turning point
**Score 5**: The inciting incident occurs within the first 10-15% of the story, ideally around the 12% mark; the reader has had enough time to understand the protagonist and their world; the incident clearly disrupts the status quo and creates desire or need in the protagonist; if delayed past 15%, compelling subplots with their own earlier inciting incidents maintain engagement
**Source**: https://www.savannahgilbo.com/blog/inciting-incident
**Notes**: Too early means the reader hasn't connected with the protagonist enough to care about what happens to them. Too late means the reader becomes frustrated waiting for the story to start. The ~12% placement gives roughly the first eighth of the story for world and character establishment.

### Prologue/Epilogue Structural Justification
**What it measures**: Whether prologues and epilogues serve essential structural functions rather than existing as default conventions
**Assumed goal**: Agent/editor gatekeeping tradition that is skeptical of prologues and epilogues as default conventions — assumes these devices require justification; contrasts with epic fantasy and historical fiction traditions where prologues are standard
**Score 0**: Prologue contains information that would be better woven into the narrative; prologue is used as an excuse for a slow opening chapter; epilogue is used because the ending didn't resolve things adequately; prologue and epilogue feel disconnected from the main story; or prologues/epilogues are used by default without considering whether they're needed
**Score 5**: If present, the prologue makes specific promises that couldn't be made any other way (worldbuilding in unfamiliar settings, establishing future stakes, introducing a different time period); the epilogue provides closure that the climax couldn't deliver (time jump to show long-term consequences, character growth validation); if absent, the story is better without them and doesn't need external framing
**Source**: https://reedsy.com/blog/guide/parts-of-a-book/prologue/
**Notes**: The primary purpose of a prologue is to "make promises to the audience." Epilogues "almost always feature a time jump." Both are optional and should be used only when they add meaningful structural value. Many agents and editors are suspicious of prologues because they're often used to excuse slow openings.

### Frame Narrative and Nested Structure Coherence
**What it measures**: Whether frame narratives and nested story structures maintain clarity and serve the overall narrative
**Assumed goal**: Frame-narrative tradition (One Thousand and One Nights, Frankenstein, Cloud Atlas) where nested stories create meaning through structural layering — only applicable to stories using frame or embedded-narrative devices
**Score 0**: The frame narrative is forgotten or abandoned partway through; nested stories have no thematic or narrative connection to the frame; transitions between narrative levels are confusing; the frame feels like a device rather than an integral part of the story; nesting levels become so deep that the reader loses track
**Score 5**: The frame narrative and inner stories are thematically linked; each layer of nesting adds meaning to the others; transitions between narrative levels are clear and purposeful; the frame provides context or perspective that transforms understanding of the inner narrative; the structure creates "Russian doll" depth where each story enriches the others
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_story
**Notes**: Classic examples include One Thousand and One Nights and Frankenstein (which nests three levels of narration). The key structural requirement is that nesting must create meaning — each layer should illuminate the others.

### Circular/Chiastic Structural Resonance
**What it measures**: Whether the story's ending echoes or mirrors its beginning in a way that creates structural satisfaction
**Assumed goal**: Ring composition / chiastic structure tradition (Mary Douglas, K.M. Weiland) — assumes narrative satisfaction arises from structural symmetry between beginning and ending; not all stories aim for circular return (e.g., tragedy, transformation narratives that deliberately avoid return)
**Score 0**: No structural relationship between beginning and ending; the story arrives at its conclusion without any echo of where it started; opportunities for meaningful mirroring are missed; or the story ends exactly where it began with no sense of change
**Score 5**: The ending mirrors the beginning through deliberate structural choices (returning to opening imagery, revisiting opening situations with transformed understanding, completing a thematic circuit); if chiastic structure is employed, mirrored elements create insight through both comparison and contrast; the final image meaningfully reflects or inverts the opening image; the reader experiences "instinctual satisfaction" from the structural symmetry
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/the-power-of-chiastic-story-structure-especially-in-series/
**Notes**: Save the Cat calls this the Opening Image / Final Image mirror. Chiastic structure (A-B-B'-A') creates resonance through repetitive symmetry. The Harry Potter series demonstrates extensive chiastic structure across seven books.

### Organic Unity of Structure
**What it measures**: Whether all structural elements are interdependent and necessary, forming a cohesive whole
**Assumed goal**: Aristotle's Poetics and New Critical organic unity — the principle that a work's parts are interdependent and no element can be removed or rearranged without damage; a foundational Western aesthetic standard, though contested by postmodern and deconstructionist traditions
**Score 0**: Structural elements feel arbitrary or interchangeable; scenes, chapters, or sections could be rearranged without impact; the form doesn't serve the content; structural choices follow convention rather than the story's needs; removing or rearranging sections wouldn't affect the reader's experience
**Score 5**: Every structural element is necessary and interdependent; the "transposal or withdrawal of any one of them would disjoin and dislocate the whole" (Aristotle); the chosen structure is the only structure that could tell this particular story this particular way; form enhances content at every level; the story functions holistically rather than as a collection of independent parts
**Source**: https://www.britannica.com/art/organic-unity
**Notes**: Organic unity is the idea that "all elements of a text must harmonize to create a cohesive meaning." This is the highest structural criterion — it asks not whether the structure follows rules, but whether the structure is the right structure for this specific story.

### Story Grid Five Commandments Per Unit
**What it measures**: Whether each structural unit (scene, act, global story) contains the five essential story event components
**Assumed goal**: Shawn Coyne's Story Grid methodology — assumes every scene, act, and global story must contain five specific event components; a prescriptive commercial-fiction editing framework not universally adopted
**Score 0**: Scenes lack identifiable inciting incidents, turning points, crises, climaxes, or resolutions; acts begin and end without clear dramatic movement; the global story is missing essential commandments; scenes feel like atmospheric passages rather than dramatic events
**Score 5**: Each scene contains: (1) an Inciting Incident that disrupts balance, (2) a Turning Point / Progressive Complication that changes direction, (3) a Crisis Question that forces a choice, (4) a Climax where the decision is made, and (5) a Resolution showing repercussions; these five commandments operate at scene level, act level, and global story level simultaneously
**Source**: https://storygrid.com/five-commandments-of-storytelling/
**Notes**: Shawn Coyne's Story Grid methodology requires these five elements at every level of narrative structure. The three acts (Beginning Hook 25%, Middle Build 50%, Ending Payoff 25%) each contain their own five commandments while also serving the global story's five commandments.

### Save the Cat Beat Completeness
**What it measures**: Whether the story contains all fifteen structural beats of the Save the Cat framework
**Assumed goal**: Stories following the commercial-fiction structural template that tracks both external plot and internal transformation
**Score 0**: Fewer than half the fifteen beats are identifiable; the A Story (plot) and B Story (theme/relationship) never connect; no identifiable Fun and Games section delivering on the premise; no Dark Night of the Soul creating the low point before the finale; the Finale lacks the five sub-components
**Score 5**: All fifteen beats are present and functional: Opening Image (0-1%), Theme Stated (5%), Setup (1-10%), Catalyst (10%), Debate (10-20%), Break Into Two (20%), B Story (22%), Fun and Games (20-50%), Midpoint (50%, false victory or defeat), Bad Guys Close In (50-75%), All Is Lost (75%), Dark Night of the Soul (75-80%), Break Into Three (80%), Finale (80-99%, with five sub-components: Gathering the Team, Executing the Plan, High Tower Surprise, Dig Deep Down, Execution of New Plan), Final Image (99-100%)
**Source**: https://www.savannahgilbo.com/blog/plotting-save-the-cat
**Notes**: Jessica Brody adapted Blake Snyder's screenwriting beats for novels. The framework is designed for commercial fiction and tracks both plot (A Story) and character transformation (B Story). Not all stories need to follow this framework, but it represents the most detailed widely-used beat sheet.

### Dan Harmon Story Circle Completeness
**What it measures**: Whether the story completes the full circular journey of departure, transformation, and return
**Assumed goal**: Dan Harmon's Story Circle (adapted from Campbell's monomyth) — assumes stories follow a departure-descent-return cycle of psychological transformation; genre-agnostic but rooted in the mythic-journey tradition that not all fiction follows
**Score 0**: The protagonist never leaves their comfort zone; or they leave but the return doesn't reflect transformation; the circular structure is incomplete — departure without return or return without change; the character's journey has no clear "low point" at the bottom of the circle
**Score 5**: All eight steps are present: (1) You — character in comfort zone, (2) Need — something is missing, (3) Go — entering unfamiliar situation, (4) Search — adapting to new world, (5) Find — getting what they wanted, (6) Take — paying a heavy price, (7) Return — going back to familiar situation, (8) Change — having been fundamentally altered; the low point occurs at the circle's bottom; the character's final state meaningfully contrasts their initial state
**Source**: https://reedsy.com/blog/guide/story-structure/dan-harmon-story-circle/
**Notes**: Dan Harmon adapted the Hero's Journey into eight simplified, genre-agnostic steps. The circle metaphor is literal — descent and ascent create momentum. The protagonist begins at the top, descends to their literal and figurative low point at the bottom, then rises transformed.

### Seven-Point Symmetry
**What it measures**: Whether the story's structure exhibits the symmetrical hook-resolution, turn-turn, pinch-pinch architecture
**Assumed goal**: Dan Wells' Seven-Point Story Structure — assumes stories benefit from symmetrical pairing of first-half and second-half beats (hook/resolution, pinch/pinch, turn/turn); a specific structural model most applicable to genre fiction with clear arcs
**Score 0**: No identifiable hook or resolution; no symmetrical relationship between the story's halves; pinch points are absent or don't escalate; the midpoint doesn't mark a shift from reaction to action; the structure is asymmetric without intentional purpose
**Score 5**: Seven points are present and symmetrical: Hook and Resolution are polar opposites; Plot Turn 1 and Plot Turn 2 mirror each other (entering the conflict / getting the final piece needed); Pinch 1 and Pinch 2 escalate (pressure / dire pressure); the Midpoint sits at the center where the protagonist shifts from reaction to action; the overall structure creates balance between the story's halves
**Source**: https://www.campfirewriting.com/learn/seven-point-story-structure
**Notes**: Dan Wells' hidden insight is the development order: start with Resolution, then Hook (its opposite), then Midpoint, then Plot Turns and Pinches. Planning from resolution backward ensures the ending is earned. The symmetry between first-half and second-half elements creates structural coherence.

### Kishotenketsu Structural Awareness
**What it measures**: Whether stories that don't center on conflict still achieve structural satisfaction through the four-act twist-and-reconciliation pattern
**Assumed goal**: Stories that derive their power from juxtaposition and surprise rather than from antagonistic conflict
**Score 0**: A story without central conflict proceeds as a flat sequence of events with no structural movement; there is no identifiable "twist" (ten) that recontextualizes what came before; the story lacks the reconciliation that gives the twist meaning; the absence of conflict is not compensated by any other structural engine
**Score 5**: The four acts function clearly: Introduction (ki) establishes characters and world; Development (sho) deepens the established elements; Twist (ten) introduces an unexpected element that recontextualizes everything; Reconciliation (ketsu) brings the first two acts and the twist into harmony; the story achieves structural satisfaction without requiring antagonistic conflict; character growth comes through self-actualization and introspection rather than opposition
**Source**: https://mythicscribes.com/plot/kishotenketsu/
**Notes**: Kishotenketsu is a Japanese/Chinese/Korean/Vietnamese narrative framework where "tension isn't the heart of the story... the twist is the high point." This criterion is relevant for evaluating stories that don't follow Western conflict-driven structure — it provides an alternative structural lens.

### Fichtean Curve Crisis Escalation
**What it measures**: Whether the story features multiple escalating crises building toward the climax, rather than a single peak
**Assumed goal**: Fichtean Curve model — assumes tension is sustained through multiple escalating crises rather than a single Freytag peak; most applicable to thrillers, mysteries, and adventure fiction where continuous escalation is a genre expectation
**Score 0**: The story has only one crisis point (the climax) with flat tension between the opening and the final confrontation; crises don't build on each other; each crisis is isolated rather than compounding; tension between crises drops to zero rather than maintaining a rising baseline
**Score 5**: Multiple crisis points punctuate the narrative, each genuinely threatening the protagonist's goals; each crisis is more intense than the last, creating a staircase effect; brief resolutions between crises maintain rising baseline tension; clear cause-and-effect relationships connect crises; reader investment is maintained through continuous escalation rather than a single delayed payoff
**Source**: https://www.novlr.org/the-reading-room/the-fichtean-curve-how-to-escalate-tension-and-conflict/
**Notes**: The Fichtean Curve differs from Freytag's Pyramid by featuring repeated cycles of rising tension rather than a single arc. Each cycle reaches higher stakes than the last. This is particularly relevant for thrillers, mysteries, and adventure fiction.

### Section Break and Transition Craft
**What it measures**: Whether scene breaks, section breaks, and transitions serve clear purposes and maintain reader orientation
**Assumed goal**: Multi-POV and multi-scene fiction where readers must be oriented across frequent shifts in time, place, or perspective — a craft concern specific to novels with complex scene structures; less relevant to single-POV linear narratives
**Score 0**: Scene breaks are used randomly without clear purpose; transitions between time/place/POV shifts are unmarked and confusing; the reader frequently loses track of when, where, and whose perspective they're in; section breaks are either overused (creating choppiness) or underused (creating walls of undifferentiated text)
**Score 5**: Section breaks are used purposefully to signal changes in time, place, or perspective; transitions open with time anchors and character identification; scene endings create choices that imply the next scene's direction; white space is used strategically around key moments; the overall pattern of breaks creates a rhythm that serves the story's pacing without creating choppiness or monotony
**Source**: https://www.mandawaller.co.uk/section-breaks-in-your-novel-help-with-pacing-tension-and-clarify/
**Notes**: Section breaks signal changes in time, place, or perspective and prevent readers from getting lost. Hard breaks (***) and soft breaks (blank lines) serve different purposes. Overuse creates choppiness; underuse creates impenetrable text blocks.

### Redundancy and Structural Efficiency
**What it measures**: Whether the story avoids repeating the same structural beat or delivering the same information multiple times
**Assumed goal**: BubbleCow's developmental editing approach — assumes each scene should have a unique five-word purpose statement with no structural duplicates; a revision-stage efficiency standard that prioritizes compression over expansiveness
**Score 0**: Multiple scenes pursue the same goal with the same type of obstacle; information is delivered multiple times through different characters or scenes; chapters chase the same outcome; emotional beats repeat without escalation; the story could be significantly compressed without losing anything
**Score 5**: Every scene has a unique five-word purpose statement; no two scenes repeat the same structural function; when similar situations recur, they escalate or transform; redundant beats have been merged or cut; the story achieves its narrative goals with maximum efficiency
**Source**: https://bubblecow.com/blog/developmental-editing-checklist
**Notes**: The developmental editing checklist recommends listing scenes with five-word purpose statements, circling repeats, and combining or cutting duplicate beats. The question is not just "is this good?" but "has this already been accomplished by another scene?"

### Microtension Continuity
**What it measures**: Whether every page of the narrative contains some form of tension, curiosity, or forward pull
**Assumed goal**: Donald Maass's "micro-tension on every page" philosophy — assumes every page needs at least one tension device (question, contrast, withheld detail) to sustain engagement; a commercial fiction standard that may not apply to contemplative or meditative prose
**Score 0**: Long passages with no questions raised, no tension, and no forward pull; exposition-heavy sections with no stakes; pages where nothing is uncertain and nothing is desired; the reader can comfortably stop reading at multiple points without feeling compelled to continue
**Score 5**: Every page contains at least one tension device: an unanswered question, a body/word mismatch, a nearby deadline, a withheld detail, a contrast, or an uncertainty; even quiet, reflective passages maintain background tension through unresolved questions; microtension sustains engagement between the macro structural beats
**Source**: https://bubblecow.com/blog/developmental-editing-checklist
**Notes**: BubbleCow recommends "one tension device per page (question mark, contrast word, withheld detail)." Microtension is the structural glue between major beats — it's what prevents readers from disengaging during the spaces between turning points.

### Rhythm Pattern Variety
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative alternates between different modes (dialogue, action, reflection, summary) to prevent monotony
**Assumed goal**: BubbleCow's developmental editing rhythm analysis — assumes novels need alternation between dialogue, action, reflection, and summary to prevent monotony; less relevant to novels deliberately written in a single dominant mode (e.g., all-dialogue novels like Gaddis's JR)
**Score 0**: Long stretches of the same mode — pages of unbroken dialogue, or pages of unbroken reflection, or pages of unbroken action; no variation in scene type or length; every chapter follows the same pattern; the story has one "gear" and stays in it
**Score 5**: The story alternates between short and long scenes; dialogue, action, reflection, and summary are mixed; scenes are labeled S (short), M (medium), or L (long) and avoid long runs of one size; the pattern of "three quick beats, then one longer scene to absorb impact" creates sustainable rhythm; genre-appropriate variation exists between dense and sparse sections
**Source**: https://bubblecow.com/blog/developmental-editing-checklist
**Notes**: The developmental editing checklist explicitly warns against "long runs of one size." The goal is not random variation but purposeful alternation that mirrors the emotional experience of the story.

### Novel Length Appropriateness
**What it measures**: Whether the story's length is appropriate for its genre and content
**Assumed goal**: Genre-publishing word-count conventions enforced by agents and editors — assumes marketability requires falling within genre norms (romance 65-80K, thriller 70-100K, fantasy 90-120K+); less relevant to literary fiction or self-published work where length conventions are more flexible
**Score 0**: The story is dramatically shorter or longer than genre expectations without justification; the narrative is padded to reach a target length; or the narrative is compressed to the point where necessary development is missing; the length doesn't serve the story's structural needs
**Score 5**: The story's length falls within genre expectations (romance 65-80K, thriller 70-100K, fantasy 90-120K+, literary 80-100K, YA 50-80K) or deviates with clear justification; every chapter earns its length; the story doesn't feel padded or rushed; structural beats have appropriate breathing room; the pacing matches reader expectations for the genre
**Source**: https://manuscriptacademy.com/word-count-by-genre-literary-agents-2026
**Notes**: Word count directly impacts pacing and structure. "A novella's lean word count means the pace has to be quick and the storytelling has to be efficient — there's no room for detours." Significantly exceeding or falling short of genre norms signals structural problems unless there's intentional justification.

### Dramatica Completeness of Argument
**What it measures**: Whether the story investigates all significant approaches to its central problem
**Assumed goal**: Melanie Anne Phillips and Chris Huntley's Dramatica theory — assumes every complete story models the mind's problem-solving process through four throughlines; a specific structural theory not widely adopted outside its practitioner community
**Score 0**: The story presents only one approach to its central problem; alternative perspectives are strawmanned or absent; the story's "argument" is one-sided; the resolution feels predetermined rather than earned through exploration; the story mind is incomplete
**Score 5**: The story functions as a "Grand Argument Story" that investigates all significant approaches to its central problem; the protagonist's linear or holistic problem-solving style is challenged; multiple throughlines (main character, obstacle character, relationship, overall story) are present; the resolution provides "the most appropriate solution" because all alternatives have been explored and found wanting
**Source**: https://dramatica.com/articles/dramatica-and-the-grand-argument-story
**Notes**: Dramatica theory posits that every complete story is "a model of the mind's problem-solving process." The story mind "works holistically to bring many disparate considerations to bear on the problem at hand." This is a high-level criterion about structural completeness.

### Cliffhanger Distribution and Trust
**What it measures**: Whether chapter-ending hooks are varied and sustainable rather than relying on constant cliffhangers
**Assumed goal**: Zeigarnik-effect-informed chapter craft (Writers Psyche, Writers Helping Writers) — assumes sustainable chapter-ending tension requires variety (~60% hooks, ~40% resolutions); a pacing model for chapter-based commercial fiction
**Score 0**: Every chapter ends on a cliffhanger; many cliffhangers are "false" (resolved immediately in the next chapter's opening with no real consequence); the reader experiences fatigue from constant artificial tension spikes; the author's hooks have become predictable and untrustworthy; the story's credibility erodes because every crisis is a wolf-cry
**Score 5**: Approximately 60% of chapters end with a genuine hook or soft cliff; approximately 40% end with satisfying mini-resolutions or character breathing moments; the mix is purposeful — calmer endings make the tense ones more effective; hooks are honest (they promise real consequences and deliver); the reader trusts that when the author creates tension, it will pay off
**Source**: https://writerspsyche.com/zeigarnik-effect-in-fiction-avoiding-reader-fatigue-via-micro-loops/
**Notes**: Research shows relentless cliffhangers lead to "reader fatigue" causing the brain to emotionally disengage as a defense mechanism. "Fake cliffhangers are broken promises — you promise danger, then deliver 'just kidding,' and the reader learns not to trust your hooks."

### Structural Approach Appropriateness
**What it measures**: Whether the chosen structural model (three-act, five-act, circular, kishotenketsu, etc.) is appropriate for the story being told
**Assumed goal**: Narrative First's anti-formulaic position — assumes no single structural model is universal and the best structure emerges from the story's needs; a meta-criterion that evaluates whether the chosen framework fits rather than whether any specific framework was followed
**Score 0**: A rigid structural template has been applied regardless of whether it fits; the story contorts to hit arbitrary beats; the structure fights against the story's natural shape; alternatively, no recognizable structure exists at all and the story wanders without architecture
**Score 5**: The structural model is chosen (or emergent) based on the story's needs; the structure enhances rather than constrains the content; if the story follows a recognizable template, it does so because the template fits, not because the template was imposed; structural innovation is present where convention would be limiting; the reader can feel the architecture without being distracted by it
**Source**: https://narrativefirst.com/articles/not-everything-is-a-heros-journey
**Notes**: Narrative First argues that fans of specific structural paradigms "cherry pick which precepts fit well with their argument." The Hero's Journey criticism literature demonstrates that no single structural model is universal. The best structure for a story is the one that serves that specific story.

### Exposition Distribution
**What it measures**: Whether background information and world-building are delivered in appropriate doses at appropriate times
**Assumed goal**: BubbleCow's "exposition through action" principle — assumes background information should be woven into dramatic scenes rather than delivered in blocks; most critical for speculative fiction with heavy world-building, less so for literary realism where context is familiar
**Score 0**: Large exposition dumps front-load all background information; "as you know, Bob" dialogue delivers information unnaturally; backstory chapters halt the narrative; or critical information is never provided, leaving readers confused; exposition is delivered when convenient for the author rather than when the reader needs it
**Score 5**: Background is delivered in "bite sizes when stakes rise, not chapters early"; exposition is woven into action and dialogue through fact-dependent choices; no paragraph contains more than three sentences of pure information without dramatic context; critical world-building is revealed through character experience rather than narration; the reader learns what they need exactly when they need it
**Source**: https://bubblecow.com/blog/developmental-editing-checklist
**Notes**: The developmental editing checklist recommends halving "any paragraph with more than three sentences of pure information." The principle: exposition should be delivered through action and dialogue when the information matters to the character's immediate choices.

## Sentinels Found

### Turning Points at Default Intervals
**Detection**: Major structural beats occur at exactly 25%, 50%, 75%, and 100% with mechanical precision, with no variation or organic placement
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs trained on structural advice divide stories into exactly equal segments. Human writers hit approximately those marks but with organic variation — beats arrive slightly early or late based on the story's needs. Mechanically precise placement suggests template-following rather than story-responsive structuring.
**Design space cost**: Loses the organic feel of a story finding its own shape; creates a metronome-like predictability that attentive readers can detect
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2407.13248v1

### Premature Turning Point Placement
**Detection**: Major setbacks and climax moments occur significantly earlier than expected (e.g., climax at 70-80% rather than 88-95%), leaving the final quarter of the story with insufficient material
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Research directly shows that LLMs "incorrectly pace their storytelling" by introducing critical turning points prematurely. This is a measurable, documented LLM behavior pattern — major setbacks (TP4) and climax moments (TP5) consistently occur earlier than in human narratives.
**Design space cost**: Leaves insufficient narrative space for development and resolution; the story's final section either rushes or pads to fill the remaining space
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2407.13248v1

### Homogeneously Positive Arc
**Detection**: The narrative maintains consistently positive emotional valence throughout, with no significant setbacks, failures, or negative developments; the arc is "Rags to Riches" without meaningful adversity
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs exhibit severe bias toward positive narrative arcs. "Rags to Riches" comprises 13% of machine narratives vs. 4.4% of human stories. "Riches to Rags" appears in only 1.3% of LLM output vs. 14.6% of human stories. This positivity bias likely stems from RLHF training optimizing for user satisfaction.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates tragedy, dark comedy, cautionary tales, and morally complex narratives from the design space; prevents the meaningful setbacks that create reader investment
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2407.13248v1

### Flat Arousal/Tension Curve
**Detection**: Tension and emotional intensity remain at approximately the same level throughout the narrative, with no significant peaks or valleys; the story's "arousal" doesn't escalate toward the climax
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLM narratives maintain lower and flatter arousal levels compared to human stories, with the gap widening from the midpoint onward. Human stories escalate intensity toward the climax; LLM stories maintain a flat line. This creates "flat narratives without suspense."
**Design space cost**: Prevents the reading experience of building excitement; eliminates the emotional payoff that comes from escalating tension; removes the "peaks and valleys" that create satisfying pacing
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2407.13248v1

### Uniform Chapter/Scene Lengths
**Detection**: All chapters or scenes are approximately the same word count (within 10-15% of each other), regardless of content type or dramatic intensity
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs produce text of uniform length and complexity. Human writers naturally produce "bursty" text with chapters that vary based on dramatic need — short chapters for urgency, long chapters for depth. Uniform length is a strong signal of machine generation because it indicates the length was determined by token budget rather than narrative need.
**Design space cost**: Loses chapter length as a pacing tool; eliminates the "jump-cut quality" of short chapters and the "deep immersion" of long chapters; creates monotonous reading rhythm
**Source**: https://michellekassorla.substack.com/p/recognizing-ai-structures-in-writing

### Every Chapter Ends on a Cliffhanger
**Detection**: Every single chapter ending is a high-tension cliffhanger with no variation in ending type; no chapters end with quiet resolution or reflective satisfaction
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs, having been trained on advice about "hooking readers," default to maximum-tension chapter endings every time. Human writers understand that approximately 40% of chapters should end with calmer resolutions to prevent reader fatigue. The pattern of relentless cliffhangers signals a system that learned the rule "end on a hook" without learning the meta-rule about sustainable variation.
**Design space cost**: Creates reader exhaustion and diminishing returns; false cliffhangers erode reader trust; prevents the breathing room that makes genuine cliffhangers effective
**Source**: https://writerspsyche.com/zeigarnik-effect-in-fiction-avoiding-reader-fatigue-via-micro-loops/

### No Identifiable Midpoint Shift
**Detection**: The story's 50% mark passes without any identifiable change in protagonist mode, revelation, or narrative direction; the second quarter and third quarter feel identical in type
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs struggle with global narrative architecture — they generate locally coherent text without maintaining awareness of the overall arc. A missing midpoint shift suggests the model is generating scene-by-scene without a structural plan. The midpoint is particularly vulnerable because it requires the model to track the protagonist's overall trajectory and introduce a calculated reversal.
**Design space cost**: The second act sags; the protagonist remains in reactive mode indefinitely; there's no structural pivot to carry the narrative into its back half
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/the-midpoint-as-the-swivel-point-of-your-storys-linked-structure/

### Scene-Sequel Imbalance
**Detection**: The narrative consists entirely of action scenes with no reflective sequels, or entirely of internal processing with no external conflict scenes; no alternation between the two modes
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs tend toward either constant action (following "show don't tell" advice literally) or constant introspection (following character-depth advice). The scene-sequel alternation requires structural planning that balances two fundamentally different modes of narration. An all-action or all-reflection pattern suggests the model has one mode and stays in it.
**Design space cost**: All-action exhausts readers and prevents emotional investment; all-reflection bores readers and prevents forward momentum; the lack of alternation destroys pacing rhythm
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scene_and_sequel

### Subplots That Never Intersect the Main Plot
**Detection**: Secondary storylines proceed in complete isolation from the main plot; subplot characters never interact with main plot characters; subplot events have no causal relationship to main plot events; the subplots could be removed without any impact on the main story
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate subplots by creating parallel but disconnected narrative threads, because maintaining causal connections between multiple storylines requires global structural awareness that token-by-token generation struggles with. Human writers weave subplots to "pressure or illuminate the central story."
**Design space cost**: Wasted page count on structurally inert material; no thematic reinforcement; the novel reads as several short stories interleaved rather than one cohesive narrative
**Source**: https://www.writersdigest.com/write-better-fiction/7-ways-to-add-great-subplots-to-your-novel

### Exposition Front-Loading
**Detection**: The first 10-20% of the novel consists primarily of exposition, backstory, world-building, and character description with minimal dramatic action; the inciting incident is delayed while "everything you need to know" is delivered upfront
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs process prompts sequentially and tend to "establish everything first" before generating narrative events. This mirrors a common amateur writing pattern that LLMs replicate. Human writers learn to weave exposition into dramatic action; LLMs tend to dump it in blocks.
**Design space cost**: Readers disengage before the story begins; information delivered without dramatic context is poorly retained; the structural promise of an engaging story is broken before it's made
**Source**: https://bubblecow.com/blog/developmental-editing-checklist

### Three-Act Structure Applied as Rigid Template
**Detection**: The story rigidly follows a named structural template (Hero's Journey, Save the Cat, etc.) with every beat mechanically hit at exactly the prescribed percentage, regardless of whether the story's content calls for those specific beats
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs that have been trained on structural writing advice can reproduce template beats mechanically. The result is a story that "feels like it was written by an algorithm" (as critics of formulaic Hero's Journey applications note). The structural template is followed but the story within it doesn't require those specific turns at those specific moments.
**Design space cost**: Stories feel predictable to readers familiar with the template; structural beats serve the template rather than the story; unique stories are forced into conventional shapes; the story loses whatever organic structure it might naturally have had
**Source**: https://narrativefirst.com/articles/not-everything-is-a-heros-journey

### Repetitive Scene Structure
**Detection**: Every scene follows the exact same internal pattern — same length setup, same type of complication, same style of resolution; the scene-level structure is copy-pasted across the entire novel
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate scenes using the same internal template repeatedly. Human writers vary scene structure — some scenes are all buildup with no resolution, some start at maximum intensity, some are structured as reveals, some as reversals. Uniform scene structure indicates a single generation template being reused.
**Design space cost**: Creates predictable reading experience at the scene level; readers learn the pattern and mentally check out during setups; eliminates the surprise that comes from structural variety within scenes
**Source**: https://michellekassorla.substack.com/p/recognizing-ai-structures-in-writing

### Absence of Structural Silence
**Detection**: The narrative never slows down for quiet moments, reflection, or atmospheric pause; every scene is at full dramatic intensity with no valleys between peaks
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs trained on "keep it interesting" advice often generate constant high-intensity content. Human writers understand that "quiet doesn't mean no tension" and that valleys are structurally necessary — they allow emotional processing and make the peaks feel higher. A narrative at constant intensity is paradoxically less engaging than one with variation.
**Design space cost**: Reader exhaustion; emotional flattening (constant intensity becomes the new baseline); loss of the contrast that makes dramatic moments dramatic; no space for character interiority
**Source**: https://darlingaxe.com/blogs/news/pacing

### Unearned Resolution
**Detection**: The climax resolves through means never established in the story (deus ex machina, previously unknown abilities, convenient coincidences, or external forces unrelated to the protagonist's journey)
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generating endings face the challenge of resolving complex setups, and they often introduce new elements rather than paying off established ones. This happens because the model may not have maintained awareness of all the setup elements planted earlier. The resolution "sounds right" locally but doesn't connect to the global structure.
**Design space cost**: Destroys the satisfying feeling that "everything was planned"; violates reader trust; undermines the protagonist's agency and arc; makes the entire preceding narrative feel pointless
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/storys-climax-4/

### Chekhov's Guns Left Unfired
**Detection**: Elements prominently introduced early in the story (objects, skills, characters, information) are never used or referenced again; the narrative creates expectations it doesn't fulfill
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generating long narratives lose track of elements introduced earlier. A gun placed on the mantle in chapter 3 may be forgotten by chapter 30 because the model's attention to early details degrades over long generation spans. Human writers track setups in outlines or revision; LLMs have no such external memory.
**Design space cost**: Readers feel cheated; the story's apparent intentionality is revealed as accidental; carefully read details turn out to be noise; reader trust in the author's design erodes
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chekhov's_gun

### Sagging Middle with Repetitive Obstacles
**Detection**: The middle 50% of the novel features the same type of obstacle repeated three or more times without escalation; the protagonist faces functionally identical challenges that don't build toward anything
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generating the middle section of a long narrative often fall into a pattern of generating "another obstacle" without tracking the escalation requirement. Each obstacle may be well-written locally but functions identically to the previous one. This is the structural equivalent of the "repetitive themes" documented in LLM-generated fiction.
**Design space cost**: Reader disengagement in the crucial middle section; loss of narrative momentum; the protagonist's journey feels circular rather than progressive; the story's escalation architecture collapses
**Source**: https://inkshift.io/resources/saggy-middle

### No Structural Distinction Between Beginning, Middle, and End
**Detection**: The story's tone, intensity, pacing, and narrative mode are indistinguishable across its three major sections; the opening quarter reads the same as the closing quarter; there is no sense of structural movement
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate text at a consistent register — the model doesn't inherently know that beginnings should establish, middles should complicate, and endings should resolve. Without explicit structural planning, the model produces a stream of narrative events at a constant level. This is the macro manifestation of the "flat arousal curve" finding.
**Design space cost**: The story lacks the fundamental shape that creates satisfying narrative experience; readers can't feel progression; the story has no architecture, only length
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2407.13248v1

### Predictable Five-Paragraph-Essay Structure at Chapter Level
**Detection**: Each chapter follows the same organizational pattern: opening thesis/hook paragraph, three body paragraphs of development, closing paragraph that summarizes and transitions; the chapter structure mirrors academic essay organization
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs are heavily trained on expository text, and this organizational pattern bleeds into fictional prose. Real fiction chapters don't follow essay structure — they follow dramatic structure. The five-paragraph pattern is a strong signal that the model is applying its most deeply trained organizational instinct rather than narrative craft.
**Design space cost**: Fiction reads like a textbook; the story's voice is subsumed by institutional writing patterns; chapter-level surprise and variety are impossible within this rigid framework
**Source**: https://michellekassorla.substack.com/p/recognizing-ai-structures-in-writing

### Memory Loss Across Structural Spans
**Detection**: Plot threads, character details, or world-building elements established in early chapters are contradicted or forgotten in later chapters; the story's structural integrity degrades with length
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs have finite context windows and struggle with "memory, structure and long-form storytelling." Characters may forget their own established traits, plot threads may vanish, and the story's internal consistency degrades as the narrative extends. This is a documented limitation of token-by-token generation over long spans.
**Design space cost**: Breaks the illusion that the story was planned as a coherent whole; creates plot holes; undermines character consistency; makes the narrative feel generated rather than crafted
**Source**: https://www.tomsguide.com/ai/writing-a-novel-in-2026-heres-why-chatgpt-alone-wont-get-you-to-the-finish-line

### Symmetrical Resolution of All Subplots
**Detection**: Every subplot resolves at exactly the same time, in exactly the same way (all positive or all negative), in a neat convergence at the climax; no subplot resolves early or remains ambiguous
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generating endings tend to resolve everything simultaneously because "ending" is a single structural signal. Human writers stagger subplot resolutions — some resolve before the climax, some during, some after, and some are left intentionally open. Simultaneous resolution of all threads signals a model that received one "wrap up" instruction and executed it uniformly.
**Design space cost**: Feels artificial and too tidy; eliminates the texture of real narrative endings where some threads resolve while others linger; prevents the layered emotional experience of staggered closures
**Source**: https://www.well-storied.com/blog/the-dos-and-donts-of-crafting-subplots

### Prologue as Info-Dump
**Detection**: The story opens with a prologue that consists entirely of exposition, world-building, or backstory delivered in summary rather than scene; the prologue has no dramatic action, conflict, or character engagement
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs responding to "write a fantasy novel" often begin with extensive world-building prologues because their training data includes many such prologues. The model defaults to establishing context before generating narrative, mirroring its inherent process of "understanding the setting before telling the story." Human writers have learned that prologues must be dramatic scenes, not encyclopedia entries.
**Design space cost**: Readers skip the prologue (or the book); the story's first impression is of static information rather than dynamic narrative; the opening promise is broken before it's made
**Source**: https://reedsy.com/blog/guide/parts-of-a-book/prologue/

### Denouement as Theme Statement
**Detection**: The story ends with characters explicitly stating the theme or lesson learned, often in dialogue that reads as a summary of the story's meaning; "I guess what we learned is..."
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs, trained on stories where themes are identifiable, often make themes explicit in their endings. This mirrors the "terminal modifying phrases" pattern identified in AI writing — appending explanatory content at the end to ensure comprehension. Human writers trust readers to derive theme from structure; LLMs explain it.
**Design space cost**: Underestimates reader intelligence; converts subtext into text; transforms a resonant ending into a lecture; the story tells readers what to think rather than letting them feel it
**Source**: https://michellekassorla.substack.com/p/recognizing-ai-structures-in-writing
