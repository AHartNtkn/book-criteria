## Criteria Found

### Causal Chain Integrity
**What it measures**: Whether each plot event logically follows from preceding events through cause-and-effect rather than arbitrary sequence.
**Assumed goal**: Aristotelian dramatic structure (Poetics), which holds that plot is a chain of necessary or probable causes — not a sequence of incidents. Violated by picaresque and episodic fiction (e.g., Don Quixote) where loosely connected adventures are the form.
**Score 0**: Events occur in sequence without causal connection; "and then" plotting where scenes could be rearranged without affecting the story; no discernible reason one event leads to the next.
**Score 5**: Every plot beat functions as both effect of prior action and cause of subsequent action; the story forms an interlocking causal web where removing any event would require rewriting subsequent events; passes the "therefore/but" test at every beat transition.
**Source**: https://storygrid.com/cause-and-effect/
**Notes**: Story Grid identifies three components of causality: conditions (prerequisites enabling events), proximate causes (immediate triggers), and effects (results that must be clear to readers). E.M. Forster's foundational distinction: "The king died, and then the queen died" is story; "The king died, and then the queen died of grief" is plot.

### The But/Therefore Test
**What it measures**: Whether consecutive story beats are connected by causal ("therefore") or adversative ("but") connectors rather than merely additive ("and then") connectors.
**Assumed goal**: Parker and Stone's screenwriting principle (South Park writers' room) that every beat must be connected by "therefore" or "but," never "and then." Violated by slice-of-life, iyashikei, and vignette-based fiction where additive sequencing is the structural intent.
**Score 0**: Most beat transitions can only be connected with "and then"; events feel like a random series of incidents; no sense that prior events generate subsequent ones.
**Score 5**: Every beat transition can be naturally connected with "therefore" or "but"; the story builds momentum through reactive, causally linked events; removing the connective words still leaves the causal relationship obvious.
**Source**: https://nathanbweller.com/creators-of-south-park-storytelling-advice-but-therefore-rule/
**Notes**: From Trey Parker and Matt Stone: "If the words 'and then' fall between any of those beats, then you're fucked." The test works by mapping story beats on a whiteboard and checking whether the connector between each pair is causal. The distinction: "and then" implies independence; "therefore" highlights causality; "but" indicates an obstacle.

### Commensurate Cause and Effect
**What it measures**: Whether the magnitude and nature of causes match the magnitude and nature of their effects throughout the narrative.
**Assumed goal**: Wrede's realist plotting principle that cause magnitude must be proportional to effect magnitude within the story's own logic. Violated by absurdist fiction (e.g., Kafka, Ionesco) and butterfly-effect narratives where disproportionate causation is the premise.
**Score 0**: Tiny causes produce enormous effects without justification, or enormous causes produce negligible effects; character reactions are wildly disproportionate to stimuli; the story relies on butterfly-effect logic without establishing why.
**Score 5**: Every cause produces an effect whose magnitude is proportional and believable; character responses match the gravity of events; when small causes do produce large effects, the amplification mechanism is clearly established.
**Source**: https://pcwrede.com/pcw-wp/plots-and-causal-chains/
**Notes**: Wrede's example: stealing a dragon's egg justifies the dragon's rage; littering near the dragon's lair does not. Both plot-level and character-level causation must be commensurate. World-building factors into proportionality: if dragons lay hundreds of eggs, one missing egg shouldn't provoke the same response as losing a single precious offspring.

### Dual-Level Causal Logic (Plot + Character)
**What it measures**: Whether causal chains operate simultaneously on both the plot level (events follow from events) and the character level (motivations and psychology drive believable actions).
**Assumed goal**: Wrede's dual-level causal model, which requires both external plot logic and internal character psychology to independently justify every event. Violated by plot-driven genres (e.g., some action thrillers, procedurals) where characters serve the plot machinery rather than the reverse.
**Score 0**: Events happen for plot reasons but characters have no believable motivation for their actions, or characters have clear motivations that the plot ignores; one level works but the other is broken.
**Score 5**: Every plot event is both externally logical (it follows from prior events) and internally logical (the characters involved have believable reasons for acting as they do); breaking either chain would break the story.
**Source**: https://pcwrede.com/pcw-wp/plots-and-causal-chains/
**Notes**: Wrede emphasizes that a cowardly character stealing a dragon egg breaks the character-level chain even if the plot-level chain is intact. Both levels must function internally for the overall causal chain to hold.

### Protagonist Agency
**What it measures**: The degree to which the protagonist actively drives the plot through their own choices and actions rather than being passively carried along by events or other characters.
**Assumed goal**: Western protagonist-centered dramatic structure (Friedman, Swain, McKee), which requires the main character to be the causal engine of the plot. Violated by witness narratives (e.g., The Great Gatsby), ensemble fiction, and naturalist fiction where characters are subject to forces beyond their control.
**Score 0**: The protagonist is a passive observer, recipient, or victim; events would unfold identically without them; other characters or coincidences drive all major plot movements; the protagonist functions as a witness to the story rather than its engine.
**Score 5**: The protagonist initiates approximately 70% of major plot movements through deliberate choices; their decisions create consequences that generate further plot; they actively solve problems rather than having solutions delivered to them; the climax depends on their specific actions.
**Source**: https://janefriedman.com/who-makes-it-happen-giving-your-characters-agency/
**Notes**: Four passive character archetypes to avoid: The Witness (observing), The Bystander (present but non-essential), The Recipient (receiving benefits without effort), The Victim (suffering without seizing control). Diagnostic: "Would events unfold identically if your character weren't present?" The 70/30 ratio (protagonist drives 70%, external forces 30%) comes from Write With Seth.

### Consequence Persistence
**What it measures**: Whether actions and events produce lasting consequences that persist and accumulate throughout the narrative rather than being forgotten or reset.
**Assumed goal**: Consequentialist plotting (Story Grid, Mythcreants), which holds that actions must produce permanent, accumulating state changes — the "moral engine" of a story. Violated by episodic TV-style fiction, Groundhog Day structures, and certain comic traditions where reset-to-status-quo is a genre convention.
**Score 0**: Actions produce no lasting effects; characters suffer injuries that vanish, make decisions that are easily reversed, face crises that leave no mark; the status quo resets between scenes; consequences exist only within the scene where they occur.
**Score 5**: Every significant action produces consequences that ripple forward through the narrative; injuries affect later capabilities; decisions close off options permanently; the world state changes in ways that constrain future possibilities; characters carry forward what they gain and lose in earlier scenes.
**Source**: https://theeditorsblog.net/2016/03/22/making-plot-less-episodic-a-readers-question/
**Notes**: The Editor's Blog identifies this as the primary distinction between causally-connected and episodic plotting. The integration test: "Scenes should be so tied to other scenes, events, and characters that were you to remove a scene, you would need to do some serious rewriting to close the gaps you created." Mythcreants emphasizes that consequences form the "moral engine" of a story.

### Stakes Escalation
**What it measures**: Whether the potential consequences of failure increase progressively throughout the narrative, with each new complication raising the personal and/or public cost of failure.
**Assumed goal**: Freytag's pyramid and its modern descendants (Bransford, Darling Axe), which require continuously rising action toward a climactic peak. Violated by meditative fiction, pastoral fiction, and stories structured around emotional plateaus rather than escalation.
**Score 0**: Stakes remain flat or decrease over the course of the story; the protagonist faces equivalent-magnitude threats throughout; the climax has lower stakes than earlier scenes; there is no sense of "it's getting worse."
**Score 5**: Stakes escalate continuously from inciting incident to climax; consequences broaden (affecting more people/things) and deepen (affecting the protagonist more personally); the final consequences are the biggest; each new complication costs more than the prior one.
**Source**: https://darlingaxe.com/blogs/news/stakes
**Notes**: The Darling Axe identifies two escalation mechanisms: broadening (affecting more people) and deepening (affecting the character more personally). Nathan Bransford uses the Lord of the Rings example: Frodo's task escalates from finding a hiding place, to leaving town, to dropping the ring off with elves, to destroying it in Mordor. The escalation tracks reader understanding of what's at stake.

### Three Levels of Stakes
**What it measures**: Whether the story operates on public (external/plot), personal (emotional/relational), and philosophical (thematic/value) stakes simultaneously, with all three connected to the protagonist's central goal.
**Assumed goal**: ProWritingAid's three-stakes framework (public/personal/philosophical), which requires simultaneous external, relational, and thematic threat dimensions. Violated by genre fiction operating on a single stakes axis (e.g., pure action-adventure with only external stakes, or pure literary introspection with only philosophical stakes).
**Score 0**: Only one level of stakes operates; threats are purely external with no personal or thematic dimension, or personal with no external manifestation; the story provides no answer to "why should anyone care about this beyond the immediate plot?"
**Score 5**: All three levels are active, coherent, and connected to the same primary goal; public stakes drive plot significance, personal stakes drive character investment, philosophical stakes drive thematic meaning; each level has a clear outcome at resolution.
**Source**: https://prowritingaid.com/three-levels-of-stakes
**Notes**: ProWritingAid defines: public stakes are externally visible consequences affecting the most people; personal stakes involve the protagonist's feelings about a specific person; philosophical stakes involve a value or belief proven wrong if the protagonist fails. The three levels need not resolve identically; protagonists may win some while losing others.

### Progressive Complications
**What it measures**: Whether obstacles facing the protagonist escalate in difficulty, requiring increasing effort and resources, while remaining relevant to the protagonist's goal and varied in type.
**Assumed goal**: Story Grid's scene-level escalation model (Gilbo, Coyne), which requires each complication to be harder than the last and culminate in a turning point. Violated by fiction structured around lateral exploration rather than vertical escalation (e.g., Haruki Murakami's wander-and-discover narratives).
**Score 0**: Obstacles are static in difficulty, repetitive in type, or unrelated to the protagonist's goal; the protagonist faces the same kind of challenge over and over; complications decrease in intensity mid-story; obstacles don't require increasing effort to overcome.
**Score 5**: Each complication is harder than the last, directly threatens the protagonist's goal, requires greater effort and new approaches, combines internal and external conflict, and culminates in a turning point that forces abandonment of the original plan.
**Source**: https://www.savannahgilbo.com/blog/progressive-complications
**Notes**: Savannah Gilbo's five-step evaluation framework: (1) Identify scene goal, (2) List complications chronologically, (3) Rank by difficulty to verify escalation, (4) Identify the turning point, (5) Assess global story impact. Failure modes: non-escalating sequences break momentum; goal-unrelated complications confuse readers; repetitive obstacle types limit character development.

### Try-Fail Cycles
**What it measures**: Whether the protagonist makes multiple genuine attempts to achieve their goal, with each attempt producing meaningful consequences that change the situation before the next attempt.
**Assumed goal**: Genre fiction craft tradition (Sanderson, Kowal, Wendig), which requires protagonists to attempt and fail multiple times before succeeding, proving the resolution was earned through struggle. Violated by literary fiction where problems are endured or accepted rather than solved, and by tragedy where the protagonist never overcomes.
**Score 0**: The protagonist succeeds on the first attempt, or fails without trying, or makes attempts that don't change the situation; there is no sense of struggle or earned progress; success feels handed to the character.
**Score 5**: The protagonist makes multiple distinct attempts, each of which meaningfully changes the status quo; failures produce consequences and complications that inform the next attempt; success (when it comes) emerges from accumulated effort and learning; each cycle uses "yes, but" or "no, and" outcomes rather than clean success or clean failure.
**Source**: https://wordsdeferred.com/2022/01/03/the-try-fail-cycle/
**Notes**: Two key outcome types: "Yes, but..." (partial success with new complication) and "No, and..." (failure plus worsening situation). September C. Fawkes identifies limitations: the model can be too rigid for stories where goals change, and constant failure without progress creates stagnation. The distinction between consequences (situation changes, same goal) and complications (new goals emerge alongside original) is important.

### Setup and Payoff Balance
**What it measures**: Whether significant story elements introduced early (setups) are meaningfully resolved later (payoffs), and whether major payoffs are properly prepared for by earlier setups.
**Assumed goal**: Chekhov's gun principle and its modern codification (Weiland, Sanderson), which requires every significant element introduced to serve a later narrative purpose. Violated by naturalist fiction that includes non-functional detail for verisimilitude, and by Barthes' "reality effect" where purposeless details signal authenticity.
**Score 0**: Significant setups are introduced but never paid off (dangling threads, broken Chekhov's guns); major payoffs arrive without any prior setup (deus ex machina, pulled-from-nowhere solutions); the first half plants seeds that the second half ignores.
**Score 5**: Every significant setup produces a meaningful payoff; every major payoff is supported by sufficient prior setup; setups are disguised enough to create surprise but visible enough to create inevitability on re-read; the balance between setup and payoff creates the sense that the ending was "both surprising and inevitable."
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/setup-and-payoff-the-two-equally-important-halves-of-story-foreshadowing/
**Notes**: Five diagnostic tests from this source: The Inevitability Test (do readers think "I should have seen this coming"?), The Reward Test (does re-reading reveal the clues?), The Coherence Test (does the payoff feel connected?), The Patience Test (would readers abandon before reaching the payoff?), The Obviousness Test (can readers predict the exact outcome?). Excessive setup eliminates surprise; insufficient setup makes payoffs feel random.

### Foreshadowing Quality
**What it measures**: Whether narrative hints and clues are embedded with sufficient subtlety that they prepare readers for future events without telegraphing them, and whether they reward attentive readers on re-reading.
**Assumed goal**: The "surprising yet inevitable" standard from well-made-play tradition (Ibsen through modern craft pedagogy — Weiland, Sanderson, Bell), which requires hidden-in-plain-sight preparation for every major development. Violated by experimental fiction that withholds causality deliberately, and by some horror where inexplicable events are the point.
**Score 0**: No foreshadowing exists (events arrive from nowhere) or foreshadowing is so heavy-handed that every development is predictable; hints either don't exist or might as well be neon signs.
**Score 5**: Foreshadowing is present but disguised, appearing to serve immediate purposes while planting seeds for later events; multiple foreshadowing techniques are used for major payoffs; re-reading reveals a rich layer of preparation that was invisible on first pass; the balance creates "both surprising and inevitable" outcomes.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/setup-and-payoff-the-two-equally-important-halves-of-story-foreshadowing/
**Notes**: Types of foreshadowing include: object-based (Chekhov's gun), character behavior patterns (Steinbeck's mouse-to-puppy-to-human progression in Of Mice and Men), dialogue-based (stated intentions/warnings), and tone/atmosphere. The "1, 2, 3 punch" approach: use at least 2-3 different foreshadowing techniques for major payoffs. Disguising the plant so audiences think it serves a different purpose is key to effective misdirection.

### Coincidence Management
**What it measures**: Whether the story's use of coincidence follows the principle that coincidences may create problems for characters but must not solve them, and whether coincidences are limited in frequency and positioned appropriately.
**Assumed goal**: Pixar's Rule 19 and the broader fair-play plotting tradition (Weiland, Stanton), which holds that coincidences may create trouble but must never solve it. Violated by magical realist fiction (e.g., Garcia Marquez) where synchronicity and fate are structural forces, and by farce where lucky timing is a genre convention.
**Score 0**: The story relies on coincidences to resolve conflicts, rescue characters from danger, or deliver critical information at convenient moments; multiple coincidences stack up throughout; the climax depends on lucky timing or chance encounters.
**Score 5**: Coincidences appear only at the inciting incident or to create trouble; all resolution comes through character action and established causality; any remaining coincidences are acknowledged by characters, embedded in plausible context, or serve only as texture rather than plot hinges.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/coincidences-fiction-youre-wrong/
**Notes**: Pixar's Rule 19: "Coincidences to get characters into trouble are great; coincidences to get them out of it are cheating." The Literary Hub identifies seven conditions under which coincidence works: (1) nothing turns on it, (2) it starts the story, (3) community context makes it plausible, (4) it's buried in psychological complexity, (5) characters don't recognize it as coincidence, (6) the story operates in a farcical or skewed universe, (7) external events mirror internal emotional states.

### Deus Ex Machina Avoidance
**What it measures**: Whether the story's resolution emerges from established causal chains and protagonist action rather than from unexpected, unforeshadowed external intervention.
**Assumed goal**: Aristotelian dramatic resolution (the protagonist's own actions must resolve the conflict), codified in modern craft by Fawkes and McKee. Violated by classical Greek drama (where divine intervention is conventional), comedy, and metafiction where the device is deliberately deployed for ironic or parodic effect.
**Score 0**: The climactic conflict is resolved by a new character, force, or event that arrives without prior establishment; the protagonist plays no active role in their own salvation; the resolution breaks the story's internal logic; readers feel cheated because the outcome was "unexpected, unlikely, and unforeseen."
**Score 5**: The resolution emerges directly from the protagonist's choices and actions using tools, knowledge, and relationships established earlier in the narrative; the audience understood the mechanism of resolution before it occurs; the solution feels inevitable in retrospect.
**Source**: https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2026/01/what-is-a-deus-ex-machina-meaning-writing.html
**Notes**: Three key detection tests: (1) Does the resolution emerge from the established causal chain or appear coincidentally? (2) Did the audience understand the mechanism before it solved the conflict? (3) Did the protagonist's choices contribute to the resolution? September C. Fawkes identifies an acceptance timeline: coincidence is most tolerable at the beginning and least tolerable at the climax. Foreshadowing = preparation; the author's ability to solve conflict with any mechanism is directly proportional to how well the reader understands that mechanism.

### Reversal and Twist Quality
**What it measures**: Whether plot reversals and twists are earned through prior narrative groundwork, feel both surprising and retrospectively inevitable, and deepen rather than contradict the story.
**Assumed goal**: The well-made-plot tradition (Ibsen, Scribe, through modern thriller craft — Bell, Maass), which requires reversals to be both foreshadowed and transformative of the reader's understanding. Violated by pulp fiction that uses twists for shock alone, and by postmodern fiction where arbitrary reversal is a deliberate anti-narrative strategy.
**Score 0**: Twists contradict established facts without justification; reversals have no foreshadowing and feel arbitrary; twists exist for shock value alone and don't deepen understanding; the story "cheats" by withholding information the viewpoint character possesses.
**Score 5**: Reversals cause readers to reinterpret everything that came before in a new light; twists are foreshadowed through emotional cues and subtle details; the reversal feels both surprising and, in retrospect, inevitable; characters react to reversals with appropriate emotional weight rather than nonchalant acceptance.
**Source**: http://writeonsisters.com/writing-craft/the-reversal/
**Notes**: Key distinction: a plot twist surprises; a reversal turns the audience's understanding of the entire story on its head. Foreshadowing through emotion ("He never looks at her when she cries") is more effective than foreshadowing through information. Timing matters: midpoint reversals need to be precisely placed. The character response test: reversals must "throw the characters" -- if they nonchalantly accept the revelation, the reversal has failed.

### Resolution Quality
**What it measures**: Whether the story's ending fulfills the promises established at the beginning, resolves major plot threads, emerges from the protagonist's climactic choice, and carries appropriate consequences.
**Assumed goal**: Story Grid's resolution framework (Coyne) and the peak-end rule from behavioral psychology, which require endings to deliver on the story's controlling idea with concrete consequences. Violated by modernist open endings (e.g., Chekhov, Carver), postmodern anti-resolution, and literary fiction that privileges ambiguity over closure.
**Score 0**: The ending introduces new elements not established earlier; major plot threads are left unresolved without thematic purpose; the resolution feels rushed or disconnected from the climax; consequences evaporate; the ending contradicts the genre contract.
**Score 5**: The resolution directly flows from the protagonist's climactic choice; every major plot thread is resolved or its non-resolution serves clear thematic purpose; consequences of the crisis persist; the ending fulfills genre promises while still feeling fresh; falling action and denouement provide proper emotional landing.
**Source**: https://storygrid.com/a-beautiful-friendship-crafting-a-satisfying-ending-to-earn-your-book-a-spot-on-the-shelf/
**Notes**: The peak-end rule means endings have disproportionate weight in how readers remember stories. Story Grid frames resolution around: "How is that working out for you... and at what price?" The controlling idea must be clear: (1) which values triumph or fail, and (2) the reasoning behind outcomes. Common resolution patterns: legacy (protagonist sacrifices, impact shown on others), return (protagonist survives but bears lasting costs), new self (old identity acknowledged, growth reaffirmed).

### Promise-Progress-Payoff Alignment
**What it measures**: Whether the story makes clear promises (genre, tone, character, conflict) in its opening, provides a sense of meaningful progress throughout its middle, and delivers a payoff that is both surprising and fulfilling relative to those promises.
**Assumed goal**: Sanderson's Promise/Progress/Payoff framework (BYU lectures, 2025 guide), which structures narrative around establishing expectations, signposting advancement, and delivering surprising-but-fulfilling conclusions. Violated by anti-plot fiction (e.g., Beckett, Robbe-Grillet) that deliberately frustrates expectations, and by literary fiction that resists progress-oriented structures.
**Score 0**: The opening establishes expectations the story never fulfills; the middle provides no sense of forward movement (stagnant information, unchanged relationships, static conflict); the ending either delivers exactly what was predicted (no surprise) or something completely disconnected from the opening (no fulfillment).
**Score 5**: Four types of promises (tone, story, character/conflict, structural) are clearly established; progress is signposted through information revelation, relationship evolution, and character growth; the payoff is "a surprising but fulfilling answer to your promise" that balances expectation and surprise.
**Source**: https://www.brandonsanderson.com/blogs/blog/brandon-sandersons-2025-guide-to-plot-lecture-2
**Notes**: Sanderson identifies three types of progress: information-based (clues, mysteries), relationship-based (interaction evolution), and character-internal (belief questioning, growth). Signposts are critical -- "A big reason that people drop off a book is that there weren't enough signposts of progress." The Gandalf strategy: make it so dark that when the promised rescue arrives, readers have forgotten the promise, making fulfillment feel miraculous rather than expected.

### Scene-Level Causal Structure
**What it measures**: Whether each scene contains a complete goal-conflict-disaster/reaction-dilemma-decision structure that causally links to adjacent scenes.
**Assumed goal**: Dwight Swain's Scene-and-Sequel method (Techniques of the Selling Writer, 1965), which mandates that every scene contains goal-conflict-disaster and every sequel contains reaction-dilemma-decision. Violated by literary fiction that uses scenes for atmosphere, reflection, or character study without goal-oriented structure.
**Score 0**: Scenes lack clear goals, conflicts, or outcomes; scene transitions are arbitrary rather than causal; scenes resolve completely without generating consequences for the next scene; the motivation-reaction sequence is absent or disordered.
**Score 5**: Every scene has a clear character goal, meaningful conflict opposing it, and a disaster or setback that propels into the sequel; sequels contain emotional reaction, dilemma with no good options, and a decision that generates the next scene's goal; motivation-reaction units follow the proper physiological sequence (feeling, reflex, rational action).
**Source**: https://www.advancedfictionwriting.com/articles/writing-the-perfect-scene/
**Notes**: From Dwight Swain via Randy Ingermanson. "Winning is boring! When a Scene ends in victory, your reader feels no reason to turn the page." The reaction sequence must follow strict physiological order: feeling (milliseconds), reflex (instinctive), then rational action/speech (conscious). Presenting them out of order destroys realism. The Story Grid's Five Commandments (inciting incident, progressive complications, crisis, climax, resolution) provides a complementary evaluation framework at the scene level.

### Five Commandments Completeness
**What it measures**: Whether every story unit (scene, sequence, act, global narrative) contains all five commandments: inciting incident, turning point progressive complication, crisis, climax, and resolution.
**Assumed goal**: Story Grid methodology (Coyne), which requires every narrative unit from scene to global story to contain all five commandments (inciting incident, turning point, crisis, climax, resolution). Violated by experimental and literary fiction that deliberately omits structural elements, and by minimalist fiction where crises and climaxes are implied rather than dramatized.
**Score 0**: Story units are missing one or more commandments; scenes lack clear inciting incidents, crises are absent, climaxes don't answer the crisis question, or resolutions are skipped; the story has structural gaps that leave readers confused about what happened and why.
**Score 5**: Every scene contains all five commandments with clear causal connections between them; the crisis presents a genuine binary choice with meaningful stakes; the climax reveals character truth through active choice under pressure; the resolution shows concrete consequences; the same protagonist drives turning point through climax.
**Source**: https://storygrid.com/five-commandments-of-storytelling/
**Notes**: Crisis must be binary: either "best bad choice" (two horrible options) or "irreconcilable goods" (two desirable options). If more than two options exist or the choice is a no-brainer, it's not a real crisis. The climax must be the protagonist's active answer, not a passive acceptance or narrator summary. The five commandments apply at every structural level from beats to global narrative.

### Reader-Writer Contract Fulfillment
**What it measures**: Whether the story establishes clear expectations through its opening (genre, tone, plot type, character, POV) and delivers on those expectations through resolution.
**Assumed goal**: Genre fiction contract theory (Cowley, Weiland, Cron), which holds that opening pages establish a binding promise about what kind of experience the reader will have. Violated by genre-bending fiction that deliberately subverts expectations (e.g., Cormac McCarthy's Blood Meridian subverting the Western), and by literary fiction that resists genre classification entirely.
**Score 0**: The opening establishes one type of story but the ending delivers another; genre conventions are violated without preparation; tonal shifts occur without warning; the story breaks its own implicit promises about what kind of experience the reader will have.
**Score 5**: The contract is established within the first 10-20% of the story; all major shifts are prepared for with early groundwork; the resolution fulfills both large promises (genre outcomes) and small promises (specific foreshadowed details); genre conventions are honored, innovated upon, or deliberately subverted with adequate preparation.
**Source**: https://www.katherinecowley.com/jawl/reader-writer-contract/
**Notes**: The contract covers five dimensions: genre, plot, character, style, and point of view. Katherine Cowley's analogy: "If you order a vegetarian meal, the meal better not have chicken in it." Helping Writers Become Authors identifies eight specific promises: every conflict ends with appropriate climax, characters behave within established personalities, significant foreshadowing gets paid off, important early characters don't disappear, cause and effect remain appropriate, protagonists play active climax roles, not every scene plays predictably, and genre conventions are honored.

### Narrative Momentum
**What it measures**: Whether the story maintains a sense of continuous forward movement that pulls readers through the narrative with increasing force.
**Assumed goal**: Commercial fiction pacing doctrine (Maass, Bell, Greenlight Coverage), which requires continuous forward propulsion through unresolved questions and accumulating pressure. Violated by contemplative literary fiction (e.g., Sebald, Woolf) where momentum is deliberately slowed or suspended for reflection, and by ambient/atmospheric fiction where stasis is the aesthetic.
**Score 0**: The story stalls repeatedly; scenes don't generate forward momentum; subplots dissipate energy rather than building it; the middle sags with no sense of advancement; readers feel no compulsion to continue.
**Score 5**: Each scene ends with a hook or unresolved question that propels into the next; the domino-chain of cause and effect creates accelerating momentum; pacing alternates between tension and release while maintaining overall forward drive; unresolved problems pile up rather than resolving completely, creating cumulative pressure toward the climax.
**Source**: https://glcoverage.com/2024/08/02/narrative-momentum/
**Notes**: The Editor's Blog identifies the key anti-momentum pattern: scenes that "resolve all issues within themselves" with "nothing to propel characters and readers forward." Techniques for maintaining momentum: leaving questions unanswered at scene ends, characters carrying forward gains and losses, multiple connecting threads between scenes, emotional continuity across scene boundaries, piling up problems rather than resolving them sequentially.

### Subplot Integration
**What it measures**: Whether subplots connect to and serve the main plot by advancing it, reflecting it, or complicating it, rather than existing as disconnected narrative threads.
**Assumed goal**: Developmental editing orthodoxy (BubbleCow, MasterClass), which requires every subplot to advance, reflect, or complicate the main plot — if it doesn't pressure the central story, it gets cut. Violated by maximalist fiction (e.g., Pynchon, Dickens) where digressions and parallel threads serve texture and scope rather than plot economy.
**Score 0**: Subplots run parallel to the main plot without ever intersecting; secondary storylines consume narrative time without contributing to the central conflict or theme; removing a subplot would have no effect on the main story; subplots resolve after the main plot.
**Score 5**: Every subplot connects to the main plot by advancing, reflecting, or complicating it; subplot resolution influences main plot outcomes; subplots deepen theme and character; secondary storylines resolve before or at the main climax; each subplot thread either pressures the central story or earns its space through thematic contribution.
**Source**: https://www.masterclass.com/articles/how-to-add-subplots-to-your-story
**Notes**: BubbleCow's developmental editing principle: "each subplot thread pressures the central story or gets cut -- if the love subplot never complicates the heist, the thread steals time without paying rent." Subplots need their own narrative arc but on a smaller scale. Good subplots integrate to the point of being inextricable from the bigger picture.

### Internal Consistency / World Logic
**What it measures**: Whether the story's established rules (physical, magical, social, technological) remain consistent throughout and whether violations are identified and justified.
**Assumed goal**: Sanderson's Laws of Magic (generalized to all world-building), which hold that a story's established rules form a contract the author must honor. Violated by surrealist fiction (e.g., Borges, Calvino) where fluid or contradictory rules are the aesthetic, and by dream-logic narratives where inconsistency is structural.
**Score 0**: Established rules are violated without acknowledgment; magic systems work differently in different scenes for plot convenience; characters possess abilities in one scene that are forgotten in another; the world's own logic contradicts itself.
**Score 5**: Rules established early hold throughout; any apparent violation is acknowledged and explained within the world's logic; characters' capabilities remain consistent or change through clearly established mechanisms; readers can trust that the story's logic won't change to suit the author's convenience.
**Source**: https://www.tckpublishing.com/the-importance-of-consistency-in-writing/
**Notes**: "Every time you break your own logic without reason, you lose reader trust." Internal consistency facilitates suspension of disbelief by making everything line up. Fantasy thrives when the world feels structured. Violations jolt readers out of the story. The ArXiv consistency study found that timeline/plot logic and factual/detail consistency are the primary failure modes in LLM-generated long-form fiction.

### Literary Causation (Character-Driven)
**What it measures**: Whether character change over time serves as a causal engine, with psychological transformation driving subsequent decisions and events, even when external action-oriented causation is minimal.
**Assumed goal**: Literary fiction tradition (Friedman, citing Forster and James), which holds that psychological transformation is the primary causal engine — character change drives events, not the reverse. Violated by plot-driven genre fiction (e.g., techno-thrillers, procedurals) where external events drive characters, and by behaviorist fiction (e.g., Hemingway) where psychology is shown only through external action.
**Score 0**: Characters exist as static vehicles for plot events; no internal change drives external events; character psychology has no visible effect on what happens; the story would be identical if the protagonist were a different person with different psychology.
**Score 5**: Small but meaningful shifts in character drive the narrative forward; time and psychological transformation replace action sequences as causal engines; happenstance is used only insofar as it influences subsequent character decisions; character change is earned through prior events and visible in subsequent choices.
**Source**: https://janefriedman.com/quiet-connections-the-role-of-causation-and-plot-structure-in-literary-fiction/
**Notes**: Jane Friedman distinguishes genre fiction (direct, visible cause-effect chains) from literary fiction (subtle, indirect causation through character change over time). In literary fiction, the character arc IS the primary narrative. Red flags: happenstance presented without character consequence, plot beats disconnected from character evolution, nonlinear structure that obscures rather than illuminates.

### Irreversibility of Key Decisions
**What it measures**: Whether the protagonist faces genuine points of no return where significant decisions cannot be undone, creating permanent consequences that constrain future options.
**Assumed goal**: Three-act structure doctrine (Field, Snyder, Fawkes), which requires "points of no return" — especially at the ~25% mark — where the protagonist's commitment becomes permanent. Violated by picaresque fiction where the protagonist can always walk away, and by some literary fiction where the absence of commitment is the thematic subject.
**Score 0**: The protagonist can undo or walk back from any decision without cost; there are no permanent consequences; the story provides easy escape routes from every commitment; nothing the character does has lasting impact on their options.
**Score 5**: The story contains clear "doors of no return" where stepping through prevents reverting to the previous state; decisions entangle the protagonist more deeply; reversals come only at significant additional cost; crises force genuine, irreversible choices that shape all subsequent possibilities.
**Source**: https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2022/09/10-signs-your-plot-is-weak-and-how-to.html
**Notes**: Sign #9 from "10 Signs Your Plot is Weak": "It's easy to undo decisions or actions." This indicates stakes and ramifications aren't substantial enough. The protagonist must reach points where they cannot go back. Related to the PONR (point of no return) which typically occurs around the 25% mark, representing the protagonist's irreversible commitment to the journey.

### Cost of Victory
**What it measures**: Whether success and resolution come at a meaningful price that reflects the stakes and sacrifices established throughout the narrative.
**Assumed goal**: Story Grid's "at what price?" resolution doctrine (Coyne), which requires every victory to carry concrete costs proportional to the stakes. Violated by romance and comedy genres that conventionally deliver costless happy endings, and by wish-fulfillment fiction where unconditional reward is the reader contract.
**Score 0**: The protagonist achieves their goal without meaningful sacrifice; victory is total and costless; established stakes evaporate at resolution; the ending is "too tidy" given what was threatened; characters who should bear scars emerge unscathed.
**Score 5**: The resolution carries concrete consequences of the protagonist's choices; victory comes with genuine loss or transformation that reflects the story's stakes; the ending answers "at what price?" with specificity; consequences of the crisis decision persist through the resolution rather than evaporating.
**Source**: https://storygrid.com/a-beautiful-friendship-crafting-a-satisfying-ending-to-earn-your-book-a-spot-on-the-shelf/
**Notes**: Story Grid frames the resolution question as "How is that working out for you... and at what price?" Common patterns: legacy (protagonist sacrifices everything, impact on others shown), return (protagonist survives but bears lasting costs), new self (old identity acknowledged while growth is reaffirmed). Failure mode: "Eliminating established stakes without consequences."

### Conflict Escalation Variety
**What it measures**: Whether the story presents diverse types of conflict and obstacles rather than repeating the same pattern, and whether conflicts produce genuine consequences rather than recycling the same confrontation.
**Assumed goal**: Fawkes' anti-circling principle and Gilbo's progressive complication model, which require each conflict to be distinct in type and to produce lasting consequences that prevent repetition. Violated by fiction where cyclical conflict is thematically central (e.g., addiction narratives, abuse cycle depictions) and by serial fiction where recurring conflict patterns are a genre expectation.
**Score 0**: The same conflict recurs without resolution or progression; arguments happen repeatedly but lead nowhere; the protagonist faces identical types of obstacles throughout; "conflict without consequences is disguised filler."
**Score 5**: Each new conflict is distinct in type and approach; conflicts produce lasting consequences that alter the situation; the story never "circles" the same confrontation; obstacles combine internal and external dimensions; variety in conflict type enables varied character growth.
**Source**: https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2022/09/10-signs-your-plot-is-weak-and-how-to.html
**Notes**: Sign #3 from "10 Signs Your Plot is Weak": "The Story Keeps Circling the Same Conflicts." The fix: attach meaningful consequences to conflicts so that outcomes influence what happens next. Savannah Gilbo's framework adds: "If your character faces the same kinds of conflict over and over, he or she loses the opportunity to grow and change."

### Anti-Episodic Integration
**What it measures**: Whether scenes are causally connected to adjacent scenes through multiple threads (emotional, informational, physical, character) rather than functioning as self-contained episodes.
**Assumed goal**: The Editor's Blog integration model, which requires scenes to connect through multiple threads (emotional, informational, physical) such that removing any scene would require rewriting. Violated by short story cycles (e.g., Dubliners, Olive Kitteridge) and vignette-based fiction where scenes are deliberately self-contained units linked by theme rather than causality.
**Score 0**: Each scene functions as a self-contained ministory; problems introduced in one scene are fully resolved before the next begins; removing any scene would require no rewriting of subsequent scenes; the story reads as a collection of loosely linked vignettes.
**Score 5**: Scenes connect through multiple mechanisms: unresolved questions carry forward, objects link disparate moments, emotional continuity persists across scene breaks, characters reference prior events, problems accumulate rather than resolving completely, dialogue echoes across chapters; removing any scene would require significant rewriting.
**Source**: https://theeditorsblog.net/2016/03/22/making-plot-less-episodic-a-readers-question/
**Notes**: Nine linking techniques from The Editor's Blog: multiple thread strategy, chain reactions with variations, emotional continuity, dialogue/language echo, objects as connectors, character recurrence, knowledge carryover, multiplied problems, unresolved ending strategy. The integration test: scenes must be "so tied to other scenes, events, and characters that were you to remove a scene, you would need to do some serious rewriting to close the gaps you created."

### Antagonist Goal Clarity
**What it measures**: Whether the antagonistic force has a specific, understandable goal and plan that directly opposes the protagonist's goal, making the conflict measurable.
**Assumed goal**: Fawkes' oppositional goal model, which requires the antagonist's specific, concrete objective to directly and measurably oppose the protagonist's. Violated by literary fiction with diffuse or internal antagonism (e.g., stories about grief, ennui, existential crisis) and by man-vs.-nature or man-vs.-self narratives where there is no goal-bearing antagonist.
**Score 0**: The antagonist's intentions are unclear or undefined; the "bad guy" simply wants to be evil without a specific plan; readers can't measure antagonist progress; the antagonistic force is vague or abstract without concrete manifestation.
**Score 5**: The antagonist has a specific, concrete goal and plan that readers can understand; the antagonist's goal directly opposes the protagonist's goal such that both cannot succeed; antagonist progress is visible and measurable; the antagonist is formidable enough to justify the protagonist's costs and struggles.
**Source**: https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2022/09/10-signs-your-plot-is-weak-and-how-to.html
**Notes**: Sign #2 from "10 Signs Your Plot is Weak": "The Antagonist Has a Vague Master Plan." This weakness is easier to overlook because focus rests on the protagonist. Even if the protagonist doesn't know the full plan, characters should form conclusions about it (which may later prove wrong). Both protagonist and antagonist goals must be specific enough that both cannot succeed simultaneously.

### Red Herring vs. Broken Promise Distinction
**What it measures**: Whether misdirection in the story functions as fair play (red herrings that are later explained) rather than broken promises (setups that are simply abandoned or information that is withheld dishonestly).
**Assumed goal**: Fair-play mystery tradition (Knox's Decalogue, Van Dine's rules, extended to all fiction by Writing Mastery), which requires misdirection through attention redirection rather than information withholding. Violated by unreliable narrator fiction (e.g., Gone Girl) where deliberate dishonesty toward the reader is the technique, and by surrealist fiction where false leads never resolve.
**Score 0**: The story introduces elements that appear significant but are simply dropped; misdirection works by withholding information rather than directing attention; readers feel "the author lied to me" rather than "the author outsmarted me"; red herrings are never explained or resolved.
**Score 5**: Every red herring pays off by being revealed as false; misdirection gives extra information and redirects attention rather than withholding truth; false leads are cleared and explained; the revelation makes readers feel cleverly misled rather than cheated; even deliberately misleading elements serve the story's architecture.
**Source**: https://www.writingmastery.com/blog/what-is-a-red-herring-how-to-mislead-readers-without-cheating-them
**Notes**: Key distinction: "Misdirection is not about withholding information. It's about giving the reader extra information and focusing their attention on that instead of the truth." A well-crafted red herring does satisfy Chekhov's Gun: "its payoff is being revealed as false." The reader contract: readers agree to suspension of disbelief, trusting the author to guide them. The author uses that trust to mislead -- but must do so fairly.

### Idiot Plot Avoidance
**What it measures**: Whether the plot requires characters to act with believable intelligence and make decisions consistent with their established competence, or whether the plot only works because characters are unreasonably stupid.
**Assumed goal**: Blish/Ebert's idiot plot test, which holds that plots must not depend on characters behaving more stupidly than their established competence allows. Violated by comedy, satire, and farce where irrational behavior is the genre convention, and by tragedy where willful blindness or hubris is the thematic engine.
**Score 0**: The plot depends entirely on intelligent characters making persistently poor decisions without justification; a single smart question or obvious action by any character would resolve the central conflict; characters avoid sharing critical information, asking obvious questions, or collaborating for no believable reason.
**Score 5**: Characters act with intelligence consistent with their established traits; when characters make mistakes, they stem from believable limitations (stress, missing information, emotional conflict, youth); conflicts arise from genuine dilemmas rather than manufactured stupidity; readers never ask "why didn't they just...?" without the story providing a satisfying answer.
**Source**: https://pcwrede.com/pcw-wp/whats-an-idiot-plot/
**Notes**: The term was originated by James Blish and popularized by Roger Ebert. Detection: readers constantly asking "What were they thinking?" or "Why didn't they just...?" Two levels: standard idiot plot (main characters make persistent poor judgments) and second-order idiot plot (entire society must be incompetent for the story to work). Valid exception: comedy, parody, satire, and tragedy where irrationality is the point.

### Contrivance Avoidance
**What it measures**: Whether plot events emerge from character actions and established world logic rather than from authorial manipulation to force convenient outcomes.
**Assumed goal**: Character-driven plotting doctrine (Hardy, Beemgee), which holds that events must emerge from character motivation and established world logic rather than authorial convenience. Violated by fable and allegory where the author openly controls events for didactic purposes, and by some postmodern fiction where visible authorial manipulation is a deliberate technique.
**Score 0**: Events happen because the author needs them to, not because characters or world logic generate them; the protagonist is "incredibly lucky" with whatever needs to happen for the plot simply occurring; information arrives at precisely the moment it's needed without established reason; characters happen to be in exactly the right place at the right time.
**Score 5**: Every plot advancement emerges from character motivation, established world mechanics, or previously planted information; the protagonist earns every advantage through action; when characters converge, their independent motivations explain the convergence; nothing feels "forced" or "convenient."
**Source**: http://blog.janicehardy.com/2017/05/6-ways-to-identify-contrived-plot.html
**Notes**: Janice Hardy identifies the core problem: "the protagonist did nothing to earn it, so there's no conflict." Contrivances happen because "writers don't pay enough attention to the story and all its intertwining threads." The most common form: whatever needs to happen for the plot to move forward does, even if the protagonist doesn't do anything but show up. The Beemgee "Daenerys Syndrome": when character actions lack sufficient causal buildup, the turn feels contrived rather than inevitable.

### Nonlinear Structure Integrity
**What it measures**: Whether stories told out of chronological order maintain causal logic across both the actual timeline and the presentation order, with each structural choice serving a clear purpose.
**Assumed goal**: Friedman's dual-timeline integrity model for literary fiction, which requires nonlinear structure to maintain causal logic on both the actual timeline and the presentation order simultaneously. Violated by fragmented/collage fiction (e.g., Jenny Offill, Anne Carson) where the absence of reconstructible chronology is the aesthetic, and by dream-sequence narratives where temporal incoherence is structural.
**Score 0**: Nonlinear structure confuses rather than illuminates; readers cannot track when events actually occur; the presentation order has no thematic justification; flashbacks or time-jumps create rather than resolve confusion; hidden information retroactively contradicts what was previously established.
**Score 5**: The story maintains integrity on two tracks: the linear story (traditional arc) and the story-as-told (thematic connections justify presentation order); each revelation is causally consistent with what came before; nonlinear choices deepen understanding; readers can reconstruct the chronological timeline without encountering contradictions.
**Source**: https://janefriedman.com/quiet-connections-the-role-of-causation-and-plot-structure-in-literary-fiction/
**Notes**: Jane Friedman: nonlinear structure must maintain integrity on two tracks. "Each beat causes the next in that it causes you... to reveal it" -- the presentation order must have its own causal logic. The Beemgee dual-timeline test: verify that characters cannot perform actions contradicting their hidden roles or motivations, even if information is withheld from the audience.

### De-escalation Avoidance
**What it measures**: Whether the story maintains or increases tension through the climax rather than allowing stakes, threats, or consequences to diminish as the story progresses.
**Assumed goal**: Fawkes' "make good on threats" principle and Freytag's rising action requirement, which hold that promised consequences must materialize or be replaced by equally severe alternatives. Violated by cozy mystery, feel-good fiction, and some YA where softening consequences is part of the tonal contract with the reader.
**Score 0**: Big threats are made but consequences lessen or don't materialize as promised; stakes gradually diminish toward the climax; the author appears reluctant to follow through on established threats; the middle is more intense than the ending.
**Score 5**: Every threatened consequence either materializes or is replaced by an equally impactful alternative; tension consistently rises toward the climax; the story "makes good on threats"; the final consequences are the most severe; any deflection of a promised consequence introduces an equally weighty alternative.
**Source**: https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2022/09/10-signs-your-plot-is-weak-and-how-to.html
**Notes**: Sign #8 from "10 Signs Your Plot is Weak": "You Don't Make Good on Threats." The author warns against weakening stakes "due to reluctance to hurt characters." Character suffering creates meaningful arcs. Basic story structure demands escalation toward climax. If consequences change, new ones must be equally impactful.

### Protagonist Goal Specificity
**What it measures**: Whether the protagonist has a clear, concrete, measurable goal that drives the narrative and against which progress and setbacks can be evaluated.
**Assumed goal**: Fawkes' goal-driven plotting model, which requires the protagonist to have a concrete, measurable objective so that progress and setbacks are legible. Violated by literary fiction centered on drift, aimlessness, or existential paralysis (e.g., Oblomov, The Stranger) where the absence of a goal is the thematic subject.
**Score 0**: The protagonist has no discernible goal, or their wants are vague and unrelated to the plot, or they aren't actively pursuing anything; readers cannot measure progress because there's nothing to measure against; the character drifts through events without striving.
**Score 5**: The protagonist has a specific, concrete objective that is actively pursued; the goal is relevant to the main plot; antagonistic forces directly oppose it; readers can clearly evaluate whether the protagonist is getting closer to or further from their goal at any point; goals may evolve across acts but remain defined.
**Source**: https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2022/09/10-signs-your-plot-is-weak-and-how-to.html
**Notes**: Sign #1 from "10 Signs Your Plot is Weak": "The Protagonist Lacks a Specific Goal." Without a defined goal, audiences cannot measure progress or setbacks. "Nothing matters because the character isn't striving for anything specific." Goals need not be lofty -- different character types pursue different objectives -- but they must be concrete and actively pursued.

### Pixar Causal Spine
**What it measures**: Whether the story follows a causal spine structure where each event generates the next through explicit causation: "Once upon a time... Every day... One day... Because of that... Because of that... Until finally..."
**Assumed goal**: Pixar's Rule 4 causal spine template ("Once upon a time... Because of that... Because of that... Until finally..."), which enforces strict event-to-event causation. Violated by associative or thematic narrative structures (e.g., essay-novels, lyric novels) where connection between segments is conceptual rather than causal.
**Score 0**: The story events cannot be mapped onto a causal spine; events happen independently of each other; no "because of that" connector can logically link consecutive events; the story reads as a list of things that happen rather than a chain of consequences.
**Score 5**: The entire story can be mapped onto the causal spine with each "because of that" connector feeling natural and inevitable; the disruption of status quo triggers a chain of causally linked events that build to a climax; every event is both a consequence of the prior event and a cause of the next.
**Source**: https://www.aerogrammestudio.com/2013/03/07/pixars-22-rules-of-storytelling/
**Notes**: Pixar Rule #4: "Once upon a time there was ___. Every day, ___. One day ___. Because of that, ___. Because of that, ___. Until finally ___." This template enforces causal structure by requiring each step to flow from the previous one through an explicit "because of that" connector. The power is in the multiple repetitions of "because of that" -- each iteration builds on accumulated causality.

## Sentinels Found

### And-Then Plotting
**Detection**: Test every major beat transition by inserting "therefore" or "but" between them. If the only connector that works is "and then," the story fails the but/therefore test. A story where more than ~25% of beat transitions are "and then" connections exhibits this sentinel.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate text by predicting the next likely token based on statistical patterns. This forward-prediction mechanism naturally produces sequential events without establishing causal connections between them. The default LLM output is additive ("and then this happened") rather than consequential ("because of this, that happened").
**Design space cost**: Excludes deliberately episodic or picaresque structures (e.g., Don Quixote, some slice-of-life narratives) where sequential, loosely-connected events are a genre feature rather than a flaw.
**Source**: https://nathanbweller.com/creators-of-south-park-storytelling-advice-but-therefore-rule/

### Deus Ex Machina Resolution
**Detection**: The climactic conflict is resolved by a character, force, object, or event that (1) was not established before the final ~20% of the narrative AND (2) the protagonist played no active role in bringing about. Both conditions must be true.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs struggle with long-range planning and often "invent" solutions at the point of resolution rather than preparing them through earlier narrative elements. The forward-generation architecture optimizes for local coherence, making it easy to write characters into corners and then manufacture convenient escapes. The NoCha benchmark found only 41.6% accuracy on global reasoning tasks requiring synthesis across entire narratives.
**Design space cost**: Excludes intentional use of deus ex machina for comedic, parodic, or metafictional purposes where the device is the point.
**Source**: https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2026/01/what-is-a-deus-ex-machina-meaning-writing.html

### Consequence-Free Action
**Detection**: The protagonist takes significant actions (fights, makes major decisions, suffers injuries, confronts antagonists) that produce no lasting effect on subsequent scenes. The status quo resets between scenes or chapters. Test: can you remove any given action scene and have the next scene play out identically?
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs have limited context windows and struggle to maintain state across long texts. Actions in one generation window may not persist into the next, creating a pattern where dramatic events have no downstream effects. The ArXiv consistency study found errors increase linearly with output length, with timeline/plot logic as a primary failure mode.
**Design space cost**: Excludes certain Groundhog Day-style narratives or absurdist fiction where consequence-free repetition is the structural premise.
**Source**: https://theeditorsblog.net/2016/03/22/making-plot-less-episodic-a-readers-question/

### Passive Protagonist
**Detection**: The protagonist initiates fewer than ~30% of major plot transitions; other characters, coincidences, or external events drive the story while the protagonist observes, receives, or suffers. Test: list all major plot transitions and mark who initiated each one.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs tend to generate "things happening to" characters rather than "characters making things happen" because reactive text (describing events and responses) is statistically more common in training data than proactive decision-making sequences. Research confirms LLMs fail at "tracking character intentions" and introduce "external motivations not established in the current plan."
**Design space cost**: Excludes deliberately passive or trapped protagonists (e.g., prison narratives, some horror) where limited agency is the thematic point.
**Source**: https://janefriedman.com/who-makes-it-happen-giving-your-characters-agency/

### Costless Victory
**Detection**: The protagonist achieves their primary goal at the climax without any meaningful sacrifice, loss, or lasting negative consequence. All threatened losses are averted. Everyone important survives unscathed. The world returns to a better-than-before status quo with no price paid.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLM training data and RLHF incentivize positive, resolved, and conflict-free outputs. Research found AI stories exhibit "homogeneously positive" affect with "flatter arousal curves and less narrative tension than human-written fiction." Models systematically "sanitise real-world conflicts" and favor "nostalgia and reconciliation" over genuine dramatic stakes.
**Design space cost**: Excludes certain comedic or feel-good genres where costless happy endings are the expected payoff.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2603.13545v1

### Homogeneous Plot Structure
**Detection**: The story follows a protagonist-returns-home-and-resolves-minor-conflict-through-reconnection-with-tradition pattern, or repeats common LLM plot archetypes: helper figure appears to guide lost protagonist, protagonist discovers hidden truth about their identity, community comes together to solve problem through teamwork.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Analysis of 11,800 AI stories found overwhelming conformity: "protagonists live in or return home to a small town and resolve minor conflict by reconnecting with tradition." Cross-model analysis identified "consistent repetition of specific names, locations, occupations, and themes" regardless of architectural differences. Human stories rarely generate repeated plot echoes even when prompted identically.
**Design space cost**: Excludes stories that deliberately use the return-home archetype (e.g., some literary fiction, pastoral narratives) but does so unfairly since many human-written stories also use this pattern intentionally.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2603.13545v1

### Plot Echo Repetition
**Detection**: Multiple distinct plot elements repeat across a story or across different generated stories with the same patterns (e.g., the same type of helper figure, the same type of landmark, the same type of resolution mechanism appearing in similar narrative positions).
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Research found that 50/100 GPT-4 continuations of the same Kafka text had the policeman give directions to "take the second left," 18/100 suggested "take the second right," and 16/100 mentioned a bakery. LLM stories showed "echo scores that increase as more of the previous plot is revealed." Human-written stories rarely generate repeated plot echoes.
**Design space cost**: Excludes stories that use deliberate repetition as a structural or thematic device (e.g., musical structures, variations on a theme).
**Source**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12415252/

### Abrupt Resolution Without Development
**Detection**: The story accelerates rapidly to resolution in the final ~10%, abandoning multiple established plot threads and resolving the central conflict in a fraction of the space used to establish it. The "drop ratio" (unresolved suspenseful elements) is conspicuously high.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLM stories exhibited 7-9x higher "drop ratios" compared to human stories, indicating "abrupt resolutions that leave key suspenseful plot elements unresolved." The forward-generation architecture means models "rapidly accelerate over time without fully resolving the plot" as they approach token limits or feel pressure toward conclusion.
**Design space cost**: Excludes deliberately abrupt or ambiguous endings used for literary effect (e.g., some modernist or postmodernist fiction).
**Source**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12415252/

### Idiot Plot Dependency
**Detection**: The central conflict would be immediately resolved if any single character asked an obvious question, shared readily available information, or took a common-sense action. The plot depends on multiple supposedly competent characters simultaneously behaving stupidly without in-story justification.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs struggle with tracking what characters know and should logically do, often maintaining conflicts that a rational character would resolve immediately. Research confirms models fail at "tracking character intentions under closed-world assumption" and introduce "external motivations not established in the current plan."
**Design space cost**: Excludes comedy, satire, and tragedy where character irrationality is the deliberate thematic point; also excludes situations where characters' poor decisions stem from believable psychological states (stress, trauma, youth).
**Source**: https://pcwrede.com/pcw-wp/whats-an-idiot-plot/

### Flat Stakes (No Escalation)
**Detection**: The consequences threatened at the midpoint of the story are equivalent to or less severe than those threatened at the inciting incident. The "so what?" intensity does not increase from beginning to end. Test: list the worst-case scenario at 25%, 50%, and 75% through the story -- if they're equivalent or decreasing, the sentinel triggers.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate each passage based on local context without maintaining a global model of escalating tension. Without explicit architectural support for tracking stakes across the full narrative, models tend to produce flat tension curves. Research found AI stories have "flatter arousal curves" than human-written fiction.
**Design space cost**: Excludes meditative or contemplative fiction where escalation is deliberately avoided in favor of exploration or atmosphere.
**Source**: https://darlingaxe.com/blogs/news/stakes

### Contradictory Plot Logic
**Detection**: The story contains events that contradict its own established rules, timelines, or cause-and-effect chains. A character knows something they shouldn't, forgets something established, possesses an ability in one scene that vanishes in another, or the timeline contains impossibilities.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: The ArXiv consistency study found five major error categories with 19 subtypes, with timeline/plot logic and factual/detail consistency as the primary failure modes. Error counts increase approximately linearly with output length. Error-bearing segments exhibit 19.24% higher token entropy, indicating model uncertainty at the point of failure.
**Design space cost**: Minimal -- internal consistency is a near-universal requirement. Excludes deliberate unreliable narration or surrealist fiction where contradictions are structural.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2603.05890

### Unearned Reversal
**Detection**: A plot twist or reversal occurs that contradicts established facts without retroactive justification, has no foreshadowing or emotional groundwork, and characters accept it without appropriate reaction. The twist exists for shock value without deepening understanding of the story.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs can generate surprising plot elements by sampling from low-probability tokens, but cannot ensure those elements are supported by earlier narrative. Forward-generation makes it easy to produce "unexpected" events but hard to produce events that are "both surprising and retrospectively inevitable." Models lack the ability to dynamically revise importance weights of earlier narrative details.
**Design space cost**: Minimal -- unearned reversals are almost universally considered a craft failure. The only exclusion is deliberate subversion of narrative expectations for metafictional purposes.
**Source**: http://writeonsisters.com/writing-craft/the-reversal/

### Dangling Setups
**Detection**: A specific element (object, character trait, piece of information, relationship dynamic) is emphasized or introduced with apparent significance in the first half of the story but receives no payoff, resolution, or further mention in the second half.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate text forward without tracking what has been "promised" to the reader. Setups may be introduced because they are statistically likely in a given narrative context, but the model has no mechanism to track unfulfilled promises and ensure they are resolved. Research confirms models fail on "evidence synthesis from non-contiguous narrative parts."
**Design space cost**: Excludes stories that deliberately leave certain elements unresolved for thematic effect (e.g., literary ambiguity, series installments planting seeds for future books).
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/setup-and-payoff-the-two-equally-important-halves-of-story-foreshadowing/

### Convenient Information Arrival
**Detection**: Characters receive critical information (a clue, a warning, a revelation, a tool) at precisely the moment they need it, through a mechanism that was not previously established and that they did nothing to seek out or earn.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs resolve narrative problems by introducing solutions at the point of need rather than planting them earlier. The forward-generation architecture means the model encounters a problem and immediately generates a solution without the ability to retroactively plant that solution's origin in earlier text.
**Design space cost**: Excludes genres where information delivery is a convention (e.g., detective fiction where clues emerge through investigation, even if the timing is tight).
**Source**: http://blog.janicehardy.com/2017/05/6-ways-to-identify-contrived-plot.html

### Narrative Derailment
**Detection**: The story suddenly introduces illogical events, off-topic content, or tonal shifts that are inconsistent with the established narrative and have no causal connection to prior events.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Users of LLM storytelling apps report narratives that "go off the rails without warning" with "bizarre or nonsensical story turns, especially during lengthy sessions." This correlates with context window limitations and the model's tendency to hallucinate when prior context becomes distant.
**Design space cost**: Excludes deliberately surrealist or absurdist fiction where illogical events are the structural premise.
**Source**: https://cuckoo.network/blog/2025/04/17/negative-feedback-on-llm-powered-storytelling-and-roleplay-apps

### Emotional Architecture Collapse
**Detection**: The story maintains a homogeneously positive or neutral emotional tone throughout, with no significant variation in intensity, tension, or emotional valence between scenes. Arousal curves are flat rather than building.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Research found AI stories produce "homogeneously positive" affect with "flatter arousal curves and less narrative tension than human-written fiction." Safety training and RLHF suppress the kind of negative emotion, conflict, and genuine distress that creates narrative tension. Models "sanitise real-world conflicts" in favor of "nostalgia and reconciliation."
**Design space cost**: Excludes deliberately meditative, pastoral, or "cozy" fiction where emotional evenness is a genre feature.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2603.13545v1

### Purposeless Detail
**Detection**: Significant narrative details (objects, setting elements, character traits) appear without serving any function in the plot, character development, or thematic architecture. They could be "easily swapped out for any other [element] without losing any meaning."
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs select details based on statistical probability rather than narrative purpose. Research on AI fiction found details "feel randomly assembled rather than purposefully chosen" with elements appearing "without clear thematic purpose." Human writers select details with intentionality; LLMs generate likely details without evaluating their contribution.
**Design space cost**: Excludes literary fiction that uses Barthes' "reality effect" -- details that exist precisely to create verisimilitude without plot function. However, even reality-effect details serve a purpose (creating atmosphere); purposeless details serve no purpose at all.
**Source**: https://countercraft.substack.com/p/mfa-vs-llm-is-openais-metafiction

### Circling Conflicts
**Detection**: The same argument, confrontation, or obstacle type recurs multiple times without meaningful change in the situation. Characters have the same fight repeatedly. The story churns without advancing. Test: can you describe the conflict at page 50 and page 200 with the same sentence?
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs with limited context may regenerate similar conflict patterns because the statistical conditions for generating that type of conflict remain unchanged. Without a model of accumulated consequences, the same trigger produces the same type of scene repeatedly.
**Design space cost**: Excludes stories where cyclical conflict is the thematic point (e.g., depictions of abusive relationships, addiction narratives) where the repetition itself is meaningful.
**Source**: https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2022/09/10-signs-your-plot-is-weak-and-how-to.html

### Context Loss Contradiction
**Detection**: A character explicitly contradicts something they said, knew, or experienced earlier in the narrative without any in-story justification (not unreliable narration, not character growth, not deliberate deception).
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs have finite context windows and lose earlier narrative information as stories grow longer. Users report bots "frequently forget older info, causing character inconsistencies" and characters who "contradict themselves or fail to recall key plot points." Error-bearing segments show higher token entropy, confirming model uncertainty.
**Design space cost**: Minimal -- simple factual contradiction is almost never a deliberate artistic choice. Excludes deliberate unreliable narration where contradiction is the technique.
**Source**: https://cuckoo.network/blog/2025/04/17/negative-feedback-on-llm-powered-storytelling-and-roleplay-apps

### Missing Causality for Events
**Detection**: Significant plot events (character departures, relationship changes, status changes, deaths) occur without the narrative exploring or establishing why they happened. Events are stated as facts without causal connection to prior events.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Research on AI fiction found "no exploration of why events occur" -- when "Mila's visits became fewer," the narrative never examines whether this stems from boredom, grief resolution, or despair. LLMs state events without the causal reasoning that connects them to prior narrative, because forward-generation optimizes for the next plausible sentence rather than causal coherence.
**Design space cost**: Excludes minimalist or elliptical literary styles where gaps in causation are deliberate invitations for reader interpretation.
**Source**: https://countercraft.substack.com/p/mfa-vs-llm-is-openais-metafiction
