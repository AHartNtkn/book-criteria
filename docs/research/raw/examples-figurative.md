# Figurative Language: Prose Exemplars

Three passages from published fiction demonstrating fresh, coherent figurative language -- metaphors and similes that illuminate character and situation rather than decorating the surface.

---

## 1. Raymond Chandler, *Farewell, My Lovely* (1940)

> He was a big man but not more than six feet five inches tall and not wider than a beer truck. He was about ten feet away from me. His arms hung loose at his sides and a forgotten cigar smoked behind his enormous fingers. Slim quiet Negroes passed up and down the street and stared at him with darting side glances. He was worth looking at. He wore a shaggy borsalino hat, a rough gray sports coat with white golf balls on it for buttons, a brown shirt, a yellow tie, pleated gray flannel slacks and alligator shoes with white explosions on the toes. From his outer breast pocket cascaded a show handkerchief of the same brilliant yellow as his tie. There were a couple of colored feathers tucked into the band of his hat, but he didn't really need them. Even on Central Avenue, not the quietest dressed street in the world, he looked about as inconspicuous as a tarantula on a slice of angel food.

**What makes the figurative language work:** Every simile and metaphor in this passage does double duty -- it characterizes Moose Malloy and simultaneously establishes Marlowe's voice. "Not wider than a beer truck" is deadpan litotes that conveys enormity through understatement. "White golf balls on it for buttons" and "white explosions on the toes" are not similes in the grammatical sense but figurative substitutions that make the clothing grotesque and loud without any editorial adjective. The clincher -- "as inconspicuous as a tarantula on a slice of angel food" -- lands because it contrasts two sensory worlds (venomous and delicate, dark and white) in a single image, making the mismatch between Malloy and his surroundings visceral rather than stated. None of these figures decorate; each one builds the physical impression and the narrator's attitude simultaneously.

Source: Raymond Chandler, *Farewell, My Lovely* (Alfred A. Knopf, 1940), Chapter 1. Passage confirmed via [Goodreads quotes](https://www.goodreads.com/work/quotes/1263111-farewell-my-lovely), [A Boat Against the Current](http://boatagainstthecurrent.blogspot.com/2016/01/quote-of-day-raymond-chandler-on.html), and [Project Gutenberg Canada](http://gutenberg.ca/ebooks/chandlerr-farewellmylovely/chandlerr-farewellmylovely-00-e.html).

## 2. Denis Johnson, "Emergency," *Jesus' Son* (1992)

> We bumped softly down a hill toward an open field that seemed to be a military graveyard, filled with rows and rows of austere, identical markers over soldiers' graves. I'd never before come across this cemetery. On the farther side of the field, just beyond the curtains of snow, the sky was torn away and the angels were descending out of a brilliant blue summer, their huge faces streaked with light and full of pity. The sight of them cut through my heart and down the knuckles of my spine, and if there'd been anything in my bowels I would have messed my pants from fear.
>
> Georgie opened his arms and cried out, "It's the drive-in, man!"
>
> "The drive-in ...." I wasn't sure what these words meant.
>
> "They're showing movies in a fucking blizzard!" Georgie screamed.

**What makes the figurative language work:** The figurative language here is not ornamental comparison -- it is the narrator's drugged perception presented as literal experience. The drive-in speaker posts become "austere, identical markers over soldiers' graves." The movie screen becomes the sky "torn away" to reveal angels. Johnson does not write "the screen looked like the sky torn open"; the narrator sees it that way, and the metaphor is indistinguishable from hallucination. When Georgie's mundane explanation breaks the vision, the deflation is both comic and devastating. The figurative layer works because it reveals the narrator's state of consciousness -- a mind so chemically scrambled that it cannot distinguish projection from revelation -- rather than showing off the author's descriptive powers.

Source: Denis Johnson, "Emergency," *Jesus' Son* (Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 1992). Passage confirmed via [Riot Material](https://www.riotmaterial.com/denis-johnsons-emergency/), [Short Stories WTF (Substack)](https://shortstorieswtf.substack.com/p/emergency), and [Rob Reynolds](https://robreynolds.me/jesus-son-by-denis-johnson/).

## 3. Vladimir Nabokov, *Lolita* (1955)

> Lolita, light of my life, fire of my loins. My sin, my soul. Lo-lee-ta: the tip of the tongue taking a trip of three steps down the palate to tap, at three, on the teeth. Lo. Lee. Ta.
>
> She was Lo, plain Lo, in the morning, standing four feet ten in one sock. She was Lola in slacks. She was Dolly at school. She was Dolores on the dotted line. But in my arms she was always Lolita.

**What makes the figurative language work:** "Light of my life, fire of my loins" are conventional endearments, but Nabokov immediately estranges them by mapping the name onto the physical mouth -- "the tip of the tongue taking a trip of three steps down the palate to tap, at three, on the teeth." This is a metaphor of pronunciation as a tiny journey, and the alliterative t-sounds force the reader to physically enact it while reading. The second paragraph then deploys a different figurative strategy: each name is a metonym for a social context (school, legal documents, casual dress), and the catalogue structure makes the final "But in my arms she was always Lolita" land as both possessive claim and self-indictment. The figurative language does not describe how something looks; it maps the obsession onto the body of the reader (mouth, tongue, teeth) and the social world (morning, school, dotted line), making the narrator's fixation inescapable from the first sentence.

Source: Vladimir Nabokov, *Lolita* (Olympia Press, 1955; Putnam, 1958), opening paragraph. Passage confirmed via [Library of Congress sample text](https://catdir.loc.gov/catdir/samples/random042/88040511.html), [Penguin Random House Canada excerpt](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.ca/books/119445/lolita-by-vladimir-nabokov/9780679410430/excerpt), and [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lolita).
