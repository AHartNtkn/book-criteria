## Criteria Found

### Fictive Dream Continuity (Gardner)
**What it measures**: Whether the prose sustains an unbroken state of imaginative immersion where the reader "forgets the words" and sees characters moving through their world
**Assumed goal**: Literary realist tradition (Gardner's school) where the highest aim is a vivid, continuous dream — experimental fiction may intentionally fracture this dream for defamiliarization effects (Brecht, metafiction)
**Score 0**: The dream is constantly broken by abstract language, mechanical errors, faulty diction, unnecessary explanation, awkwardly inserted details, or inappropriate authorial attitude (frigidity or sentimentality). The reader is perpetually aware of the text as text and cannot enter the story world.
**Score 5**: The prose creates a sustained, vivid fictive dream through masterful language control, precisely described action, sensory specificity engaging all five senses, and a strong sense of place. Readers forget they are reading and experience events as if present.
**Source**: https://www.jordanmposs.com/blog/2022/7/30/notes-on-the-fictive-dream
**Notes**: Gardner: "Vivid detail is the life blood of fiction." Details provide "proofs" that events are genuinely happening. Gardner avoids rigid rules — what matters is whether techniques serve the fictive dream, not adherence to prescriptive formulas. The dream can be broken by: lack of concrete detail, abstract language, passive voice, weak sentence structures, distracting diction, unnecessary explanation, or "faults of soul" (inappropriate writer attitude).

### Narrative Transportation
**What it measures**: The degree to which readers experience psychological relocation into the story world through focused attention, emotional engagement, mental imagery, and cognitive detachment from physical reality
**Assumed goal**: Narrative psychology tradition (Green & Brock) where high transportation indicates successful story engagement — some avant-garde fiction may aim for alienation effects that resist transportation
**Score 0**: Readers remain fully anchored in physical reality, experiencing no emotional engagement, mental imagery, or attentional absorption. They critically evaluate each claim and feel no connection to the narrative world.
**Score 5**: Readers become fully transported — they lose awareness of surroundings, experience vivid mental imagery, feel strong emotions aligned with characters, and temporarily adopt narrative-congruent beliefs. The "sleeper effect" causes impact to strengthen over time rather than fade.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_theory_(psychology)
**Notes**: Green & Brock's 15-item scale evaluates: emotional involvement, cognitive attention, suspense, lack of awareness of surroundings, and mental imagery. Transportation encompasses seven features: focused attention, emotional engagement, mental imagery, cognitive detachment from reality, belief/attitude shift, reduced counterarguing, and long-term efficacy. Transportation differs from absorption (personality trait), flow (broader construct), immersion (primarily aesthetic/visual), and identification (character-focused). Strong narratives can "overcome any moderators" regardless of audience characteristics. Story-related moderators include character development quality, character intentions clarity, personal relevance, fiction vs. nonfiction status, and narrative perspective.

### Reading Flow State
**What it measures**: Whether the text produces an optimal reading experience characterized by absorption, smooth processing, temporal distortion, and intrinsic enjoyment
**Assumed goal**: Positive psychology / Csikszentmihalyi framework where flow represents the highest quality of subjective experience during reading — difficulty-oriented literary fiction may prize productive struggle over smooth flow
**Score 0**: Reading feels effortful, choppy, and laborious. The reader is constantly aware of processing difficulty, never loses track of time, and derives no intrinsic pleasure from the reading act itself.
**Score 5**: The reader experiences deep absorption, loses awareness of self and time, processes text smoothly and fluently with thoughts/emotions/images emerging spontaneously, and feels optimally challenged. Reading pleasure and motivation to continue are maximized.
**Source**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6300572/
**Notes**: The Reading Flow Short Scale (RFSS) measures two dimensions: Absorption (optimal challenge, time distortion, complete immersion, comprehension) and Smooth Processing (fluent reading, spontaneous imagery/emotion, narrative grasp, self-obliviousness). Flow peaks when text challenge perfectly matches reader skill level. Flow correlates strongly with reading pleasure (r=0.48), motivation to continue (r=0.53), suspense (r=0.71), presence (r=0.69), and identification (r=0.68). Mean global flow during 20-min fiction reading was 5.5/7, above midpoint — readers generally do experience flow.

### Empathic Character Connection
**What it measures**: Whether characters evoke empathic engagement through which readers simulate characters' mental states, emotions, and physical experiences
**Assumed goal**: Character-driven fiction tradition where reader-character empathy is the primary vehicle for emotional experience — plot-driven or idea-driven fiction may subordinate empathy to other engagement mechanisms
**Score 0**: Characters are opaque, flat, or repellent in ways that prevent any empathic connection. The reader cannot simulate their inner states, feels no emotional investment in outcomes, and would not care if any character ceased to exist.
**Score 5**: Characters evoke deep empathic engagement across cognitive and affective dimensions. Readers simulate characters' thoughts and feelings, experience neural activation in mirror neuron and mentalizing networks, and feel genuine concern, joy, fear, or grief on behalf of characters.
**Source**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3559433/
**Notes**: Research distinguishes cognitive empathy (understanding what characters think/feel) from affective empathy (feeling what characters feel). Fiction is uniquely suited to cognitive empathy through detailed moment-by-moment descriptions of inner thoughts and feelings. Effects demonstrate a "sleeper effect" — influence increases over a week rather than immediately after reading. Literary fiction particularly improves empathy because characters' minds are "depicted vaguely, forcing readers to fill in the gaps." Neurologically, fully engaged readers feel as if they have "actually become the characters, losing their sense of self-awareness."

### Character Identification
**What it measures**: Whether readers psychologically adopt characters' perspectives, goals, and mental states as a form of narrative transportation into first-person experience
**Assumed goal**: Immersive fiction tradition where deep identification with protagonists is the goal — literature that deliberately creates distance (Brechtian Verfremdung) may resist identification to provoke critical thought
**Score 0**: Readers feel no connection to any character's perspective. They observe events as distant spectators with no investment in character goals or outcomes. No dimension of identification (spatiotemporal, perceptual, cognitive, moral, emotional, embodied) is activated.
**Score 5**: Readers fully internalize the protagonist's viewpoint, goals, and mental states. They use the same "neural machinery" to think about the character that they use for self-reflection. Identification spans all six dimensions and readers feel genuine psychological relocation into the character's first-person perspective.
**Source**: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2017.01190/full
**Notes**: Six dimensions: spatiotemporal (sense of being in character's location), perceptual (seeing through character's eyes), cognitive (adopting character's expectations and intentions), moral (taking character's moral perspective), emotional (empathy for character), and embodied (physical simulation). Identification increases with perceived similarity between character and reader. Increased identification leads to greater suspense. Those with sufficient trait identification use the same neural machinery for characters and self.

### Emotional Mode Craft (Maass)
**What it measures**: Whether the text uses appropriate emotional craft modes — inner (reporting feelings), outer (showing through action), and other (creating reader emotions characters don't feel) — to produce genuine reader emotional response rather than merely labeling emotions
**Assumed goal**: Literary agent / commercial fiction tradition (Donald Maass) where the goal is making readers "feel deeply" and go on "an emotional journey of their own" — minimalist fiction may rely more heavily on outer mode and restraint
**Score 0**: Emotions are labeled with generic phrases ("her guts twisted in fear") that trigger no reader response. Only surface-level primary emotions are reported. No secondary or conflicting emotions explored. Characters feel exactly what readers expect in every situation.
**Score 5**: The text deploys all three modes masterfully. Inner mode explores surprising secondary emotions (excitement during horror, tenderness during danger). Outer mode allows readers to infer emotions from action and subtext. Other mode creates a dialogue where readers feel things characters don't — the reader goes on their own emotional journey distinct from characters'.
**Source**: https://janefriedman.com/produce-emotional-response/
**Notes**: Maass: the key question is not "how can I get across what characters are going through?" but "how can I get readers to go on an emotional journey of their own?" Generic emotional phrases fail because readers have encountered them repeatedly. Success requires emotional surprise through unexpected secondary feelings. Example: Bradbury's Fahrenheit 451 shows Montag feeling excitement during book-burning — not expected horror — forcing readers to confront their own responses. Third-level emotions: What does character feel? What else? What else again? Then create analogies, moral judgments, alternatives, justifications, and character-specific observations.

### Micro-Tension (Maass)
**What it measures**: Whether every page, paragraph, and line contains moment-by-moment tension arising from conflicting emotions, ideas, or expectations — not from plot action but from internal pressure
**Assumed goal**: Commercial and literary fiction craft tradition (Donald Maass) where "tension on every page" is the fundamental requirement for sustained reading — some contemplative or meditative fiction may deliberately create low-tension texture
**Score 0**: Pages contain no internal pressure. Characters experience single, predictable emotions. Dialogue proceeds without subtext. Exposition delivers information without emotional investment. Action is described in purely visual terms without emotional force. The reader can skim freely without losing anything.
**Score 5**: Every page contains conflicting emotions, ideas at war, questions posed before answers given. Dialogue tension comes from "inside those who are saying it." Exposition makes characters emotionally invested in the past. Setting description ties to POV character's inner tension. The reader cannot skim because every line carries pressure.
**Source**: https://writeswithtools.com/2020/03/02/microtension-what-is-it-and-how-do-we-get-it-onto-the-page/
**Notes**: Maass defines micro-tension as moment-by-moment suspense about "what will happen IN THE NEXT FEW SECONDS" rather than major plot events. It arises from conflicting emotions, not outer action. Sources: contradictory thoughts/words/actions, character doubt about future, misinterpreted emotions, uncertainty about present, negative past feelings mixed with positive present. Dialogue tension comes from differing moral codes, opinions, desires, perceptions. "Action, when related in strictly visual terms, feels flat. Emotions are needed to give action force." Poetry techniques include: phrasing for micro-hooks (anticipation before resolution), turning phrases (subverting reader expectations), rhythm-meaning juxtaposition (lighthearted rhythm with melancholy content).

### Suspense and Anticipation
**What it measures**: Whether the text creates and sustains psychological tension through information control, uncertainty about outcomes, and exploitation of the reader's need for narrative completion
**Assumed goal**: Thriller/mystery tradition where suspense is the dominant engagement mechanism — literary fiction may use more diffuse forms of uncertainty (existential, relational, epistemological) rather than plot-based suspense
**Score 0**: All outcomes are predictable. No information is withheld. No questions remain open. The reader experiences no uncertainty about what will happen next and no compulsion to continue reading.
**Score 5**: Suspense operates on multiple levels simultaneously — immediate (next scene), mid-range (chapter arc), and global (overall story question). The text exploits the Zeigarnik effect (unfinished tasks create psychological tension), uses dramatic irony, foreshadowing, ticking clocks, and information asymmetry. The reader's amygdala and dopamine-reward system are activated through contained, manageable anxiety.
**Source**: https://crimereads.com/the-psychology-of-suspense/
**Notes**: Suspense activates amygdala and adrenal glands — readers "micro-dose themselves with the same neurotransmitters that would be activated if an actual emergency was happening." Readers enjoy suspense because: (1) Control and safety — contained, manageable anxiety, (2) Narrative completion — the mind craves complete stories, (3) Cognitive engagement — solving mysteries provides competence, (4) Play and imagination — counteracts depression. The Zeigarnik Effect: people recall interrupted tasks better; humans naturally desire closure and deprivation triggers interest. Cliffhangers exploit this but lose effectiveness when overused (Law of Diminishing Returns).

### Curiosity Gap Management
**What it measures**: Whether the text creates and manages gaps between what readers know and what they want to know, using the information gap to sustain forward momentum without frustrating readers
**Assumed goal**: Engagement-oriented fiction where curiosity drives page-turning — literary fiction may create different kinds of gaps (thematic, philosophical, existential) rather than plot-information gaps
**Score 0**: Either no questions are raised (nothing to be curious about), or questions are raised and immediately answered (no gap), or gaps are sustained so long without any closure that readers disengage from frustration.
**Score 5**: Multiple curiosity gaps operate at different timescales — small mysteries introduced chapter-by-chapter and solved one or two chapters later, larger mysteries covering whole acts, and persistent mysteries spanning the entire book. Curiosity stimulates the brain's dopamine reward system. Gaps are filled as tension builds rather than stringing readers along indefinitely.
**Source**: https://mythcreants.com/blog/the-promise-and-peril-of-using-curiosity-in-stories/
**Notes**: George Loewenstein's Information Gap Theory (1994): the space between what readers know and what they don't creates a sense of deprivation and motivates readers to find the missing piece. Curiosity boosts engagement without drawbacks when readers should have less information anyway. But it shouldn't be relied on too much — instead of stringing readers along for the whole book, add a mystery hook and fill audiences in as tension builds, so readers remain engaged when curiosity fades. Curiosity stimulates dopamine release, making experience more enjoyable and memorable.

### Catharsis
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative builds to emotional release through accumulated investment in characters, producing the cleansing of emotions (pity, fear, grief, joy) that Aristotle identified as the purpose of dramatic art
**Assumed goal**: Aristotelian dramatic tradition where catharsis through pity and fear is the highest emotional achievement — stoic or nihilist fiction may deliberately withhold catharsis to leave readers in productive discomfort
**Score 0**: No emotional build-up occurs, so no release is possible. Characters undergo events without accumulated reader investment. Endings arrive without emotional weight. The reader closes the book feeling nothing.
**Score 5**: The narrative accumulates emotional investment through deep character identification, then delivers catharsis through reversal (peripeteia) and recognition (anagnorisis). Readers experience the cleansing of powerful emotions — pity for characters, fear for their fate — and emerge feeling they have been through something genuinely transformative.
**Source**: https://www.litcharts.com/literary-devices-and-terms/catharsis
**Notes**: Aristotle: tragedy arouses pity and fear "in such a way as to culminate in a cleansing of those passions." The most effective catharsis comes through plots combining reversal and recognition — e.g., Oedipus discovering his true parentage. Requires strong identification with characters: readers must see themselves in characters to achieve cathartic emotional investment. Empathy is the crucial prerequisite. Research shows cathartic experiences through literature can increase empathy and emotional intelligence.

### Emotional Arc Shape
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative follows a discernible and effective emotional trajectory — a shape of fortune and misfortune that creates reader satisfaction through its pattern of change
**Assumed goal**: Data-driven storytelling tradition (Vonnegut, Reagan et al.) where identifiable emotional arc shapes correlate with reader engagement and commercial success — experimental fiction may deliberately create illegible or flat arcs
**Score 0**: The emotional valence is flat throughout — no meaningful rises or falls in fortune, no pattern of change, no emotional trajectory. Or the arc is incoherent, lurching randomly without building toward anything.
**Score 5**: The story follows a clear, compelling emotional arc with meaningful rises and falls that build toward satisfying culmination. The arc shape (rags-to-riches, man-in-a-hole, Cinderella, Icarus, Oedipus, or complex combinations) creates emotional resonance through its pattern of change, with the most powerful arcs combining hope, loss, and redemption.
**Source**: https://cdanfort.w3.uvm.edu/research/2016-reagan-epj.pdf
**Notes**: Reagan et al. analyzed 1,700+ stories via sentiment analysis and found six basic emotional arc shapes: rags-to-riches (steady rise), tragedy (steady fall), man-in-a-hole (fall then rise), Icarus (rise then fall), Cinderella (rise-fall-rise), Oedipus (fall-rise-fall). The most successful arcs are Icarus, Oedipus, and man-in-a-hole. The Cinderella arc "generates the deepest reader satisfaction because it combines hope, loss, and redemption." Emotional arcs work through "emotional contagion" — mirror neurons fire in sympathy. Vonnegut originally theorized these shapes in his rejected master's thesis.

### Stakes and Reader Investment
**What it measures**: Whether characters have something meaningful to lose and whether the text makes readers care about those potential losses through deeply personal, specific, and universal stakes
**Assumed goal**: Broadly applicable fiction tradition where reader investment depends on caring about what characters stand to lose — nihilist or anti-narrative fiction may deliberately deplete stakes to explore emptiness
**Score 0**: Nothing meaningful is at risk. Characters pursue goals with no sense of what failure would cost. Stakes are abstract, generic, or disconnected from character identity. The reader has no emotional investment in any outcome.
**Score 5**: Stakes operate at three levels simultaneously: external (plot-level consequences), personal (relationship and identity costs), and philosophical (thematic meaning). Stakes are deeply specific to individual characters yet tap universal human experiences. Conflicting desires create Sol Stein's "crucible" — characters cannot simultaneously achieve competing goals they desperately want.
**Source**: https://tiffanyyatesmartin.medium.com/high-stakes-fiction-making-your-reader-care-2130e00c8611
**Notes**: Stakes are "how important something is, how much the character (and thus the reader) cares." The fundamental rule: "If you want your reader to invest, the character must want or be in danger of losing something he or she cares enormously about." Three levels: public (drives plot), personal (drives character), philosophical (drives theme). Rather than adding "exciting stuff," focus on "adding more meaning." Make strongest possible choices: if a character leaves a relationship, strengthen motivation by having them leave despite desperately loving their partner. Even modest events create deep investment when characters profoundly care and readers profoundly care about characters.

### Scene vs. Summary Balance
**What it measures**: Whether the text uses an appropriate balance of in-scene writing (real-time dramatic action) and summary/narrative telling to sustain continuous reader engagement
**Assumed goal**: Contemporary realist fiction tradition where scenes are the primary engagement unit — modernist stream-of-consciousness or essayistic fiction may favor different balances
**Score 0**: The text is predominantly summary or exposition with no scenes, creating distance and preventing immersion. Or it is all scene with no variation, exhausting the reader. The balance does not serve reader engagement.
**Score 5**: Scenes carry the primary emotional and dramatic weight through real-time action, dialogue, and sensory detail. Summary is used strategically for transitions, time passage, and necessary-but-not-central information. The balance creates continuous engagement through alternation of immersive experience and efficient narration.
**Source**: https://www.storyinliteraryfiction.com/essays-on-writing/writing-in-scene/
**Notes**: In-scene writing creates immediacy and reader participation by showing action as it unfolds. 15 competencies identified: direct viewing with present-tense feeling, strategic tense switching, sensory detail attribution rooted in character, strong verbs, crafted dialogue, pinpoint imagery ("fewest pieces" suggesting the whole), timeline adherence, progressive tenses, momentum through specific verbs and concrete nouns, effective sentence construction, idea management, clear antecedents, minimal stage direction, and balance of scene/summary. "Every story needs a carefully considered balance" of both elements.

### Show vs. Tell Effectiveness
**What it measures**: Whether the text renders experience through concrete sensory detail, action, and implication rather than asserting emotional or thematic content for the reader to accept
**Assumed goal**: Immersive fiction tradition where "showing" creates the strongest reader engagement — lyrical or essayistic fiction may deliberately "tell" with style and insight as a legitimate aesthetic choice
**Score 0**: Emotions, character traits, and thematic content are exclusively told through direct assertion. The reader is informed about characters' feelings rather than experiencing them through action, dialogue, sensory detail, or behavior. No implication or inference is required.
**Score 5**: The text transmits experience rather than asserting information. Readers feel they experienced events alongside characters through vivid imagery, descriptive verbs, immersive detail, action, dialogue, and interior experience. Important emotional moments, character development scenes, and plot turning points are shown. The reader "forgets that they're reading."
**Source**: https://writers.com/show-dont-tell-writing
**Notes**: "Rather than asserting something for the reader to accept, your writing transmits something for the reader to experience." Showing reveals information "through action, dialogue, sensory details, behavior, or interior experience, in ways that invite the reader to feel and deduce rather than just receive." Use showing for important emotional moments, character development, and turning points. Use telling for transitions, time passage, and necessary-but-not-central information. Balance is key.

### Sensory Embodiment
**What it measures**: Whether the prose engages multiple senses to create visceral, embodied reader experience through specific, concrete sensory detail rooted in character perspective
**Assumed goal**: Immersive fiction tradition where multi-sensory detail creates the deepest reader transportation — minimalist fiction may achieve powerful effect through selective, sparse sensory anchoring
**Score 0**: Description is exclusively visual and generic. No tactile, olfactory, auditory, or gustatory detail is present. The reader cannot physically feel the story world. Sensory descriptions are cliches ("gentle breeze," "blooming flowers") that convey nothing specific.
**Score 5**: All five senses are engaged strategically. Sensory details are specific, surprising, and rooted in the POV character's experience. Tactile details create embodied immersion. Smell triggers visceral memory responses. The specificity of sensory language maximizes visceral reader reaction. Neuroscience confirms readers' brains respond to sensory language as if experiencing the described sensations.
**Source**: https://rivereditor.com/guides/how-to-write-sensory-details-that-immerse-readers-2026
**Notes**: Studies show brains respond to sensory language as if experiencing described sensations. Smell is closely tied to memory, making even brief scent descriptions powerfully evocative. Tactile details may be most immersive because they're most physical. The more specific the sensory language, the more visceral the reader's reaction. Sensory details should be rooted in character perspective, not narrator's distant observation. The goal: "taking readers out of their world and putting them fully into yours."

### Subtext and Implication
**What it measures**: Whether the text communicates meaning through what is unsaid, implied, or beneath the surface — through loaded silences, contradictory actions, and layers of meaning that require reader interpretation
**Assumed goal**: Literary fiction tradition influenced by Hemingway's Iceberg Theory where deeper meanings live below the surface — genre fiction may use more explicit communication while still employing dialogue subtext
**Score 0**: Everything is stated explicitly. Characters say exactly what they mean. No hidden layers of meaning exist. The reader is never required to interpret, infer, or "read between the lines." The text is entirely surface.
**Score 5**: Subtext permeates dialogue, action, and description. Characters talk past each other, pursue hidden agendas, and communicate through what they don't say. Recurring motifs carry deepening significance. The reader actively participates in meaning-making by connecting dots and interpreting implications. The text rewards re-reading.
**Source**: https://www.writersdigest.com/write-better-fiction/how-to-use-subtext-and-the-art-of-dramatic-tension-in-fiction
**Notes**: Hemingway's Iceberg Theory: deeper meanings should live below the surface, like the bulk of an iceberg beneath water. Subtext draws readers in "by providing just enough information that forces them to connect the dots and arrive at conclusions on their own." Techniques: subtle gestures, loaded silences, recurring motifs, dialogue where characters speak past each other. Research from 2025 shows dialogue with strong subtext increases reader engagement by 35%. "Poignancy lies in the space between characters' lines."

### Narrative Voice Distinctiveness
**What it measures**: Whether the prose has a recognizable, authentic voice that builds emotional bonds and turns passive reading into active engagement
**Assumed goal**: Fiction tradition across genres where distinctive voice is the soul of a story — pastiche or multi-voiced fiction may deliberately shift voice for structural effect
**Score 0**: The prose has no identifiable personality or perspective. It reads as generic, interchangeable with any other text. No distinctive word choices, rhythms, observations, or attitude distinguish it. It could have been written by anyone or no one.
**Score 5**: The voice is immediately recognizable and authentic — distinctive word choices, sentence rhythms, observations, and attitude create a unique personality on the page. The voice builds emotional bonds with readers, making them feel they are in the presence of a specific, compelling intelligence. The voice lends authenticity that makes characters and events feel real.
**Source**: https://spines.com/narrative-voice-development-crafting-a-unique-style/
**Notes**: Voice encompasses tone, word choice, perspective, and style. "A strong voice builds emotional bonds, inviting readers to invest emotionally and turning passive reading into an engaging journey." Authenticity is critical: "the authenticity of a voice makes characters compelling and relatable, even if the reader does not necessarily agree with their actions." Distinctive voice is a common reason readers continue or DNF books — narration "can make or break fiction."

### Dramatic Irony
**What it measures**: Whether the text strategically places readers in a position of superior knowledge relative to characters, creating engagement through anticipation, cognitive dissonance, and emotional tension
**Assumed goal**: Fiction tradition from Sophocles to modern thrillers where information asymmetry between reader and character generates suspense — some fiction deliberately keeps readers in equal or lesser knowledge positions
**Score 0**: Readers and characters always have identical information. No information asymmetry exists. There is no gap between what the reader knows and what characters know, and therefore no anticipatory tension.
**Score 5**: The text strategically reveals information to readers that characters lack, creating sustained dramatic irony. Readers actively anticipate consequences, feel the danger characters don't see, and experience cognitive dissonance that drives engagement. The gap between reader and character knowledge generates suspense, superior understanding, and emotional investment in every scene.
**Source**: https://thewritepractice.com/dramatic-irony/
**Notes**: Dramatic irony puts readers in "a position of superior understanding, knowing more than the unwitting character does." This creates a form of cognitive dissonance — "our brains are constantly trying to resolve this discrepancy, keeping us engaged." The reader feels "like active participants in the story rather than passive observers." Superior knowledge "pumps up the suspense in every scene because you see the danger the character doesn't."

### Surprise and Misdirection
**What it measures**: Whether the text delivers plot developments that are simultaneously unexpected and retrospectively inevitable — surprising the reader while making them admire the craft of the setup
**Assumed goal**: Fiction tradition where surprise deepens rather than cheapens emotional experience — literary fiction may aim for subtler surprises of insight and perspective rather than plot twists
**Score 0**: All developments are predictable. The reader is never surprised. Or surprises are cheap shocks with no foreshadowing — they feel arbitrary rather than earned. The reader feels either bored or cheated.
**Score 5**: The text employs masterful misdirection — establishing expectations through red herrings, character assumptions, and structural cues, then delivering revelations that recontextualize everything. Surprises deepen emotional resonance and invite re-reading. Good misdirection makes readers "look back at various telltale clues peppered throughout the story, hopefully leading them to admire the author's skill."
**Source**: https://www.writersdigest.com/write-better-fiction/misdirection-is-the-secret-sauce-of-plot-twists-and-surprise-endings
**Notes**: J.K. Rowling: "Misdirection is the key to a good story." Techniques include: character-based misdirection (hidden motives, faulty memories, ambiguous speech), red herrings, environmental elements, the false/penultimate ending. Subtlety is critical — readers feel manipulated without sufficient hints. The dual narrative structure: construct two conflicting story versions sharing same characters and settings, weave them together subtly. The best twists "don't just surprise — they deepen the emotional resonance of the story."

### Opening Hook
**What it measures**: Whether the first pages create sufficient engagement — through conflict, curiosity, voice, character connection, or in medias res action — to compel continued reading
**Assumed goal**: Commercial fiction tradition where the first page determines whether readers continue — literary fiction may use slower openings that build engagement through voice, imagery, or thematic promise
**Score 0**: The opening generates no questions, no tension, no character connection, and no reason to continue. It begins with lengthy exposition, generic description, or world-building that provides no emotional or narrative hook.
**Score 5**: The opening immediately engages through conflict, curiosity, emotional connection, distinctive voice, or plunging into action (in medias res). Questions are raised. A character's emotional state or predicament draws the reader in through empathy. The reader feels compelled to continue within the first page.
**Source**: https://writerfulbooks.com/hooking-readers-from-the-first-line-how-to-craft-a-strong-opening/
**Notes**: The first page needs a point of conflict with tension. Successful openings generate questions, create emotional connections, or establish distinctive voice. In medias res "plunges readers right into the center of the action." Emotional connection: showing a character's intense emotional response taps into empathy. Be wary of long descriptive passages that don't generate questions. Opening with dialogue reveals conflict, intrigue, and relationship dynamics. Momentum must be sustained — even if initial conflict resolves, forward propulsion into the rest of the novel is required.

### Chapter-Level Forward Momentum
**What it measures**: Whether chapter endings and beginnings create sufficient forward momentum — through unresolved questions, cliffhangers, character reactions, or escalation — to sustain reading across structural breaks
**Assumed goal**: Page-turner tradition where readers should feel compelled at every chapter boundary — literary fiction may use chapter breaks for thematic resonance, reflection, or tonal shift rather than cliffhanger compulsion
**Score 0**: Chapters end at natural stopping points with all questions resolved. The reader has no reason to begin the next chapter. Every chapter boundary is a convenient place to put the book down.
**Score 5**: Chapter endings create forward momentum through varied techniques — unresolved questions, cliffhangers, revelations, emotional reactions that demand exploration, or escalation of stakes. The reader cannot stop at chapter boundaries. Multiple layers of mystery operate at different timescales.
**Source**: https://rivereditor.com/guides/how-to-write-chapter-endings-that-make-readers-keep-reading-2026
**Notes**: Unanswered questions are the primary driver of chapter-to-chapter compulsion. Authors can "button on character" — returning to protagonist for emotional reaction before scene ends, getting resonance while providing surprise. Constant cliffhangers exhaust readers (Law of Diminishing Returns). Dan Brown: cliffhangers "pose big questions at the end of a chapter," stopping midway through action rather than at natural conclusions. Varied hooks matter more than pure shock value.

### Reader-Writer Contract Fulfillment
**What it measures**: Whether the text establishes narrative promises through its opening, genre, foreshadowing, and characterization — and whether it fulfills those promises with satisfying payoffs
**Assumed goal**: Broadly applicable fiction tradition where reader trust depends on promises kept — deconstructive or subversive fiction may deliberately violate contracts to make a point, but must establish new implicit contracts of their own
**Score 0**: Promises are made but broken. Foreshadowing leads nowhere. Significant characters disappear. Genre conventions are ignored without purposeful subversion. The climax is resolved by deus ex machina. The reader feels betrayed.
**Score 5**: Every promise is fulfilled or purposefully transformed. Foreshadowing pays off. Characters remain true to established personalities while surprising within those boundaries. The protagonist actively resolves the climax through foreshadowed means. Genre conventions are honored or meaningfully subverted. "The best payoffs recontextualize earlier events, making readers want to immediately reread."
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/8-promises-youre-making-to-readersand/
**Notes**: Eight promises: (1) conflict requires appropriate resolution, (2) characters stay true to personality, (3) foreshadowing demands payoff, (4) important characters cannot disappear, (5) cause and effect must align, (6) protagonists must actively resolve the climax, (7) provide surprises within expectations, (8) honor genre conventions. Genre itself is a contract: "romances require couples falling in love; action stories demand explosions." Breaking promises makes readers feel cheated or doubt the writer's competence.

### Pacing Rhythm and Variation
**What it measures**: Whether the text varies its pace dynamically — alternating between fast and slow, tension and release, action and reflection — to create an emotional rhythm that sustains engagement
**Assumed goal**: Broadly applicable fiction tradition where dynamic pacing mirrors a "heartbeat" pattern — some literary fiction may deliberately sustain a single pace for hypnotic or meditative effect
**Score 0**: The pace is monotonous — either relentlessly fast (exhausting the reader) or relentlessly slow (boring the reader). No variation exists. No alternation between tension and release. The reader either burns out or tunes out.
**Score 5**: Pacing varies dynamically with purpose. Fast-paced sections generate excitement and urgency through short sentences, quick scene shifts, and escalating stakes. Slower sections foster reflection, emotional connection, and character development. The alternation creates a rhythm of tension and release that mirrors a heartbeat. Resolution timing is precisely calibrated — neither rushed nor prolonged.
**Source**: https://www.writersdigest.com/pacing-for-emotional-impact-in-fiction-building-tension-and-release-in-a-novel
**Notes**: Micro-pacing controls "how a reader feels in real time" while macro-pacing guides "the marathon of your plot." Every scene should have its own tension arc, even quiet moments — tension may come from what's not being said or from internal struggle. Sentence architecture: short sentences accelerate, longer constructions decelerate. Scene/chapter breaks create natural pauses and cliffhangers. Study method: read compelling works twice — "once for pleasure and once to try to analyze why the story worked so well."

### Scene-Sequel Structure (Bickham)
**What it measures**: Whether the text alternates between scenes (real-time dramatic action building to disaster) and sequels (emotional reaction, thought, decision) to create a continuous motivation-reaction cycle
**Assumed goal**: Commercial fiction craft tradition (Bickham/Swain) where scene-sequel rhythm is the fundamental unit of reader engagement — literary fiction may compress, expand, or rearrange sequel components
**Score 0**: No sequel reactions follow dramatic scenes. Characters act without processing. Or the text is all reaction/contemplation with no dramatic action. The motivation-reaction cycle is broken, leaving readers either overwhelmed or disconnected.
**Score 5**: Scenes build through goal-conflict-disaster, followed by sequels of emotion-thought-decision that bridge to the next scene. The author compresses, expands, or rearranges sequel components strategically. The emotional roller coaster of ups, downs, twists, and loops maintains continuous reader engagement.
**Source**: https://writershelpingwriters.net/2015/01/writing-patterns-fiction-scene-sequel/
**Notes**: Bickham's three sequel components: emotion, thought, decision. Without sequels, "the reader will be extremely dissatisfied with the story and probably won't finish it." Flexibility in scene-sequel use allows "an emotional roller coaster of ups, downs, twists, turns, and loops." The Motivation-Reaction-Unit is the fundamental building block: there is always an external force (motivation) causing a character to act (reaction). Authors can compress sequels as readers become more familiar with characters.

### Suspension of Disbelief
**What it measures**: Whether the text maintains the reader's voluntary acceptance of fictional premises by providing sufficient "human interest and semblance of truth" (Coleridge) to sustain immersion
**Assumed goal**: Fiction tradition from Coleridge onward where readers willingly suspend disbelief when fiction provides internal consistency and emotional truth — metafiction may deliberately expose the artifice
**Score 0**: The text contains so many implausibilities, contradictions, or violations of established rules that the reader cannot maintain belief in the fictional world. Internal logic is violated. Characters act in ways that defy both the story's rules and any recognizable psychology.
**Score 5**: The reader fully suspends disbelief, accepting even fantastical premises as real within the story. The text provides sufficient internal consistency, emotional authenticity, and "semblance of truth" that the fictional contract holds. "Poetic faith" is maintained through the narrative's value and coherence.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suspension_of_disbelief
**Notes**: Coleridge (1817): "that willing suspension of disbelief for the moment, which constitutes poetic faith." Suspension of disbelief is a voluntary act — readers know elements aren't real but allow themselves to believe temporarily for emotional and aesthetic impact. Neurally, the brain enters a "perceiving mode, engaging less intensely with the faculties for acting or planning to act." Key mechanism: infusing "human interest and a semblance of truth" into fantastical narratives. Internal inconsistency is the primary threat.

### Emotional Resonance and Memorability
**What it measures**: Whether the story creates lasting emotional impact that lingers in the reader's mind after finishing — the haunting quality that makes certain stories unforgettable
**Assumed goal**: Literary fiction tradition where lasting emotional impact is the ultimate measure of success — entertainment-focused fiction may prioritize in-the-moment pleasure over lingering resonance
**Score 0**: The reader closes the book and immediately forgets the experience. No emotions linger. No scenes or images recur in memory. The story made no lasting impression.
**Score 5**: The story haunts the reader. Specific scenes, images, and emotional moments recur in memory days and weeks later. The reader's understanding of characters and themes continues to deepen after finishing. Emotional resonance was achieved through subtlety rather than explicitness — "the power of what remains unsaid can be profound." The story changes how the reader thinks or feels about something.
**Source**: https://theregalwriter.com/2018/02/21/what-makes-a-story-memorable/
**Notes**: "Readers do not remember every plot point, but they do remember how characters made them feel." Key to memorability: "first you have to make readers fall in love — then you must break their hearts." Elements: memorable characters (goals, flaws, transformation, unique voices), emotional connection (if it doesn't evoke emotion, it won't be remembered), distinctive voice, unique setting, meaningful takeaway. The SUCCESs framework: Simple, Unexpected, Concrete, Credible, Emotional, Story. Subtlety over explicitness: conveying emotions indirectly through actions and unsaid words builds lasting resonance.

### Boredom Prevention
**What it measures**: Whether every scene progresses the narrative question, whether character interaction is maximized, whether information is delivered through discovery rather than dumps, and whether the reader always has a reason to keep reading
**Assumed goal**: Broadly applicable fiction tradition where boredom = failure — experimental fiction may deliberately slow or withhold to create productive discomfort, but even this requires a different form of engagement
**Score 0**: Scenes don't progress the narrative question. Characters avoid interaction and react in their heads. Information arrives in exposition dumps disconnected from story events. The reader has no motivation to continue. Reading feels like a task.
**Score 5**: Every scene significantly progresses the driving narrative question. Characters interact rather than ruminate in isolation. Details are revealed through discovery "just in time for the reader to understand what's going on." Pacing kicks into high gear by the midpoint. The reader always has both a question to answer and an emotional investment in finding the answer.
**Source**: https://wintersediting.com/eradicating-reader-boredom/
**Notes**: Key causes of boredom: scenes that don't progress the narrative question ("even dramatic events can feel blah when they don't contribute to the core plot"), excessive focus on themes/character development rather than plot events, characters avoiding each other while reacting internally, exposition dumps. "Character interaction is the single greatest antidote" for boredom. "The bored reader is the writer's worst nightmare; boredom indicates only apathy, whereas even anger at least indicates emotional involvement." If the writer is bored, the reader will be too.

### Unreliable Narrator Cognitive Engagement
**What it measures**: Whether an unreliable narrator creates productive cognitive engagement through discrepancies between narration and reality that the reader must actively resolve
**Assumed goal**: Literary fiction tradition (Booth, Nabokov, Ishiguro) where unreliable narration creates deeper engagement through active reader interpretation — straightforward narration may serve different engagement goals equally well
**Score 0**: The narrator is unreliable in ways that create only confusion, not engagement. The reader cannot determine what actually happened or has no basis for evaluating reliability. Or the narrator is straightforwardly reliable and the technique is not used.
**Score 5**: Unreliable narration creates an interactive reading experience where readers become active participants in deciphering reality. Discrepancies between narrator's statements and other textual evidence prompt critical thinking and close attention to detail. The gap between what the narrator says and what actually happened creates suspense, surprise, and deep cognitive engagement. Awareness of unreliability affects emotional investment through sustained uncertainty about truth.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unreliable_narrator
**Notes**: Wayne C. Booth coined the term in The Rhetoric of Fiction (1961). Ansgar Nunning's cognitive theory: unreliability rests on "the reader's values and her sense that a discrepancy exists between the narrator's statements and perceptions and other information given by the text." Engages readers "on a deeper cognitive level, prompting them to think critically and pay closer attention to details." The reader becomes "an active participant in the story, forced to engage in critical thinking and interpretation." This is an optional technique — not all fiction uses or needs unreliable narration.

## Sentinels Found

### Emotional Label-Dumping
**Detection**: Three or more emotion-labeling phrases per page (e.g., "a wave of relief washed over her," "his heart sank," "fear gripped her") without corresponding concrete action, gesture, or sensory detail that renders the emotion through showing
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs default to stating emotions rather than rendering them through action and detail. Models reach for "the abstract category when the specific instance would do the work." This is the "telling instead of showing" failure documented in Columbia University research on LLM creative writing.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates the entire outer mode and other mode of emotional craft (Maass). Readers who receive emotion labels instead of rendered experience cannot go on their own emotional journey. The text produces recognition of emotion categories rather than felt emotion.
**Source**: https://www.craftliterary.com/2025/03/26/show-dont-tell-what-ai-cant-do/

### Homogeneous Emotional Positivity
**Detection**: Sentiment analysis of the narrative shows a consistently positive valence with no sustained negative intervals, no genuine darkness, and no emotional texture — the story resolves all conflict through reconciliation, reconnection, and community
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLM-generated stories are "homogeneously positive and lack tension." RLHF alignment training prioritizes avoiding controversial content, producing "conflict-avoidant and sanitized narratives." Analysis of 11,800 AI stories found they "overwhelmingly conform to a single narrative plot structure" ending in reconnection with tradition and community.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates tragedy, catharsis, moral complexity, genuine stakes, and the full range of human emotional experience. Fiction that cannot sustain negative emotion cannot achieve the tension-release rhythm that drives reader engagement. The story becomes what reviewers describe as "eerie placidity" even during alarming events.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2603.13545v1

### Flat Event Escalation
**Detection**: Story events maintain a consistent intensity level throughout — no meaningful escalation of stakes, tension, or emotional pressure from beginning to middle to climax. The midpoint feels the same as the opening.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: StoryScope research specifically identified "notably flat event escalation" as a Claude signature. AI models struggle to build progressive intensity because each generation step treats the current context without a global sense of where the narrative needs to be going emotionally.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates the rising action that drives reader investment. Without escalation, there is no building anticipation, no sense of approaching climax, and no satisfaction in resolution. The "emotional arc" (Reagan et al.) requires meaningful rises and falls.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.03136

### Generic Sensory Description
**Detection**: Sensory descriptions consist of stock pairings ("gentle breeze," "warm sunlight," "the smell of fresh coffee," "birds singing") that could apply to any scene in any story, with no character-specific perspective or surprising specificity
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: AI's algorithm "remixes and regurgitates what has already been described" and produces predictable pairings because training favors common word combinations. Models strip specificity systematically — replacing "McCoy Tyner comping in fourths behind a Coltrane solo lasting eleven minutes" with "complex harmonies." This "semantic ablation" replaces precise vocabulary with near-synonyms carrying less information.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates the visceral embodiment that neuroscience shows triggers reader mirror neurons. Generic sensory detail fails to activate the brain's sensory processing regions. The specific, surprising detail that makes prose memorable has low probability in token prediction and is systematically avoided.
**Source**: https://betterthangood.xyz/blog/ai-writing-has-no-voice/

### Tidy Single-Track Plots
**Detection**: The narrative follows one protagonist through one conflict to one clean resolution, with no subplots, parallel threads, moral ambiguity in the resolution, or loose ends. The conflict resolves through a single mechanism (typically communication, reconciliation, or community action).
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: AI stories "over-explain themes and favor tidy, single-track plots" (StoryScope). The dominant AI narrative pattern is: "protagonist lives in or returns home to a small town and resolves a minor conflict by reconnecting with tradition and organising community events." Alignment training suppresses the kind of conflict and tension important for narrative.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates narrative complexity, thematic ambiguity, and the "both/and" nature of life that micro-tension depends on. Real fiction requires moral complexity where "human stories frame protagonist choices as more morally ambiguous" (StoryScope finding about human vs. AI fiction).
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.03136

### Characters Sound Identical
**Detection**: Remove all dialogue tags and attribution — if it is impossible to distinguish which character is speaking from voice, diction, syntax, and concerns alone, the characters share a single voice
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: "Generic AI generates dialogue for 'Character A' and 'Character B' — interchangeable voices with no history, no quirks, no relationship dynamics." By chapter twelve, "everyone starts merging into one voice." Models lack persistent character models and generate from the same token distribution regardless of which character is "speaking."
**Design space cost**: Eliminates the reader's sense of distinct personalities, kills dialogue subtext (which depends on characters having different agendas and communication styles), and prevents the character-driven identification that is the primary vehicle for emotional engagement.
**Source**: https://narrator.sh/blog/why-ai-fiction-sounds-robotic-fix

### Absence of Subtext
**Detection**: Characters say exactly what they mean. Dialogue answers questions directly. No loaded silences, no talking past each other, no hidden agendas, no gap between what characters say and what they actually want. If you can understand a conversation by reading only the dialogue with no action beats or context, subtext is absent.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: "AI is trained to generate phrase patterns; it's not trained to generate silence." Models cannot "be oblique or indirect, cannot let details speak for themselves." AI responds to imperatives by answering directly — the cooperative principle that makes chatbots useful makes fiction hollow.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates the Hemingway iceberg — the deeper meanings beneath the surface that make fiction resonate. Subtext increases reader engagement by 35% (2025 research). Without subtext, dialogue becomes information exchange rather than the compression of human relationship dynamics.
**Source**: https://www.craftliterary.com/2025/03/26/show-dont-tell-what-ai-cant-do/

### Dream Sequence or Flashback as Revelation
**Detection**: A dream sequence or extended flashback is used to convey crucial backstory, emotional state, or thematic content that should have been dramatized in present-time scene action
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: StoryScope research found GPT specifically "over-indexes on dream sequences." LLMs use dream sequences and flashbacks as crutches because these structures allow exposition and emotional content to be delivered without the difficulty of dramatizing it through present-time scene interaction.
**Design space cost**: Dream sequences and flashbacks break the temporal continuity of the fictive dream, interrupt forward momentum, and often deliver information that would be more engaging if revealed through character interaction and present-time discovery. They are a form of telling masquerading as showing.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.03136

### "Not X; Y" Sentence Pattern
**Detection**: Recurring use of the "Not X; Y" or "It was not X. It was Y" rhetorical construction — e.g., "The pull from the deep was no current. It was a summons."
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: This construction appears "constantly across AI outputs" as a signature pattern. Models overuse this structure because it creates an illusion of profundity through negation-then-assertion, and because it appeared frequently enough in training data to become a default pattern for emphasis.
**Design space cost**: When overused, this pattern becomes a recognizable tic that breaks the fictive dream. Instead of creating genuine revelation through dramatized discovery, it substitutes a rhetorical formula. The reader begins to notice the machine behind the prose.
**Source**: https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction

### Lists of Three Descriptors
**Detection**: Descriptions consistently cluster into triads — e.g., "a geometry of madness, a wound in the fabric of what is, older than law and time" — without variation in list length or structure
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: AI outputs cluster descriptors artificially in sets of three. This reflects the "rule of three" being overlearned from training data, producing a rhythmic predictability that human writers naturally vary.
**Design space cost**: Creates structural monotony that trained readers detect as mechanical. Real prose varies its descriptive rhythm — sometimes one precise detail, sometimes five sprawling ones, sometimes an absence. Enforced triads replace organic variety with formula.
**Source**: https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction

### Overwrought Purple Prose
**Detection**: Descriptions use multiple layered metaphors, excessive adjectives/adverbs, and ornate language for mundane scenes — e.g., "Her heaving bosom was constrained by the tight bodice of her gown, like two trembling doves longing to take flight"
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: "Modern AI tools generate purple prose because they are trained on predictive text patterns, often favoring flowery, and cliché adjectives." GPT-4o specifically uses more purple prose than other models. LLM texts are "typically full of redundant exposition, overwrought metaphors, and florid descriptions."
**Design space cost**: Purple prose "disrupts the narrative flow by attracting undue attention to its flamboyant style." It breaks the fictive dream by drawing attention to the writing itself rather than the story. Excessive emotion and cliches destroy the emotional credibility that creates genuine reader response.
**Source**: https://authors.ai/for-florid-purple-prose-just-ask-an-ai/

### Conflict Resolved Through Communication
**Detection**: The central conflict is resolved when characters simply talk about their feelings, communicate honestly, or have a heart-to-heart conversation that dissolves all tension — without requiring sacrifice, change, loss, or genuine difficult action
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: AI stories systematically "defuse conflict with unearned platitudes" and favor "nostalgia and reconciliation" over genuine dramatic resolution. RLHF-aligned models prioritize being "helpful, harmless, and honest," producing conflict-avoidant narratives where talking solves everything.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates the dramatic requirement for change, sacrifice, or genuine cost. If conflict can be resolved by communication alone, the stakes were never real. This prevents catharsis (which requires accumulated suffering), denies protagonists agency in the climax, and violates the reader-writer contract that promises meaningful resolution proportional to the conflict established.
**Source**: https://www.matthewjockers.net/2025/12/31/the-seductive-allure-of-generative-ai-for-storytelling/

### Low Entropy / High Predictability Prose
**Detection**: The prose uses exclusively common word choices, conventional syntax, and safe constructions. Every sentence follows the most probable word sequence. No surprising vocabulary, unusual phrasing, or distinctive construction appears anywhere.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: University of Mannheim research measured LLM story continuations having "two to four times lower entropy than human fiction, with the gap widening after RLHF." Models converge on "the statistically average way of expressing any given idea" because they have "no internal reason to prefer a specific or unusual expression over a generic one." RLHF annotators under time pressure prefer immediately clear text over material rewarding sustained reading.
**Design space cost**: Low entropy prose cannot create surprise, voice, or memorability — the unusual phrasing that makes prose memorable "has low probability and is systematically avoided." It produces what Barthes called "readerly" text (delivering passive meaning) rather than "writerly" text (inviting co-creation). Readers detect the absence of what the article calls "cost" — "no one is home."
**Source**: https://betterthangood.xyz/blog/ai-writing-has-no-voice/

### External Character Description Default
**Detection**: Characters are introduced or described through external physical appearance ("tall woman with auburn hair and green eyes") rather than through action, voice, distinctive behavior, or internal experience
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: StoryScope research found Gemini specifically "defaults to external character description." LLMs describe characters the way a camera would, not the way another person would — through outward attributes rather than behavioral or psychological specificity.
**Design space cost**: External description alone creates no character identification. Readers connect through action, voice, and psychology, not through hair color and height. This approach prevents the deep POV immersion that creates embodied reading experience and eliminates the character-driven empathy that is fiction's primary engagement mechanism.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.03136

### Hedging Qualifiers and Weasel Phrasing
**Detection**: Narrative prose contains qualifiers ("somewhat," "perhaps," "it could be argued"), hedge words ("might," "generally"), weasel attributions ("has been described as"), or perpetual false balance that strips the prose of conviction and authority
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: AI chatbots "tend to attribute opinions or claims to some vague or unsourced authority" (weasel wording). Models produce text with "the same hedging qualifiers appearing in every other paragraph" because RLHF training prioritizes avoiding controversy. This creates prose that "feels perpetually balanced to the point of becoming wishy-washy."
**Design space cost**: Fiction requires conviction. Characters must believe things, want things, and act decisively. A narrative voice full of hedging qualifiers creates the same "bland, deferential, faintly eager-to-please centre" in fiction as in chatbot responses. It strips the prose of the authority and specificity that create immersion and trust.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Signs_of_AI_writing

### Emotional Tone Inconsistency
**Detection**: The emotional tone shifts abruptly and inappropriately between adjacent passages — e.g., a grief scene suddenly becomes upbeat, or a tense moment dissolves into casual humor without narrative justification
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: "Emotional inconsistency is one of the most noticeable problems" in AI fiction — "the emotional tone of AI-generated poems and stories being inconsistent throughout — for example, a poem intended to convey grief might suddenly change to a strangely upbeat tone." Models lack persistent emotional tracking across extended passages.
**Design space cost**: Emotional inconsistency breaks the fictive dream and destroys the accumulated emotional investment that drives catharsis. If the text cannot maintain tonal coherence, it cannot build the progressive emotional intensity that creates reader engagement. The reader's mirror neuron response requires consistent emotional context.
**Source**: https://aiinscreentrade.com/2025/01/28/the-human-touch-why-ai-cant-replicate-true-emotional-depth/

### Sycophantic Resolution
**Detection**: The narrative resolves by validating every character's perspective, avoiding any judgment, cost, or loss. Everyone learns and grows. All relationships are repaired. The ending is aggressively reassuring with no lingering ambiguity, discomfort, or cost.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Anthropic research found sycophancy to be "a general behavior of AI assistants, likely driven in part by human preference judgments favoring sycophantic responses." In fiction, this manifests as resolutions that comfort rather than challenge, validate rather than judge, and resolve rather than leave open. The model avoids leaving any reader uncomfortable.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates moral complexity, tragic possibility, and the productive discomfort that great fiction creates. A story that validates everyone has no point of view. The reader-writer contract requires the author to have something to say — a vision of the world that makes judgments about human behavior. Sycophantic resolution is the narrative equivalent of "a committee voting on good sentences."
**Source**: https://www.matthewjockers.net/2025/12/31/the-seductive-allure-of-generative-ai-for-storytelling/

### Semantic Ablation
**Detection**: Proper nouns are replaced with category labels ("a famous musician" instead of naming them), precise vocabulary with vague near-synonyms ("complex harmonies" instead of "comping in fourths"), and vivid metaphors with conventional similes. The text is systematically less specific than the situation demands.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Models strip precision systematically through "semantic ablation" — replacing specifics with generics at every level. "Instead of 'McCoy Tyner comping in fourths behind a Coltrane solo lasting eleven minutes,' models produce generic 'complex harmonies.'" Unusual phrasing has low probability and is systematically avoided.
**Design space cost**: Specificity is the mechanism of immersion. Gardner: "Vivid detail is the life blood of fiction." Every act of semantic ablation reduces the reader's ability to visualize, embody, and believe in the fictional world. Generic prose cannot activate the sensory processing regions that create embodied reading experience.
**Source**: https://betterthangood.xyz/blog/ai-writing-has-no-voice/

### Structural Predictability (PowerPoint Architecture)
**Detection**: The narrative follows a predictable structural template — introductory frame, three supporting developments (often of equal length), summative resolution. Transitions use formulaic connectors. The structure can be predicted from the first few pages.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: AI-generated text follows "predictable architecture: introductory frame, three supporting points (often bulleted), summative paragraph beginning 'In conclusion.'" This structure reflects training on expository text and the model's inability to vary structural approaches. "The verbal equivalent of a PowerPoint slide advancing."
**Design space cost**: Structural predictability eliminates surprise and the tension that comes from not knowing where the story is going. Human writing builds momentum through "rhythm, surprise, withholding/delivery" — "attention mechanics" that RLHF "selects against."
**Source**: https://betterthangood.xyz/blog/ai-writing-has-no-voice/

### Repetitive Name/Location/Occupation Defaults
**Detection**: Characters are given the same limited set of names, placed in the same limited set of locations, or assigned the same limited set of occupations regardless of story context — e.g., immigrant narratives default to Chicago and New York, characters are disproportionately writers or professors
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Research found "consistent repetition of specific names, locations, occupations, and themes" across five different AI systems regardless of architectural differences. This represents the model's probability distribution concentrating on the most common training data examples rather than generating contextually appropriate variety.
**Design space cost**: Repetitive defaults signal that the story world is not imagined but assembled from the most statistically probable components. Specificity of setting, naming, and vocation is essential for creating believable fictional worlds. When every story defaults to the same elements, it reveals the absence of authorial vision and world-building.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2603.13545v1

### Three-Chapter Degradation
**Detection**: The quality, distinctiveness, and engagement level of the prose degrades noticeably after the first two to three chapters — initial quality cannot be sustained across the full length of the work
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: "Stories pass initial quality checks but deteriorate significantly, making evaluation unreliable without full reading." This reflects the "context poisoning" problem — once AI prose settles into mediocre patterns, the model compounds the problem by generating similar constructions, "unable to break the cycle." Each chapter draws more heavily on the already-generated (and increasingly generic) context.
**Design space cost**: Reader engagement requires sustained quality. A story that starts strong and degrades is worse than a consistently mediocre one, because it creates and then betrays reader expectations. The reader-writer contract established in the opening is broken by the declining middle and end.
**Source**: https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction
