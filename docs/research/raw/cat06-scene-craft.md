## Criteria Found

### Scene Goal Clarity
**What it measures**: Whether the POV character has a clear, concrete, specific goal established early in the scene that the reader can identify and track progress against.
**Assumed goal**: Swain/Bickham dramatic scene model requiring every scene to open with an identifiable character goal -- experimental or literary fiction (e.g., Woolf, Sebald) may deliberately withhold explicit goals to create ambiguity or impressionistic texture.
**Score 0**: No identifiable character goal exists in the scene. The character drifts through events without wanting anything specific. Readers cannot articulate what success or failure would look like.
**Score 5**: The POV character's scene goal is concrete, specific, and established within the first few paragraphs. The goal connects to the larger plot goal, has physical manifestation even when emotional/mental, and readers can measure progress toward it throughout the scene.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/structuring-your-storys-scenes-pt-3/
**Notes**: Weiland identifies five categories of scene goals: concrete acquisitions, incorporeal desires, physical escape, mental escape, emotional escape. The goal must be "inherent to the plot" -- if accomplishing or failing it wouldn't affect the story's outcome, the scene likely lacks purpose. Advanced Fiction Writing states the goal "must be specific and clearly definable."

### Scene-Level Conflict and Opposition
**What it measures**: Whether the scene contains genuine opposition that tests the character's pursuit of their goal, creating struggle and requiring difficult choices rather than mere obstacles to navigate.
**Assumed goal**: Dramatic fiction tradition (Aristotle through McKee) requiring agon -- deliberate opposition generating struggle -- as the engine of scenes. Slice-of-life or contemplative fiction may feature scenes where tension arises from internal states rather than external opposition.
**Score 0**: No opposition exists, or obstacles are trivially overcome without struggle, hard choices, or consequences. The character moves from goal to achievement without meaningful resistance.
**Score 5**: The character faces opposition that escalates throughout the scene, forces genuine choices with real consequences, and tests their commitment to the goal. The conflict stems from an antagonistic force (person, environment, internal contradiction) that has its own logic and cannot be easily dismissed.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/create-meaningful-obstacles-via-conflict/
**Notes**: Hardy distinguishes obstacles from conflict: "An obstacle is merely something in the way, while conflict involves struggle, challenge, and consequence." The diagnostic questions are: Does the obstacle intentionally try to prevent the protagonist? Does overcoming it require hard choices or mental/emotional struggle? If merely executing a predetermined solution suffices, conflict depth is lacking.

### Scene Turn / Polarity Shift
**What it measures**: Whether the value-charged condition of the character's life changes from the beginning to the end of the scene -- a shift in polarity that gives the scene dramatic purpose.
**Assumed goal**: McKee's value-shift model where scenes must change a charged value from positive to negative or vice versa -- slice-of-life fiction or deliberately anti-dramatic literary fiction may resist explicit polarity shifts in favor of tonal modulation.
**Score 0**: The value-charged condition of the character's life is identical at the scene's end to its beginning. Nothing meaningful has changed. The scene is a "nonevent" with activity but no change.
**Score 5**: A clear, significant polarity shift occurs. The value at stake (love/hate, safety/danger, hope/despair, knowledge/ignorance) is identifiably different at the scene's close. The shift occurs through a specific turning point (action, revelation, or change of heart) and affects subsequent story developments.
**Source**: https://mckeestory.com/do-your-scenes-turn/
**Notes**: McKee defines a scene as "an action through conflict in more or less continuous time and space that turns the value-charged condition of a character's life on at least one value with a degree of perceptible significance." The keyword is "significant" -- surface-level changes don't suffice. Four polarity patterns exist: negative to positive, positive to negative, negative to double negative, positive to double positive.

### Turn Variety Across Scenes
**What it measures**: Whether the polarity shifts vary in direction, mechanism, and which value changes from scene to scene, preventing monotonous repetition of the same pattern.
**Assumed goal**: McKee/Story Grid analytical framework requiring variety in turning points to maintain reader engagement -- highly formulaic genre fiction (e.g., cozy mysteries) may intentionally cycle through predictable patterns as a feature, not a bug.
**Score 0**: Every scene turns the same way -- all revelations, all the same polarity direction, all shifting the same value. The pattern becomes monotonous and predictable.
**Score 5**: The direction of polarity shifts alternates across scenes. The mechanism varies (some turns via action, some via revelation, some via change of heart). Different values are at stake in different scenes. The degree of charge increases as the story develops.
**Source**: https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2020/03/positive-and-negative-scene-values.html
**Notes**: Fawkes emphasizes: "Series of identical patterns feel tedious." The Story Grid methodology tracks whether turns happen via action versus revelation and flags sequences of 10+ consecutive same-type turns as a reader disengagement risk. Ellis adds: "You should vary the direction of polarity shift from scene to scene."

### Scene Disaster / Outcome Quality
**What it measures**: Whether scenes end with outcomes that propel the story forward rather than resolving neatly, and whether the five outcome types (Yes, Yes-But, No, No-And, No-But) are deployed with strategic variety.
**Assumed goal**: Swain/Bickham dramatic scene model where disaster (usually "no, and furthermore") is the default outcome -- comedy or romance may use "yes, but" more frequently; literary fiction may prefer ambiguous outcomes that resist clean categorization.
**Score 0**: Scenes consistently resolve with clean success ("yes" endings) that eliminate forward tension, or outcomes are muddled and unclear. The story loses momentum because nothing goes meaningfully wrong.
**Score 5**: Scene outcomes strategically vary among the five types. "Yes, but" and "No, and furthermore" dominate the middle of the story, creating escalating complications. Clean "yes" endings are reserved for climactic moments. Each outcome creates a new situation requiring the character to respond.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/types-of-scene-endings/
**Notes**: Weiland details: "Yes" -- success with no complications (use sparingly); "Yes, But" -- success with complications (highly versatile, could sustain entire novel); "No" -- complete blockage (use with extreme caution, kills momentum); "No, And..." -- failure with partial insight; "No, But..." -- failure with illusory gain (creates dead ends, use sparingly).

### Sequel (Reaction) Quality
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative provides adequate space for the POV character to process disasters emotionally before making decisions, following the reaction-dilemma-decision pattern rather than skipping directly from disaster to new action.
**Assumed goal**: Swain/Bickham scene-sequel model requiring emotional processing between action scenes -- thriller and action genres deliberately compress sequels for pace; literary fiction may expand them to become primary narrative focus.
**Score 0**: No sequel exists between scenes. Characters jump from one disaster directly into a new goal without emotional processing, logical deliberation, or decision-making. Alternatively, sequels are present but skip reaction (jumping to decision) or skip dilemma (character knows exactly what to do without weighing options).
**Score 5**: After each significant disaster, the character processes emotionally (reaction), weighs options with genuine uncertainty (dilemma), and commits to a course of action that feels earned given the emotional/logical arc (decision). The sequel's length is proportional to its dramatic importance, with the longest sequel typically at the "dark night of the soul."
**Source**: https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2021/10/sequel-structure-according-to-swain.html
**Notes**: Fawkes notes: "Even 'ridiculous' decisions become plausible through sufficient reaction/dilemma development." The decision "must feel believable given the character's emotions and thought process." Sequels that skip reaction create characters who seem robotic; sequels that skip dilemma create characters who seem omniscient.

### Scene Necessity (The Deletion Test)
**What it measures**: Whether each scene is structurally necessary -- whether removing it would cause the plot, character development, or relationship evolution to break down.
**Assumed goal**: Commercial fiction where every scene must "earn its keep" via plot advancement or character development -- literary fiction may include scenes that serve mood, atmosphere, or thematic resonance without direct plot or character-arc advancement.
**Score 0**: Multiple scenes fail the deletion test. Removing them would not affect plot progression, character development, or relationship evolution. The story contains filler, research dumps, Point-A-to-Point-B transitions, rehashed information, and scenes exploring minor characters without story purpose.
**Score 5**: Every scene is load-bearing. Removing any single scene would create a gap that readers would notice -- either plot logic would break, a character relationship would lose a necessary development step, or essential information would be missing.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/unnecessary-scenes/
**Notes**: Weiland identifies four types of unnecessary scenes: minor character relationship exploration, dramatized Point-A-to-Point-B transitions, scenes recovering old ground (court scenes, reports to superiors), and research dumps. The diagnostic: "If you deleted this entire scene, what would happen to the rest of the story?"

### Scene-Level Stakes
**What it measures**: Whether the reader can identify what the character stands to lose or gain in this specific scene, and whether those stakes are clearly communicated regardless of their magnitude.
**Assumed goal**: Mainstream dramatic fiction (Johns, Maass) requiring every scene to have identifiable consequences for failure -- experimental fiction may deliberately obscure or complicate the concept of stakes to explore different narrative modes.
**Score 0**: No identifiable stakes exist. The character pursues a goal but there is no articulated consequence for failure. Alternatively, stakes are so vague ("bad things will happen") that they fail to register emotionally.
**Score 5**: The reader can articulate precisely what the character stands to lose and why it matters to them specifically. Small but transparent consequences outweigh massive but ambiguous ones. Scene-level stakes connect to but are distinct from overall story stakes.
**Source**: https://www.kevintjohns.com/are-you-killing-the-tension-high-stakes-writing-mistakes/
**Notes**: Johns emphasizes: "It's not about how big the stakes are. It's about whether they're clearly communicated." Every scene should answer: "What does the character want immediately, and what's the cost of failure?" The Novelry adds that micro-stakes and mini-conflicts in each chapter "force you to justify the existence of each and every scene."

### Scene Causality (Inter-Scene)
**What it measures**: Whether scenes connect causally to each other -- whether you can say "because of that" rather than "and then" between consecutive scenes, forming a causal chain rather than an episodic sequence.
**Assumed goal**: Aristotelian plot tradition (through E.M. Forster's "The king died and then the queen died of grief" example) requiring causal linkage between narrative events -- picaresque, episodic, or deliberately fragmented fiction (e.g., Vonnegut) may resist causal chains as a structural choice.
**Score 0**: Scenes are episodic -- connected only by featuring the same characters. No scene's outcome drives the next scene's situation. Removing or reordering scenes would not create logical problems.
**Score 5**: Every scene's outcome creates the conditions for the next scene. Conflict in one scene becomes the motivating cause for the next. Emotional responses in one scene drive decisions in the following scene. Unexpected objects, locations, and actions are foreshadowed in earlier scenes. The sequence feels inevitable.
**Source**: https://www.beemgee.com/blog/cause-effect-scene-connectedness/
**Notes**: Beemgee states: "No scene should exist from which you could not draw a line of connection to another scene." Alderson adds: "When this linking breaks down, scenes come out of the blue, and your story turns episodic." Causal chains operate on two levels: plot logic and characterization logic.

### Scene Causality (Intra-Scene)
**What it measures**: Whether the beats within a scene follow a cause-and-effect logic -- whether each action motivates the next reaction, following Swain's Motivation-Reaction Unit (MRU) structure.
**Assumed goal**: Swain/Bickham MRU model requiring strict alternation between external motivation and internal reaction in physiologically correct order -- stream-of-consciousness or experimental prose may deliberately violate MRU order for artistic effect.
**Score 0**: Beats within scenes lack causal connection. Characters act without identifiable motivation. Reactions precede their causes. The physiological sequence (feeling, reflex, rational action) is consistently violated without artistic purpose.
**Score 5**: Each beat follows logically from the previous one. External stimuli (motivation) are presented objectively, followed by internal responses (reaction) in the correct physiological order: feeling first, then reflex, then rational action/speech. Motivation and reaction are kept in separate paragraphs for clarity.
**Source**: https://www.advancedfictionwriting.com/articles/writing-the-perfect-scene/
**Notes**: Ingermanson states the critical rule: "Never mix Motivation and Reaction in the same paragraph." The reaction sequence must follow: 1) Feeling (immediate emotional response, milliseconds), 2) Reflex (instinctive action, tenths of a second), 3) Rational Action/Speech (conscious behavior, slower). Violation of this order creates a "jarring" effect.

### Scene Opening Quality
**What it measures**: Whether scenes open with hooks that engage the reader immediately, establish the scene's situation efficiently, and avoid common opening mistakes (delayed inciting incident, backward-looking exposition, situational rather than specific events).
**Assumed goal**: Commercial fiction craft (Puma, Story Grid) requiring scenes to hook readers within the first few lines -- literary fiction may open scenes with deliberate slowness, establishing mood or atmosphere before introducing conflict.
**Score 0**: Scenes consistently open with exposition dumps, backstory, character routines, or situational description without a specific inciting moment. The inciting incident is delayed or absent. Openings look backward at past events rather than forward at coming conflict.
**Score 5**: Scenes open with hooks that create forward-looking curiosity. The inciting incident occurs within the first few lines. The opening establishes a specific, pinpointable event (not a general situation) that forces new conflict. The character's scene goal or the stakes are made clear early.
**Source**: https://loripuma.com/blog/top-three-mistakes-starting-scenes
**Notes**: Puma identifies three opening mistakes: 1) The inciting incident doesn't force genuinely novel conflict, 2) The inciting incident happens too late, 3) The inciting incident is situational rather than a specific moment "as it occurs." Fawkes adds that scene openings should look forward, not backward, and that backstory/flashbacks work best immediately after turning points, not at openings.

### Scene Ending Quality (Exit Hooks)
**What it measures**: Whether scenes end in a way that compels readers to continue -- creating unresolved questions, unfinished business, or new complications rather than resolving neatly.
**Assumed goal**: Page-turner fiction tradition (commercial, thriller, mystery) where every scene ending must create compulsion to read the next scene -- literary fiction may end scenes with resolution, stillness, or ambiguity that invites reflection rather than forward propulsion.
**Score 0**: Scenes consistently end with complete resolution, tying off all threads. No questions remain. No complications arise. Readers have no reason to turn the page beyond mild interest in what happens next.
**Score 5**: Scene endings deploy strategic variety among outcome types. The last line occupies the "power position," leaving an impression or hook to the next scene. Questions hang in the air. New complications have been introduced that the character must face. The ending generates enough suspense to compel the reader into the next scene.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/types-of-scene-endings/
**Notes**: Fictionary emphasizes that "exit hooks keep readers reading" and their "primary purpose is to make the reader ask questions they can only answer by reading on." The article on scene endings identifies five types (Yes, Yes-But, No, No-And, No-But), each with specific strategic uses. Jane Friedman states: "Never fully satisfy readers at scene conclusions."

### Enter Late, Exit Early
**What it measures**: Whether scenes begin as close to the conflict/action as possible and end immediately after the core dramatic purpose is achieved, cutting unnecessary preamble and aftermath.
**Assumed goal**: Screenwriting-influenced fiction craft (Hitchcock principle: "Drama is life with the dull bits cut out") prioritizing economy and forward momentum -- literary fiction may deliberately include the "dull bits" as texture, atmosphere, or thematic material.
**Score 0**: Scenes begin with extensive setup -- characters arriving, exchanging pleasantries, settling into locations -- before any conflict or inciting incident occurs. Scenes continue well past their dramatic purpose with characters winding down, departing, or reflecting without new information.
**Score 5**: Scenes open in media res, as close to the action or conflict as possible. They conclude immediately after the core purpose is achieved. Preamble and aftermath are cut or compressed into summary. Only material that serves plot, character, or theme remains.
**Source**: https://screenplayreaders.com/get-into-scene-late-leave-early/
**Notes**: The technique "forces writers to identify and retain only the most critical narrative elements." Exceptions exist: scenes where details become plot-critical later, scenes introducing main characters, and situations where additional context genuinely serves narrative function. But the default should be entry into the conflict and exit from the outcome.

### Physical Grounding and Blocking
**What it measures**: Whether characters are physically grounded in space during scenes -- whether the reader can track where characters are, how they move, and how they interact with their physical environment.
**Assumed goal**: Immersive fiction tradition (from theatre blocking through cinematic prose) requiring readers to maintain spatial awareness -- some literary or postmodern fiction deliberately degrounds characters from physical space for aesthetic or philosophical reasons.
**Score 0**: Characters float in undefined space ("white room syndrome"). No spatial information is provided. During dialogue, characters seem to be disembodied heads. Physical actions, gestures, and environmental interactions are absent or generic.
**Score 5**: Characters occupy specific, identifiable spaces. Movement is motivated by character psychology and story needs. Physical actions during dialogue reveal character (idiosyncratic tics, meaningful interactions with objects). The reader can visualize the spatial relationships between characters and their environment without strain.
**Source**: https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2024/03/how-to-fix-talking-heads-in-your-story.html
**Notes**: Fawkes identifies five types of lines that should appear in scenes: dialogue, description, blocking, introspection, and summary. George coins "THAD" (Talking Head Avoidance Device) for meaningful activity during dialogue. The activity "has to matter to the plot, has to bring something new to the table" -- not mindless busywork.

### Sensory Grounding
**What it measures**: Whether scenes engage multiple senses to create immersive physical reality rather than relying solely on visual description or abstract narration.
**Assumed goal**: Immersive fiction craft tradition (from writing workshops through Fictionary's editorial model) recommending engagement of three or more senses per scene -- minimalist fiction may deliberately restrict sensory information for spare, stark effect.
**Score 0**: Scenes lack sensory detail entirely (white room syndrome) or engage only the visual sense superficially. The reader cannot see, hear, smell, feel, or taste the fictional world. Environment feels like a backdrop rather than a lived space.
**Score 5**: Scenes engage at least two or three senses naturally through the POV character's experience. Sensory details are specific (not generic), filtered through the character's perception, and contribute to mood, character, or plot. Internal senses (temperature, pressure, heartbeat) supplement the five external senses.
**Source**: https://www.writingmastery.com/blog/avoiding-white-room-syndrome-tips-to-ground-your-fiction-effectively
**Notes**: Writing Mastery states white room syndrome "stems from a lack of sensory detail, spatial cues, and environmental grounding." Fictionary recommends "three senses (on average) per scene to bring settings to life." The San Francisco Writers Conference notes there are "more than twenty specific senses humans experience" beyond the traditional five.

### Setting-Character Integration
**What it measures**: Whether the physical environment is actively integrated with character action and emotion rather than existing as inert backdrop, and whether characters interact with their surroundings meaningfully.
**Assumed goal**: Deep POV fiction craft (from free indirect discourse tradition through contemporary close-third) requiring setting to be experienced through the character's perceptual lens -- omniscient narration may describe settings independently of character perspective.
**Score 0**: Setting is described separately from character action, feeling like stage directions. Characters do not interact with their environment. Description is detached from POV character's emotional state. Environment exists as decoration rather than an active story element.
**Score 5**: Characters move through, interact with, and react to their environment continuously. Setting mirrors or contrasts character emotion. Environmental details are filtered through the POV character's perception and colored by their mood. The environment functions as an active force that shapes character behavior and reveals personality.
**Source**: https://jamigold.com/2021/06/characters-settings-making-them-interact/
**Notes**: Gold states: "A scene isn't just an interaction between two characters, it's an interaction between two characters and their environment." Inkdrop Lit adds: "In deep POV, readers don't just see the world; they feel it as the character does." First Editing emphasizes: "Setting isn't decoration -- it's an active force that shapes and reveals who characters are."

### Character Interiority Quality
**What it measures**: Whether the scene provides appropriate access to the POV character's internal experience -- thoughts, feelings, unspoken reactions -- creating the connection between external events and internal character response.
**Assumed goal**: Close-POV fiction tradition (deep third, first person) requiring interiority as the "glue between inner and outer worlds" -- objective or camera-eye narration (e.g., Hemingway's "Hills Like White Elephants") deliberately withholds interiority to create subtext through omission.
**Score 0**: No interiority exists -- the scene reads like a screenplay or stage directions. Alternatively, interiority is so excessive that it overwhelms action and dialogue, creating stasis. Or interiority violates POV boundaries (head-hopping without scene breaks).
**Score 5**: Interiority is balanced with action and dialogue. Internal responses illuminate why characters make their choices. Thoughts advance plot or build character without redundantly explaining what's already shown. The technique (direct monologue, indirect thought, free indirect discourse) is consistent and appropriate to the narrative voice.
**Source**: https://nownovel.com/interiority-in-fiction-the-glue-between-inner-and-outer-worlds/
**Notes**: NowNovel identifies four techniques: stream of consciousness, internal monologue, free indirect discourse, and direct narration. Too little interiority creates "action-heavy narratives resembling films without motivation clarity." Too much "bogs down pacing." Advanced Fiction Writing states interior monologue "must advance the plot or build character."

### Subtext Within Scenes
**What it measures**: Whether scenes operate on multiple levels -- with an unspoken undercurrent of meaning beneath the surface dialogue and action -- or whether everything is "on the nose" with characters directly stating their thoughts, feelings, and intentions.
**Assumed goal**: Sophisticated dramatic fiction tradition (from Chekhov and Hemingway through McKee) requiring gap between surface text and underlying meaning -- some genres (children's fiction, instructional fiction) may legitimately operate with minimal subtext for clarity.
**Score 0**: All dialogue is "on the nose" -- characters say exactly what they mean, feel, and intend. There is no gap between words and meaning. No tension exists between what characters say and what they think or feel. Readers have nothing to interpret.
**Score 5**: Dialogue and action carry multiple layers of meaning. What characters don't say is as important as what they say. Body language, behavior, silence, and pauses reveal emotions that words conceal. Readers actively interpret the gap between surface and depth, creating engagement through participation.
**Source**: https://www.writersdigest.com/write-better-fiction/how-to-use-subtext-and-the-art-of-dramatic-tension-in-fiction
**Notes**: Author's Pathway states: "The subtext is what she and her daughter hold back. What they do not say adds a layer of tension." Key techniques include contrast between words and actions, silence and pauses, body language revealing unconscious thoughts, and allowing multiple interpretations. Fawkes notes that "stories without subtext feel slow because readers become passive spectators rather than active participants."

### Scene Emotional Arc
**What it measures**: Whether the character's emotional state develops and changes over the course of a single scene rather than remaining flat, and whether that progression is causally connected to scene events.
**Assumed goal**: Emotionally engaged fiction craft (Weiland's scene-level emotional tracking) requiring internal change within each scene -- hardboiled or stoic narrative voices may deliberately flatten emotional display while still implying internal change.
**Score 0**: The character's emotional state is identical at the end of the scene as at the beginning -- flat, plateaued, or randomly oscillating without connection to events. Alternatively, emotional changes are too rapid to be believable (from despair to elation in one scene without extraordinary cause).
**Score 5**: Emotional intensity builds progressively through the scene, connected to turning points and events. Emotional changes follow one of three valid patterns: intensity change (same emotion, increasing strength), emotion switch (one emotion family to another via triggering event), or umbrella arc (multiple emotions accumulating into complex response). The arc feels authentic to the specific character.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/characters-emotional-arc/
**Notes**: Weiland identifies common mistakes: "waxing and waning" (emotions fluctuating multiple times without progression), "disconnected jumps" (shifting between feelings without causal connection), "flatlined emotions" (same intensity throughout), "emotional absence" (character feeling nothing), and "too rapid changes." Generally, "it's more powerful to show emotional intensity increasing rather than decreasing."

### Scene-Level Plot-Character-Theme Integration
**What it measures**: Whether each scene simultaneously advances plot, develops character, and expresses theme -- whether these three dimensions are intertwined rather than operating independently.
**Assumed goal**: Workshop-tradition fiction craft (Weiland, Coyne) requiring all three story dimensions to be present in every scene -- genre fiction may prioritize plot over character/theme in action scenes; literary fiction may prioritize character/theme over plot in contemplative scenes.
**Score 0**: Scenes serve only one dimension: pure plot advancement without character development or thematic resonance, or pure character study without plot movement, or thematic lecturing without dramatic action.
**Score 5**: Plot events simultaneously reveal character and express theme. The character's internal struggle with their Lie/Truth is externalized through plot conflict. Theme operates as subtext -- never stated explicitly but present as "the guiding principle." Emotional arcs relate to thematic content. The character's scene-level decision reflects their position on the thematic spectrum.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/how-to-intertwine-plot-character-and-theme-in-every-scene/
**Notes**: Weiland's comprehensive scene checklist requires: plot relevance to overall objectives, measurable plot progression, character internal change, emotional transformation, connection to thematic Truth/Lie dynamic, and organic cause-and-effect consequences. She notes: "Even if the character doesn't realize it, what he's seeking in every scene is the theme."

### Scene Structure Completeness (Five Commandments)
**What it measures**: Whether each scene contains all five essential structural elements identified by the Story Grid methodology: inciting incident, turning point progressive complication, crisis, climax, and resolution.
**Assumed goal**: Story Grid editorial methodology (Shawn Coyne) requiring all five commandments in every scene -- minimalist or experimental fiction may deliberately omit elements (e.g., no resolution, forcing the reader to construct it).
**Score 0**: Scenes lack identifiable structural elements. No inciting incident launches the scene. No progressive complications build tension. No crisis forces a decision. No climax enacts a choice. No resolution reveals consequences.
**Score 5**: All five commandments are present and identifiable. The inciting incident launches the scene's action. Progressive complications escalate. A crisis forces a best-bad-choice or irreconcilable-goods decision. The climax shows the character's active choice. The resolution reveals the value shift and immediate consequences.
**Source**: https://storygrid.com/editor-roundtable-how-to-analyze-a-scene/
**Notes**: The Story Grid scene event synthesis captures: ending value, outcome of protagonist's initial strategy, the climax action (their choice), and the crisis tradeoff (what they risk). Coyne requires "brutally honest documentation of what actually occurred on the page, not authorial intent."

### Scene-Level Information Management
**What it measures**: Whether the scene strategically controls the flow of information -- revealing enough to maintain engagement while withholding enough to sustain curiosity, creating a "curiosity gap" between what readers know and what they want to know.
**Assumed goal**: Suspense-driven fiction craft (mystery, thriller) requiring careful calibration of what readers learn when -- literary fiction may operate with full information transparency, deriving tension from dramatic irony rather than mystery.
**Score 0**: Either all information is dumped at once (eliminating curiosity) or too little is provided (creating confusion rather than mystery). Revelations don't raise new questions. The reader has no information asymmetry to maintain engagement.
**Score 5**: Each revelation answers one question while raising new ones. Readers have enough information to understand and care about what's happening, with a crucial piece missing that keeps them reading. Front-loading, gradual revelation, and delayed revelation are deployed strategically. The gap between what readers know and what they want to know is maintained throughout.
**Source**: https://www.pw.org/content/withholding_and_revelation_managing_information_in_fiction
**Notes**: Savannah Gilbo distinguishes curiosity from confusion: "Being mysterious means you've given readers a clear foundation to stand on -- understanding the situation, the characters, and what's at stake -- but there's a crucial piece of information missing." The deep lore show adds: "Front-loading information gives readers important details early, creating tension through dramatic irony."

### Scene-Level Setup and Payoff
**What it measures**: Whether scenes properly set up elements that pay off later (either within the scene or in subsequent scenes) and whether the scene itself delivers on promises made by earlier setups.
**Assumed goal**: Chekhov's Gun principle (from Chekhov through Pressfield and contemporary craft) requiring every narrative element to be relevant -- maximalist or digressive fiction (e.g., Pynchon) may deliberately include elements that don't pay off conventionally.
**Score 0**: The scene introduces elements (objects, information, character details) that never pay off, or delivers payoffs for setups that were never established. Chekhov's guns are hung on walls but never fired. The scene feels disconnected from the larger narrative web.
**Score 5**: Elements introduced in the scene serve clear narrative purposes -- either paying off within the scene or setting up future payoffs. Earlier setups from previous scenes are paid off at appropriate moments. The reader experiences "aha-moments" of recognition when connections become clear. No false promises are made.
**Source**: https://janefriedman.com/writing-scenes-setup-payoff/
**Notes**: Friedman emphasizes Chekhov's principle: "Every sentence hangs a rifle on the wall that eventually must discharge." Fawkes's promise-progress-payoff framework maps directly to scenes: setup phase (promise), action phase (progress), resolution phase (payoff). "Rushing toward payoff without sufficient buildup" is a common weakness.

### Scene Pacing Proportionality
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative investment (word count, detail, real-time treatment) in each scene is proportional to the scene's dramatic importance rather than uniform across all scenes.
**Assumed goal**: Proportional pacing tradition (Stieffel, Fawkes) where "the number of words needed does not depend upon how much time the event takes; it depends upon how important that moment is" -- experimental fiction may deliberately invert proportionality for defamiliarization.
**Score 0**: All scenes receive equal narrative investment regardless of importance. Trivial transitions get as much space as climactic confrontations. Important moments are rushed while unimportant moments are lingered over. The proportion between real-time narration and summary is not calibrated to significance.
**Score 5**: Critical scenes (conflict, decision, revelation, transformation) receive expanded treatment -- more detail, more real-time narration, more sensory engagement. Less important transitions are compressed into summary. The reader's experience of time matches the story's sense of importance. Description, narration, action, and dialogue are proportioned to maximize each scene's impact.
**Source**: https://kristenstieffel.com/pacing-matter-proportion/
**Notes**: Stieffel states: "Pacing is proportional. Spend lots of time on the important parts of your story, and less time on the least important parts." Fawkes adds: "Spending disproportionate words on minor elements or too few on important ones creates pacing mismatches." The Write Practice emphasizes: "A summary covers a longer period of time in a shorter passage, while a scene covers a short period of time in a longer passage."

### Scene Internal Pacing Curve
**What it measures**: Whether pacing within individual scenes follows a purposeful curve -- typically tightening as the climactic moment approaches and loosening afterward -- rather than maintaining a uniform tempo throughout.
**Assumed goal**: Dramatic fiction craft (Fawkes, Story Grid) where scene-internal pacing should mirror the scene's structural arc -- prose-poem or meditative fiction may maintain deliberate uniformity as an aesthetic choice.
**Score 0**: The scene maintains a single unchanging tempo from start to finish. No acceleration toward the turning point. No release afterward. The beat distribution is uniform and monotonous.
**Score 5**: Pacing tightens as the scene approaches its climactic moment -- sentences shorten, action quickens, details sharpen. After the turning point, pacing loosens for reaction and processing. The proportional distribution of setup, rising action, climax, and falling action feels purposeful.
**Source**: https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2022/03/pacing-within-scenes.html
**Notes**: Fawkes states: "Usually the closer we get to a climactic moment, the tighter, more intense the pacing gets. After the climax, the pacing can be a little looser." Additional factors: "Too much setup without hooks creates boredom." "Starting in medias res feels fast but risks disorientation." "Predictability" -- when outcomes become obvious but scenes continue -- makes audiences impatient.

### Scene Structure Variety
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative deploys different scene structures, lengths, tempos, and types across the work rather than repeating the same structural template for every scene.
**Assumed goal**: Varied-pacing fiction craft tradition (from Swain's scene-sequel alternation through contemporary editorial wisdom) requiring structural diversity to prevent reader fatigue -- genre fiction with rigid structural expectations (e.g., romance beat sheets) may have less structural variation by design.
**Score 0**: Every scene follows the same structural template -- same length, same tempo, same pattern of action-to-reflection, same type of turning point. The reading experience becomes monotonous regardless of content variation. Scene-sequel balance is missing or uniform.
**Score 5**: Scenes vary in length, structure, tempo, and type. Action scenes alternate with reflective sequels. Some scenes open in medias res while others build slowly. Dialogue-heavy scenes alternate with action-heavy or introspection-heavy scenes. The structural variety itself creates rhythm and prevents reader fatigue.
**Source**: https://writershelpingwriters.net/2022/03/scenes-vs-sequels-whats-a-good-balance/
**Notes**: Writers Helping Writers notes: "Too much of anything -- action, dialogue, description, introspection -- in a row can hurt pacing." Wikipedia's pacing entry states: "Any speed can become monotonous if it lacks variety, whether your story moves fast or slow." The scene-sequel alternation provides natural variety: "Scenes tend to be more plot or action-oriented (proactive), while sequels tend to be more character or reaction-oriented (reactive)."

### Scene Transition Quality
**What it measures**: Whether transitions between scenes are smooth and purposeful -- using bridging techniques, logical connections, and mood continuity -- rather than jarring or disorienting.
**Assumed goal**: Seamless narrative fiction tradition requiring readers to move between scenes without confusion or friction -- postmodern or experimental fiction may use deliberately jarring transitions for defamiliarization or fragmentation effects.
**Score 0**: Transitions between scenes are jarring, confusing, or nonexistent. The reader cannot orient themselves in the new scene's time, place, or POV. No logical, thematic, or emotional connection bridges consecutive scenes.
**Score 5**: Transitions use bridging techniques (mirrored imagery, thematic echo, emotional continuity). New scenes establish time, place, and POV quickly. The connection between consecutive scenes -- whether causal, thematic, or contrastive -- is clear. Chapter and scene breaks occur at moments that serve pacing.
**Source**: https://www.plottopunctuation.com/article-scenecraft-2-how-to-create-smooth-scene-transitions.html
**Notes**: Plot to Punctuation recommends "choosing something at the end of the previous scene that can be mirrored in the beginning of the next." Fawkes suggests two tricks: linking through themes/imagery and linking through mirrored actions. Jarring transitions result from overloaded establishing information or failure to provide context and continuity.

### POV Discipline Within Scenes
**What it measures**: Whether scenes maintain consistent point of view without unauthorized head-hopping, and whether the chosen POV is exploited fully for its unique advantages.
**Assumed goal**: Close-POV fiction tradition (contemporary third-limited, first-person) requiring strict POV discipline within scenes -- omniscient narration legitimately moves between minds but still requires controlled, purposeful transitions rather than accidental slippage.
**Score 0**: POV shifts randomly within scenes without scene breaks. The reader receives information the POV character could not know. Head-hopping occurs without purpose. The advantages of the chosen POV are not exploited (e.g., first person without voice distinctiveness; close third without interiority).
**Score 5**: POV is consistent throughout each scene. The reader experiences only what the POV character can perceive, think, and feel. Sensory details, vocabulary, and observation priorities reflect the specific character's personality. POV shifts only occur at scene or chapter breaks with clear re-orientation.
**Source**: https://writershelpingwriters.net/2019/01/all-important-considerations-when-crafting-a-scene/
**Notes**: Writers Helping Writers advises: "Maintain consistent point of view and consider whether changing it would improve the scene." Multiple sources flag head-hopping as a common scene-craft failure. Advanced Fiction Writing notes that "the character whose thoughts you convey must be the scene's POV character."

### Scene vs. Summary Deployment
**What it measures**: Whether the story appropriately chooses between full dramatization (scene) and narrative compression (summary) for each story event, dramatizing important moments and summarizing less important ones.
**Assumed goal**: Workshop-tradition fiction craft (Friedman, Fawkes) recommending that important moments be dramatized while transitions and routine events be summarized -- literary maximalism (e.g., Knausgaard) may deliberately dramatize the mundane as its central artistic project.
**Score 0**: Important moments are summarized while trivial events are fully dramatized. Or the entire narrative is either all scene (exhausting, no compression) or all summary (distant, no immersion). The choice between scene and summary appears arbitrary rather than strategic.
**Score 5**: Crucial moments of conflict, decision, revelation, and change are fully dramatized in real-time scenes. Transitional periods, routine events, and previously-revealed information are compressed into summary. The ratio feels deliberate and serves the story's genre and emotional needs.
**Source**: https://janefriedman.com/writing-scene/
**Notes**: Friedman states summary is acceptable for: time condensing ("Night fell"), location transitions, and previously-revealed information. Fawkes adds: "Information important for plot but not for experience should use summary rather than full scenes." Write It Scared emphasizes: the choice of what to dramatize vs. summarize is one of the most important craft decisions a writer makes.

### Proactive vs. Reactive Character Balance
**What it measures**: Whether the POV character actively drives scene events through choices and initiatives rather than passively reacting to circumstances imposed by others, while still allowing genuine reactive processing after disasters.
**Assumed goal**: Character-agency fiction tradition (Johns, Swain) requiring protagonists to be proactive drivers of action -- literary fiction exploring powerlessness or systemic oppression may deliberately feature reactive protagonists to mirror their thematic concerns.
**Score 0**: The character is consistently passive -- bouncing from event to event without initiating action, making choices, or driving the scene forward. "A pinball bouncing from event to event" with no stakes, only randomness.
**Score 5**: In action scenes, the character proactively pursues goals, confronts obstacles, and makes deliberate choices. In sequel/reaction scenes, the character processes and decides rather than simply enduring. The balance between proactive pursuit and reactive processing creates a rhythm of agency and vulnerability.
**Source**: https://www.kevintjohns.com/are-you-killing-the-tension-high-stakes-writing-mistakes/
**Notes**: Johns states: "If your hero is just a pinball bouncing from event to event, there are no stakes -- only randomness." Swain's model inherently creates this balance: scenes are proactive (goal-driven), sequels are reactive (processing-driven). The key is that even in reactive moments, the character ultimately makes a decision that drives new proactive action.

### Scene Beat Rhythm
**What it measures**: Whether the scene is composed of identifiable beats -- exchanges of action/reaction that create a rhythmic pulse -- rather than undifferentiated narration.
**Assumed goal**: Cinematic prose tradition (C.S. Lakin, Storm Writing School) requiring fiction to be built from discrete action/reaction beats that readers can visualize -- prose-poetry or lyrical fiction may prioritize flow over beat structure.
**Score 0**: The scene reads as undifferentiated narration without identifiable beats. No clear exchanges of action and reaction. No rhythm of stimulus-response. Events blur together without distinct moments.
**Score 5**: Individual beats are clearly identifiable -- each consisting of an action/stimulus followed by a character reaction. Beats build upon each other to create escalation. The rhythm of beats creates momentum, and variation in beat length and intensity prevents monotony.
**Source**: https://www.livewritethrive.com/2025/04/26/33309/
**Notes**: C.S. Lakin states: "At the scene level, a beat is an exchange of behavior in action/reaction -- there's an action or stimulus, and a character reacts, then another action to which the character reacts, with each action/reaction constituting a beat." Storm Writing School adds: "A series of beats creates a scene." Since "readers want and expect cinematic scenes (vividly visual and in deep POV), it behooves fiction writers to learn to structure scenes by building them with beats."

### Four-Question Scene Evaluation
**What it measures**: Whether each scene can answer all four evaluative questions: what it accomplishes for plot, what the audience should feel, what the audience should think, and what the audience should wonder afterward.
**Assumed goal**: Writer's Digest editorial tradition requiring scenes to be self-contained mini-stories serving multiple narrative functions simultaneously -- episodic or vignette-based fiction may not require all four functions in every unit.
**Score 0**: The scene cannot answer even one of the four questions clearly. Its purpose in the narrative is unclear, it generates no specific emotional response, conveys no thematic meaning, and creates no forward-looking curiosity.
**Score 5**: The scene answers all four: 1) "The purpose of this scene is to ___" (plot), 2) "The audience should feel ___" (emotion), 3) "The audience should think ___" (theme), 4) "The audience should wonder ___" (suspense). The answers are specific rather than generic, and the scene functions as a self-contained mini-story.
**Source**: https://www.writersdigest.com/write-better-fiction/write-strong-scenes-types-of-scenes
**Notes**: Writer's Digest requires a "dynamic opening which thrusts the reader right into the scene, a middle that delivers something essential to the novel as a whole, and an ending that generates enough suspense to compel the reader into the next scene." Each scene should function as "a separate short story, able to stand on its own."

### Promise-Progress-Payoff at Scene Level
**What it measures**: Whether individual scenes follow the three-part framework of making a promise (setup), showing progress (character effort), and delivering a payoff (resolution of whether the objective was achieved).
**Assumed goal**: Fawkes/commercial fiction craft framework applying story-level structure fractally to scene level -- anti-climactic or deliberately frustrating literary fiction may withhold payoff or subvert promise as a technique.
**Score 0**: Scenes make no identifiable promise (no setup), show no progress (no forward movement), and deliver no payoff (no resolution). Or promises are made but never paid off. Or payoffs arrive without setup.
**Score 5**: The scene opening establishes clear promises about what will happen (time, place, characters, objective, stakes). The middle shows the character taking action toward the objective with forward momentum. The scene peaks with clarity about whether the objective was achieved, fulfilling or deliberately subverting the opening promise.
**Source**: https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2024/10/promise-progress-payoff-in-stories-acts.html
**Notes**: Fawkes illustrates with Harry Potter's first Quidditch match: Promise (match will occur, Harry must catch the Snitch), Progress (gameplay with complications), Payoff (Harry catches the Snitch). The key evaluation: "Scenes work best when they deliver on their initial setup, show meaningful character effort or progression, and resolve with clarity."

### Escalation Within Scenes
**What it measures**: Whether conflict and complications within a scene escalate progressively rather than remaining at a constant level or repeating the same degree of difficulty.
**Assumed goal**: Dramatic fiction tradition (Aristotle's rising action through Swain's escalating conflict) requiring progressive complication within scenes -- absurdist or cyclical fiction may deliberately maintain or repeat conflict levels for thematic effect.
**Score 0**: Conflict within the scene remains at the same level throughout. Obstacles repeat at the same difficulty. The character faces the same degree of opposition at the end as at the beginning. No escalation occurs.
**Score 5**: Complications intensify progressively throughout the scene. Each obstacle is more difficult than the last. The opposition grows stronger as the character becomes more committed. The escalation builds toward the scene's climactic moment, creating a rising curve of tension.
**Source**: https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2021/09/scene-structure-according-to-dwight-v.html
**Notes**: Fawkes on Swain's conflict: "Complications and developments intensify throughout, making the opposition progressively stronger." The Story Grid terms this "turning point progressive complication" -- the complication that turns the scene must represent a progression from earlier complications, not a repetition.


## Sentinels Found

### Uniform Scene Template
**Detection**: All or nearly all scenes follow an identical structural pattern -- same length, same opening type, same turning point mechanism, same ending type -- with no variation in structure across the work.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate text by predicting the most likely next token, which produces structural uniformity. Once a scene template is established, the model replicates it because deviation requires the kind of creative risk-taking that token prediction actively resists. Record Crash notes AI fiction shows "identically structured paragraphs throughout."
**Design space cost**: Eliminates the pacing variety that comes from alternating scene types, lengths, and structures. Every scene feels the same regardless of content, producing reader fatigue.
**Source**: https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction

### Scenes Without Polarity Shifts
**Detection**: Multiple scenes end with the same value-charge they began with -- activity occurs but nothing meaningful changes for the character.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs can generate surface-level activity (dialogue, description, movement) without tracking the underlying value states that give scenes dramatic purpose. The model produces content that looks like a scene but is actually a "nonevent" in McKee's terms.
**Design space cost**: Scenes without value shifts fail to advance the story. They consume reader attention without delivering narrative progress, creating the feeling that "nothing happens."
**Source**: https://mckeestory.com/do-your-scenes-turn/

### Missing Sequel / No Emotional Processing
**Detection**: Characters jump directly from one disaster to the next goal without any emotional reaction, deliberation, or decision-making between scenes.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs trained on action-heavy fiction tend to skip sequel beats because they are less "dramatic" and harder to generate convincingly. The model optimizes for forward momentum at the expense of psychological plausibility. Grief scenes in AI fiction "sound like Hallmark cards" when they are attempted.
**Design space cost**: Characters feel robotic -- moving from crisis to crisis without human emotional processing. Reader empathy breaks because the character never processes what happened to them.
**Source**: https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2021/10/sequel-structure-according-to-swain.html

### White Room Dialogue (Talking Heads)
**Detection**: Dialogue exchanges occur with zero physical grounding -- no blocking, no description, no environmental interaction, no sensory detail. Characters are disembodied voices.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs default to generating dialogue without spatial context because dialogue is the most common training text pattern. Physical grounding requires maintaining spatial state across paragraphs, which token prediction handles poorly. The result is what Fawkes calls "heads floating in space, talking."
**Design space cost**: The reader cannot visualize the scene. Character personality that would be revealed through gesture, movement, and environmental interaction is lost. Scenes feel like radio plays rather than immersive fiction.
**Source**: https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2024/03/how-to-fix-talking-heads-in-your-story.html

### Scenes Opening with Exposition Dumps
**Detection**: Scenes consistently begin with paragraphs of backstory, world-building, character history, or situational description before any inciting incident or action occurs.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs front-load context because their architecture requires establishing contextual tokens before generating action tokens. This produces scenes where the model "explains" the situation before dramatizing it -- the opposite of the "enter late" principle. Fawkes notes: "Many writers have the tendency to want to look backward at a past moment in scene openings."
**Design space cost**: Scene openings lose their hook. Readers must push through inert exposition to reach the dramatic content. The scene's pacing is back-loaded rather than front-loaded.
**Source**: https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2022/03/pacing-within-scenes.html

### All Scenes End with Clean Resolution
**Detection**: Every scene concludes with the character's goal achieved, complications resolved, and no unfinished business -- "Yes" endings throughout.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs are trained to produce complete, coherent text units. Each scene generation tends toward closure because the model treats the scene as a self-contained completion task. The "Yes, But" and "No, And Furthermore" outcomes that drive narrative forward require deliberate structural planning that token prediction doesn't perform.
**Design space cost**: Forward momentum dies. If every scene resolves cleanly, there is no reason for the reader to continue. The cumulative effect of escalating complications -- the engine of plot -- is absent.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/types-of-scene-endings/

### On-the-Nose Dialogue (No Subtext)
**Detection**: Characters state their emotions, intentions, and motivations directly in dialogue. There is no gap between what characters say and what they mean. No tension exists between surface and depth.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate the most probable next token, which means characters say what needs to be communicated rather than what a psychologically real person would say. Creating subtext requires withholding information -- generating tokens that don't directly convey the intended meaning -- which conflicts with the model's optimization for clarity and coherence.
**Design space cost**: Reader engagement drops because there is nothing to interpret. The "magic of writing subtext -- the tension between the lines of dialogue, the unspoken pauses" -- is absent. Scenes feel flat and obvious.
**Source**: https://www.writersdigest.com/write-better-fiction/how-to-use-subtext-and-the-art-of-dramatic-tension-in-fiction

### Oscillating Prose Modes
**Detection**: The prose alternates between two distinct registers within scenes -- overwrought purple prose and terse, cliched action sentences -- without a consistent narrative voice.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Record Crash identifies this as a primary AI fiction tell: "loquacious ponderous similes and tiny sentences made out of cliches." LLMs lack a stable authorial voice and instead sample from different stylistic registers in their training data, producing an inconsistent mixture that no single human author would produce.
**Design space cost**: The narrative voice -- the single most distinctive element of a fiction author -- is absent. The reader cannot identify "who is telling this story" because the voice changes paragraph to paragraph.
**Source**: https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction

### Context Poisoning (Escalating Cliche)
**Detection**: Once a scene establishes a mediocre stylistic pattern (cliche, repetitive structure, overwrought metaphor), subsequent paragraphs replicate and amplify that pattern rather than course-correcting.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Unlike human writers who notice and correct repetition, LLMs "stick to instructions and generate text that fits the previous paragraph." Once the context window contains a cliche, the model is more likely to generate similar cliches. Record Crash calls this "context poisoning" -- "once a story reaches a pattern of cliches, it won't stop going to the same well."
**Design space cost**: Quality degrades monotonically within scenes. What begins as mediocre prose becomes increasingly formulaic, and the model cannot self-correct.
**Source**: https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction

### Flat Emotional Arcs Within Scenes
**Detection**: Characters maintain the same emotional state and intensity from the beginning to the end of scenes. No emotional progression, no building intensity, no shifts triggered by scene events.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs establish a character's emotional state early in a scene and then maintain it as contextual "fact" rather than tracking how events should modify emotional intensity. The model generates consistent emotional tags rather than progressive emotional arcs. AI emotional scenes are noted to be "well-executed but boring and paint-by-numbers."
**Design space cost**: Characters feel static within scenes. The emotional progression that makes scenes feel alive -- building from mild concern to genuine fear, from curiosity to horror -- is absent. Scenes read as emotional snapshots rather than emotional journeys.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/characters-emotional-arc/

### Generic Scene Stakes
**Detection**: Scene stakes are stated in vague, generic terms ("everything was at stake," "failure meant disaster," "this was important") without specifying precisely what the character stands to lose and why it matters to them personally.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate high-drama language ("the stakes couldn't be higher") without grounding stakes in specific character circumstances. Token prediction produces dramatic-sounding but content-free stakes because specificity requires maintaining character-state knowledge that the model handles unreliably.
**Design space cost**: Vague stakes fail to register emotionally regardless of their stated magnitude. "It's not about how big the stakes are. It's about whether they're clearly communicated."
**Source**: https://www.kevintjohns.com/are-you-killing-the-tension-high-stakes-writing-mistakes/

### Obstacles Without Consequences
**Detection**: Characters encounter obstacles that are overcome without struggle, hard choices, or lasting consequences. The obstacles exist but the character simply navigates past them.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate obstacles because they recognize they should exist structurally, but then resolve them easily because the model is optimizing for forward narrative movement. Hardy's example: "Reaching the oracle wasn't hard, because no matter how difficult those obstacles might have seemed, they caused no struggle or challenge."
**Design space cost**: The distinction between obstacles and genuine conflict is lost. Without struggle, hard choices, and consequences, the scene lacks dramatic tension regardless of how many "obstacles" appear.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/create-meaningful-obstacles-via-conflict/

### Rule-of-Three Lists in Scene Description
**Detection**: Description, action, and emotional beats consistently appear in lists of exactly three items ("The room was dark, cold, and silent. She felt fear, confusion, and determination.").
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs overuse the "rule of three" because it is a statistically common pattern in training data. Record Crash identifies this pattern specifically: "Lists of exactly three items (e.g., 'geometry of madness, a wound in the fabric of what is, older than law and time')." This produces description that sounds processed rather than observed.
**Design space cost**: Sensory description loses the organic irregularity of human observation. Real perception doesn't arrive in neat triads.
**Source**: https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction

### Episodic Scene Sequence
**Detection**: Consecutive scenes are connected only by featuring the same characters. No scene's outcome drives the next scene's situation. Scenes could be reordered without creating logical problems.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate each scene as an independent unit. Maintaining causal state across scenes (what happened in scene 3 must affect scene 4's opening conditions) requires long-range state tracking that token prediction handles poorly. The result is "and then" sequencing rather than "because of that" causality.
**Design space cost**: The story lacks narrative drive. Without causal chains, "scenes come out of the blue, and your story turns episodic." The reader has no sense that events are building toward something.
**Source**: https://www.beemgee.com/blog/cause-effect-scene-connectedness/

### Formulaic Scene Openings
**Detection**: Scenes consistently open with the same pattern -- e.g., always with setting description, always with dialogue, always with the character waking up or arriving somewhere.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs learn a "scene opening template" from training data and reapply it consistently. Human writers naturally vary their openings; LLMs converge on whatever pattern is most statistically probable. The monotony of identical openings is a structural tell even when the content varies.
**Design space cost**: Scene openings -- the first impression of each new narrative unit -- become predictable and lose their ability to hook the reader. Structural monotony at the opening position is particularly damaging because it is the reader's first experience of each new scene.
**Source**: https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction

### Sensory Detail Absence or Overload
**Detection**: Either scenes contain no sensory detail at all (white room syndrome) or they stack excessive, implausible sensory metaphors on single concepts ("the air tasted like shattered glass laced with forgotten promises").
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs either omit sensory detail because it requires spatial-state maintenance, or overgenerate it because metaphor-heavy prose is well-represented in training data. Record Crash identifies "eyeball kicks -- extreme, nonsensical metaphors" as an AI tell. The binary failure (absent or excessive) reflects the model's inability to calibrate.
**Design space cost**: When absent, scenes lack immersion. When excessive, scenes become absurd. The middle ground -- specific, character-filtered, purposeful sensory detail -- is where craft lives.
**Source**: https://www.writingmastery.com/blog/avoiding-white-room-syndrome-tips-to-ground-your-fiction-effectively

### Passive Protagonist Throughout Scenes
**Detection**: The POV character consistently reacts to events rather than initiating them. Other characters or circumstances drive every scene while the protagonist observes, endures, or follows.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs tend to generate reactive prose because the most common narrative pattern in training data is "something happened, then the character responded." Generating proactive characters requires the model to plan future events (what the character will attempt) before describing them, which conflicts with left-to-right token generation.
**Design space cost**: "If your hero is just a pinball bouncing from event to event, there are no stakes -- only randomness." Reader investment requires the character to drive events through choice and initiative.
**Source**: https://www.kevintjohns.com/are-you-killing-the-tension-high-stakes-writing-mistakes/

### Repeated Emotional Tags Instead of Emotional Arcs
**Detection**: Characters are tagged with the same emotion repeatedly throughout a scene ("she felt nervous... she was nervous... her nervousness grew...") rather than showing emotional progression through changing behavior, thought, and physiological response.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs maintain character emotional states as repeated tokens rather than modeling emotional progression. The model generates "she felt X" declarations because they are syntactically simple and contextually consistent. The more complex craft of showing emotional change through behavior requires multi-paragraph planning.
**Design space cost**: Emotional telling replaces emotional showing. The reader is informed of the character's emotion rather than experiencing it through the character's changing thoughts, actions, and physical sensations.
**Source**: https://writershelpingwriters.net/2025/09/how-to-differentiate-stories-from-ai-slop/

### Scene-Level Information Dump
**Detection**: A scene's primary function is to deliver information to the reader through characters explaining things to each other (maid-and-butler dialogue), extended internal exposition, or narrator-directed lectures.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs handle information delivery more reliably than dramatization because exposition is structurally simpler than conflict. When the model needs to convey plot information, it defaults to the most direct method (characters stating facts) rather than embedding information in dramatic action.
**Design space cost**: Information delivery without dramatic purpose creates scenes that fail the deletion test. Friedman states scenes are not "explanatory lectures to readers" or "extended backstory or location histories."
**Source**: https://janefriedman.com/writing-scene/

### Seven Deadly Static Scene Types
**Detection**: The scene is identifiable as one of the seven "nothing happens" types: daily routine, backstory dump, chitchat, excessive description, pointless internal monologue, been-there-done-that repetition, or "just killing time" filler.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Each of these seven types represents a common LLM failure mode. Daily routines and chitchat are statistically common in training data. Backstory dumps arise from the model's need to establish context. Excessive description results from the model generating tokens without tracking dramatic purpose. LLMs cannot self-diagnose "is something happening in this scene?"
**Design space cost**: Weiland's diagnostic: "What is the purpose of this scene? If you cannot provide an enthusiastic, sentence-length answer, consider eliminating it." Each of these seven types consumes reader attention without delivering narrative value.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/most-common-mistakes-series-is-nothin/

### Emotional Flatness in High-Stakes Scenes
**Detection**: During scenes that should be emotionally intense (confrontation, loss, revelation, decision), the prose maintains the same emotional temperature as routine scenes. No heightened language, no physiological detail, no altered perception.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Writers Helping Writers notes AI "can't dig into what situations feel like and what they mean to our characters." LLMs generate structurally appropriate content (the right events happen) without modulating the prose register to match emotional intensity. The result is "grief scenes that sound like Hallmark cards" and characters who "cry on cue while their internal transformations feel hollow."
**Design space cost**: The most important scenes in the story -- the ones that should hit hardest emotionally -- read with the same flat affect as transitional scenes. The reader cannot distinguish crucial moments from routine ones by prose quality alone.
**Source**: https://writershelpingwriters.net/2025/09/how-to-differentiate-stories-from-ai-slop/

### Entrance/Exit Ceremony
**Detection**: Scenes consistently include characters arriving, exchanging greetings, settling in, and then at the end, saying goodbye, getting up, and departing -- rather than entering late and exiting early.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate "complete" social interactions because greetings and departures are high-frequency patterns in training data. The model lacks the editorial judgment to recognize that these social ceremonies add nothing dramatic. The Hitchcock principle -- "drama is life with the dull bits cut out" -- requires active trimming that the model doesn't perform.
**Design space cost**: Significant portion of each scene is consumed by dramatically inert material. The story moves at the pace of real life rather than the pace of drama.
**Source**: https://screenplayreaders.com/get-into-scene-late-leave-early/

### Setup Without Payoff / Payoff Without Setup
**Detection**: The scene introduces elements (objects, character traits, setting details) that never become relevant, or delivers dramatic moments that depend on information never established.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate scene-local content without tracking the broader narrative web of setups and payoffs. Objects described in one scene are not tracked for later discharge. Climactic moments may reference information the model generated on the fly rather than information properly set up. This reflects the model's inability to plan across scene boundaries.
**Design space cost**: Unfired Chekhov's guns create false expectations. Payoffs without setups feel like deus ex machina. The narrative web of promise and fulfillment -- which creates reader satisfaction -- is absent.
**Source**: https://janefriedman.com/writing-scenes-setup-payoff/

### Disproportionate Scene Investment
**Detection**: Trivial scenes (travel, routine, transitions) receive the same word count and detail as crucial scenes (confrontation, revelation, decision). No scene is treated as more important than any other.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate at a relatively uniform density because token prediction doesn't distinguish between dramatically important and unimportant content. The model gives each scene roughly equivalent treatment because it lacks the editorial judgment to compress the unimportant and expand the crucial.
**Design space cost**: "Pacing is proportional." When trivial scenes get the same investment as crucial ones, the reader cannot tell which moments matter. Important scenes fail to land because they are not distinguished from filler.
**Source**: https://kristenstieffel.com/pacing-matter-proportion/
