## Criteria Found

### Diction Precision and Intentionality
**What it measures**: Whether word choices are specific, accurate, and deliberately selected for their denotative and connotative effects rather than reaching for the first available or most generic option.
**Assumed goal**: All schools of prose craft agree that word choice should be intentional; the debate is about the TYPE of words chosen (plain vs. ornate), not whether they should be chosen with care.
**Score 0**: Words are vague, generic, or randomly selected. Vocabulary is either inappropriately simple or inappropriately complex for the narrative context. Abstract nouns dominate where concrete ones would serve. Words are used imprecisely (near-misses in meaning). Connotation is ignored.
**Score 5**: Every word earns its place. Denotation and connotation are both controlled. Vocabulary fits the narrative register. Concrete, specific nouns and precise verbs dominate. When uncommon words appear, they appear because no common word captures the exact meaning. The writer demonstrates Flaubert's "le mot juste" — the exact right word in every position.
**Source**: https://writers.com/word-choice-in-writing, https://liberalarts.oregonstate.edu/wlf/what-diction-literature-definition-examples, https://www.craftliterary.com/2024/01/24/insinuating-life-diction-syntax-short-story/
**Notes**: Diction encompasses both denotation (literal dictionary meaning) and connotation (contextual associations). Differences in characters' diction can reveal subtle ironies. Diction types include formal, informal, colloquial, slang, abstract, concrete, pedantic, and poetic. Oregon State defines diction as "an author's or narrator's or character's choice of words."

### Verb Strength and Dynamic Action
**What it measures**: Whether the prose uses precise, active, dynamic verbs rather than relying on weak verbs propped up by adverbs, or on nominalizations that drain energy from sentences.
**Assumed goal**: The Strunk-White-King tradition where active, precise verbs are the engine of good prose, and where adverb+weak verb combinations signal lazy writing that tells rather than shows.
**Score 0**: Prose relies on "was," "had," "got," "went," and other colorless verbs. Adverbs prop up weak verbs throughout ("walked slowly," "said angrily"). Nominalizations convert verbs into nouns requiring helper words ("made a decision" instead of "decided"). Passive voice obscures agency throughout.
**Score 5**: Verbs are precise and dynamic, carrying the weight of the prose ("sauntered," "snapped," "devoured" instead of "walked slowly," "said angrily," "ate quickly"). Passive voice appears only when the agent is genuinely unknown or when centering the receiver serves the narrative. Adverbs appear only when meaning genuinely shifts ("almost laughed" vs. "ran quickly").
**Source**: https://bubblecow.com/blog/writing-craft/style-and-voice/common-style-mistakes-that-weaken-prose/, https://www.themarginalian.org/2013/03/13/stephen-king-on-adverbs/, https://www.well-storied.com/blog/how-fiction-writers-can-improve-quality-of-prose
**Notes**: Stephen King: "I believe the road to hell is paved with adverbs." The replacement test: Can you replace the adverb+verb with a single stronger verb? BubbleCow suggests adverb density of fewer than 5 per 100 words as a diagnostic metric. Nominalizations ("provide an explanation" -> "explain") are a specific verb-weakening pattern.

### Sentence Variety and Rhythmic Variation
**What it measures**: Whether the prose varies sentence length, structure, and type to create rhythmic interest, or whether it falls into monotonous repetitive patterns.
**Assumed goal**: The Gary Provost tradition where prose is musical composition — short sentences punch, medium sentences carry, long sentences build — and the writer orchestrates them deliberately.
**Score 0**: Sentences are uniformly the same length and structure. The prose reads like a metronome. Subject-verb-object repeats without variation. No fragments, no complex sentences, no deliberate variation. Alternatively, all sentences are long and complex with no short ones for contrast.
**Score 5**: Sentence length and structure vary deliberately. Short sentences create tension, impact, or emphasis. Longer sentences provide reflection, description, or emotional build. The prose has what Gary Provost called "a pleasant rhythm, a lilt, a harmony." Occasionally a sentence "burns with energy and builds with all the impetus of a crescendo." Fragments appear sparingly for emphasis. The overall effect is musical.
**Source**: https://www.aerogrammestudio.com/2014/08/05/this-sentence-has-five-words/, https://www.dabblewriter.com/articles/what-makes-good-prose, https://bubblecow.com/blog/writing-craft/style-and-voice/common-style-mistakes-that-weaken-prose/
**Notes**: Gary Provost's famous passage demonstrates the monotony of five-word sentences vs. the music of varied lengths. BubbleCow recommends average sentence length of 15-20 words as a baseline. Well-Storied notes that short/choppy sentences increase pace (effective in action, mental instability) while longer sentences provide a more languid reading experience.

### Syntactic Sophistication (Hypotaxis vs. Parataxis)
**What it measures**: Whether the writer commands a range of syntactic structures — from simple paratactic sequences to complex hypotactic constructions with subordinate clauses — and deploys them purposefully to create meaning.
**Assumed goal**: The CRAFT Literary tradition where syntax is not neutral scaffolding but an active meaning-making tool — where the arrangement of clauses within a sentence creates suspense, restraint, urgency, or reflection at the sentence level.
**Score 0**: Syntax is accidental and uncontrolled. All sentences follow the same clause pattern. The writer shows no awareness of how clause arrangement creates effect. Subordinate clauses appear randomly rather than purposefully. Subject-verb distance is either always minimal or always excessive.
**Score 5**: The writer deliberately employs hypotactic structures (complex sentences with subordinate clauses delaying the main clause) to create suspense or intellectual density, and paratactic structures (simple sequential clauses) to create urgency, restraint, or matter-of-factness. The "fundament" (subject + predicate) is positioned to control pacing — at the end for suspense, at the beginning for immediacy. Syntactic choices serve the emotional and thematic content of each passage.
**Source**: https://www.craftliterary.com/2024/01/24/insinuating-life-diction-syntax-short-story/
**Notes**: CRAFT Literary uses Kevin Barry's lush hypotactic prose as an example of syntax serving elegiac, introspective material, and Raymond Carver's spare paratactic prose as syntax mirroring psychological withholding. The "truth window" technique — breaking from established syntactic patterns momentarily — creates piercing clarity by violating reader expectations.

### Sound and Euphony
**What it measures**: Whether the prose attends to the sonic qualities of language — consonance, assonance, euphony, cacophony — as elements of craft, or whether sound is accidental.
**Assumed goal**: The Le Guin/Francine Prose tradition that prose is never fully divorced from sound, that sentences should work both visually and aurally, and that sonic qualities contribute to meaning and emotional effect.
**Score 0**: No awareness of how words sound together. Accidental tongue-twisters or awkward sound collisions throughout. Prose that stumbles when read aloud. No use of consonance, assonance, or other sound devices. The sonic texture is random.
**Score 5**: The writer selects words partly for their sonic qualities. Hard consonant sounds (crack, slam, jab) appear in violent or urgent passages. Soft consonants and long vowels (meandering, luminous, solitude) appear in contemplative passages. Strategic repetition of sounds (consonance, assonance) intensifies meaning. Prose reads smoothly aloud. As Francine Prose noted: a sentence you can hardly pronounce without stumbling needs reworking.
**Source**: https://www.supersummary.com/sound-devices/, https://liberalarts.oregonstate.edu/wlf/what-are-euphony-and-cacophony, https://www.ursulakleguin.com/steering-the-craft, https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reading_Like_a_Writer
**Notes**: Le Guin's Steering the Craft includes an exercise specifically about sound: "Write a paragraph to a page of narrative that's meant to be read aloud, using onomatopoeia, alliteration, repetition, rhythmic effects." Euphony uses long vowels, semi-vowels, and soft consonants (l, m, n, r) to calm; cacophony uses explosive consonants to unsettle. Monosyllabic words with hard consonants feel faster and more violent; polysyllabic words with softer sounds feel slower and more contemplative.

### Prose Transparency vs. Opacity (Windowpane vs. Stained Glass)
**What it measures**: Where the prose sits on the spectrum from transparent (invisible, reader looks THROUGH the prose to the content) to opaque (ornate, the prose itself IS the content and demands attention as a crafted object), and whether this position is deliberate and consistent.
**Assumed goal**: There are two legitimate and opposing traditions here. The Orwell "windowpane" tradition holds that "Good prose should be transparent, like a window pane" — style should not draw attention to itself. The Nabokov/Joyce "stained glass" tradition holds that style IS content and linguistic play is the point. The criterion is not WHICH position the writer takes but whether the position is (a) deliberate and (b) consistent with the work's genre and aims.
**Score 0**: The prose's position on the transparency-opacity spectrum appears accidental. The writer alternates randomly between invisible and ornate prose with no apparent purpose. Genre-inappropriate: literary fiction that reads like a manual, or thriller prose that reads like Proust. The style-content relationship is unexamined.
**Score 5**: The writer has a clear, deliberate position on the transparency-opacity spectrum that serves the work's aims. If transparent: the prose efficiently delivers story without calling attention to itself (Orwell, Hemingway, Leonard). If opaque: the prose rewards attention to its own craft and linguistic texture (Nabokov, Morrison, McCarthy). If the writer modulates between the two, the shifts serve specific narrative purposes.
**Source**: https://www.midwestromancewriters.com/post/windowpane-vs-stained-glass-prose, https://rlfconsultants.com/orwellian-versus-stained-glass-window-prose/, http://mindfulpleasures.blogspot.com/2012/06/transparency-translucency-opacity-some.html, https://www.goodreads.com/quotes/147338-good-prose-should-be-transparent-like-a-window-pane
**Notes**: The Mindful Pleasures blog identifies a three-point continuum: transparent (Orwell, Hemingway), translucent (most literary fiction), and opaque (Proust, Bernhard, Faulkner, Nabokov). Translucent prose "tends to lengthen sentences to the point of absurdity, to promiscuously compound subordinate clauses, to break up the reading process by means of footnotes, endnotes, parentheses."

### Authorial Voice Distinctiveness
**What it measures**: Whether the prose has a recognizable, distinctive authorial personality that persists across scenes and would be identifiable even out of context, or whether it reads as generic, interchangeable writing.
**Assumed goal**: The tradition (Bransford, Fawkes, MasterClass) that voice is "the sensibility with which an author writes — a perspective, an outlook on the world, a personality and style that is recognizable even out of context" — and that the more distinctive and authentic the voice, the more compelling the prose.
**Score 0**: The prose has no discernible personality. It could have been written by anyone. It reads as generic "competent writing" without attitude, worldview, or verbal quirks. There is no sense of a particular human intelligence behind the words. The diction, rhythm, and concerns are interchangeable.
**Score 5**: The prose has a distinctive verbal personality — particular rhythms, obsessions, humor, attitudes, and relationship with language — that would be recognizable across different passages. Like Toni Morrison's "florid, almost trance-like narration" or Hemingway's "terse, economical prose," the voice is unmistakably itself. This does not mean flashy — Carver's voice is distinctive precisely in its restraint.
**Source**: https://www.masterclass.com/articles/how-to-find-your-writing-voice, https://nathanbransford.com/blog/2010/05/how-to-craft-great-voice, https://blog.homeforfiction.com/2018/01/03/authorial-voice-develop-style-fiction/, https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2022/02/author-voice-vs-narrator-voice-vs.html
**Notes**: Fawkes defines voice as "What a Person Thinks or Talks about + How She Says It = Voice." Author voice includes preferred genres, tropes, themes, vocabulary breadth, sentence structure preferences, and even what the writer avoids writing. Bransford: voice is "a perspective, an outlook on the world." The Russian nesting doll structure: author voice (largest) contains narrator voice, which contains character voice(s).

### Voice Layering (Author/Narrator/Character)
**What it measures**: Whether the prose properly distinguishes between and manages the nested layers of voice — authorial voice, narrator voice, and individual character voices — or whether all voices collapse into one undifferentiated register.
**Assumed goal**: The Fawkes/Write It Scared tradition that fiction contains nested voice layers (author > narrator > character), each with distinct properties, and that skillful prose manages these layers without confusion — the narrator can dip into character voice, but characters should not accidentally adopt the narrator's diction.
**Score 0**: All characters, the narrator, and the implied author speak with the same voice. A teenager and a professor use the same vocabulary and syntax. Dialogue and narration are tonally identical. There is no voice differentiation across the cast. The "Russian nesting doll" has collapsed into a single layer.
**Score 5**: Author voice provides the overarching sensibility. The narrator has a distinct voice suited to the story's needs (omniscient, close third, etc.). Individual characters have recognizable verbal fingerprints — particular vocabulary, syntax, cadence, and speech patterns reflecting their backgrounds, education, and personalities. The writer can shift between character perspectives with distinct vocal signatures. As in Twain, where Tom Sawyer and Huckleberry Finn have different narrators despite the same authorial hand.
**Source**: https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2022/02/author-voice-vs-narrator-voice-vs.html, https://www.writeitscared.co/blog-3/layers-of-voice-author-narrator-character, https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/character-voices/
**Notes**: Daniel Handler (author) creates Lemony Snicket (narrator) as a distinct voice entity. Character voice is driven by "what they think" (content) as much as "how they talk" (style). What remains unsaid also communicates voice. Using dialogue tics, personalized slang, and metaphor families enhances character distinctiveness. A "style profile" for each character including key phrases, attitudes, and speech patterns is recommended.

### Tonal Control and Modulation
**What it measures**: Whether the prose establishes and maintains an appropriate tone — the attitude of the narrator toward story events — and modulates it purposefully when it changes, rather than shifting tone arbitrarily or maintaining a rigid monotone.
**Assumed goal**: The Saunders tradition that tonal consistency does not mean rigidity — "A person is not well-represented by a single, static voice" — but that tonal shifts must reflect character growth, escalating stakes, or psychological state changes rather than authorial carelessness. Worldview consistency matters more than voice uniformity.
**Score 0**: Tone lurches between registers without apparent purpose. Serious passages suddenly become flippant. Comic passages turn grim without transition. The reader feels whiplash. Alternatively, the tone is a rigid monotone that never modulates regardless of emotional content. If you throw in a passage outside the tone you've set — a poetic bit in serious prose or a joke in a tragic scene — readers are jolted out of the story.
**Score 5**: Tone is established early and maintained as a coherent baseline. When tone shifts, it shifts purposefully — reflecting character development, rising stakes, or changing psychological state (as in Saunders' "Jon," where the narrator's voice becomes progressively more articulate as urgency increases). Tonal modulation is gradual enough that readers feel the shift as narrative progression rather than betrayal. The changing tone reflects a changing narrator responding to events.
**Source**: https://georgesaunders.substack.com/p/consistency-of-tone, https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/story-tone-in-fiction/, https://theeditorsblog.net/2013/04/19/tone-mood-style-the-feel-of-fiction/, https://nownovel.com/tone-in-writing/
**Notes**: Saunders warns against "trying too hard to keep the voice consistent" — this counterproductive effort amounts to resisting natural voice evolution. "If you write a few lines, and like them, and then start trying to duplicate that tone — you've already fallen out of relation with your story." Morrison shifts tones with extraordinary control, with passages of lyrical beauty alternating with passages of stark horror, corresponding to explorations of memory, trauma, and survival.

### Register Appropriateness and Consistency
**What it measures**: Whether the prose uses a level of formality (register) appropriate to its narrative context, characters, and genre, and maintains register consistency within scenes while allowing purposeful register shifts across contexts.
**Assumed goal**: The tradition that register is a key component of believability and immersion — a character's language should reflect their social position, education, emotional state, and setting — and that register violations (a medieval peasant saying "no worries, bro") break the fictional contract.
**Score 0**: Register is inappropriate to the work's setting or genre. Characters speak at the same level of formality regardless of context. A historical novel uses modern slang without ironic purpose. A literary novel reads like a corporate memo. Register shifts happen within single paragraphs without narrative justification.
**Score 5**: The prose's register matches its narrative context. Formal and informal language appear in appropriate proportions. Characters code-switch between registers when contexts change (speaking to a friend vs. an authority figure). Register is period-appropriate in historical fiction. The narrator's register suits the narrative stance. Register shifts between characters reflect their distinct social positions and backgrounds.
**Source**: https://wordsliketrees.wordpress.com/2020/08/16/register/, https://nownovel.com/tone-in-writing/, https://historicalnovelsociety.org/language-historical-fiction-an-exploration-of-style-idiom-anachronism/
**Notes**: Register ranges from vulgar through casual, consultative, formal, to frozen/ritualistic. First-person narrators tell their stories through specific registers with powerful impact on reader perception. A character under duress might shift from articulate, complex sentences to clipped, fragmented utterances. In historical fiction, linguistic anachronisms — words implying ideas that had not yet entered anybody's mind — throw readers out of the illusion.

### Figurative Language Quality
**What it measures**: Whether metaphors, similes, and other figurative language are fresh, character-appropriate, and purposeful — or whether they are clichéd, dead, mixed, or generic.
**Assumed goal**: The Orwell tradition that you should "never use a metaphor, simile or other figure of speech which you are used to seeing in print" — combined with the craft principle that figurative language should emerge from the POV character's worldview (a chef's metaphors draw from cooking, a sailor's from the sea).
**Score 0**: Figurative language consists entirely of clichés ("cold as ice," "heart of gold," "eyes like jewels"). Dead metaphors pass without awareness. Mixed metaphors combine incompatible frames ("a tsunami of silence choked the room"). Similes and metaphors are generic rather than character-specific. Alternatively, figurative language is so overwrought it becomes purple prose.
**Score 5**: Metaphors and similes are fresh, surprising, and precise. They emerge organically from character perspective — a mechanic's metaphors involve engines, not flowers. Dead metaphors are avoided or deliberately revived. Extended metaphors sustain a single frame without mixing. Figurative language illuminates rather than decorates — each comparison reveals something about both the tenor and the vehicle. The writer knows when NOT to use figurative language.
**Source**: https://thejohnfox.com/2022/05/100-best-metaphors-ever-written-in-novels/, https://www.liminalpages.com/write-powerful-metaphors/, https://jerichowriters.com/metaphors-dos-and-donts/, https://www.orwellfoundation.com/the-orwell-foundation/orwell/essays-and-other-works/politics-and-the-english-language/
**Notes**: Orwell identified "staleness of imagery" as a common fault of modern prose. Writers should reject the first comparison that comes to mind — it's probably a cliché. Character-specific metaphors (a chef using food metaphors) build voice and characterization simultaneously. Mixed metaphors combine incompatible frames and jar readers. The best metaphors work both for what they reveal and what they conceal.

### Economy of Language (Concision vs. Density)
**What it measures**: Whether the prose achieves its effects with appropriate economy — neither wasting words on padding nor starving the reader of necessary detail — balancing Strunk's "omit needless words" with Le Guin's "crowding" (loading every rift with ore).
**Assumed goal**: The Le Guin "crowding and leaping" tradition where prose should be "crowded with sensations, meanings and implications" while simultaneously leaping over everything not in focus — combined with Strunk's "Omit needless words" and the recognition that "concise writing does not always have the fewest words, but it always uses the strongest ones."
**Score 0**: Prose is padded with filler words (just, really, very, sort of, kind of, actually), redundant pairs (past history, close proximity), throat-clearing openers (It's important to note...), and empty expletives (There is... It is...). Alternatively, prose is so stripped that it starves the reader of necessary sensory or emotional detail.
**Score 5**: Every word earns its place. Filler is eliminated. Redundancies are pruned. The prose is dense with meaning without being verbose. It follows Le Guin's "crowding" principle — vivid, exact, accurate, concrete — while simultaneously "leaping" over what doesn't serve the narrative trajectory. As Le Guin put it: "What you leave out is infinitely more important than what you leave in."
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Elements_of_Style, https://www.ursulakleguin.com/steering-the-craft, https://bubblecow.com/blog/writing-craft/style-and-voice/common-style-mistakes-that-weaken-prose/, https://www.well-storied.com/blog/how-fiction-writers-can-improve-quality-of-prose
**Notes**: Strunk & White's Rule 17: "Omit needless words." Le Guin's "crowding" means avoiding flabby language and clichés, never using ten vague words when two will do. BubbleCow identifies specific padding patterns: throat-clearing openers ("It's important to note..."), redundant pairs ("past history"), empty expletives ("There is a man who wants to speak" -> "A man wants to speak"), hedging overload ("perhaps," "maybe," "seems").

### Filter Word and Psychic Distance Control
**What it measures**: Whether the prose controls psychic distance — the felt closeness between reader and character — through deliberate use or avoidance of filter words (saw, heard, felt, thought, realized, noticed), rather than defaulting to distancing language.
**Assumed goal**: The close-POV tradition where removing filter words brings the reader closer to the character's experience — "A light flickered" vs. "She saw a light flicker" — while recognizing that filters serve a purpose when noticing delay, doubt, or denial matters.
**Score 0**: Filter words proliferate throughout close-POV narration: "She saw the door open. She heard footsteps. She felt afraid. She thought about running." Every perception is mediated through explicit character acknowledgment, creating constant distance between reader and experience.
**Score 5**: Psychic distance is deliberately controlled. In close POV, perceptions are presented directly ("The door opened. Footsteps. Her stomach clenched") without unnecessary filtering. Filter words appear only when the ACT of perceiving matters — when the character's noticing, realizing, or remembering is itself significant. The prose moves fluidly between close and distant perspectives with purposeful control.
**Source**: https://bubblecow.com/blog/writing-craft/style-and-voice/common-style-mistakes-that-weaken-prose/, https://www.louiseharnbyproofreader.com/blog/filter-words-in-fiction-purposeful-inclusion-and-dramatic-restriction, https://pdhines.com/show-character-emotion-filter-words/
**Notes**: Common filter words: saw, heard, noticed, realized, felt, thought, remembered, knew, wondered, decided, watched, looked, seemed. BubbleCow: "She saw a light flicker" -> "A light flickered" (when perception is obvious). Keep filters when noticing delay, doubt, or denial matters to the story. The recommended balance is roughly 70% showing (gesture, setting, subtext) and 30% internalization.

### Micro-Pacing (Sentence/Paragraph Level)
**What it measures**: Whether sentence and paragraph construction serve the pacing needs of each moment — short, staccato sentences for tension and action; longer, flowing sentences for reflection and description; paragraph breaks for speed or density for contemplation.
**Assumed goal**: The tradition that prose pacing operates at the sentence and paragraph level, not just the scene level — that "pacing is about pressure, not speed" and that "the rhythm of your sentences should mirror the content."
**Score 0**: Sentence length and paragraph structure bear no relationship to emotional content. Action scenes use long, meandering sentences. Contemplative moments use rapid-fire fragments. Dense text blocks appear during chase scenes. One-line paragraphs appear during quiet reflection. The prose's micro-pacing works against its content.
**Score 5**: In high-tension scenes, sentences average under twenty words, paragraphs are one to three sentences, and monosyllabic words with hard consonants dominate. In contemplative scenes, sentences stretch, paragraphs expand to four to six sentences, and polysyllabic words with softer sounds create a measured pace. White space on the page increases speed; density slows it. The writer understands that "a thriller isn't good because it's fast; it's good because it's tense."
**Source**: https://sudowrite.com/blog/mastering-pacing-how-to-control-your-storys-rhythm-and-write-pacing-like-a-pro/, https://atmospherepress.com/improve-story-pacing-with-sentence-and-word-counts/, https://www.gilliamwritersgroup.com/blog/microstructures-how-paragraph-shape-affects-narrative-pace
**Notes**: Atmosphere Press: "In high-tension scenes, aim for an average sentence length under twenty words." The real magic of pacing lies in contrast — alternating fast and slow sections. Paragraph shape acts visually on the page: "Dense blocks may daunt or reassure; lots of white space may invite quick reading or provoke unease."

### Showing vs. Telling Balance
**What it measures**: Whether the prose effectively uses sensory detail, action, and concrete imagery to immerse readers in experience (showing), while knowing when efficient summary (telling) serves the narrative better — rather than defaulting entirely to one mode.
**Assumed goal**: The Chekhov tradition ("Don't tell me the moon is shining; show me the glint of light on broken glass") balanced with James Scott Bell's caution that "if you try to do it constantly, the parts that are supposed to stand out won't, and your readers will get exhausted." The goal is strategic deployment of showing for key moments and telling for transitions.
**Score 0**: The prose defaults entirely to telling: "She was angry. The room was beautiful. He felt sad." Emotions are labeled rather than embodied. Alternatively, the prose tries to "show" everything including mundane transitions, exhausting the reader with constant sensory overload and never allowing efficient summary.
**Score 5**: Key emotional and dramatic moments are shown through concrete detail, action, and sensory imagery. The reader experiences the character's anger through physical manifestation and behavioral change rather than the word "angry." Transitions and less important moments are efficiently told. The writer knows which moments deserve the full showing treatment and which are better summarized.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Show,_don't_tell, https://blog.reedsy.com/show-dont-tell/, https://www.well-storied.com/blog/how-fiction-writers-can-improve-quality-of-prose
**Notes**: Chekhov (attributed): "Don't tell me the moon is shining; show me the glint of light on broken glass." Bell: "Sometimes a writer tells as a shortcut, to move quickly to the meaty part of the story or scene." Well-Storied recommends finding "a healthy balance between telling and showing."

### Information Density and Reader Trust
**What it measures**: Whether the prose trusts the reader to make inferences and draw connections, or whether it over-explains, repeats, hedges, and spoon-feeds information — and conversely, whether it provides enough context for the reader to follow without confusion.
**Assumed goal**: The Hemingway "iceberg theory" tradition (what you leave out is as important as what you include) combined with the craft principle that over-explaining insults the reader while under-explaining loses them — the sweet spot trusts readers to be intelligent participants.
**Score 0**: The prose explains everything twice. Subtext is made explicit. Emotions are named after being shown. The same information appears in dialogue, narration, and internal thought. "He slammed the door. He was angry." Or alternatively, critical information is so deeply buried that readers are lost and confused.
**Score 5**: The prose trusts readers to infer. Subtext remains sub. Emotions are embodied rather than labeled. Information appears once, in the most effective mode. The iceberg's mass is below the surface. As Hemingway practiced: "If a writer of prose knows enough of what he is writing about he may omit things that he knows and the reader... will have a feeling of those things."
**Source**: https://www.sleuthsayers.org/2022/08/finding-sweet-spot-between.html, http://blog.janicehardy.com/2018/02/how-over-explaining-will-kill-your-novel.html, https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iceberg_theory, https://tobylitt.wordpress.com/2020/02/14/writing-and-shit-part-32-when-your-writing-is-too-dense-or-not-dense-enough/
**Notes**: Fiction University: over-explaining is "often symptomatic of authorial insecurity, where we distrust our ability to explain things well enough the first time." Toby Litt: "Density is one of the first things a reader notices about a book and one of the last things they remember about it." Hemingway's iceberg theory: "the dignity of movement of an ice-berg is due to only one-eighth of it being above water."

### Cliche Avoidance
**What it measures**: Whether the prose avoids figurative clichés (stock phrases and dead metaphors), prose rhythm clichés (predictable sentence patterns), and descriptive clichés (stock descriptions like "piercing blue eyes") — or whether it relies on pre-fabricated language throughout.
**Assumed goal**: The Orwell rule: "Never use a metaphor, simile or other figure of speech which you are used to seeing in print." Clichés indicate that the writer is reaching for pre-fabricated language rather than observing freshly.
**Score 0**: The prose is built from stock phrases: "at the end of the day," "cold as ice," "heart of gold," "light at the end of the tunnel." Characters have "piercing eyes" and "chiseled jaws." Emotions are described in clichéd terms ("butterflies in her stomach," "his heart sank"). The writer has substituted cultural shorthand for fresh observation.
**Score 5**: The prose avoids or subverts familiar phrases. Descriptions emerge from fresh observation rather than cultural shorthand. When familiar territory must be covered (describing a sunset, a kiss, a fight), the writer finds an angle that hasn't been used a thousand times. If clichés appear, they are deliberately subverted or placed in a character's mouth for characterization purposes.
**Source**: https://www.orwellfoundation.com/the-orwell-foundation/orwell/essays-and-other-works/politics-and-the-english-language/, https://www.scribophile.com/academy/cliches-and-their-place-in-prose, https://writers.com/how-to-avoid-cliches-in-writing, https://www.well-storied.com/blog/how-fiction-writers-can-improve-quality-of-prose
**Notes**: Orwell's "dying metaphors" — phrases like "ring the changes," "Achilles' heel," "swan song" — are used without the writer knowing what is truly being said. Three types of clichés: figurative (stock phrases), character (stereotypes), and plot (predictable story beats). You can subvert a cliché by altering it slightly to render the phrasing less familiar.

### Dialogue Tag and Attribution Craft
**What it measures**: Whether dialogue attribution is handled with skill — using "said" as the invisible default, employing action beats for both attribution and characterization, and avoiding "said bookisms" (opined, interjected, exclaimed) and adverbial tags ("said angrily") that tell rather than show.
**Assumed goal**: The Leonard/King tradition that dialogue tags should be invisible — Leonard's "Never use a verb other than 'said' to carry dialogue" and King's insistence that the reader should forget they are reading — where the goal is for attribution to convey who is speaking without interrupting the flow of dialogue.
**Score 0**: Every dialogue line uses a different fancy tag ("opined," "exclaimed," "murmured," "interjected," "queried"). Adverbs pile onto tags ("said softly," "whispered quietly," "shouted loudly"). Action beats are absent. The attribution itself becomes the focus rather than the dialogue.
**Score 5**: "Said" and "asked" are the primary tags, rendered invisible by familiarity. Action beats replace tags where possible, simultaneously attributing dialogue and revealing character ("'We should leave.' His keys rattled in his hand"). Tags with adverbs appear only in the rarest cases where no other technique conveys the meaning. The reader always knows who is speaking without the attribution calling attention to itself.
**Source**: https://ladyknightediting.com/said-bookism/, https://www.writingclasses.com/toolbox/tips-masters/elmore-leonard-10-rules-for-good-writing, https://www.themarginalian.org/2013/03/13/stephen-king-on-adverbs/, https://bubblecow.com/blog/writing-craft/style-and-voice/common-style-mistakes-that-weaken-prose/
**Notes**: Leonard Rule 3: "Never use a verb other than 'said' to carry dialogue." Leonard Rule 4: "Never use an adverb to modify the verb 'said.'" King: use adverbs in dialogue attribution "only in the rarest and most special of occasions." "Said" is invisible — the brain disregards it while processing dialogue. Said bookisms draw the reader's attention away from the story. BubbleCow: "Cluttered: 'We should leave,' he opined nervously / Clean: 'We should leave,' he said. His keys rattled in his hand."

### Specificity and Concrete Detail
**What it measures**: Whether the prose traffics in specific, concrete, sensory details that create vivid mental images — or in abstract, vague, conceptual language that fails to engage the reader's senses and imagination.
**Assumed goal**: The tradition (Miller, MasterClass, LitReactor) that "fiction's currency is specifics" — that concrete details create engagement, bring stories to life, and make characters vivid, while abstract language feels vague and disconnected.
**Score 0**: The prose deals entirely in abstractions and generalities. "The room was nice." "She felt bad." "It was a beautiful day." No sensory details engage sight, sound, smell, taste, or touch. Settings are generic. Objects are unnamed. The reader cannot form mental images from the language provided.
**Score 5**: The prose is rich with specific, concrete, sensory details. Not "a car" but "a rust-spotted Corolla." Not "she felt bad" but the specific physical and behavioral manifestations. Settings engage multiple senses. Objects are named. The reader can see, hear, smell, taste, and touch the world being described. As the principle goes: "Fiction occurs when the particular penetrates the general."
**Source**: https://kristynjmiller.com/2021/05/22/the-power-of-specificity-in-fiction/, https://www.masterclass.com/articles/how-to-use-concrete-details-to-enhance-your-writing, https://litreactor.com/columns/big-a-little-a-writing-between-the-concrete-and-abstract
**Notes**: LitReactor: "Fiction occurs when the particular penetrates the general, when the specific occurs within the abstract." Balance matters: "Too much specificity can result in rambling purple prose, whereas too little will inevitably result in a forgettable world and flat, two-dimensional characters." The ability to move between specific and abstract is "one of the writer's greatest tools."

### Free Indirect Style Execution
**What it measures**: Whether the prose (when using close third person) successfully employs free indirect discourse — blending the character's voice into the narration without explicit tags like "she thought" — or whether it clumsily signals every shift between narration and character interiority.
**Assumed goal**: The Woolf/Austen/Flaubert tradition of free indirect discourse where the narrator's voice and the character's consciousness merge seamlessly, giving readers the intimacy of first person with the flexibility of third person.
**Score 0**: The prose rigidly separates narration from character thought. Every internal moment is tagged: "She thought that the room was ugly." "He wondered if she would come." The narrator's voice never takes on the character's diction or perspective. Or the prose attempts free indirect style but confuses whose consciousness is being represented.
**Score 5**: Character consciousness flows seamlessly into narration. "The room was ugly" conveys the character's judgment without "she thought." The prose shifts fluidly between narrator perspective and character interiority, with the character's diction, syntax, and emotional state coloring the narration. The technique is executed so smoothly the reader may not notice it operating.
**Source**: https://writers.com/what-is-free-indirect-discourse, https://thehistoryquill.com/free-indirect-discourse-what-it-is-and-why-you-should-use-it/, https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_indirect_speech
**Notes**: Free indirect discourse combines the advantages of first and third person while eliminating the disadvantages. Virginia Woolf in To the Lighthouse and Mrs Dalloway frequently relies on free indirect discourse. Rather than using 'he/she thought' or italics, the character's voice blends into the narration itself. D.H. Lawrence uses it extensively in The Rainbow and Women in Love.

### Purple Prose Avoidance
**What it measures**: Whether the prose maintains appropriate restraint in its descriptive and figurative language, or whether it tips into purple prose — writing so extravagant, flowery, and overwrought that it draws excessive attention to itself while lacking substance.
**Assumed goal**: The tradition that prose should serve the story rather than showcase the writer's vocabulary — where ornate language is acceptable when it genuinely adds meaning (Morrison, McCarthy) but pathological when it substitutes decoration for content.
**Score 0**: The prose is consistently overwrought. Adjectives pile up three or four per noun. Every moment gets the full metaphorical treatment regardless of importance. Descriptions are florid to the point of self-parody. The pace crawls under the weight of unnecessary ornamentation. The voice sounds artificial because the purple prose reflects the author's preening rather than the character's perspective.
**Score 5**: Descriptive intensity is calibrated to narrative importance. Ordinary moments get clean, efficient prose. Significant moments may earn more elaborate treatment, but the elaboration adds meaning rather than mere decoration. The writer knows the difference between rich prose (Morrison's lyrical precision) and purple prose (adjective-stuffed decoration that substitutes for substance).
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Purple_prose, https://lizverity.com/author-advice/writing-advice/beware-of-overwritng-purple-prose-with-examples/, https://thewritepractice.com/purple-prose/
**Notes**: Purple prose is characterized by excessive adjectives, adverbs, and metaphors; long sentences and multisyllabic words; and prose that "calls attention to itself" and "gets in the way of the story." The voice sounds artificial because "most of the time, purple prose isn't a reflection of the character's style; it's the author's." There is no precise rule — deciding if text has fallen victim is subjective.

### Style-Content Alignment
**What it measures**: Whether the prose style serves and reinforces the content and themes of the work — whether form follows function — or whether style and content are disconnected, with the prose working against what the story is trying to accomplish.
**Assumed goal**: The tradition that form and content are inseparable in great fiction — that Carver's spare prose mirrors his characters' emotional withholding, that McCarthy's Biblical cadences serve his apocalyptic themes, that Woolf's stream-of-consciousness style embodies her exploration of subjective experience.
**Score 0**: Style and content work at cross-purposes. A story about restraint and repression is written in lush, indulgent prose. A story about chaos and breakdown is written in neat, orderly paragraphs. A comedy uses the sentence structures of a funeral dirge. The writer has a "style" that they apply regardless of what the story needs.
**Score 5**: Style IS meaning. The prose's rhythm, diction, syntax, and texture reinforce what the story is doing thematically and emotionally. Spare prose serves stories of restraint. Lush prose serves stories of sensory immersion. Fragmented syntax serves stories of psychological disintegration. The writer adapts their style to serve each work's specific needs, as Kevin Barry's hypotactic prose serves elegiac material and Carver's parataxis serves stories of suppressed emotion.
**Source**: https://www.craftliterary.com/2024/01/24/insinuating-life-diction-syntax-short-story/, https://iu.pressbooks.pub/prosefiction/chapter/chapter-6-language/, https://africanliteraturereviews.wordpress.com/content-style-in-literature/
**Notes**: CRAFT Literary: Kevin Barry's lush hypotactic prose suits elegiac, introspective material; Carver's sparse parataxis serves stories about restraint, distance, and suppressed emotion. The "truth window" — breaking from established style momentarily — works precisely because it violates the established style-content alignment.

### POV Consistency at Prose Level
**What it measures**: Whether the prose maintains consistent point-of-view discipline at the sentence level — staying within a single character's perspective within a scene — or whether it "head-hops" between characters' thoughts and perceptions without marking the transition.
**Assumed goal**: The close-POV tradition dominant in contemporary commercial and literary fiction, where staying in one character's head per scene allows deeper emotional immersion and where head-hopping diffuses the reader's connection with any single character.
**Score 0**: The narration jumps between characters' internal states within a single paragraph or scene without transition markers. "Alice felt relief. Ben wondered if she'd share." The reader must re-read passages to determine whose perspective is being represented. The POV character's knowledge is violated — they observe things they couldn't know.
**Score 5**: Each scene maintains a consistent POV. Other characters' states are inferred through observable behavior, not reported directly. When POV shifts between scenes or chapters, clear markers (scene breaks, chapter breaks) signal the transition. The POV character's knowledge boundaries are respected. In omniscient POV, perspective shifts are handled with deliberate transitions rather than random jumps.
**Source**: https://darlingaxe.com/blogs/news/head-hopping, https://www.advancedfictionwriting.com/blog/2014/04/30/head-hopping-fiction-writing/, https://theeditorsblog.net/2011/09/10/head-hopping-gives-readers-whiplash/
**Notes**: Head-hopping "diffuses the most powerful thing about limited POV": deep emotional immersion. Solutions: describe behaviors instead of reporting thoughts, let the POV character make intuitive guesses using words like "seemed" or "appeared," use scene breaks for genuine POV shifts. The more immersive the POV, the more head-hopping stands out.

### Earned Moments and Restraint
**What it measures**: Whether the prose earns its emotional, descriptive, and stylistic moments through contrast and restraint — using quiet passages to make loud moments louder, and exercising discipline about when to deploy its full power — or whether it runs at maximum intensity throughout, depleting its own impact.
**Assumed goal**: The tradition that "the quiet scenes are those delicate solos that carry the emotions, contrasting beautifully with climactic crescendos" — that prose needs dynamic range, and that restraint makes intensity possible.
**Score 0**: Every paragraph operates at maximum emotional or descriptive intensity. Metaphors appear in every sentence. Emotions are always at peak levels. There is no quiet moment, no restraint, no contrast. The prose equivalent of a film that's all explosions — each one diminishing the impact of the next.
**Score 5**: The prose exercises strategic restraint. Quiet passages predominate, creating space for the moments of intensity to land with full impact. The writer trusts that "silence, hesitation, and half-finished gestures can be more powerful than dialogue." Elaborate prose appears at moments of genuine narrative importance. As in music, the rests are as important as the notes.
**Source**: https://www.fictionyogi.com/post/silence-stillness-in-storytelling, https://writerslife.org/quiet-moments-in-fiction/, https://authorspathway.com/types-of-writing/fiction/the-art-of-the-quiet-scene-how-stillness-creates-powerful-fiction/, https://mosby.substack.com/p/quiet-fiction-loud-fiction
**Notes**: "Well-executed quiet scenes create the contrast that makes explosive moments even more impactful." "The absence of action is what makes the action feel explosive when it finally comes." LOUD fiction employs simpler, direct sentences that propel plot forward (Fight Club); Quiet fiction favors reflective, lyrical prose allowing deeper exploration.

### Sensory Engagement
**What it measures**: Whether the prose engages multiple senses (sight, sound, smell, taste, touch, kinesthetic) to create immersive experience, or whether it relies almost entirely on visual description.
**Assumed goal**: The MasterClass/Jericho Writers tradition that sensory language creates vivid imagery by engaging multiple senses, and that non-visual senses (smell, taste, touch, sound) often create stronger emotional connections than visual description alone.
**Score 0**: Description relies entirely on sight. Scenes are described as if viewed through a camera. Sound, smell, taste, touch, and kinesthetic sensation are absent. The world feels flat and visual-only. The reader is a spectator rather than a participant.
**Score 5**: Multiple senses are engaged throughout the prose. The reader experiences scenes through sound, smell, taste, touch, and movement as well as sight. Non-visual senses appear naturally through the POV character's experience. Sensory details are specific (not "it smelled bad" but what it specifically smelled like). The world feels three-dimensional and inhabitable.
**Source**: https://www.masterclass.com/articles/sensory-imagery-in-creative-writing, https://jerichowriters.com/sensory-language-examples-in-fiction/, https://creativewriting.co.nz/mastering-sensory-details-in-writing/
**Notes**: The five types of sensory imagery: visual (sight), auditory (hearing), gustatory (taste), olfactory (smell), tactile (touch), plus kinesthetic (movement/body sensation). MasterClass: "Describing how something tastes, smells, sounds, or feels — not just how it looks — makes a passage come alive."

## Sentinels Found

### AI Vocabulary Density Sentinel
**Detection**: Text contains 3+ of the following words in high frequency: "delve," "tapestry," "vibrant," "landscape" (abstract), "realm," "embark," "vital," "moreover," "crucial," "pivotal," "foster," "underscore," "showcase," "meticulous," "testament," "garner," "bolster," "intricate," "interplay," "enhance," "enduring."
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: These words appear with statistically abnormal frequency in LLM output because they exist in the high-probability center of the model's token distribution. Carnegie Mellon researchers could identify which LLM produced text with 97% accuracy based on characteristic word choices alone.
**Design space cost**: A human author might legitimately use any individual one of these words. But the co-occurrence of multiple AI-favored words at high density is vanishingly rare in human fiction. This sentinel may flag academic or formal prose styles.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Signs_of_AI_writing, https://walterwrites.ai/most-common-chatgpt-words-to-avoid/, https://plusai.com/blog/the-most-overused-chatgpt-words

### Copulative Avoidance / "Stands As" Sentinel
**Detection**: The text repeatedly uses constructions like "serves as," "stands as," "marks," "represents" where a simple "is" would suffice — avoiding the copulative verb in favor of elevated alternatives.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs systematically avoid the copulative "is/was" in favor of more "impressive" phrasing, producing constructions like "The building serves as a landmark" instead of "The building is a landmark." This is a byproduct of training on text where variety was rewarded.
**Design space cost**: Some narrative styles deliberately avoid "is/was" (avoiding the "being verb") as a craft choice. This sentinel may flag prose that intentionally pursues dynamic verb usage.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Signs_of_AI_writing

### Rule of Three Overuse Sentinel
**Detection**: The text repeatedly uses three-item lists — "adjective, adjective, and adjective" or "phrase, phrase, and phrase" — as a dominant structural pattern, appearing in more than 20% of descriptive or analytical passages.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs overuse the tricolon (rhetorical device presenting concepts in threes) because the training data contains many examples of this persuasive technique and the model treats it as a default organizational structure. Human writers use tricolons occasionally; LLMs use them reflexively.
**Design space cost**: The rule of three is a legitimate rhetorical device with a long literary tradition. This sentinel may flag prose that deliberately employs classical rhetoric. The issue is frequency and reflexiveness, not existence.
**Source**: https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction, https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11231544/, https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Signs_of_AI_writing

### "Not X; Y" Negation-Assertion Sentinel
**Detection**: The text repeatedly uses the formulaic construction where a statement is negated and then replaced with a more dramatic assertion: "It was no current. It was a summons." "This was not a meeting. This was a reckoning."
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: This is a specific syntactic pattern that LLMs favor for creating artificial dramatic emphasis. The negation-assertion structure appears statistically safe and "literary" to the model. Human writers use it occasionally; LLMs use it as a default intensification device.
**Design space cost**: This is a legitimate literary construction when used sparingly. A writer who employs it once or twice per chapter for genuine dramatic effect would be falsely flagged. The indicator is repetitive, reflexive use as a go-to pattern.
**Source**: https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction

### Alternating Prose Density Sentinel
**Detection**: The text oscillates between "loquacious ponderous similes" and "tiny sentences made out of clichés" in a regular, predictable pattern — dense purple passage followed by stark simple passage — without narrative justification for the alternation.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs oscillate between two modes: ornate (generating from the "literary" part of their training data) and plain (generating from more casual text). The alternation is mechanical rather than purposeful. Human writers who vary prose density do so in response to content; LLMs do it in response to statistical regression toward different modes.
**Design space cost**: Deliberate alternation between dense and sparse prose is a real craft technique (used for pacing and emphasis). The sentinel targets the UNMOTIVATED, regular, mechanical quality of the alternation rather than alternation per se.
**Source**: https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction

### Subtext Vacuum Sentinel
**Detection**: The text explicitly states the emotional meaning or thematic significance of every scene, image, or exchange — leaving nothing for the reader to infer. Jokes are explained. Themes are announced. After "He looked at the door," the text adds "feeling a strong desire to leave."
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs treat ambiguity, omission, and ellipsis as failure states. They are trained to be "helpful" and "complete," which means they connect every logical step and spell out every implication. Human fiction lives in subtext — what is left unsaid, implied, or suggested. LLMs fill every gap.
**Design space cost**: Some genres (children's fiction, very commercial thriller) may legitimately operate with less subtext. Highly transparent prose styles may also surface more meaning explicitly. This sentinel may flag prose that prioritizes accessibility over subtlety.
**Source**: https://www.thealgorithmicbridge.com/p/10-signs-of-ai-writing-that-99-of, https://alexanderwales.com/adventures-in-ai-text-generation-pt-2-of/

### Semantic Ablation Sentinel
**Detection**: The text systematically replaces specific, concrete, unusual, or high-entropy language with generic, safe, common alternatives. Unconventional metaphors are smoothed to standard ones. Domain-specific jargon is replaced with generic equivalents. Precise adjectives become "important," "significant," or "beautiful."
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Semantic ablation is "the algorithmic erosion of high-entropy information" — a structural byproduct of greedy decoding and RLHF that strips away rare, precise, and complex tokens to maximize statistical probability. The model gravitates toward the center of the distribution, discarding "tail" data.
**Design space cost**: Plain, accessible prose styles legitimately favor common words over unusual ones (Hemingway, Carver). This sentinel targets the pattern of systematic, uniform genericization — where the prose is bland across ALL dimensions rather than strategically simple.
**Source**: https://www.techedubyte.com/ai-writing-generic-boring-dangerous-semantic-ablation/, https://www.theregister.com/2026/02/16/semantic_ablation_ai_writing/

### Harmless Filter / RLHF Blandness Sentinel
**Detection**: The prose avoids all jagged, petty, weird, cynical, or harsh language. It never uses words like "grubby," "sour-mouthed," "mean-spirited," or other vocabulary carrying negative judgment. Instead, it defaults to words like "vital," "crucial," "dynamic" — an HR-department-approved vocabulary.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: RLHF training incentivizes blandness by penalizing outputs that human raters might find offensive or harsh. This "effectively lobotomizes vocabulary of strong emotion or judgment." The result is prose that is "cautious, inoffensive, anodyne, predictable, neutral, and unmemorable."
**Design space cost**: Some fiction (certain romance, inspirational, or children's genres) may legitimately maintain a positive, gentle register throughout. This sentinel would falsely flag those. It targets the absence of ANY tonal edge rather than the presence of positivity.
**Source**: https://www.thealgorithmicbridge.com/p/10-signs-of-ai-writing-that-99-of, https://www.wordrake.com/resources/weaknesses-of-ai-generated-writing

### Abstraction Trap Sentinel
**Detection**: The text uses a high ratio of abstract conceptual vocabulary to concrete, tangible terms. Words like "comprehensive," "foundational," "nuanced," "landscape," "framework" dominate where specific details, names, and sensory language should appear.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs default to abstract vocabulary because abstract terms have wider applicability and higher statistical safety. "The comprehensive landscape of their relationship" is safe; "She picked his socks off the kitchen counter again" requires specific knowledge the model may not have. Abstract language lets the model sound knowledgeable without committing to specifics.
**Design space cost**: Essayistic or philosophical fiction (Musil, Kundera) legitimately employs abstract language as a mode. This sentinel targets narrative fiction that substitutes abstraction for the concrete specificity that fiction requires.
**Source**: https://www.thealgorithmicbridge.com/p/10-signs-of-ai-writing-that-99-of, https://www.wordrake.com/resources/weaknesses-of-ai-generated-writing

### Latinate Bias Sentinel
**Detection**: The text systematically prefers Latinate, multisyllabic words over Germanic, monosyllabic equivalents: "utilize" for "use," "facilitate" for "help," "demonstrate" for "show," "approximately" for "about," "commence" for "begin."
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs are stuck in "business casual" register, preferring the more formal, Latinate option because it is associated with authority and competence in training data. Human writers — especially fiction writers — naturally mix Germanic and Latinate vocabulary based on context and character.
**Design space cost**: Academic narrators, formal first-person voices, or satire of bureaucratic language may legitimately employ Latinate vocabulary. This sentinel targets the uniform, uncontextual preference for Latinate words across all contexts.
**Source**: https://www.thealgorithmicbridge.com/p/10-signs-of-ai-writing-that-99-of

### Treadmill Effect Sentinel
**Detection**: The text hovers over the same ideas at a pragmatic level, restating and rephrasing rather than introducing new information. Paragraphs feel like they are saying the same thing in different words. The rate of new information revelation is very slow despite high word count.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: The autoregressive nature of LLMs means the model "knows next word from latest word, not final destination." It has no thesis to argue toward, no revelation to build toward. It generates plausible-next-sentences that are locally coherent but globally static. The result is prose that feels busy but goes nowhere.
**Design space cost**: Stream-of-consciousness fiction (Woolf, Joyce) may deliberately circle and rephrase as a representation of consciousness. Meditative prose may revisit ideas. This sentinel targets unintentional circularity rather than deliberate thematic recursion.
**Source**: https://www.thealgorithmicbridge.com/p/10-signs-of-ai-writing-that-99-of

### Equivocation Seesaw Sentinel
**Detection**: The text repeatedly uses a structure where one clause makes a claim and the next immediately hedges it: "While X has benefits, it's important to note Y has challenges." "Although impressive, significant hurdles remain." The prose constantly balanced around "however," "on the other hand," "nevertheless."
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs are trained to maximize accuracy while minimizing offense, which produces habitual hedging. Every claim is immediately qualified. This reflects the model's inability to commit to a position — it generates the balanced, both-sides view that minimizes the chance of being "wrong."
**Design space cost**: Genuinely ambivalent narrators or unreliable narrators who equivocate may legitimately employ this pattern. Academic or philosophical fiction may also use it. The indicator is the mechanical, reflexive nature of the hedging rather than purposeful ambivalence.
**Source**: https://www.thealgorithmicbridge.com/p/10-signs-of-ai-writing-that-99-of

### Monotone Emotional Register Sentinel
**Detection**: All characters express emotions with the same vocabulary, syntax, and intensity level. A teenager and a professor grieve the same way on the page. Dialogue lacks individual emotional fingerprints. Emotions are labeled rather than embodied differently per character.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs have a single "voice" that they apply to all characters because they lack the ability to maintain distinct psycholinguistic models for multiple fictional personalities simultaneously. The result is that "a teenager and a professor use the same vocabulary and syntax" — the LLM's default neutral register.
**Design space cost**: Minimalist fiction with a deliberately flat emotional affect (certain Camus, Carver) may present emotions uniformly as a stylistic choice. This sentinel targets the accidental rather than deliberate uniformity.
**Source**: https://www.junia.ai/blog/llm-default-voice-ai-writing, https://www.thealgorithmicbridge.com/p/10-signs-of-ai-writing-that-99-of

### Em Dash Overuse Sentinel
**Detection**: The text uses em dashes (—) at a rate significantly higher than typical human prose — more than 3-4 per 1000 words — particularly in constructions where commas, colons, or parentheses would be more natural.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Earlier GPT models strongly favored em dashes as a punctuation choice, and while newer models have reduced this tendency, it remains a statistical tell. ChatGPT "frequently employs em dashes—like this—in a way that is not commonly taught in high schools or used in everyday writing."
**Design space cost**: Some human authors (Emily Dickinson's poetic tradition, certain essayistic novelists) use em dashes heavily as a deliberate stylistic choice. This sentinel may flag legitimate stylistic preferences.
**Source**: https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction, https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11231544/, https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Signs_of_AI_writing

### Purple Prose / Overwrought Metaphor Sentinel
**Detection**: The text consistently produces florid, overwrought descriptions with stacked metaphors, excessive adjectives, and ornate language regardless of the scene's emotional importance. Every room is described as if it were the most significant room in literature.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs are "trained on the entire internet including fanfiction, amateur blogs, and old Victorian books" and the model is "a predictive engine that guesses that the word after 'eyes' should be 'sparkling' or 'orbs.'" The model defaults to ornate description because flowery language appears extensively in training data.
**Design space cost**: Maximalist literary fiction (Rushdie, Pynchon, Morrison) may legitimately employ dense, elaborate prose. Gothic fiction and certain fantasy subgenres expect ornate description. The indicator is the uncalibrated, uniform ornateness rather than strategic deployment.
**Source**: https://authors.ai/for-florid-purple-prose-just-ask-an-ai/, https://alexanderwales.com/adventures-in-ai-text-generation-pt-2-of/

### Low Burstiness Sentinel
**Detection**: Sentence length and structure show low statistical variance across the text. Sentences cluster around the same length (typically 15-25 words). Syntactic patterns repeat with little variation. The text lacks the unpredictable variation in sentence structure characteristic of human writing.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: "Burstiness" — variation in sentence length and structure — is lower in AI-generated text than in human writing. Human writing "typically exhibits perplexity scores ranging from 20 to 50, reflecting the 'chaos' and unpredictability of human thought." LLMs produce more uniform, predictable sentence structures.
**Design space cost**: Deliberately minimalist prose (Carver, Hemingway) may have low burstiness as a stylistic choice — uniformly short, spare sentences. This sentinel targets the narrow, unremarkable MEDIUM-length uniformity rather than deliberate constraint.
**Source**: https://quillbot.com/blog/ai-writing-tools/burstiness-and-perplexity/, https://gptzero.me/news/perplexity-and-burstiness-what-is-it/

### Emotions-as-Information Sentinel
**Detection**: The text treats emotions as data to be communicated rather than experiences to be felt. Characters' feelings are labeled ("she felt angry"), explained ("because of the betrayal"), and resolved ("she decided to forgive him") in rapid sequence, often within a single paragraph. Emotional complexity is reduced to named states.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: "AI doesn't get emotion wrong because it lacks vocabulary. It gets emotion wrong because it thinks feelings are information to be communicated rather than experiences to be felt." LLMs cannot model the messy, contradictory, often-inarticulable nature of human emotion, so they substitute labeling for embodiment.
**Design space cost**: Some narrative traditions (fairy tales, certain genre fiction, Brechtian distancing) deliberately name emotions rather than embodying them. This sentinel targets the pattern in fiction that INTENDS emotional immersion but achieves only emotional annotation.
**Source**: https://lindamayeadams.com/2024/05/05/the-problem-with-ai-and-fiction-writing/, https://medium.com/@ajtracysk/ai-cant-feel-but-it-can-flinch-how-to-write-emotion-that-readers-actually-feel-6561ecead79b

### Sensing Without Sensing Sentinel
**Detection**: The text makes sensory claims that demonstrate statistical association rather than embodied understanding. "Silk" is always "smooth." Spider webs are always "delicate." Rain is always "cleansing." Sensory descriptions consist of the most statistically common adjective paired with each noun rather than fresh, specific, experiential observation.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs associate sensory descriptors through co-occurrence statistics rather than embodied experience. They pair "silk" with "smooth" because that pairing appears most frequently in training data, not because they have touched silk. Human writers who have touched silk might note its coolness, its tendency to catch on rough skin, or its surprising weight — observations that come from embodied experience rather than word association.
**Design space cost**: Extremely conventional descriptive prose might use common sensory pairings out of laziness rather than AI generation. This sentinel captures a quality of description rather than definitively identifying AI origin.
**Source**: https://www.thealgorithmicbridge.com/p/10-signs-of-ai-writing-that-99-of

### Neat Resolution / Anti-Ambiguity Sentinel
**Detection**: Every emotional, thematic, or narrative thread is explicitly resolved. Endings wrap up cleanly. Characters state what they've learned. Ambiguity is eliminated rather than cultivated. The text avoids leaving anything unresolved, unexplained, or open to interpretation.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs are trained on text where "completeness" is valued, and endings that "close the loop" score well with evaluators. The model treats open-endedness as incompleteness rather than as a deliberate artistic choice. "Ideas get sorted into sections too quickly... endings arrive softly and close the loop without pushing anything forward."
**Design space cost**: Certain genre fiction (romance, mystery) has genre conventions requiring resolution. Children's fiction may also require clarity. This sentinel is most relevant to literary fiction where ambiguity is a feature, not a bug.
**Source**: https://blog.scribeofai.com/unedited-ai-writing/

### "Additionally/Furthermore" Sentence-Opening Sentinel
**Detection**: The text frequently opens sentences with transitional words like "Additionally," "Furthermore," "Moreover," "Importantly," "Notably" — particularly when these transitions add no information and merely pad between statements.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs use mechanical connectors at a "predictable, regular frequency" unusual in human prose. "Additionally" as a sentence-opener is particularly diagnostic — human writers rarely begin fictional prose sentences with it, but LLMs use it as a default transition.
**Design space cost**: Essayistic fiction or academic narrators may legitimately use formal transitions. This sentinel targets their appearance in narrative fiction where they are stylistically inappropriate.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Signs_of_AI_writing, https://blog.frohrer.com/how-to-detect-llm-writing-in-text/

### Self-Contained Scene Syndrome Sentinel
**Detection**: Each generated scene or passage functions as a "standalone" piece rather than a fragment that depends on surrounding context. Scenes include their own setup, development, and mini-resolution without relying on information from previous or subsequent scenes.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate text within a context window and optimize for local coherence rather than global narrative dependency. Each passage tries to be self-sufficient because the model cannot guarantee what came before or will come after. Human fiction scenes are deliberately incomplete — they depend on the reader's accumulated knowledge from earlier scenes.
**Design space cost**: Certain experimental fiction (linked short stories, vignette collections) may deliberately create self-contained scenes. This sentinel targets the accidental self-containment of passages that should be interdependent.
**Source**: https://alexanderwales.com/adventures-in-ai-text-generation-pt-2-of/

### Uniform Sentence Beginnings Sentinel
**Detection**: A high proportion of sentences begin with the same grammatical construction — typically subject-verb ("She walked," "He noticed," "The room was") or the same word/phrase. More than 30% of sentences in a passage begin with the same pattern.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs tend to fall into repetitive sentence-opening patterns because each sentence is generated based on local context, and the most statistically probable next-sentence-beginning after a completed sentence is often the same construction that began the previous one.
**Design space cost**: Deliberate anaphora (rhetorical repetition of sentence openings for emphasis) is a legitimate literary device. This sentinel targets unintentional, unrhythmic repetition rather than purposeful anaphora.
**Source**: https://blog.frohrer.com/how-to-detect-llm-writing-in-text/, https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction

### Promotional Language in Narration Sentinel
**Detection**: The narration uses language that sounds like marketing copy: "boasts a," "vibrant," "showcasing," "exemplifies," "commitment to," "groundbreaking," "renowned," "diverse array." Characters or settings are described with the vocabulary of a tourism brochure or press release.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs are trained on massive amounts of web content, much of which is promotional. When describing fictional places or characters, the model reaches for promotional vocabulary because it dominates the training data's descriptive register. Human fiction writers rarely describe a character's house as "boasting" a garden.
**Design space cost**: Satirical fiction that deliberately parodies promotional language would be falsely flagged. Otherwise, this sentinel has low design space cost — promotional language in fiction narration is almost always a defect.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Signs_of_AI_writing, https://blog.frohrer.com/how-to-detect-llm-writing-in-text/

### Exposed Subtext Sentinel
**Detection**: The text explicitly states the thematic or psychological subtext that should be operating beneath the surface. Characters explain their own motivations. The narrator announces the theme. Metaphors are followed by their interpretation. Show is immediately followed by tell.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs "plop the subtext into the text, telling instead of showing." The model cannot maintain two layers of meaning (surface text and subtext) simultaneously, so it collapses them into one layer — making everything explicit. This is the prose equivalent of a comedian explaining their own jokes.
**Design space cost**: Didactic fiction, children's literature, or certain postmodern styles may deliberately surface subtext. This sentinel is most relevant to fiction that aspires to literary depth where subtext should operate implicitly.
**Source**: https://alexanderwales.com/adventures-in-ai-text-generation-pt-2-of/, https://www.thealgorithmicbridge.com/p/10-signs-of-ai-writing-that-99-of

### Low Epistemic Marker Density Sentinel
**Detection**: The text shows unusually low frequency of epistemic markers (words expressing uncertainty, possibility, or degree of knowledge: "might," "perhaps," "seems," "I think," "probably," "as if," "sort of") and discourse markers ("well," "you know," "I mean," "like") compared to human prose.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Research shows ChatGPT essays have "substantially fewer epistemic markers" compared to human writing, which "exhibited higher usage of modals, epistemic markers, and discourse markers." Human writing integrates "more personal references and features of involvement, making their writing more nuanced."
**Design space cost**: Highly authoritative omniscient narration or certain formal prose styles may legitimately have low epistemic marker density. First-person narrators and close-third POV should generally show more epistemic markers reflecting the character's genuine uncertainty.
**Source**: https://www.refsmmat.com/notebooks/llm-style.html, https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11231544/
