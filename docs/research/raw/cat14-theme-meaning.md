## Criteria Found

### Thematic Presence and Identifiability
**What it measures**: Whether the work has a discernible central theme that can be experienced by readers, even if it cannot be reduced to a simple statement.
**Assumed goal**: Literary fiction tradition following Flannery O'Connor's position that meaning must be "embodied" and "made concrete" in the story, experienced rather than abstracted -- some experimental or purely entertainment-focused fiction deliberately minimizes thematic content, and didactic fiction makes theme fully extractable from form.
**Score 0**: The work has no discernible theme. After reading, the reader cannot identify what the story is about beyond its surface events. Plot events occur without accumulating into any larger meaning. The story is purely incident without significance.
**Score 5**: The work possesses a rich, clearly experienceable theme that pervades every element of the narrative. The reader senses what the story means without the author ever needing to state it. As O'Connor wrote: "A story is a way to say something that can't be said any other way, and it takes every word in the story to say what the meaning is."
**Source**: https://lpei4.wordpress.com/on-writing-esp-fiction-and-creative-process/flannery-oconnor-on-meaning/
**Notes**: O'Connor: "When you can state the theme of a story, when you can separate it from the story itself, then you can be sure the story is not a very good one." The meaning of fiction is "experienced meaning" not "abstract meaning."

### Controlling Idea Coherence (McKee)
**What it measures**: Whether the story conveys a single, unified controlling idea expressed through the relationship between value change and cause across the entire narrative.
**Assumed goal**: Robert McKee's "Story" framework where the controlling idea is "the purest form of a story's meaning, the how and why of change, the vision of life the audience members carry away" -- works with deliberately fragmented or postmodern structures may intentionally resist a single controlling idea, and episodic fiction (picaresque, slice-of-life) may distribute meaning differently.
**Score 0**: The story has no unified controlling idea. Scenes convey contradictory meanings without purpose. The value changes at the climax are arbitrary and disconnected from the rest of the story. The reader carries away no coherent vision of life.
**Score 5**: Every scene's climactic value change either proves the truth of the theme or its opposite, creating a dialectical tension that resolves at the climax. The controlling idea can be expressed as a single sentence with both a value statement and a cause: e.g., "Children live fulfilling lives when parents allow them to take risks." The entire story feels like it conveys the same meaning.
**Source**: https://www.shortform.com/blog/what-is-a-controlling-idea/
**Notes**: McKee: "A Controlling Idea may be expressed in a single sentence describing how and why life undergoes change from one condition of existence at the beginning to another at the end." The dialectic approach alternates between theme and anti-theme, creating tension that mirrors life's complexity.

### Theme-Plot Integration
**What it measures**: Whether theme and plot are organically unified such that the plot serves as a metaphor for the thematic content, rather than being separable elements.
**Assumed goal**: The Western literary tradition (Aristotle through New Criticism) where unity of form and content is a primary aesthetic value -- plot-driven genre fiction (thriller, mystery) may deliberately foreground plot mechanics over thematic exploration, and some postmodern fiction deliberately ruptures this integration.
**Score 0**: Theme and plot exist as separate layers. The plot could be swapped for an entirely different plot without changing the theme, or vice versa. Events happen without thematic significance. As Weiland warns, the story feels "fragmented or disjointed."
**Score 5**: Plot functions as "a metaphor for whatever the character is changing internally, for the story's Lie and Truth." Physical events dramatize internal transformation. Neither plot nor theme could exist without the other. The plot is the theme made visible through action.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/the-secret-to-writing-strong-themes/
**Notes**: Weiland: "One necessarily creates the other." The trifecta of plot, character, and theme should be inseparable. The ending of the story "proves what your story was really about."

### Theme-Character Arc Integration
**What it measures**: Whether the protagonist's character arc embodies and dramatizes the theme through internal transformation.
**Assumed goal**: Character-arc-driven fiction (Weiland, McKee, Truby) where the protagonist's journey from Lie to Truth IS the theme -- flat-arc protagonists (e.g., James Bond, Sherlock Holmes) use a different model where the character tests the world rather than being transformed, and some literary fiction features anti-arcs or stasis.
**Score 0**: Characters undergo changes unrelated to the theme, or characters don't change at all despite the story having thematic aspirations. The protagonist's internal struggle has no connection to the story's broader meaning.
**Score 5**: The character's Lie and Truth directly encode the thematic argument. The character's internal battle between Lie and Truth becomes "an externalized moral or philosophical argument" within external conflict. Every lesson the character learns relates to the theme. The climactic decision crystallizes the thematic premise in concrete action.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/the-secret-to-writing-strong-themes/
**Notes**: Weiland's framework: "These vital elements--character arc and theme--pivot upon the fulcrum of the Lie Your Character Believes and the Truth Your Character Believes, with the Truth being the transcendent theme of the entire story." Characters who embrace the Truth = Positive Change Arcs; those who reject it = Negative Change Arcs.

### Scene-Level Thematic Integration
**What it measures**: Whether each individual scene advances or challenges the theme, rather than theme existing only at the macro level.
**Assumed goal**: Craft tradition (McKee, Weiland) where "every scene's climactic value change should either prove the truth of your theme, or the opposite of your theme" -- fast-paced genre fiction may legitimately have scenes whose primary purpose is plot momentum, and experimental fiction may use thematic disruption as a technique.
**Score 0**: Individual scenes have no connection to theme. Scenes serve only plot mechanics without thematic weight. A scene's emotional arc and lesson do not relate to the broader thematic premise.
**Score 5**: Every scene tests, challenges, or advances the thematic argument. Scene goals connect to the overarching theme. Character emotional arcs within scenes reflect the broader thematic journey. Even action-oriented scenes carry thematic weight through character choices and consequences. "Every single sentence, paragraph, and scene in your story can and should be put to the same test: Does this support my theme in some way?"
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/how-to-intertwine-plot-character-and-theme-in-every-scene/
**Notes**: Integration method: "If you've already successfully intertwined plot and character...then you can be almost positive theme is already present as the glue holding them together." Theme operates primarily through subtext at the scene level.

### Theme Through Action, Not Exposition
**What it measures**: Whether theme emerges from what characters DO (choices, consequences, dramatic action) rather than what they SAY (dialogue, internal monologue, narration).
**Assumed goal**: The "show don't tell" tradition (Chekhov, Hemingway, Flannery O'Connor) where fiction conveys meaning through dramatization rather than exposition -- some literary traditions (essay-novel, philosophical fiction like Mann or Musil) deliberately incorporate discursive passages, and didactic fiction (allegory, parable) uses explicit statement as a feature.
**Score 0**: Theme is delivered through exposition: characters make speeches about the theme, the narrator explains the meaning, or an authority figure voices the lesson directly. Characters function as "mouthpieces for the author's views." The theme is told, not shown.
**Score 5**: Theme emerges entirely from the consequences of character decisions. "What matters is not what the character says...but what the character does." Characters can even contradict the theme in dialogue while their actions prove it. As Barringer states: "Theme isn't imposed on the story. Theme emerges from the way the hero lives."
**Source**: https://medium.com/@authordavidbarringer/theme-comes-from-the-heros-choices-417794f52d08
**Notes**: Three moments for character voice: debating (during internal conflict over decisions), taking action (minimize self-reflection during dramatic execution), and processing consequences (allow emotional reaction after outcomes). Theme arises especially from the relationship between the character's two major decisions: entering Act 2 and entering the final act.

### Theme vs. Message Distinction
**What it measures**: Whether the work maintains the distinction between universal theme (inclusive, applicable to all readers) and specific message (exclusive, applicable only to the story's characters and situation).
**Assumed goal**: The mainstream literary craft tradition where theme operates at the universal level and message at the particular level -- message-fiction (propaganda, allegory, polemic) deliberately collapses this distinction, and some postmodern fiction problematizes the concept of universality itself.
**Score 0**: The work confuses message for theme, attempting to make a specific situational moral universally applicable. The result is heavy-handed moralizing. As one source notes: the problem is "however spot-on in its intent--was more about the story's message than its theme." Or: message-fiction where "everything in the story is there for the sake of discussing the message, promoting it (usually as the only way)."
**Score 5**: Theme operates at the universal level (justice, mercy, courage, love) while messages emerge through the specific situations of particular characters. "Theme is inclusive and message is exclusive." The reader can extract broad human truths while understanding that the specific circumstances belong only to these characters.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/storys-theme-2/
**Notes**: Weiland: "Theme is big stuff. Theme is justice and mercy. Theme is do unto thy neighbor. Theme is joy, peace, and love. Message, on the other hand, is found in the specific story situations that illustrate the thematic principles." Spider-Man example: theme (power demands responsibility) is universal; message (becoming a spider-powered vigilante) is specific to Peter Parker.

### Thematic Subtlety vs. Heavy-Handedness
**What it measures**: Whether the theme is embedded organically in the narrative or imposed through didactic techniques that undermine reader trust and engagement.
**Assumed goal**: Literary fiction tradition where the best themes emerge implicitly through dramatic action (Chekhov: "the artist should be not the judge of his characters") -- deliberately didactic traditions (religious allegory, moral fable, Brechtian theater) use overt messaging as a feature, and some satirical fiction is intentionally heavy-handed for comic or rhetorical effect.
**Score 0**: Theme is blatantly stated by characters, narrators, or structural devices. Warning signs include: a wise character voicing the theme directly ("And that's why it's so important to share"); the narrator explaining symbolic meaning; interior monologue interrupting scenes to spell out significance; all opposition being strawman-thin. The work "wears its symbolism on its sleeve."
**Score 5**: Theme operates entirely through subtext. Readers discover meaning through dramatic action, character behavior, and structural patterns rather than being told what to think. "The talent part is where you make your theme so much part of the story and characters that no one notices it unless you point it out." The work trusts readers to make connections.
**Source**: https://litreactor.com/columns/the-heavy-hand-of-didacticism ; https://kidlit.com/heavy-handed-imagery-and-theme/ ; https://maxonwriting.com/2017/04/18/being-a-better-writer-the-heavy-hand-of-the-writer/
**Notes**: LitReactor: Didacticism is "a writer's proclivity to be overly explanative, making the work appear as an educational guide instead of a means of enlightenment." Key paradox: "the more abstract a concept, the more the listener has a tendency to specify it...By contrast, the more specific a concept, the more the reader has a tendency to abstract it." Solution: build from concrete specifics upward. Kidlit: imagery that belongs "on a classroom poster, not at the heart of a story." Heavy-hand blog: "A heavy-handed writer does not trust the audience to figure things out."

### Moral/Philosophical Complexity
**What it measures**: Whether the thematic argument presents genuine complexity with competing valid perspectives, rather than a one-sided moral premise.
**Assumed goal**: Literary fiction tradition valuing moral ambiguity and complexity (Dostoevsky's polyphonic novel, Chekhov's refusal to judge) -- genre fiction with clear moral frameworks (epic fantasy good-vs-evil) deliberately simplifies, allegorical fiction requires clear moral mapping, and didactic fiction subordinates complexity to persuasion.
**Score 0**: The thematic premise is one-sided. All counterpoint has been stripped from the narrative. Opposing characters are strawmen. "You'll never gain a complex moral argument if the 'bad' side is so bad, no one in his right mind would ever argue for it." The reader feels the jury has been rigged.
**Score 5**: The work presents a genuine dialectic where opposing positions have legitimate weight. The antagonist's perspective is comprehensible and even sympathetic. McKee's thematic square is fully populated: positive (Truth), contradictory (Lie), contrary (compromise), and negation of the negation (extreme darkness). Readers engage in genuine consideration rather than automatic agreement. As one source states: "It's not the author's job to make up the readers' mind. Rather, it's your job to present all the facts, so they can make up their own minds."
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/create-complex-moral-argument-storys-theme/ ; https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/deepening-your-storys-theme-with-the-thematic-square/
**Notes**: McKee's four corners: Positive (Truth), Contradictory (direct opposite/Lie), Contrary (nuanced middle ground, "somewhat negative but not fully the opposite"), Negation of the Negation (extreme where theme inverts entirely, "qualitatively worse"). Truby adds: each character should have a different approach to the central moral problem. Dostoevsky's polyphonic novel: multiple "idea-voices" co-exist and compete without the mediation of a monologising authorial voice.

### Theme as Question vs. Theme as Answer
**What it measures**: Whether the work poses thematic questions that invite reader exploration and interpretation, or delivers thematic answers that foreclose inquiry.
**Assumed goal**: Literary fiction tradition following Chekhov ("You are confusing two concepts, the solution of the problem and the correct formulation of the problem. Only the second is required of the artist") -- didactic fiction, parable, and allegory deliberately provide answers, and genre fiction with clear moral frameworks often resolves thematic questions definitively.
**Score 0**: The story presents a pre-determined thematic answer and engineers all events to prove it. There is no genuine inquiry, only demonstration of a conclusion the author reached before writing. The reader is a passive recipient of a lesson. "Anyone who says that the artist's sphere leaves no room for questions, but deals exclusively with answers, has never done any writing."
**Score 5**: The story poses profound thematic questions and explores them from multiple angles without forcing a single answer. "Not a single problem is resolved in Anna Karenina or Eugene Onegin, and yet the novels satisfy you completely because all the problems they raise are formulated correctly." Characters embody different answers to the central question. The reader is invited to draw their own conclusions. Theme functions as inquiry, not decree.
**Source**: https://www.themarginalian.org/2021/08/31/chekhov-art-problems-questions/ ; https://medium.com/@annebellows/on-writing-theme-or-make-it-a-question-9842481478f4
**Notes**: Chekhov: "The artist observes, selects, guesses, and arranges; every one of these operations presupposes a question at its outset." Bellows: "questions just naturally invite answers," making them more effective than vague thematic statements. Wrede: dwelling on the question "is what will really make the book memorable for your reader."

### Interpretive Richness and Ambiguity
**What it measures**: Whether the work supports multiple valid interpretations and rewards re-reading with new discoveries of meaning.
**Assumed goal**: Literary fiction and New Critical tradition where ambiguity enriches rather than confuses, and where texts gain value from supporting multiple readings -- genre fiction with clear resolutions deliberately minimizes ambiguity for reader satisfaction, and commercially-oriented fiction prioritizes accessibility over interpretive depth.
**Score 0**: The work has exactly one possible interpretation. Every element is explained, every symbol decoded, every question answered. There is nothing beneath the surface. Re-reading yields no new insights. The text is "predictable and artistically dull."
**Score 5**: The work supports multiple valid readings along multiple dimensions. Ambiguity is deliberate and enriching, "mirror[ing] the complexity of human experience, reflecting the often indeterminate nature of reality." Readers return to discover new meanings. The text invites "participatory reading" where "readers contribute their interpretations, thus co-creating the text's meaning."
**Source**: https://www.gilliamwritersgroup.com/blog/ambiguity-in-literature-fostering-participatory-reading
**Notes**: Balance is critical: excessive ambiguity may "frustrate readers or lead to wildly divergent interpretations" while insufficient ambiguity produces "predictable and artistically dull" work. Formalists view ambiguity as enriching the work's depth and aesthetic experience, not as a flaw.

### Subtext as Thematic Vehicle
**What it measures**: Whether the work conveys thematic meaning through what is left unsaid, implied, or shown through behavior rather than through surface-level text.
**Assumed goal**: Modern literary craft tradition (Hemingway's iceberg theory, Chekhov) where "if there's one thing that separates great fiction from the rest, it's the ability to create meaning beneath the surface" -- some literary traditions (oral storytelling, fairy tale, myth) use surface-level statement effectively, and transparent prose styles may minimize the gap between surface and subtext.
**Score 0**: Everything in the work operates at surface level. Characters say exactly what they mean. There is no gap between what is said and what is meant. Dialogue is "on-the-nose" -- "a character speaks with no subtext. They say exactly what they are thinking or feeling in no uncertain terms." No thematic meaning exists below the surface text.
**Score 5**: Thematic meaning operates primarily beneath the surface. Characters discuss one topic while meaning something else. The gap between behavior and stated intention reveals character truth. "Subtext collects around what's unsaid, creating meaning through omission." As Francine Prose says: "More is going on under the surface than on it. One mark of badly written dialogue is that it is only doing one thing, at most, at once."
**Source**: https://janefriedman.com/the-art-and-purpose-of-subtext/ ; https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/5-ingredients-need-story-subtext/
**Notes**: Weiland's five ingredients: space between fixed points, explicit subtext (author knows what's beneath), maintained concealment, dichotomy/internal conflict, and silent spaces in dialogue. The "heart of subtext" is "a definite dichotomy between interior and exterior behavior."

### Symbolism and Image System Effectiveness
**What it measures**: Whether the work employs symbols and image systems that reinforce theme through concrete imagery operating below conscious awareness.
**Assumed goal**: Literary fiction tradition following McKee where image systems form "a category of images embedded in the story that repeats from beginning to end" and operate subconsciously -- minimalist fiction may deliberately eschew symbolic layering, and some genre fiction uses functional rather than symbolic imagery.
**Score 0**: The work either lacks symbolic imagery entirely, or symbols are arbitrary (present without thematic connection), or symbols are heavy-handed (blatantly obvious, "wearing their symbolism on their sleeve"). Cliched imagery (butterflies for transformation, water for cleansing) used without innovation.
**Score 5**: The work employs a coherent image system -- a category of imagery that repeats with variation from beginning to end, reinforcing theme subconsciously. "The images must rhyme in ways that will amalgamate power and bring emotional unity to the story. In order to resonate, the images must emerge from the subconscious, and they must appeal to the reader's subconscious as well. They can't be out in the open, or the effect will be ruined." Symbols evolve in meaning as the story progresses, with "subtlety and constant variation."
**Source**: https://timweed.net/reaching-for-the-sublime-image-systems-in-fiction/ ; https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2022/05/strengthening-story-with-symbolism.html
**Notes**: McKee: image systems form patterns that "can't be so obvious that an unschooled reader will recognize it consciously." Image systems cannot be pre-planned -- they must be "recognized and brought forward in revision." Fawkes distinguishes: symbolism communicates "something abstract in a more concrete way"; motifs are symbols that recur and evolve; image systems are categories of imagery. Internal symbolism (text creates the association, like the thimble-as-kiss in Peter Pan) vs external symbolism (culturally pre-loaded, like water with cleansing).

### Motif Development and Evolution
**What it measures**: Whether recurring elements (images, phrases, objects, actions) accumulate meaning through repetition and variation, reinforcing theme.
**Assumed goal**: Craft tradition where motifs function as "connective tissue throughout a narrative, creating cohesion and emotional depth" -- some minimalist fiction deliberately avoids pattern-making, and commercial fiction may use motifs decoratively rather than thematically.
**Score 0**: The work has no recurring elements, or recurring elements repeat identically without variation or accumulation of meaning. Motifs are present but disconnected from theme. Or motifs are so heavy-handed they become annoying: "Repeating the exact same thing over and over to the audience can get annoying."
**Score 5**: Motifs recur with meaningful variation, each appearance deepening or complicating the thematic resonance. The motif has "emotional significance for the character," ties "intrinsically into your theme," and is "simple and clear in its symbolism." Motifs evolve by changing context rather than repeating identically. Example: doors in Frozen shift meaning as characters transform, communicating thematic resolution through variation of a single motif.
**Source**: https://www.livewritethrive.com/2022/08/15/using-motifs-in-a-powerful-way-in-your-fiction/ ; https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2022/05/strengthening-story-with-symbolism.html
**Notes**: Criteria for effective motifs: emotional significance for the character, tied to theme, simple and clear symbolism. Motifs gain power through variation, not repetition. Lakin: "one object she owns that is special to her" -- introduce early, reference at pivotal moments, incorporate in final scenes.

### Thematic Premise as Dramatic Argument (Egri/Truby)
**What it measures**: Whether the story's thematic content functions as a dramatic argument with a clear premise that drives character and conflict.
**Assumed goal**: Dramatic writing tradition (Egri's "Art of Dramatic Writing," Truby's "Anatomy of Story") where every great story has a premise expressed as "character quality through conflict leads to conclusion" -- episodic and slice-of-life fiction may not operate through a single dramatic argument, and postmodern fiction may deliberately subvert premise-driven structure.
**Score 0**: The story has no identifiable premise driving its dramatic action. Events occur without testing any proposition about human nature. The moral argument is absent or incoherent. Characters don't embody different approaches to a central moral problem.
**Score 5**: The story has a clear premise (e.g., "Honesty defeats duplicity") that every line and action serves. The moral argument is dramatized through a character web where each character represents a different approach to the central moral problem. The premise functions as "the very DNA of the script, informing every line and every action." Truby: "every great story is a moral argument that traces the change in the hero's moral character from the beginning of the tale to the end."
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lajos_Egri ; https://www.beabrilliantwriter.com/anatomy-of-story-truby/
**Notes**: Egri divides story-building into three parts: premise (theme), character, and conflict. Truby: story is an organism where theme (moral argument) is like the brain. The "symbol web" connects theme to every story element. Advanced form: Truby's "unique moral vision" where expertise shows "in webs of character, plot, story world, and symbol that are as broad and as detailed as their moral arguments."

### Thematic Subplots and Mirroring
**What it measures**: Whether subplots explore different facets of the central theme, providing thematic counterpoint, reinforcement, or complication.
**Assumed goal**: Mainstream fiction craft tradition where subplots function as "microcosms that mirror or contrast the main plot, allowing you to explore different facets of the theme" -- single-plotline fiction (novellas, short stories) may not have subplots, and some experimental fiction uses subplots for structural rather than thematic purposes.
**Score 0**: Subplots exist but have no thematic relationship to the main plot. They are purely additional storylines that neither mirror, contrast, nor complicate the central theme. Secondary characters don't embody different perspectives on the thematic statement.
**Score 5**: Subplots function as thematic microcosms. Secondary characters embody different perspectives on the thematic statement, with subplots either supporting or arguing against it. Supporting characters hold "conflicting viewpoints stemming from different interpretations of the same theme." Truby's "four corners of opposition" are populated by supporting characters who externalize different aspects of the protagonist's internal thematic conflict.
**Source**: https://janefriedman.com/3-ways-to-use-theme-to-deepen-your-story/
**Notes**: Subplots serve multiple thematic functions: mirror (reinforce the main theme by showing it in a different context), contrast (challenge the main theme by showing an alternative approach), and complicate (introduce nuance by showing edge cases or exceptions).

### Thematic Unity Across Story Elements
**What it measures**: Whether all major story elements (setting, dialogue, imagery, structure, character, plot) work in concert to express the theme, achieving organic unity.
**Assumed goal**: Western literary tradition from Aristotle through New Criticism where "unity refers to the quality of a literary work where all its elements work together to create a coherent whole" -- deliberately fragmented or collage-based fiction may resist unity, and some postmodern works achieve meaning through disunity.
**Score 0**: Story elements work at cross-purposes. Setting has no thematic resonance. Dialogue delivers information without thematic layering. Structure doesn't reinforce meaning. The story feels like disparate elements assembled without a unifying principle.
**Score 5**: Every element contributes to thematic expression. Setting reinforces theme. Dialogue operates on multiple levels including thematic subtext. Structure mirrors thematic content. The work achieves what New Criticism calls organic unity: "every part of the text contributes to its overall meaning and effect, forming a singular experience for the reader." The story "becomes greater than the sum of its parts" (Truby's "designing principle").
**Source**: https://www.britannica.com/art/organic-unity ; https://teambooktu.com/john-trubys-designing-principle
**Notes**: Truby's designing principle: "the internal logic of the story, what makes the parts hang together organically so that the story becomes greater than the sum of its parts." A single symbol or metaphor can serve as the designing principle (e.g., the red letter in The Scarlet Letter, the whale in Moby Dick).

### Thematic Resonance (Emotional Impact of Theme)
**What it measures**: Whether the theme creates emotional impact that lingers with the reader beyond the final page, rather than being merely intellectual.
**Assumed goal**: Fiction craft tradition where "theme is vital to a story for many reasons but arguably the most important is that it is through thematic ideas that a writer most creates resonance and emotion in an audience" -- purely intellectual or experimental fiction may prioritize cognitive engagement over emotional resonance, and commercial fiction may achieve emotional impact through other means (suspense, romance).
**Score 0**: The theme, if identifiable, leaves no emotional impression. The reader finishes and feels nothing about the ideas explored. The theme was an intellectual exercise that never engaged the reader's feelings, values, or sense of what matters.
**Score 5**: The theme creates lasting emotional and intellectual impact. The reader carries the thematic vision "into their lives" (McKee). The story creates experiences that "resonate with readers both on a conscious and subconscious level and stay with them long after the book closes." The theme has been proven through character suffering, growth, and consequence, making it feel earned rather than asserted.
**Source**: https://fiveable.me/key-terms/introduction-contemporary-literature/thematic-resonance ; https://janefriedman.com/3-ways-to-use-theme-to-deepen-your-story/
**Notes**: Thematic resonance is achieved when "every element of your story supports the theme in some meaningful way." It operates like an iceberg: the majority sits beneath the surface, supporting plot and story structure.

### Earned Thematic Resolution
**What it measures**: Whether the story's thematic conclusion feels earned through the dramatic events and character choices rather than imposed or arbitrary.
**Assumed goal**: Mainstream fiction craft where endings must feel "earned" -- the protagonist "needs to make a choice, the hardest choice they've made in the entire story" that proves the theme through action -- experimental or open-ended fiction may deliberately resist resolution, and tragedy earns its resolution differently than comedy.
**Score 0**: The thematic resolution feels arbitrary, imposed, or unearned. A deus ex machina resolves the thematic question. The character's final state doesn't follow from their choices and growth. The ending "proves" a theme the story never actually explored. Consequences are avoided through contrivance.
**Score 5**: The thematic resolution emerges inevitably from the dramatic action. The protagonist's climactic decision crystallizes the thematic premise through the hardest choice in the story. "The ending of your story will prove what your story was really about." As Weiland notes: avoiding consequences (particularly through deus ex machina) destroys organic theme. The resolution feels both "inevitable yet surprising."
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/two-ways-to-write-organic-themes/ ; https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/story-structure-climax/
**Notes**: Weiland: "Thematic meaning depends on what's at stake. Final stakes should reflect established patterns: life-and-death stories need life-or-death climaxes; relational stories need relational consequences." Warning signs: characters remain undeveloped despite action, events without emotional exploration, endings that resolve stakes arbitrarily.

### Philosophical Depth Without Didacticism
**What it measures**: Whether the work explores philosophical or moral ideas through dramatic experience rather than through lectures, essays, or expository passages that halt the narrative.
**Assumed goal**: Literary fiction tradition from Dostoevsky through Camus where philosophical inquiry is dramatized through character struggle -- the novel of ideas (Mann, Musil, Gaarder) deliberately incorporates discursive passages, and philosophical fiction like Sophie's World risks "resembling a textbook in novel form."
**Score 0**: Philosophical ideas are presented through exposition, lectures, or characters functioning as "mere vessels for philosophical discussions rather than as fully developed individuals." Philosophy is "disconnected from the story's emotional core." Dialogue sounds artificial or unnatural when conveying ideas.
**Score 5**: Philosophical themes are "integral to character development and plot progression." Ideas emerge through "conflicts, character choices, and consequences." Characters "struggle with ideas in a way that aligns with their personal conflicts, motivations, and the story's stakes." Philosophy is dramatized through action, as in Crime and Punishment, where Raskolnikov's ideas are tested through lived consequences.
**Source**: https://www.gilliamwritersgroup.com/blog/philosophical-fiction-navigating-the-novel-of-ideas
**Notes**: Warning signs of failure: characters as mouthpieces, artificial dialogue, narrative becoming "too abstract or theoretical," philosophy becoming "overly didactic." Good works of fiction "study values by testing them in imagined situations, testing them hard, and being absolutely fair to both sides."

### Thematic Complexity (Multiple/Competing Themes)
**What it measures**: Whether the work handles thematic complexity through layered, interacting themes rather than a single simplistic thesis.
**Assumed goal**: Literary fiction tradition where "stories tend towards complex and intertwining themes, which often ask more questions than they answer" -- focused genre fiction may deliberately maintain thematic simplicity for clarity, and short fiction may explore a single theme deeply rather than multiple themes broadly.
**Score 0**: The work has either a single simplistic theme with no nuance, or multiple themes that are disconnected and compete for attention without interacting meaningfully. "Too many themes risk diluting focus" without enriching complexity.
**Score 5**: The work handles multiple interacting themes where primary and secondary themes reinforce and complicate each other. Like To Kill a Mockingbird, where "the primary theme of racial injustice is supported by secondary themes of childhood innocence and moral education." McKee's thematic square is fully populated, providing "several incomplete or conflicting angles" on the central truth.
**Source**: https://www.firstdraftpro.com/blog/multiple-themes ; https://writerunboxed.com/2024/06/27/complex-themes-in-fiction/
**Notes**: Literary fiction, science fiction, and crime fiction show the highest thematic complexity. Love and adventure novels show high diversity. War genre tends to be the most monothematic. Maintaining one primary theme supported by secondary themes prevents dilution while building complexity.

### Meaning Inseparable from Form
**What it measures**: Whether the story's meaning can only be experienced through the story itself, rather than being reducible to a paraphrase or moral.
**Assumed goal**: Literary fiction tradition following O'Connor ("a story is a way to say something that can't be said any other way") and the New Critical principle that form and content are inseparable -- fable and parable traditions are designed for extractable morals, and commercial fiction often aims for memorable takeaways that can be stated simply.
**Score 0**: The story's meaning can be fully captured in a sentence or two. "You tell a story because a statement would be inadequate" -- but this story could have been a statement. Removing the story and keeping only the moral loses nothing essential. The form is merely a delivery vehicle for a pre-existing idea.
**Score 5**: The story's meaning can only be accessed through the experience of reading it. "The meaning of a story has to be embodied in it, has to be made concrete in it." Paraphrasing the theme inevitably reduces and distorts it. "It takes every word in the story to say what the meaning is." When asked what the story means, "the only proper thing is to tell him to read the story."
**Source**: https://lpei4.wordpress.com/on-writing-esp-fiction-and-creative-process/flannery-oconnor-on-meaning/
**Notes**: O'Connor: "The meaning of fiction is not abstract meaning but experienced meaning." This criterion values the irreducibility of narrative meaning -- the idea that fiction does philosophical work that philosophy alone cannot do.

### Reader Agency in Meaning-Making
**What it measures**: Whether the work invites active reader participation in constructing meaning, rather than delivering meaning passively.
**Assumed goal**: Modern literary fiction tradition following Chekhov ("When I write, I reckon entirely upon the reader to add for himself the subjective elements that are lacking in the story") -- commercially-oriented fiction may deliberately minimize ambiguity for broader accessibility, and some readers prefer clearly-stated meanings.
**Score 0**: The work leaves no room for reader interpretation. Every meaning is spelled out. Every symbol is explained. Every moral is stated. The reader is a passive consumer of pre-digested meaning. The author functions as "judge of his characters and their conversations" rather than allowing readers to judge.
**Score 5**: The work deliberately creates space for reader interpretation. "Drawing conclusions is up to the jury (the readers), and the writer's only job is to be talented -- to know how to distinguish important testimony from unimportant, to place characters in proper light and speak their language." The reader participates in meaning-making, supplying "the subjective elements that are lacking in the story."
**Source**: https://nealabbott.wordpress.com/2014/08/25/writing-advice-from-anton-chekhov-part-three-ambiguity/
**Notes**: Chekhov: "The artist should be not the judge of his characters and their conversations, but only an unbiased observer." The writer observes, selects, guesses, and arranges -- but the final interpretive act belongs to the reader. This mirrors Hemingway's iceberg theory.

### Thematic Freshness and Originality
**What it measures**: Whether the work explores its theme in a way that feels fresh, specific, and individual rather than generic, cliched, or formulaic.
**Assumed goal**: Literary fiction tradition valuing original insight and individual vision -- genre fiction often deliberately engages with familiar thematic territory (good vs evil, love conquers all) because readers find comfort in thematic familiarity, and some traditions (fairy tale, myth) gain power from thematic archetypes.
**Score 0**: The theme is a cliche or platitude expressed without originality. "Love conquers all," "be yourself," "good triumphs over evil" presented without nuance, complication, or individual perspective. The thematic content could have been generated by assembling common wisdom without any original thought.
**Score 5**: The theme explores familiar territory through an original lens that reveals new truths about well-worn subjects. Even universal themes gain specificity through the particular characters and situations. The work offers what Truby calls a "unique moral vision" -- not just a moral argument but one shaped by the author's distinctive understanding of the world.
**Source**: https://medium.com/@pirangy/the-unique-moral-vision-john-truby-the-anatomy-of-story-p-137-2bb2a5cc543
**Notes**: Truby distinguishes between sophisticated moral arguments (literary classics) and "moral shorthand" (popular films). Even simplified versions succeed when they offer audiences "a creed they could live by." The key is that the moral vision feels authored -- shaped by a particular intelligence -- rather than assembled from generic parts.

## Sentinels Found

### Explicit Moral Statement at Story End
**Detection**: The story ends with a character, narrator, or structural device explicitly stating the moral, lesson, or theme of the story. Look for phrases like "and so she learned that...", "the moral of the story is...", "in the end, what mattered was...", or any passage where the theme is spelled out rather than demonstrated through action.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs are trained on internet content where articles end with uplifting summaries and blog posts wrap up with lessons learned. This training creates a strong pattern of "moralizing to put a bow on a story" (Richmond). LLMs default to finding a lesson because "they're trained on internet articles and blog posts that tend to end on an uplifting note." Human fiction writers learn to resist this impulse; LLMs reproduce the pattern reliably.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates fable, parable, and moral tale traditions where explicit statement is a feature. May flag Aesop-style children's literature. Also eliminates deliberately ironic uses of stated morals.
**Source**: https://medium.com/a-writers-life/i-asked-chatgpt-to-write-fiction-and-it-turned-into-a-moral-showdown-b9092993337b ; https://writershelpingwriters.net/2025/09/how-to-differentiate-stories-from-ai-slop/

### Theme Stated Through Character Speech Rather Than Dramatized
**Detection**: A character delivers a speech, monologue, or dialogue passage that directly articulates the story's theme or moral position, particularly if the speech is positioned climactically and the other characters respond with agreement or transformation.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs default to having characters verbalize meaning rather than demonstrate it through action, because generating dialogue about ideas is mechanically easier than constructing dramatic sequences whose meaning emerges from structure. The LLM produces characters as "mouthpieces" because it generates text sequentially and the most probable next token after a climactic moment is a speech, not a silent action.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates philosophical fiction where characters legitimately debate ideas (Dostoevsky, Mann). Eliminates speeches that are dramatically earned (Atticus Finch's courtroom address). Eliminates character voice as a thematic vehicle when the speech reveals character as much as theme.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/create-complex-moral-argument-storys-theme/

### Universal Positive Resolution Without Complication
**Detection**: The story resolves all conflicts positively, all characters learn their lessons, all relationships are repaired, and the thematic question is answered with an unambiguous affirmation. No loose ends, no unresolved tensions, no cost to the resolution.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs are "overtly optimistic and end on a positive note" because their training data and RLHF alignment reward positive, helpful, uplifting content. AI "doesn't tend to be comfortable with unfinished thoughts or open-ended imagery." The neat resolution reflects the model's preference for closure and positivity rather than the messy, costly, ambiguous resolutions of human experience.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates comedy (which conventionally ends in harmony and marriage), romance (which requires an HEA/HFN), and children's literature (which often resolves positively). Also eliminates stories that genuinely earn their positive resolution through sufficient complication.
**Source**: https://www.quora.com/When-I-write-stories-with-AI-I-have-noticed-it-tends-to-be-overtly-optimistic-and-ends-on-a-positive-note-a-lot-despite-the-real-world-not-being-so-nice-Why

### No Thematic Counter-Argument Present
**Detection**: The story presents a single thematic position without any character, subplot, or event that genuinely challenges or complicates that position. All evidence supports the thesis; no evidence contradicts it.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate text that follows the path of least resistance. Once a thematic direction is established, the model continues reinforcing it because contradicting the established pattern would require a higher-order structural decision the model doesn't naturally make. Message-fiction where "all counterpoint must be stripped from the narrative" is the path of least resistance for sequential token generation.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates fable and allegory where single-thesis structure is a feature. Eliminates very short fiction where space constraints limit thematic complexity. May also flag satire where one-sided presentation serves an ironic purpose.
**Source**: https://maxonwriting.com/2015/06/08/being-a-better-writer-theme-vs-message/

### Generic/Platitudinous Theme
**Detection**: The theme can be expressed as a common inspirational poster phrase: "believe in yourself," "love conquers all," "be true to who you are," "friendship is the real treasure," "the journey matters more than the destination." No specificity, complication, or individual perspective distinguishes the thematic content from a greeting card.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs produce "the sum total of all those parts" -- what emerges from averaging all training data is "generic, bland, and boring." The most statistically probable thematic content is the most common thematic content: widely-shared platitudes. Human writers develop specific, individually-shaped thematic visions through lived experience; LLMs converge on the mean.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates children's literature where simple universal themes are appropriate and valuable. Eliminates works that deliberately engage with familiar themes but execute them with originality. May flag stories that explore common themes in genuinely fresh ways if the detection method only examines the theme statement itself.
**Source**: https://lindamayeadams.com/2024/05/05/the-problem-with-ai-and-fiction-writing/ ; https://writershelpingwriters.net/2025/09/how-to-differentiate-stories-from-ai-slop/

### Surface-Level Thematic Details Without Structural Integration
**Detection**: The story contains thematically-suggestive objects, images, or dialogue references, but these elements don't connect to character arc, plot structure, or each other. Symbols appear without accumulating meaning. Thematic imagery is decorative rather than structural.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs can generate individual sentences with thematic imagery because such sentences are common in training data, but they cannot maintain structural coherence across a long text. "The specific details in the story feel random. Not drawn to deepen characters or resonate with themes" (Michel on OpenAI fiction). The LLM generates locally plausible thematic details without the global structural awareness needed to make them cohere.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates impressionistic or atmospheric fiction where thematic imagery is deliberately loose. Eliminates surrealist fiction where disconnection is a feature. May flag early drafts where thematic integration is still being developed.
**Source**: https://countercraft.substack.com/p/mfa-vs-llm-is-openais-metafiction

### Flowery But Incoherent Metaphors for Depth
**Detection**: The text uses elaborate, poetic-sounding metaphors to create an impression of thematic depth, but the metaphors collapse under scrutiny. Examples: "Grief, as I've learned, is a delta," "the womb of oblivion." The language sounds profound but doesn't actually mean anything coherent.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs excel at generating text that pattern-matches to "profound literary prose" without actually achieving it. Michel identifies this as "flowery but nonsensical sentences" that merely sound profound. The model generates high-probability sequences from literary training data, producing what sounds like depth because it resembles deep writing in its surface features, without the underlying meaning that makes deep writing actually deep.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates deliberately obscure literary styles (certain poetry, experimental prose) where opacity is a feature. Eliminates writing where metaphors require domain knowledge to decode. May flag ambitious metaphorical writing that doesn't quite land.
**Source**: https://countercraft.substack.com/p/mfa-vs-llm-is-openais-metafiction

### Thematic Idea Merely Repeated Rather Than Developed
**Detection**: The central thematic idea is stated or illustrated multiple times but never deepened, complicated, challenged, or evolved. Each appearance of the theme is essentially the same as the first, just with different surface details. The theme has no "arc."
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs lack the capacity for thematic escalation -- deepening or complicating a central concept as the narrative progresses. Michel found that rather than developing its central idea, AI fiction presents a concept that "is just repeated with different flowery but incoherent metaphors." Sequential token generation is good at maintaining a topic but poor at systematically increasing its complexity across a long text.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates certain meditative or contemplative fiction where thematic repetition without development is a deliberate structural choice (musical rather than dramatic). May flag circular narratives that return to their starting point by design.
**Source**: https://countercraft.substack.com/p/mfa-vs-llm-is-openais-metafiction

### All Characters Agree on the Moral Position
**Detection**: Every sympathetic character shares the same moral/thematic position. Disagreement exists only between clearly-good and clearly-bad characters. No sympathetic character genuinely holds a view that contradicts the story's thesis.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs align characters with the "correct" moral position because RLHF training rewards helpful, harmless responses. The model struggles to create characters who are sympathetic AND hold morally questionable positions because this creates the kind of tension the model has been trained to avoid. Message-fiction structure where "all of the characters either must agree with the message or be part of the opposition (which again, is straw-thin)" is the default LLM output.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates stories where genuine moral consensus is the point (community stories, stories about solidarity). Eliminates stories where all characters share a worldview that the reader is meant to question (unreliable narrator techniques). May flag genre fiction with clear moral frameworks.
**Source**: https://maxonwriting.com/2015/06/08/being-a-better-writer-theme-vs-message/

### Theme Disconnected from Character Wound/Stakes
**Detection**: The story's thematic content has no connection to what the protagonist personally cares about, fears, or has been damaged by. The theme exists as an intellectual exercise above the character's lived experience rather than emerging from their specific psychological reality.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs can generate thematic content and character content separately but struggle to integrate them organically. "AI can't dig into what situations feel like and what they mean to our characters and their hopes for the future." The model produces themes and characters in parallel tracks rather than having theme emerge from the character's specific wound and need.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates ensemble fiction where no single character carries the thematic weight. Eliminates fiction where theme is expressed through social systems rather than individual psychology. May flag plot-driven fiction where theme operates through situation rather than character.
**Source**: https://writershelpingwriters.net/2025/09/how-to-differentiate-stories-from-ai-slop/

### Cliched Symbolic Imagery
**Detection**: The story uses well-worn symbolic associations without innovation: butterflies for transformation, storms for emotional turmoil, dawn for new beginnings, winter for death/ending, spring for rebirth, mirrors for self-reflection, cages for imprisonment, broken chains for freedom.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs draw on the highest-frequency symbolic associations in their training data. Since these cliched associations appear most frequently in the corpus, they are the most probable outputs. Human writers learn to innovate beyond stock symbolism; LLMs converge on the most common patterns. The resulting imagery "belongs on a classroom poster, not at the heart of a story" (Kole).
**Design space cost**: Eliminates archetypal fiction deliberately engaging with universal symbolic language (myth, fairy tale). Eliminates writing for young readers where conventional symbolism may be appropriate. May flag effective use of conventional symbols in fresh contexts.
**Source**: https://kidlit.com/heavy-handed-imagery-and-theme/

### Absence of Subtext in Thematic Dialogue
**Detection**: Characters discuss the story's themes directly and transparently, with no gap between what is said and what is meant. Thematic dialogue operates at only one level. No character avoids, deflects, or approaches the theme obliquely.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate dialogue that is "on-the-nose" because the most probable token following a thematic setup is the direct statement of the theme. Creating subtext requires the model to generate text that says one thing while meaning another -- a two-level operation that sequential token prediction handles poorly. "On-the-nose dialogue is not only unnatural, it's unexciting."
**Design space cost**: Eliminates philosophical fiction where direct thematic dialogue is the form. Eliminates fiction where characters are articulate about ideas (academic novels, intellectual characters). May flag naturalistic dialogue in communities where direct speech about values is cultural.
**Source**: https://screencraft.org/blog/how-to-avoid-writing-on-the-nose-dialogue/ ; https://mystorydoctor.com/eliminating-on-the-nose-dialoge/

### Loss of Thematic Thread Across Long Text
**Detection**: The story establishes thematic content early but loses track of it as the narrative progresses. Themes introduced in the first third are absent or contradicted in the final third. The story's ending addresses different thematic concerns than its beginning established.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs have limited effective context windows for maintaining thematic coherence across long texts. One author using AI found that "by chapter three, she had no idea what the book's theme would be" because "she had followed the AI down the rabbit hole." The model generates locally coherent text that drifts thematically because it lacks the global structural awareness needed to maintain a thematic through-line across tens of thousands of words.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates fiction that deliberately shifts thematic focus as a structural choice (novels with distinct parts, coming-of-age stories where the protagonist's concerns genuinely change). May flag complex novels where thematic evolution is subtle.
**Source**: https://lindamayeadams.com/2024/05/05/the-problem-with-ai-and-fiction-writing/

### Uniform Thematic Pattern Across Stories
**Detection**: When examining multiple stories by the same source, they all follow the same thematic structure: introduce a question, explore it briefly, resolve it positively with a neat lesson. The thematic approach is indistinguishable from one story to the next.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Research on LLM fiction generation found "a strikingly uniform narrative pattern" across multiple models. All fiction stories generated by LLMs tend to follow the same template. This uniformity suggests mechanical generation rather than individual artistic vision. Human authors develop distinctive thematic preoccupations and approaches that vary from work to work.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates authors who deliberately work within a consistent thematic framework (e.g., Graham Greene's Catholic guilt, Cormac McCarthy's violence and grace). May flag genre fiction where thematic templates are conventional.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2512.12839 ; https://countercraft.substack.com/p/mfa-vs-llm-is-openais-metafiction

### Thematic Safety (Avoidance of Moral Discomfort)
**Detection**: The story's thematic content avoids any position that might make readers uncomfortable, challenge their assumptions, or present morally ambiguous situations without resolution. All moral questions have clear, socially-acceptable answers. No character holds a position the average reader would find genuinely challenging or disturbing.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs are RLHF-trained to avoid controversy, offense, and moral discomfort. This training directly impacts thematic content: the model generates thematically "safe" fiction that avoids genuinely challenging moral territory. The resulting themes are "unreliable, drab, useless, and off topic... a thoughtless mishmash blended together out of middling consensus." Real literature often makes readers uncomfortable -- that discomfort is the mechanism of moral inquiry.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates genuinely comforting literature (cozy mystery, comfort reads) where thematic safety is a feature for the target audience. Eliminates children's literature where age-appropriate thematic content is necessary. May flag fiction from traditions that value harmony over conflict.
**Source**: https://www.noemamag.com/what-ai-teaches-us-about-good-writing/ ; https://www.publicbooks.org/aib/
