## Criteria Found

### Voice Distinction Between Characters
**What it measures**: Whether each character speaks with a recognizably individual voice that reflects their background, personality, education, and worldview, such that dialogue lines could be attributed without tags.
**Assumed goal**: Realist and character-driven fiction tradition (Austen, Dickens, Flannery O'Connor, Elmore Leonard) where characters are individuated through speech patterns — theatrical traditions (Shaw, Wilde) similarly require this for dramatic clarity, while some experimental or allegorical fiction (Kafka, Beckett) deliberately flattens voice distinction to create existential uniformity
**Score 0**: All characters speak with the same vocabulary, syntax, rhythm, and register. Removing dialogue tags makes speakers indistinguishable. Characters function as mouthpieces for the author's voice rather than as distinct individuals.
**Score 5**: Each character has a verbal fingerprint comprising unique word choice, sentence structure, cadence, emotional filter, speech habits, and cultural markers. A reader can identify who is speaking from the dialogue alone with tags removed. Voice shifts appropriately with context (a character speaks differently to their boss vs. their friend) while maintaining core identity.
**Source**: https://penultimateword.com/fiction/dialogue-in-fiction-part-2-the-essentials/
**Notes**: PenUltimate identifies voice distinction factors: age and generation, education level, geographic origin and ethnicity, socioeconomic background, personality type, individual speech habits and expressions. Recommends creating character sheets listing idiosyncratic speech patterns, slang preferences, expletives, and filler words. The voice distinction test: strip dialogue tags and check if speakers remain identifiable. Bibisco identifies five signs characters sound alike: same vocabulary, same rhythm, same tone, same emotional expression, same level of complexity.

### Subtext and Indirection
**What it measures**: Whether dialogue operates on multiple levels, with surface conversation carrying unstated meaning, hidden agendas, and emotional undercurrents beneath the literal words.
**Assumed goal**: Literary fiction tradition from Chekhov through Hemingway and Carver where what characters do not say matters as much as what they say — the iceberg theory where the story's deeper meaning lies beneath the surface. Screenwriting tradition (McKee, Mamet) also treats subtext as foundational. Genre fiction with explicit emotional content (romance) may deliberately surface feelings more directly at key moments.
**Score 0**: Characters state exactly what they think and feel in the most straightforward manner. Every motivation is explicitly articulated. Every emotion is directly named. There is no gap between what characters say and what they mean. Dialogue reads as therapy-session transcripts where people articulate their deepest feelings to each other without resistance.
**Score 5**: Dialogue operates on multiple simultaneous levels. Characters discuss surface topics while actually communicating about deeper concerns. Hidden agendas, unspoken desires, and power plays lurk beneath every exchange. What is left unsaid generates tension and meaning. Readers must actively interpret the gap between words and intentions. Moments of directness are earned and devastating precisely because indirection is the norm.
**Source**: https://www.writersdigest.com/write-better-fiction/how-to-use-subtext-and-the-art-of-dramatic-tension-in-fiction
**Notes**: Writer's Digest identifies five subtext elements: (1) know character motivation, (2) embed subtext in dialogue, (3) reveal emotion through behavior, (4) use setting to stage scenes, (5) be intentional with prose. McKee: beneath every line of dialogue, the writer must create a desire, intent, and action. Lisa Ferland identifies three layers of dialogue: the said, the unsaid, and the unsayable. NotMFA on Hemingway's "Hills Like White Elephants": subtext is not ornamental—it IS the structure. The conflict is unstated not because Hemingway is coy, but because the characters can't afford to say what they mean.

### On-the-Nose Dialogue Avoidance
**What it measures**: Whether dialogue avoids having characters state their thoughts, feelings, motivations, and exposition directly without nuance, indirection, or subtext.
**Assumed goal**: Post-Hemingway literary fiction and professional screenwriting tradition where direct statement of emotion or motivation is considered amateur craft — didactic fiction (morality tales, some YA) and theatrical traditions (Greek chorus) sometimes deliberately use direct statement for rhetorical or pedagogical purposes
**Score 0**: Characters routinely say exactly what they think and feel. Motivations are spelled out explicitly. Emotions are declared rather than shown. Readers can predict every response before characters speak. The dialogue reads as if characters are narrating their internal states to each other.
**Score 5**: Characters rarely if ever state their feelings directly. Emotions emerge through word choice, what is avoided, physical behavior, and the gap between statement and action. When direct emotional honesty occurs, it is earned through preceding restraint and creates powerful impact through contrast. Readers must infer character states from behavioral evidence.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/on-the-nose-dialogue/
**Notes**: K.M. Weiland identifies on-the-nose dialogue as a "sure bet" the entire story will lack subtlety. Three core fixes: (1) make conversations about what isn't said, (2) employ irony, (3) use strategic silence. Cincinnati Review identifies it as the second most common dialogue mistake in submissions. The fix: write on-the-nose first, then revise so characters say unrelated things that mask real feelings. Exception: sparse moments of directness work when characters lose emotional control after consistently guarding themselves.

### Dialogue Compression and Naturalism Balance
**What it measures**: Whether dialogue achieves the impression of natural speech while being strategically compressed to remove the filler, repetition, and meandering of actual conversation.
**Assumed goal**: Mainstream commercial and literary fiction where dialogue must sound natural while serving narrative economy — maximalist fiction (David Foster Wallace, William Gaddis) may deliberately include uncompressed speech for verisimilitude or satirical effect, while minimalist fiction (Carver, Hemingway) compresses further than mainstream norms
**Score 0**: Dialogue either reads as a literal transcription of real speech (full of filler words, greetings, repetition, small talk, and non-sequiturs that serve no narrative purpose) or reads as overly formal and stilted, nothing like how humans actually speak. Characters speak in complete, grammatically perfect sentences with no contractions.
**Score 5**: Dialogue creates a convincing illusion of natural speech through strategic compression. Filler is eliminated except where it characterizes. Sentence fragments replace full sentences where natural. Contractions are used appropriately. Small talk and greetings are elided or summarized. Every line does narrative work while sounding effortlessly conversational. The compression is invisible to the reader.
**Source**: https://www.southwestwriters.com/dialogue-compression-the-key-to-realistic-dialogue/
**Notes**: SouthWest Writers: the fundamental goal is making dialogue more efficient than normal speech yet still sound realistic. Cut meaningless filler (well, um, hey, right?), employ sentence fragments, prioritize contractions. PenUltimate: dialogue in fiction is artifice, not realism. RB Kelly argues dialogue doesn't need to sound natural—it needs to sound effective. The paradox: fiction dialogue is not at all like real-life dialogue, even though it aims to present real life realistically.

### Conflict and Tension Within Exchanges
**What it measures**: Whether dialogue scenes contain meaningful conflict, opposing agendas, or tension that gives exchanges dramatic energy rather than functioning as neutral information transfer.
**Assumed goal**: Dramatic fiction tradition from Aristotle through McKee where conflict is the engine of drama — meditative or contemplative fiction (Marilynne Robinson, W.G. Sebald) may deliberately mute conflict in exchanges to create atmosphere and interiority rather than dramatic tension
**Score 0**: Characters exchange information cooperatively with no underlying tension. No character wants anything the other is reluctant to give. Conversations are pleasant and agreeable. There are no competing agendas, no things at stake, no power dynamics. Dialogue reads as cooperative Q&A.
**Score 5**: Every dialogue scene contains some form of conflict, whether explicit confrontation, subtle tension, competing agendas, veiled threats, or the friction of characters who want different things. Conflict intensity varies appropriately—some scenes feature faint undercurrents while others escalate to full confrontation. Characters pursue individual agendas that shape their responses, and someone is always being thwarted, evaded, or resisted.
**Source**: https://janefriedman.com/conflict-in-dialogue/
**Notes**: K.M. Weiland identifies four types of dialogue conflict: (1) opposing views, no stakes, (2) opposing views, with stakes, (3) opposing goals, low stakes, (4) opposing goals, high stakes. Monotonous conflict dulls impact—variation is essential. CRAFT Literary cites Elizabeth Bowen: "Speech is what the characters do to each other." Dialogue functions as vigorous interaction, not merely exposition. David Mamet's three questions for every scene: Who wants what? What happens if they don't get it? Why now?

### Dialogue-Action Integration (Beats)
**What it measures**: Whether dialogue is integrated with physical action, gesture, and environmental interaction to create a complete scene rather than disembodied "talking heads."
**Assumed goal**: Immersive fiction tradition where scenes engage all senses and ground readers in physical reality — sparse or minimalist dialogue traditions (Hemingway, Beckett) may deliberately strip action beats for compression or existential effect, while screenplay-adjacent fiction may lean heavily on action beats as a visual storytelling tool
**Score 0**: Extended passages of dialogue float in a void with no physical action, setting detail, or body language. Characters are disembodied voices ("talking heads" or "white room syndrome"). Alternatively, action beats are purely mechanical busywork (nodding, smiling, sipping coffee) that adds bulk without meaning.
**Score 5**: Physical actions and environmental details are woven into dialogue to create complete scenes. Action beats reveal character emotion, advance plot, create subtext, or contradict spoken words. Characters interact with their environment meaningfully. The balance between speech and action maintains rhythm without interrupting conversational flow. Every beat serves the scene rather than filling space.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/are-your-characters-talking-heads/
**Notes**: Elizabeth George coined "Talking Head Avoidance Devices" (THADs)—purposeful activities during dialogue. THADs must serve the plot and reveal character, not just add movement. C.S. Lakin: action beats show what a character is doing before, during, or after dialogue—gestures, movements, facial expressions. They replace many dialogue tags while adding characterization. Writer's Digest: beats work best when there is something the reader should take in beyond the words. They shouldn't be tossed in for the sake of adding movement.

### Exposition Handling in Dialogue
**What it measures**: Whether information necessary for the reader is delivered through dialogue naturally and without contrivance, avoiding the "As You Know, Bob" pattern where characters tell each other things they already know.
**Assumed goal**: Post-Victorian fiction tradition where exposition must be disguised within dramatic action — earlier narrative traditions (Dickens, Dumas) and genre fiction (hard SF) sometimes accept more direct exposition, and theatrical prologues (Greek drama, Shakespeare) deliberately deliver exposition through address
**Score 0**: Characters routinely tell each other things both already know purely for the reader's benefit. Exposition is delivered in speeches rather than emerging from conflict. "As You Know, Bob" / "maid and butler" dialogue is frequent. Characters become transparent information-delivery vehicles. Information dumps are disguised as conversation but fool nobody.
**Score 5**: Exposition emerges organically from character need, conflict, and situation. Information is delivered because a character genuinely needs to know it, is arguing about its implications, or is revealing it under emotional pressure. The naive-character, disguise-with-desire, disguise-with-conflict, and emotional-overlay techniques are deployed as appropriate. Readers absorb necessary information without noticing it was delivered.
**Source**: https://stormwritingschool.com/exposition-in-dialogue/
**Notes**: Storm Writing School identifies four techniques: (1) naive character who legitimately needs explanation, (2) disguise with desire—urgency justifies information delivery, (3) disguise with conflict—embed exposition in disagreement, (4) emotional overlay—personal stakes onto information. Mamet: any time any character is saying to another "as you know," the scene is a failure. Terry Pratchett lampoons the fantasy version: "As you know, your father, the king..." The trope dates to Euripides—prologues where minor characters establish exposition.

### Dialogue Tag Craft
**What it measures**: Whether dialogue attribution is handled with invisible, purposeful tags that serve the scene rather than drawing attention to themselves through overuse of creative verbs, adverbs, or said-bookisms.
**Assumed goal**: Invisible-prose tradition (Elmore Leonard, Stephen King) where the mechanics of attribution should disappear so readers experience only the characters — literary fiction with a strong authorial voice (Nabokov, Pynchon) may occasionally use distinctive attribution as stylistic choice, and children's literature traditionally accepts more varied tags
**Score 0**: Dialogue tags are ornate ("he exclaimed," "she retorted," "he opined gravely"), drawing constant attention to the attribution rather than the dialogue. Adverbs prop up weak dialogue ("she said angrily"). Physical impossibilities are used as tags ("she laughed," "he smiled" as speech verbs). Every line has a tag even when the speaker is obvious. The tags constitute "telling" rather than showing.
**Score 5**: Attribution is invisible. "Said" and "asked" predominate, functioning like punctuation readers skip. Tags appear only when needed for speaker clarity. Action beats replace many tags, simultaneously identifying the speaker and adding characterization or subtext. No adverbs modify "said." No physical impossibilities are used as speech verbs. Tag placement varies for rhythm. Some exchanges run without any attribution, relying on turn-taking and voice distinction.
**Source**: https://litreactor.com/columns/on-dialogue-tags-why-anything-besides-said-and-asked-is-lazy-writing
**Notes**: LitReactor compares expressive dialogue tags to "laugh tracks of the writing world"—if emotions require explicit labeling through tags, the writing isn't doing its job. Elmore Leonard's Rule 3: "Never use a verb other than 'said' to carry dialogue." Rule 4: "Never use an adverb to modify the verb 'said'...a mortal sin." Stephen King's target in On Writing was specifically adverbs in dialogue attribution. Writer's Digest: dialogue cannot be smiled, laughed, giggled, or sighed—these are physical actions, not speech verbs. TV Tropes history: "said books" were lists of said-alternatives; publishing industry shifted away from them. Rewritten Rule 3 (Steven James) offers nuance: while avoiding obtrusive synonyms, repetitive "said" can become tiresome; use attributions strategically for pacing and status dynamics.

### Register and Sociolect Accuracy
**What it measures**: Whether characters' speech accurately reflects their socioeconomic class, education, region, age, profession, and cultural background through appropriate register, dialect, and sociolect without resorting to stereotype or caricature.
**Assumed goal**: Realist fiction tradition where characters are grounded in specific social and cultural contexts (Dickens, Twain, Toni Morrison, Junot Diaz) — universal or archetypal fiction (fairy tales, allegory) may deliberately abstract characters from specific social markers
**Score 0**: All characters speak in the same register regardless of background. Alternatively, dialect is rendered as heavy phonetic spelling that reads as mockery or caricature rather than characterization. Professional jargon, generational slang, and class markers are absent or inaccurate. Characters from different backgrounds are linguistically interchangeable.
**Score 5**: Each character's register accurately reflects their background without becoming caricature. Dialect is conveyed through syntax, diction, and idiom rather than excessive phonetic respelling. Characters code-switch appropriately between contexts (formal vs. informal, professional vs. personal). Class, education, age, and regional markers emerge naturally through vocabulary and sentence structure. Research-backed authenticity is evident without becoming a linguistics exercise.
**Source**: https://penultimateword.com/fiction/dialect-in-dialogue-how-to-write-authentic-dialect-and-foreign-accents/
**Notes**: PenUltimate: rely on diction, syntax, and idiom rather than excessive nonstandard spellings. Reactor (POC Guide): dialects can be weaponized to reinforce harmful stereotypes by rendering dialect as "broken" English. ServiceScape: a perpetual dilemma—how far to go in accuracy without losing the reader. Oxford Academic: in literary texts, code-switching functions differently than in everyday conversation because dialogues are embedded within a central narrative voice. Words Like Trees: the most obvious place to consider register in fiction is character dialogue—a character speaking informally to a friend may switch to formal register in an official setting.

### Silence, Evasion, and Non-Response as Tools
**What it measures**: Whether the text effectively uses what characters do not say, refuse to say, or avoid saying as meaningful dialogue tools that reveal character, create tension, and communicate through absence.
**Assumed goal**: Hemingway's iceberg theory and minimalist fiction tradition where omission carries meaning — maximalist fiction may fill silence with interior monologue, and theatrical traditions use silence differently (Pinter's pauses are structural; Beckett's silences are existential)
**Score 0**: Every question receives a direct answer. Every topic is addressed head-on. Characters never avoid, deflect, evade, or stay silent. There is no negative space in the dialogue—every possible response is spoken. The text never uses non-response as communication.
**Score 5**: Silence, evasion, deflection, subject changes, and non-answers are deployed as meaningful communication tools. A character's refusal to answer reveals more than any answer could. Silence shifts power dynamics. Pauses create tension. What characters avoid discussing becomes as important as what they say. The text uses em dashes for interruptions, ellipses for trailing off, and narrative description for meaningful silences. Non-response is distinct from oversight—readers understand the character chose not to respond.
**Source**: https://www.reallyrg.com/blog/how-to-use-silence-to-write-better-dialogue
**Notes**: ReallyRG: silence shows power dynamics—the character who speaks less often holds power. Strategic pauses force others to become uncomfortable, rushing to fill the void with unintended revelations. Bookfox identifies four types of silence: (1) pacing—slowing narrative tempo, (2) characterization—silence as defining trait, (3) atmosphere—silence as mood, (4) dialogue—characters not responding to every line. Hemingway's "Hills Like White Elephants" is the canonical example—the central conflict is never named. Eichenlaub: reserve direct answers for significant moments; making answers rare makes them meaningful.

### Dialogue Rhythm and Pacing
**What it measures**: Whether dialogue exchanges have varied rhythm through sentence length variation, speech pattern modulation, strategic interruptions, and appropriate pacing that matches the emotional intensity of the scene.
**Assumed goal**: Craft-conscious fiction tradition where prose rhythm is a deliberate tool (Fitzgerald, Morrison, McCarthy) — dialogue-heavy genre fiction (thriller, crime) uses rhythm primarily for pace, while literary fiction may use rhythm for aesthetic and emotional effect
**Score 0**: All dialogue lines are roughly the same length and follow the same syntactic pattern. The exchange has no rhythmic variation—no short punchy lines after long ones, no interruptions, no trailing off. The pace never shifts to match emotional content. Rapid exchanges read as slow; tense moments lack urgency; contemplative moments lack space.
**Score 5**: Dialogue rhythm is precisely controlled. Short sentences create urgency and impact. Longer lines slow pace for reflection or building tension. Interruptions (em dashes) convey characters cutting each other off. Ellipses indicate trailing thoughts. The rhythm of exchanges matches emotional content—fast stichomythia for conflict, longer turns for monologue and revelation. Sentence length variation within dialogue creates a natural rise and fall. Rhythm shifts signal emotional changes.
**Source**: https://livinghappy.substack.com/p/dialogue-and-pacing-rhythm-tension
**Notes**: Vaia: dialogue rhythm impacts character development by conveying personality, emotions, and relationships through speech patterns. Wikipedia (Pace): dialogue speeds up narrative pacing in contrast to description. Steven James (Rewritten Rule 4): extended monologues can reveal character powerfully—build tension beforehand "like a garden hose, the more pressure, the more dramatic the release." Distinguish dashes (interruption) from ellipses (trailing thoughts). CMOS Shop Talk: em dashes for abrupt cutoffs, ellipses for gradual trailing. Lyss Em: use interruptions sparingly—"fiction isn't Morse code."

### Power Dynamics in Conversation
**What it measures**: Whether dialogue reveals and enacts power relationships between characters through who speaks more, who interrupts, who defers, who evades, who controls topic, and who holds information.
**Assumed goal**: Fiction aware of social power structures (Austen, Morrison, Atwood, Ishiguro) where conversation is a site of power negotiation — egalitarian or utopian fiction may deliberately flatten power dynamics, and absurdist fiction (Ionesco) may subvert them for comic or philosophical effect
**Score 0**: All characters participate equally in conversation regardless of their relative power, status, knowledge, or emotional investment. No character interrupts, dominates, defers, or controls the topic. Power imbalances that should be evident in the scene are invisible in the dialogue. Who speaks most has no relationship to who holds power.
**Score 5**: Dialogue enacts power relationships through multiple channels: who speaks more or less, who interrupts, who changes subject, who asks questions vs. who answers, who uses formal vs. informal register, who controls the agenda. A character's silence can dominate a conversation. Power shifts within scenes are reflected in dialogue dynamics. Characters negotiate power through speech even when discussing seemingly neutral topics.
**Source**: https://lithub.com/danielle-evans-on-writing-into-power-dynamics/
**Notes**: Evans: when writing characters, especially women and Black women, be aware of people who are aware of power as a thing that has to be negotiated. Well-Storied (Tip #8): in multi-character scenes, resist equal speaking distribution; let power dynamics emerge naturally. ReallyRG: the character who speaks less or controls silence often holds power. In tense situations, strategic pauses force the other character to become uncomfortable, rushing to fill the void.

### Multi-Character Conversation Management
**What it measures**: Whether scenes with three or more speaking characters maintain clarity about who is speaking, give each character a distinct purpose in the conversation, and manage the additional complexity without confusion or monotony.
**Assumed goal**: Commercial and literary fiction where scenes frequently involve groups — ensemble cast genres (mystery dinner parties, workplace fiction, family sagas) especially require this skill, while two-character-focused fiction (romance) encounters it less frequently
**Score 0**: In multi-character scenes, readers cannot tell who is speaking. Characters have no distinct reason to be in the conversation. Speaker identification requires re-reading. Some characters are present but never speak or react. Dialogue tags become so frequent they overwhelm the exchange. The scene reads as a series of disconnected statements rather than a genuine group conversation.
**Score 5**: Each character in a multi-character scene has a distinct voice, a clear purpose for being present, and a recognizable stake in the conversation. Speaker identity is maintained through voice distinction, strategic tags, and action beats without over-tagging. Characters react to each other (agreements, interruptions, sidebar exchanges) rather than simply taking turns. Some characters speak more than others based on personality and stakes. The conversation has a clear dynamic—alliances, opposition, mediators.
**Source**: https://www.masterclass.com/articles/how-to-write-dialogue-between-multiple-characters
**Notes**: MasterClass: three characters are manageable; more gets trickier. Fiction University (Hardy): when there's even the slightest chance readers may not know who's speaking, clearly indicate the speaker. Helping Writers Become Authors: six questions to control multi-character scenes. Industrial Scripts: plan multi-character dialogue before writing—know each character's goal and function in the scene. Key technique: use physical actions and staging to differentiate characters.

### Dialogue Purpose and Economy
**What it measures**: Whether every dialogue exchange serves at least one narrative function (advancing plot, revealing character, building tension, delivering information, establishing mood) and whether exchanges that serve no function are cut.
**Assumed goal**: Craft-conscious fiction tradition where every element earns its place — slice-of-life fiction (Nicholson Baker, early Updike) may deliberately include seemingly purposeless conversation for texture, verisimilitude, and thematic resonance
**Score 0**: Extended dialogue exchanges serve no narrative purpose. Characters engage in small talk, pleasantries, greetings, and leave-takings that don't characterize or advance plot. Phone calls and family dinners are fully rendered when a sentence of summary would suffice. Dialogue is present because "characters need to talk" rather than because the story requires it.
**Score 5**: Every dialogue exchange performs at least one narrative function, and the best exchanges perform multiple functions simultaneously (revealing character while advancing plot while building tension). Purposeless pleasantries are elided. Conversations enter late and leave early. Each line earns its place. Dialogue that could be replaced by summary is either cut or upgraded to carry additional weight.
**Source**: https://penultimateword.com/fiction/dialogue-in-fiction-part-2-the-essentials/
**Notes**: PenUltimate identifies four primary purposes: (1) heighten emotional stakes and increase conflict, (2) advance plot, (3) provide information and backstory, (4) develop character. Cincinnati Review: unnecessary dialogue is one of the most common issues—many phone calls and family dinners don't propel the plot or give new understanding. C.S. Lakin: every line must serve a specific purpose; remove random chatting. Dialogue should accomplish multiple goals simultaneously. Well-Storied: remove any conversation that doesn't impact reader understanding.

### Internal Monologue-Dialogue Integration
**What it measures**: Whether the interplay between what characters say aloud and what the POV character thinks creates productive tension, irony, or depth rather than redundancy or confusion.
**Assumed goal**: Close-third and first-person fiction tradition where readers have access to the POV character's interior life alongside their speech — omniscient narration handles this differently by accessing multiple characters' thoughts, and objective narration (dramatic method) deliberately excludes interiority
**Score 0**: Internal monologue either redundantly restates what was just said in dialogue ("I hate you," she said. She really did hate him.) or is entirely absent during dialogue scenes, leaving the POV character's inner experience invisible. Alternatively, internal monologue overwhelms dialogue, with characters thinking paragraphs between each spoken line, destroying conversational rhythm.
**Score 5**: Internal monologue and dialogue work in productive tension. The POV character's thoughts contradict, complicate, or add irony to what they or others say aloud. Internal reactions are brief and sharp during fast exchanges, longer during pauses. The gap between said and thought creates dramatic irony and character depth. Balance shifts appropriately—more interiority during slower scenes, less during rapid exchanges.
**Source**: https://janefriedman.com/internal-dialogue/
**Notes**: Jane Friedman: internal dialogue is the greatest tool for gaining reader confidence. KillZone Blog: keep most internal thought indirect; use direct thought for special emphasis. The sharp contrast between dialogue and powerful inner thoughts can hook readers when peppered sporadically. There should be a balance between action (external) and reaction (internal) at all times. Too little introspection distances readers; too much interrupts action. CRAFT Literary: eliminate interior thoughts within dialogue exchanges to maintain momentum—internal monologue slows pacing and reduces verbal sparring intensity.

### Dialogue-to-Narration Balance
**What it measures**: Whether the proportion of dialogue to narration/description is appropriate for the genre, pacing needs, and emotional content of each scene and the work as a whole.
**Assumed goal**: Genre-aware fiction craft where dialogue proportion varies by form — thriller and romance skew dialogue-heavy (50-70%+), literary fiction varies widely, and description-heavy genres (epic fantasy, Gothic) may use less dialogue; within any work, proportion should shift scene-by-scene based on dramatic needs
**Score 0**: The work is either wall-to-wall dialogue with no narrative breaks (resulting in "talking heads" without grounding, pacing, or interiority) or wall-to-wall narration with minimal dialogue (resulting in distance, lecture-like telling, and absence of character voice). No variation in proportion across scenes.
**Score 5**: Dialogue proportion shifts appropriately by scene: fast-paced conflict scenes lean dialogue-heavy; atmospheric or reflective moments lean toward narration; emotional confrontations balance both. The work's overall proportion fits its genre and tone. Within scenes, dialogue and narration alternate to create rhythm, provide visual page breaks, and maintain reader engagement. Neither element dominates inappropriately.
**Source**: https://www.writersdigest.com/write-better-fiction/how-to-balance-action-narrative-and-dialogue-in-your-novel
**Notes**: Writer's Digest: the three elements—dialogue, action, and narrative—should be woven together to engage the reader at an emotional level. Jane Friedman: dialogue-heavy subgenres feature 50-70%+ dialogue. Page after page of narration bogs down pacing; extended dialogue without breaks creates disembodied voices. Action sequences typically need less dialogue; emotional confrontations need more. The balanced scene has a rhythm readers instinctively recognize.

### Character Name Overuse in Dialogue
**What it measures**: Whether characters address each other by name with realistic frequency rather than the artificially high frequency common in weak fiction.
**Assumed goal**: Naturalistic dialogue tradition where conversation should reflect how people actually address each other — theatrical traditions may use names more frequently for audience clarity, and children's fiction may use names more for young reader comprehension
**Score 0**: Characters constantly address each other by name in every exchange, far exceeding how people actually speak. Direct address appears multiple times per page or even per exchange. The overuse feels like a sales technique or parental scolding rather than natural conversation.
**Score 5**: Characters rarely address each other by name except when getting attention, introducing someone, or making an emphatic point. Name usage reflects real conversational patterns. Most exchanges rely on context and turn-taking rather than name-calling for speaker identification. When names are used for emphasis, the rarity makes the emphasis land.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/dont-overuse-names-in-dialogue/
**Notes**: K.M. Weiland: direct address serves only three legitimate purposes: (1) at conversation beginnings, (2) getting attention, (3) emphasizing points. Overuse neutralizes the emphasis effect. Self-Publishing School: people rarely say each other's names unless introducing, warning, or in high emotion. The overuse reminds readers they're reading fiction—it breaks immersion. C.S. Lakin identifies it as one of the top dialogue mistakes.

### Non-Linear Exchange Patterns
**What it measures**: Whether dialogue avoids the predictable question-answer "ping-pong" pattern where every question receives a direct answer and every statement receives a direct response, instead incorporating the deflections, tangents, topic changes, and non-sequiturs of real conversation.
**Assumed goal**: Literary and craft-conscious fiction where dialogue mirrors the unpredictability of human communication (Pinter, Mamet, Denis Johnson) — procedural or instructional fiction (courtroom scenes, police interrogation) may use more direct Q&A where the form demands it
**Score 0**: Every dialogue exchange follows a strict question-answer pattern. Character A asks, Character B answers. Character B asks, Character A answers. No question goes unanswered. No statement is deflected. No topic is changed. The rhythm is predictably linear and regular, lulling readers to sleep.
**Score 5**: Characters frequently don't answer questions directly. They deflect with other questions, change subjects, give oblique responses, or ignore the question entirely. Conversations go on tangents that reveal character. Statements provoke unexpected responses. The unpredictability of real conversation is captured—exchanges feel alive precisely because they don't follow a neat pattern. Direct answers are rare and therefore powerful when they arrive.
**Source**: https://anthonyeichenlaub.com/writing-dialogue-dont-answer-that-question/
**Notes**: Eichenlaub: reserve direct answers for significant moments—making answers rare makes them meaningful. Characters can employ sideways responses, question escalation, physical deflection, topic pivots, and denial strategies. Steven James (Rewritten Rule 1): real dialogue includes tangents, interruptions, and topic shifts—"The scene is not about what the scene is about." Charles Harris: ping-pong dialogue is predictable and dull—challenge yourself: don't answer the question. Emma Darwin: ping-pong dialogue gets detached from other kinds of action in the scene.

### Monologue and Long Speech Management
**What it measures**: Whether extended character speeches are earned, purposeful, and broken up effectively rather than reading as lectures or author mouthpieces.
**Assumed goal**: Modern fiction pacing conventions where reader attention spans favor concise exchanges — theatrical monologue traditions (Shakespeare, Tennessee Williams) and literary fiction (Dostoevsky, Saul Bellow) deliberately use extended speech for rhetorical effect
**Score 0**: Characters deliver long uninterrupted speeches that read as lectures or author soapboxes. No other character reacts, interrupts, or responds during the speech. The speech doesn't reveal character so much as deliver the author's message. Extended monologues appear without the pressure buildup that would justify them. Formatting doesn't handle multi-paragraph speeches correctly.
**Score 5**: When extended speeches occur, they are earned by prior tension buildup ("like a garden hose, the more pressure, the more dramatic the release"). Other characters react during or between sections of the speech. The speech reveals character through its content and delivery, not just its information. Multi-paragraph speeches are correctly formatted (no closing quotes until the final paragraph). Monologues are used sparingly and for scenes demanding deep reflection.
**Source**: https://writersandpublishersnetwork.com/monologues-dialogue-and-characters/
**Notes**: Steven James: extended monologues reveal character attitude and personality powerfully; build tension beforehand. General rule: one character speaking three sentences in a row is borderline monologue. Break up long speeches with action beats, reactions from others, and environmental description. If presenting as one long uninterrupted block, the modern reader will feel transported to Victorian England. Grammarly: ground monologues in specific emotion (grief, doubt, anger) and build toward a moment of realization.

### Emotion Shown Through Speech Patterns Rather Than Declared
**What it measures**: Whether characters' emotional states are conveyed through changes in their speech patterns, word choice, rhythm, and behavior rather than through explicit emotional declarations or adverbial tags.
**Assumed goal**: Show-don't-tell craft tradition (Chekhov, the MFA consensus, all major craft books) where prose demonstrates rather than labels emotional states — sentimental fiction traditions and some romance deliberately allow more emotional declaration for cathartic effect
**Score 0**: Emotions are conveyed through adverbs in dialogue tags ("she said angrily"), through characters explicitly stating their feelings ("I'm so frustrated right now"), or through narrative declaration ("He was clearly upset"). Speech patterns don't change when characters are emotional. A calm character and a furious character speak with identical syntax, vocabulary, and rhythm.
**Score 5**: Emotional shifts manifest in speech itself: a calm character's sentences shorten and fragment when angry; a verbose character goes quiet under stress; word choice shifts toward profanity or formality under pressure; rhythm accelerates or decelerates; a character who normally uses complete sentences trails off mid-thought. Body language and action beats show emotion rather than telling. The reader infers the emotion from behavioral evidence without being told.
**Source**: https://writershelpingwriters.net/2015/01/show-dont-tell-revealing-true-emotion-dialogue/
**Notes**: Inkshift: adverbs in dialogue tags tend to tell the reader how to feel rather than allowing the character's words and actions to paint the picture. Stephen King specifically targeted adverbs in dialogue attribution. C.S. Lakin: if you need an adverb to explain how dialogue is delivered, either the dialogue itself isn't doing its job, or you've chosen the wrong verb. Replace "said angrily" with action that shows anger. Well-Storied (Tip #6): incorporate posture, eye contact, mannerisms, and physical reactions alongside spoken words.

### Grounding Dialogue in Physical Space
**What it measures**: Whether dialogue scenes are situated in a specific, sensory environment rather than occurring in an undefined "white room" where characters exist as disembodied voices.
**Assumed goal**: Immersive fiction tradition where scenes engage all senses and physical environment matters — radio drama and some experimental fiction deliberately strips physical context for effect
**Score 0**: Dialogue occurs in an undefined space. There is no sense of where characters are, what they see, hear, or physically interact with. Extended exchanges have no environmental detail whatsoever. Characters float in a void. The reader cannot visualize the scene. This is "white room syndrome" applied to dialogue scenes.
**Score 5**: Dialogue scenes are anchored in specific environments that affect the conversation. Characters interact with their surroundings (handling objects, noticing changes, responding to environmental pressures). Setting details emerge through the dialogue and action beats rather than in separate descriptive blocks. The environment creates opportunities for subtext (a character fidgeting with a wedding ring, a ticking clock, a meal going cold). Physical space constrains and shapes the conversation.
**Source**: https://www.writingmastery.com/blog/avoiding-white-room-syndrome-tips-to-ground-your-fiction-effectively
**Notes**: The Little Bookish: white room syndrome refers to scenes where characters exist in an undefined, underdeveloped setting with dialogue and action but little sense of where the scene is happening. Writing Mastery: let characters move, gesture, or react to their environment while speaking—breaking up monotony and enriching the scene. Writer's Digest: embedding subtext in environment—Fitzgerald's ticking clock in The Great Gatsby, Jackson's pile of rocks in "The Lottery."

## Sentinels Found

### All Characters Sound Identical
**Detection**: Take any dialogue exchange with 3+ characters. Remove all dialogue tags and action beats. If every speaker is indistinguishable from every other—same vocabulary, same sentence length, same register, same rhythm—the sentinel triggers.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate dialogue from a single probability distribution (the "LLM default voice") and lack persistent character-level language models. All characters converge toward the same statistical center of the training data. As Coyote Tracks documents: "nobody has a personality beyond the prose repeating one or two characteristics."
**Design space cost**: Fiction that deliberately flattens voice for existential effect (Beckett, Kafka) would be flagged. Distinguish: intentional uniformity serves a thematic purpose and is typically accompanied by other experimental markers.
**Source**: https://coyotetracks.org/blog/ai-writing/

### Emotional Over-Articulation / Therapy-Speak Dialogue
**Detection**: Characters explicitly state their emotions to each other using direct emotional vocabulary: "I'm worried about us," "I feel betrayed," "That makes me feel vulnerable," "I understand your frustration." Check whether characters name their emotional states in dialogue rather than demonstrating them through behavior.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs default to explicit emotional labeling because their training data includes therapy/self-help patterns and because explicit statement is the statistically safest way to convey emotion. As AJ Writes documents: AI produces dialogue that sounds like "watching a very sophisticated chatbot perform humanity"—characters "all sound like they went to the same therapy retreat."
**Design space cost**: Therapeutic fiction, YA fiction where adolescents process emotions verbally, and some literary fiction (Sally Rooney) uses more explicit emotional language deliberately.
**Source**: https://medium.com/@ajtracysk/how-i-use-ai-to-write-dialogue-that-doesnt-sound-like-a-chatbot-having-feelings-5fe9df77a353

### Zero Subtext — Everything Is On-the-Nose
**Detection**: For each dialogue exchange, check: does any character say exactly what they mean with no indirection, deflection, or gap between statement and intention? If characters consistently state motivations, feelings, and agendas directly, the sentinel triggers.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs optimize for clarity and information transfer. Subtext requires modeling the gap between what a character would say and what they mean, which requires a theory of mind the LLM lacks. The LLM's probability distribution favors the most direct, clear expression of any given meaning. Now Novel's comparison found AI "tells readers exactly what's happening and why" while human dialogue "suggests rather than states."
**Design space cost**: Didactic fiction, children's literature, and moments of cathartic honesty in otherwise indirect fiction all use on-the-nose dialogue deliberately.
**Source**: https://www.nownovel.com/blog/dialogue-writing-examples/

### Cooperative Q&A Pattern / No Deflection
**Detection**: Examine dialogue exchanges for question-answer patterns. If every question receives a direct, cooperative answer with no evasions, deflections, counter-questions, subject changes, or non-answers, the sentinel triggers.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs are trained on instruction-following data where direct answers are rewarded. Their default mode is cooperative information exchange. They lack the social modeling to understand why a character would evade, deflect, or refuse to answer. The resulting dialogue reads as an interview or FAQ rather than conversation.
**Design space cost**: Procedural fiction (courtroom drama, police interrogation) may have characters forced to answer directly. Exposition-heavy scenes in hard SF may require direct information exchange.
**Source**: https://anthonyeichenlaub.com/writing-dialogue-dont-answer-that-question/

### Repetitive Generic Action Beats
**Detection**: Search for high-frequency generic action beats: nodded, smiled, sighed, raised an eyebrow, let out a breath, shook their head, bit their lip, crossed their arms, ran a hand through their hair. If the same three to five beats repeat throughout dialogue scenes, the sentinel triggers.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs draw from a narrow palette of action beats because these are the most statistically common in training data. They lack the scene-specific imagination to generate beats that arise from the particular environment and emotional context. Descriptionary: "If you searched your manuscript for 'sighing' and words used to replace sighing, you'll find more than you thought." AI text shows structural repetition like "She sighs, a mixture of relief and anticipation" followed by "She murmurs, a mixture of joy and love."
**Design space cost**: Some minimalist fiction deliberately restricts the action vocabulary. A narrow beat palette in a single short scene is not necessarily a problem.
**Source**: https://descriptionary.wordpress.com/2023/12/08/overused-action-words-in-fiction-writing/

### "As You Know, Bob" Exposition Dumps
**Detection**: Check for dialogue where characters tell each other information both would already know. If one character explains to another their shared history, the rules of their world, or the nature of their relationship purely for reader benefit, the sentinel triggers.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs need to establish context and their training data includes many examples of expository dialogue. They lack the social awareness to recognize that two characters who share a history wouldn't verbally rehearse it. The path of least resistance for an LLM delivering exposition is through dialogue.
**Design space cost**: Some genres (hard SF, secondary-world fantasy) require more worldbuilding exposition. The question is whether the exposition is delivered through contrivance or through genuine character need.
**Source**: https://www.helenhawkins-author.com/as-you-know-bob-how-to-spot-and-avoid-info-dumps-and-exposition/

### Excessive Character Name Address
**Detection**: Count how often characters address each other by name in dialogue. If characters use each other's names more than once per page of dialogue (outside of introductions or emotionally heightened moments), the sentinel triggers.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs use character names as speaker disambiguation tokens because they lack persistent conversational context tracking. Each generated line needs to re-establish who is being addressed, resulting in unnaturally frequent name usage that human speakers would find stilted.
**Design space cost**: Some genres (children's fiction) use names more frequently for reader clarity. Emotionally intense scenes may use names for emphasis.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/dont-overuse-names-in-dialogue/

### Identical Scene-Level Repetition of Dialogue Beats
**Detection**: Check for near-identical sentences, descriptions, or exchanges appearing multiple times across different scenes. If the same confrontation, the same urgent message, or the same reaction appears verbatim or near-verbatim in different chapters, the sentinel triggers.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs have no persistent memory of what they have already generated. Coyote Tracks documents characters delivering "the same urgent comm message about conspiracies three times with minimal variation." This is a fundamental architectural limitation: the model generates each passage semi-independently and converges on the same statistically likely output.
**Design space cost**: Deliberate literary repetition (as in oral tradition, liturgical fiction, or Gertrude Stein) uses repetition structurally and thematically. The distinction is whether repetition is a controlled device or an artifact.
**Source**: https://coyotetracks.org/blog/ai-writing/

### Uniform Sentence Length in Dialogue
**Detection**: Measure the variance in sentence/line length within dialogue exchanges. If dialogue lines cluster tightly around a mean length with minimal variation (no short punchy lines, no long complex ones, no fragments), the sentinel triggers.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs produce text with statistically averaged sentence length because their generation process optimizes for the most probable next token across the entire training distribution. Junia AI: LLM default voice shows "similar sentence lengths throughout," "predictably-wrapping paragraphs," and "absence of fragmented sentences." Human dialogue rhythm requires deliberate variation.
**Design space cost**: Some highly controlled, formal dialogue scenes may use uniform line length deliberately (ritual exchanges, military protocol).
**Source**: https://www.junia.ai/blog/llm-default-voice-ai-writing

### No Silence or Non-Response in Dialogue
**Detection**: Check whether every line of dialogue receives a verbal response. If no character ever stays silent, declines to respond, changes the subject without answering, or is described as not speaking when a response is expected, the sentinel triggers.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs are trained on turn-taking data where every prompt receives a response. They have no model of strategic silence or meaningful non-response. AJ Writes: "AI fills silences with meaning when silence IS the meaning." The cooperative turn-taking default of instruction-tuned models makes silence invisible to the generator.
**Design space cost**: Fast-paced action dialogue and comedy may legitimately maintain rapid turn-taking with few silences.
**Source**: https://medium.com/@ajtracysk/how-i-use-ai-to-write-dialogue-that-doesnt-sound-like-a-chatbot-having-feelings-5fe9df77a353

### Genre/Emotion Announcement in Dialogue
**Detection**: Check whether dialogue lines explicitly announce genre elements, emotional states, or thematic content: "This dark forest is truly terrifying," "As a detective, I must solve this mystery," "Our love is the most important thing." If characters verbalize what should emerge from context, the sentinel triggers.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs reinforce genre markers because their training data associates certain genres with certain phrases. Now Novel's comparison found AI dialogue "reads as shoehorning in the genre at every opportunity"—genre doesn't need to announce itself. The LLM's optimization for clarity produces characters who narrate their own experience rather than living it.
**Design space cost**: Parody and metafiction deliberately lampshade genre conventions. Some pulp fiction uses genre announcement as style.
**Source**: https://www.nownovel.com/blog/dialogue-writing-examples/

### Hedge Words and Synthetic Politeness Markers
**Detection**: Check dialogue for hedge words and politeness markers characteristic of LLM default voice: "perhaps," "it seems," "I understand," "that's a valid point," "to be fair," "if I'm being honest." If characters consistently speak with the diplomatic caution of an AI assistant rather than with the directness, rudeness, or awkwardness of real people, the sentinel triggers.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: RLHF training optimizes LLMs for helpful, harmless, honest output, which produces dialogue saturated with diplomatic hedging and courtesy markers. Junia AI documents: "overly-polite courtesy markers," "balanced, non-committal phrasing." Real people are blunt, rude, evasive, and tactless in ways that RLHF-trained models avoid.
**Design space cost**: Characters who are genuinely diplomatic, therapeutic professionals, or cultural contexts where indirectness is valued may use these markers authentically.
**Source**: https://www.junia.ai/blog/llm-default-voice-ai-writing

### Participial Phrase Overuse in Action Beats
**Detection**: Count the frequency of present participial phrases (-ing constructions) used as action beats around dialogue: "she said, turning toward the window," "he muttered, running a hand through his hair," "she whispered, pulling her coat tighter." If these appear at two to five times the rate of other beat constructions, the sentinel triggers.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Research documents that AI-generated text uses participial phrases at two to five times the rate found in human-written text. The construction "main clause + comma + -ing verb phrase" is a statistically overrepresented pattern in LLM output because it is a common, grammatically safe way to attach action to dialogue.
**Design space cost**: Participial phrases are legitimate English constructions. The issue is disproportionate frequency, not presence.
**Source**: https://elearningindustry.com/how-to-tell-if-something-is-written-by-ai-telltale-signs-to-look-for

### "A [Noun] of [Emotion]" Construction Repetition
**Detection**: Search for the pattern "a [noun] of [emotion/abstract noun]": "a wave of relief," "a surge of anger," "a mix of emotions," "a flicker of doubt," "a pang of guilt," "a rush of adrenaline." If this construction appears more than twice in a work, and especially if it appears in or around dialogue, the sentinel triggers.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs overuse this construction because it is a common, grammatically safe way to describe emotional states. Thousands of training examples use "a tapestry of emotions" and "the weight of the moment." The construction tells rather than shows, substituting a formula for specific sensory detail.
**Design space cost**: Occasional use is fine in any prose. The issue is frequency and dependence on this single pattern for emotional description.
**Source**: https://seanjkernan.substack.com/p/13-signs-you-used-chatgpt-to-write

### Talking Heads Without Physical Grounding
**Detection**: Find dialogue passages of 10+ consecutive lines with no action beats, no setting detail, no physical description, and no internal monologue. If characters exist as pure disembodied voices for extended stretches, the sentinel triggers.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs tend to generate dialogue as a sequence of alternating speech acts without the physical-world modeling needed to intersperse environmental detail, body language, and spatial awareness. The model generates text token by token and the easiest continuation of a dialogue line is another dialogue line.
**Design space cost**: Minimalist fiction and rapid-fire exchanges in thriller/crime fiction may deliberately strip beats for pace. Short stretches of unadorned dialogue (4-6 lines) are normal between beats.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/are-your-characters-talking-heads/

### Cliche Phrase Clustering in Dialogue
**Detection**: Check dialogue and surrounding prose for clusters of stock phrases: "the weight of [noun]," "a silence stretched between them," "eyes that held [emotion]," "couldn't help but [verb]," "let out a breath [he/she] didn't know [he/she] was holding," "the air was thick with [noun]." If three or more stock phrases appear within the same scene, the sentinel triggers.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs are, as one critic put it, "hyper-accelerators for cliches"—they generate language through probability, making cliche the default output. Coyote Tracks found the phrase "the weight of the crown" appeared 8 times in one AI novel and 45 times in another. Stock phrases are the statistical mode of the training distribution.
**Design space cost**: Cliches exist in human writing too. The diagnostic is clustering—three or more in a single scene signals pattern rather than occasional lapse.
**Source**: https://coyotetracks.org/blog/ai-writing/

### Said-Bookism / Ornate Dialogue Tags
**Detection**: Check for creative dialogue tag verbs beyond said/asked/whispered/shouted: "he exclaimed," "she retorted," "he opined," "she breathed," "he intoned," "she mused." If more than 20% of dialogue tags use ornate alternatives, the sentinel triggers.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs trained on fiction data have absorbed both the "said-bookism" tradition and the modern "said-only" convention, but many models default to variety-seeking in tags because varied vocabulary scores well in language model objectives. The result is tag overuse that marks the text as generated rather than crafted.
**Design space cost**: Children's literature, some genre fiction, and certain authorial styles (J.K. Rowling) use more varied tags as a stylistic choice. Historical fiction may use period-appropriate attribution.
**Source**: https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/SaidBookism

### Adverb-Heavy Dialogue Attribution
**Detection**: Count adverbs modifying dialogue tags: "she said quietly," "he said angrily," "she asked nervously," "he replied coldly." If more than 10% of dialogue tags include an adverb, the sentinel triggers.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs use adverbs as an easy shortcut to signal emotion because explicit emotional labeling is statistically common in training data. The adverb tells the reader what the dialogue should convey but doesn't—it's a compensation mechanism for dialogue that fails to show emotion through content and context. Stephen King and Elmore Leonard both specifically condemned this pattern.
**Design space cost**: J.K. Rowling famously uses adverbs in dialogue tags as a stylistic choice, and some genre fiction uses them for pacing. The question is whether the adverb replaces effective dialogue or supplements it.
**Source**: https://inkshift.io/resources/adverbs-fiction
