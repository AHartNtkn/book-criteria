## Criteria Found

### Sensory Specificity
**What it measures**: Whether sensory details are specific and concrete rather than generic and abstract.
**Assumed goal**: Literary realism tradition (Chekhov, Munro, Trevor) where precise observed detail creates verisimilitude and emotional truth through selection of telling particulars, rather than poetic/impressionist tradition where sensory experience may be deliberately blurred or fragmented.
**Score 0**: Sensory details are entirely generic ("beautiful sunset," "gentle breeze," "the forest was beautiful"), abstract ("it felt threatening"), or absent. Details could describe any scene in any setting and carry no information about this particular moment, place, or character.
**Score 5**: Every sensory detail is precisely observed, particular to the specific scene, and could not be transplanted to a different context without loss. Details reveal character psychology, advance thematic concerns, or carry narrative weight beyond mere decoration. The reader can reconstruct the specific sensory experience rather than a category of experience.
**Source**: https://www.craftliterary.com/2025/03/26/show-dont-tell-what-ai-cant-do/ and https://www.writersdigest.com/mfa-confidential-blog/the-importance-of-specific-details
**Notes**: CRAFT Literary notes AI "summarizes the feelings one might have" rather than rendering specific sensory particulars. Abigail Thomas (Writer's Digest) advises "Eliminate all abstract nouns" and provides the example of rejecting generic descriptions of rural beauty in favor of "newspapers between the sheets and blankets for insulation"—a tangible, memorable detail.

### Sensory Balance Across Modalities
**What it measures**: Whether the text engages senses beyond sight, and whether sensory modality selection is purposeful.
**Assumed goal**: Immersive literary fiction tradition (Morrison, Proulx, Erdrich) where multi-sensory description creates embodied reading experience—not visual-cinematic fiction (screenwriting-adjacent prose) where visual primacy is intentional.
**Score 0**: Description relies exclusively on visual imagery with no engagement of sound, smell, touch, or taste. Non-visual senses are never invoked, or are invoked only through cliché ("the smell of coffee").
**Score 5**: Multiple sensory modalities are engaged strategically, with the dominant sense chosen to serve the scene's emotional and narrative needs. Non-visual senses provide unique information that sight cannot (e.g., recognizing someone by cologne before seeing them, or noticing tension through quality of silence). Sensory foregrounding shifts across scenes based on context.
**Source**: https://writersvictoria.org.au/writing-life/the-power-of-using-the-senses-in-your-writing/ and https://allaboutwritingcourses.com/2025/05/30/sensory-writing-techniques-beyond-sight/
**Notes**: Writers Victoria notes "seeing is the predominant sense" and visual dominance infects descriptive prose. All About Writing argues foregrounding a non-visual sense "can be particularly impactful" and that "each sense can provide unique information that sight might miss."

### Economy of Description
**What it measures**: Whether description achieves its effects with minimal words, without unnecessary elaboration or padding.
**Assumed goal**: Minimalist literary tradition (Hemingway, Carver, Hempel, Davis) where restraint amplifies impact through omission and reader inference—not maximalist tradition (Pynchon, Wallace, Rushdie) where exhaustive detail and digression create meaning through accumulation.
**Score 0**: Descriptions are padded with redundant adjectives, unnecessary adverbs, and details that carry no narrative, characterological, or thematic weight. Every noun is modified; every scene is exhaustively catalogued.
**Score 5**: Every descriptive word earns its place. Adjectives and adverbs are rare and precise. The text trusts the reader to infer and imagine. Description is compressed to its essential elements, achieving maximum impact with minimum verbiage.
**Source**: https://bookishbay.com/hemingways-iceberg-theory-art-of-minimalist-writing/ and https://www.masterclass.com/articles/literary-minimalism-explained
**Notes**: Hemingway's Iceberg Theory holds that "the deeper meaning of a story resides beneath the surface" and that omitted material strengthens the visible text. MasterClass defines literary minimalism as writing "devoid of flowery, excessively descriptive language" that "prioritizes brevity, allowing the reader to make up for a lack of verbiage with their imagination."

### Maximalist Density and Accumulation
**What it measures**: Whether dense, layered description creates meaning through accretion, sensory saturation, and linguistic richness.
**Assumed goal**: Maximalist literary tradition (McCarthy, Morrison, Faulkner, Melville) where the weight, texture, and accumulation of prose creates an immersive, almost hallucinatory effect—not minimalist tradition where less is more.
**Score 0**: Dense description is merely bloated—piling on adjectives and metaphors without coherence, rhythm, or thematic purpose. The accumulation is chaotic rather than orchestrated.
**Score 5**: Dense description creates a unified sensory and thematic experience where each detail amplifies the others. Accumulation builds toward an effect that no single detail could achieve alone. The prose has internal rhythm and architecture despite its density. Every element contributes to a cohesive atmospheric and thematic design.
**Source**: https://www.masterclass.com/articles/minimalist-literature-and-maximalist-literature-guide and https://theconversation.com/cormac-mccarthys-fearless-approach-to-writing-207709
**Notes**: Maximalism emphasizes "abundance of detail, complexity, and the interplay of multiple themes and voices." McCarthy's prose in Blood Meridian is described as "dense, with details piling up one after another" but with purposeful architecture. Morrison's writing is characterized by "vivid lyrical quality and emotional intensity" where "prose flows like poetry."

### Figurative Language Originality
**What it measures**: Whether metaphors, similes, and other figurative language are fresh, surprising, and contextually appropriate rather than clichéd or generic.
**Assumed goal**: Literary fiction tradition valuing original perception (Nabokov, Woolf, Atwood) where figurative language reveals new ways of seeing—not genre fiction tradition where transparent, functional prose avoids drawing attention to itself.
**Score 0**: Figurative language consists entirely of clichés ("heart of gold," "cold as ice," "tears fell like rain"), dead metaphors, or stock comparisons that add no new perception. Metaphors are interchangeable with any other scene.
**Score 5**: Every metaphor or simile creates a surprising but apt connection that illuminates something specific about this moment, character, or situation. Figurative language is contextually grounded (thematically coherent with setting, character, genre) and reveals something that literal description could not. The reader sees the subject newly through the comparison.
**Source**: https://frictionlit.org/mastering-figurative-language-a-guide-to-metaphors-similes-and-analogies/
**Notes**: F(r)iction states "A strong metaphor will be surprising but accessible; it says something new without confusing the reader." Weak metaphors are "clichés like 'bite the bullet' or 'life is a journey' that feel derivative, unimaginative, and unoriginal." Thematic coherence matters: "if a story takes place in a bakery, using figures of speech related to baking, sweets, or cooking might make a lot of sense."

### Figurative Language Coherence
**What it measures**: Whether metaphors, similes, and figurative patterns are internally consistent and do not clash, mix, or undermine each other.
**Assumed goal**: Controlled literary craft (any tradition) where the writer deliberately manages figurative vehicles for coherent effect—not deliberately surrealist or absurdist fiction where mixed imagery is a purposeful technique.
**Score 0**: Metaphors are mixed ("At first she was a weighted barge, then a sunflower tracking the light"), vehicles clash within sentences, or multiple unrelated figurative frameworks collide within a single passage ("five-car metaphor pile-up"). Figurative language creates confusion rather than clarity.
**Score 5**: Figurative vehicles are consistent within passages and across the work. Extended metaphors are sustained without breaking. When vehicles shift, the transition is deliberate and controlled. The figurative framework reinforces rather than undermines the literal content.
**Source**: https://www.writingclasses.com/toolbox/ask-writer/what-is-a-mixed-metaphor and https://janefriedman.com/do-you-torture-your-metaphors-the-problem-of-self-conscious-writing/
**Notes**: Gotham Writers defines mixed metaphor as "a misuse of metaphoric language—it mixes two figurative phrases in a way which doesn't logically make sense." Jane Friedman identifies "forced metaphors" as comparisons "inserted randomly without genuine connection to the scene's tone or meaning" and states a metaphor fails when it "creates connotations that contradict intended meaning."

### Extended/Controlling Metaphor Craft
**What it measures**: Whether extended or controlling metaphors sustain and develop across passages or the entire work, creating unified meaning.
**Assumed goal**: Literary fiction that uses sustained figurative architecture (Atwood, Morrison, Ishiguro) to create layered meaning—not transparent-prose fiction that avoids calling attention to figurative frameworks.
**Score 0**: No sustained figurative patterns exist, or attempts at extended metaphor break down, become redundant, or lose coherence partway through. The metaphor is abandoned or contradicted.
**Score 5**: A central metaphor or network of metaphors sustains across the work, developing in complexity and revealing new facets as the narrative progresses. The controlling metaphor illuminates theme, character, and plot simultaneously without becoming heavy-handed or monotonous.
**Source**: https://noamleon.substack.com/p/writing-tool-5-types-and-use-of-metaphors and https://www.gilliamwritersgroup.com/blog/when-all-the-worlds-a-stage-crafting-extended-metaphors
**Notes**: Noam Leon Kaestner describes the controlling metaphor as "one comparison becomes the backbone of the narrative" that "guides how plot and characters are perceived." Challenges include "maintaining consistency, avoiding redundancy, and ensuring accessibility for readers."

### Objective Correlative
**What it measures**: Whether emotions are expressed through concrete external particulars (objects, situations, chains of events) rather than stated directly.
**Assumed goal**: Modernist and post-modernist literary tradition (Eliot, Hemingway, Chekhov) where emotion is evoked through sensory correlatives rather than named—not sentimental fiction tradition where direct emotional statement is valued for its accessibility and warmth.
**Score 0**: Emotions are stated directly and abstractly ("she felt sad," "he was angry," "a wave of grief washed over her") with no concrete external objects, situations, or events that embody the emotion. The reader is told what to feel rather than given materials from which to feel it.
**Score 5**: Emotions are never named but are unmistakably evoked through precisely chosen external particulars—objects, physical actions, environmental details, chains of events—that serve as "the formula of that particular emotion" (Eliot). The reader experiences the emotion through sensory engagement with concrete details rather than through abstract labels.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Objective_correlative and https://www.britannica.com/art/objective-correlative
**Notes**: Eliot defined it as "a set of objects, a situation, a chain of events which shall be the formula of that particular emotion; such that when the external facts, which must terminate in sensory experience, are given, the emotion is immediately evoked." Lady Macbeth's compulsive handwashing is cited as a successful correlative for guilt. Balachandra Rajan criticizes Eliot's assumption that "there is a verbal formula for any given state of emotion" that evokes identical responses across all audiences.

### Pathetic Fallacy Control
**What it measures**: Whether the attribution of human emotions to natural/environmental elements is handled with craft and purpose, avoiding mechanical mirroring.
**Assumed goal**: Sophisticated literary fiction (any period) that uses environmental emotion deliberately and with awareness of the device's artificiality—not folk-tale or fairy-tale traditions where nature-as-mirror is a welcomed convention.
**Score 0**: Weather and environment mechanically mirror character emotions in the most predictable way (rain during sadness, sunshine during happiness, storms during anger). The environment functions as a crude emotional amplifier with no subtlety, irony, or counterpoint.
**Score 5**: When natural/environmental elements reflect or comment on human emotions, they do so with awareness and nuance—through irony (devastating news on a beautiful day), displacement (calm environment during internal turmoil), or subtle resonance rather than direct mirroring. The writer controls the device rather than defaulting to it unconsciously.
**Source**: https://www.litcharts.com/literary-devices-and-terms/pathetic-fallacy and https://bibisco.com/blog/pathetic-fallacy-definition-and-examples-in-narrative/
**Notes**: bibisco notes "stormy weather during an argument and sunshine during a happy ending are so expected that they risk feeling predictable. A character receiving devastating news on a beautiful spring day can be more unsettling than the same scene in pouring rain, because the contrast between inner and outer worlds creates its own kind of tension." Ruskin coined the term in 1856 as a critical term, not a compliment.

### Show vs. Tell in Description
**What it measures**: Whether the text renders experience through concrete sensory detail and action rather than summarizing or explaining what the reader should understand.
**Assumed goal**: Dramatized literary fiction tradition (James, Chekhov, Flaubert) where scenes are rendered through specific detail—not essayistic or philosophical fiction (Musil, Kundera, Bernhard) where discursive telling is a deliberate narrative strategy.
**Score 0**: Description consists entirely of summary statements ("the room was creepy," "she was beautiful," "the city was impressive"), abstract characterizations, and authorial assertions about qualities the reader should infer from evidence.
**Score 5**: Scenes are rendered through specific, observable details that allow readers to experience and interpret for themselves. The writer provides evidence (concrete sensory detail, significant action, telling gesture) and trusts readers to draw conclusions. Abstract qualities are demonstrated rather than asserted.
**Source**: https://writers.com/show-dont-tell-writing and https://blog.reedsy.com/show-dont-tell/
**Notes**: Writers.com notes showing "transmits something for the reader to experience through a mix of vivid imagery, descriptive verbs, and immersive details" while telling merely asserts. Reedsy emphasizes that "a novel that contains only showing would be incredibly long" and that "scenes that are important to the story" should be dramatized while transitional material can be told.

### Description-Action Integration
**What it measures**: Whether descriptive passages are woven into ongoing action and character movement rather than delivered as static blocks that halt narrative momentum.
**Assumed goal**: Propulsive literary fiction (le Carré, Atwood, Tartt) where description serves narrative momentum—not contemplative fiction (Sebald, Woolf, Proust) where static description and digression are the point.
**Score 0**: Description exists in isolated blocks that stop the story dead—paragraphs of pure setting description with no character action, no narrative movement, and no connection to the scene's dramatic purpose. Characters disappear while the author describes.
**Score 5**: Description is embedded in character action and perception. Setting details emerge as characters move through and interact with environments. Description advances characterization or plot while establishing atmosphere. The reader never feels the story has paused for a descriptive set-piece.
**Source**: https://janefriedman.com/effective-setting/ and http://blog.janicehardy.com/2010/11/making-it-happen-tips-on-writing-action.html
**Notes**: Jane Friedman advises "don't bring the action to a screeching halt by interrupting with long, un-integrated description" and states that "when setting integrates with plot, character, and mood, description will blend seamlessly into the other elements." Janice Hardy provides the integration example: "He ducked behind the marble pillar, its cold surface scraping his cheek."

### Description Filtered Through POV
**What it measures**: Whether descriptive passages are colored by the observing character's psychology, knowledge, emotional state, and sensory priorities rather than delivered from a neutral omniscient camera.
**Assumed goal**: Close-POV literary fiction (most contemporary literary fiction) where perception is subjective and revealing—not classical omniscient narration (Tolstoy, Austen, Eliot) where an authoritative narrator describes from outside any single character.
**Score 0**: Description reads as a neutral camera recording—an impartial inventory of details with no trace of a perceiving consciousness. Any character could be looking at this scene and the description would be identical. The POV character's personality, emotion, and knowledge do not color what is noticed or how it is described.
**Score 5**: Every descriptive detail reveals the perceiving character—what they notice (and don't notice), the language they use to process it, the emotional valence they assign to it, and the associations it triggers for them. Different characters would describe the same scene differently. Description is simultaneously world-building and characterization.
**Source**: https://janefriedman.com/point-of-view/ and https://theeditorsblog.net/2016/11/28/the-perspective-from-inside-a-character/
**Notes**: Jane Friedman states "In deep third or first-person POV, descriptions of people, objects, places, or events shouldn't sound like they're coming from an impartial observer." The Editor's Blog notes "the viewpoint character's perception of and reaction to sights, sounds, odors, touch, and taste add to tone."

### Atmosphere and Mood Creation
**What it measures**: Whether descriptive choices create a coherent emotional atmosphere that pervades the scene or work.
**Assumed goal**: Atmospheric literary fiction (du Maurier, Shirley Jackson, McCarthy, Gothic tradition) where mood is a primary vehicle for meaning—not plot-driven fiction where atmosphere is subordinate to event.
**Score 0**: No discernible mood or atmosphere emerges from the description. Descriptive details are chosen randomly without emotional coherence, or the description is too sparse to create any atmospheric effect. The scene has no emotional texture.
**Score 5**: Description creates a unified, palpable atmosphere through coordinated choices of detail, diction, rhythm, and sensory emphasis. The reader feels immersed in a specific emotional environment. Mood is created through accumulation of carefully chosen concrete details rather than through direct statement ("it was eerie").
**Source**: https://writershelpingwriters.net/2008/08/creating-atmosphere/ and https://theeditorsblog.net/2013/04/19/tone-mood-style-the-feel-of-fiction/
**Notes**: The Editor's Blog defines mood as "the atmospheric effect of tone" and "the overall feeling for the audience an author creates." It is "not the reader's emotions, but the atmosphere (the vibe) of a scene or story." Word choice, sensory details, character perspective, and syntax all contribute.

### Tonal Consistency in Description
**What it measures**: Whether the descriptive register, diction, and imagery maintain a consistent emotional tone within scenes and across the work, or jar the reader with unexplained shifts.
**Assumed goal**: Unified literary craft (any tradition) where tonal control demonstrates authorial command—not experimental fiction (Barthelme, Brautigan) where deliberate tonal collision is a technique.
**Score 0**: Descriptive tone lurches between registers without cause—ornate literary description in one paragraph, casual colloquial in the next; serious atmospheric detail followed by incongruously comic imagery. The reader is jolted out of the fictional dream by tonal whiplash.
**Score 5**: The descriptive register is consistent and purposeful. When tonal shifts occur, they are motivated by narrative events (a character's mood change, a revelation, a shift in time or place) and are executed deliberately rather than accidentally. Diction, imagery, and rhythm cohere within each scene and across the work.
**Source**: https://theeditorsblog.net/2013/04/19/tone-mood-style-the-feel-of-fiction/ and https://lindasclare.com/2021/12/writing-a-consistent-tone/
**Notes**: The Editor's Blog warns that "if you throw in a passage or scene outside the tone you've set—say the story unfolds in serious prose, then suddenly adds a poetic bit or a joke, readers may be jolted out of your story" and compares the effect to "a sheet of glass had shattered and the pieces were off kilter."

### Defamiliarization / Making Strange
**What it measures**: Whether description refreshes the reader's perception of familiar objects, settings, or experiences by presenting them in unfamiliar ways.
**Assumed goal**: Modernist and literary fiction tradition (Tolstoy through Shklovsky's lens, Kafka, Nabokov, McCarthy) where literature's purpose is to disrupt "automatized" perception—not genre fiction tradition where transparent description serves plot efficiency.
**Score 0**: Description presents everything in the most familiar, expected way. Objects are described through their most conventional attributes. Nothing in the description challenges the reader's habitual perception or makes them see something they've seen before as if for the first time.
**Score 5**: Description consistently makes the familiar strange—presenting ordinary objects, settings, or experiences through unexpected angles, unusual sensory emphasis, or language that forces the reader to perceive freshly rather than recognize automatically. The reader sees the world newly through the description.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Defamiliarization and https://www.gilliamwritersgroup.com/blog/defamiliarization-the-art-of-making-strange
**Notes**: Shklovsky argued that "habitual perception, what he called 'automatization,' deadens the senses" and that art should "convey the sensation of an object as something visible, not as something recognizable." Techniques include: describing a familiar scene using only non-visual senses, shifting perspective to non-human viewpoints, magnifying mundane details with intense focus, and using unconventional word choices.

### Word-Level Precision (Le Mot Juste)
**What it measures**: Whether individual word choices demonstrate precision, with each word being the most exact available term for the intended meaning, connotation, and rhythm.
**Assumed goal**: Precision-oriented literary tradition (Flaubert, Nabokov, James) where every word is scrutinized for exact fit—not spontaneous/improvisational tradition (Kerouac, some Beat writers) where first-thought-best-thought energy is valued over surgical revision.
**Score 0**: Words are approximately correct but not precise—near-synonyms are used interchangeably, connotations clash with intended meaning, words are chosen for their sound or impressiveness rather than their exact denotation. The thesaurus has replaced the dictionary.
**Score 5**: Every word is the inevitable, irreplaceable choice for its position. Denotation, connotation, rhythm, and sonic compatibility with surrounding language all align. The text could not be improved by substituting any single word with a synonym. The word choices demonstrate what Flaubert meant by "no such thing as a synonym."
**Source**: https://bookishbay.com/le-mot-juste/ and https://www.britannica.com/art/mot-juste
**Notes**: Bookish Bay states le mot juste "represents not just correctness, but perfection—that single term whose meaning, connotation, and rhythm achieve an absolute, irreplaceable alignment with the writer's intent." Flaubert "would spend entire days crafting a single paragraph, striving to find the exact word." Practical techniques: exhaustive enumeration and subtraction of candidate words; synonym scrutiny; contextual audition by reading aloud; the principle of omission (testing if removal diminishes meaning).

### Prose Rhythm in Description
**What it measures**: Whether descriptive passages employ purposeful variation in sentence length, structure, and cadence to create emotional effects and maintain reader engagement.
**Assumed goal**: Prose-conscious literary fiction (any tradition) where the sound of sentences matters—not purely transparent-prose fiction where rhythm is subordinate to information delivery.
**Score 0**: Descriptive passages have monotonous sentence length and structure—either all short declarative sentences or all long complex sentences with no variation. The prose has no rhythm, no rise and fall, no deliberate pacing through sentence construction.
**Score 5**: Sentence length and structure vary purposefully within descriptive passages. Short sentences create impact, urgency, or emphasis at key moments. Long sentences create atmosphere, contemplation, or sensory immersion. The rhythm of the prose reinforces the emotional content of the description—fast when the scene demands speed, slow when it demands lingering.
**Source**: https://fiveable.me/english-prose-style/unit-3/sentence-length-rhythm/study-guide/w7TJLJPJ88fdUs5F and https://famouswritingroutines.com/writing-tips/the-sound-of-writing-how-rhythm-and-cadence-affect-your-story/
**Notes**: Fiveable states "Sentence length is the most fundamental lever of prose rhythm" and "varying it creates the natural rise and fall that keeps readers engaged." Famous Writing Routines notes "short sentences punch and create impact, urgency, and emphasis" while "long sentences flow and create atmosphere, depth, and contemplation."

### Scene Anchoring and Spatial Clarity
**What it measures**: Whether description provides sufficient orienting detail for the reader to know where they are, when they are, and what the physical space looks like at the start of scenes.
**Assumed goal**: Clear narrative fiction (mainstream literary and genre fiction) where the reader should always know their spatial and temporal location—not deliberately disorienting fiction (some experimental, dream-logic, or stream-of-consciousness work) where spatial confusion is the point.
**Score 0**: Scenes begin with no spatial or temporal grounding—characters speak and act in a void. The reader cannot picture where the scene takes place, what time it is, or what the physical environment looks like. New scenes begin without any reorientation. ("White Room Syndrome" / "Talking Heads Syndrome")
**Score 5**: Every scene opens with sufficient anchoring detail—POV, place, and time are established in the first few paragraphs without being heavy-handed. The reader always knows where they are. New locations are introduced with enough specific detail to create a mental image, while familiar locations receive lighter touches.
**Source**: https://fictionary.co/journal/three-ways-to-know-if-a-scene-is-anchored/ and https://www.writingmastery.com/blog/avoiding-white-room-syndrome
**Notes**: Fictionary defines scene anchoring as orienting the reader in POV, setting, and time within the first few paragraphs. Writing Mastery describes White Room Syndrome as "the feeling where the characters are more or less floating in an undefined space" and advises "at least two or three details about the setting every time you change scenes."

### Description as Characterization
**What it measures**: Whether descriptive choices about what a character notices, how they describe it, and what language they use reveal character psychology, values, background, or emotional state.
**Assumed goal**: Character-driven literary fiction (Austen, James, Woolf, contemporary literary fiction) where interior life is revealed through perception—not plot-driven fiction where description serves world-building rather than characterization.
**Score 0**: All characters notice and describe the same things in the same way. Description is functionally identical regardless of whose perspective it comes through. No information about character psychology, values, or emotional state can be inferred from what is described or how.
**Score 5**: What a character notices reveals what they care about. How they describe it reveals how they think. What they ignore reveals their blind spots. Different characters observing the same space would produce recognizably different descriptions. The selection and framing of descriptive detail is itself a characterization tool.
**Source**: https://janefriedman.com/effective-setting/ and https://theeditorsblog.net/2013/04/19/tone-mood-style-the-feel-of-fiction/
**Notes**: Jane Friedman notes "what a character notices reveals their psychology. Different characters noticing different objects in the same room reveals their distinct perspectives." The Editor's Blog states "What the viewpoint character focuses on" and "through his changing reactions" establish both tone and character.

### Significant Detail Selection
**What it measures**: Whether descriptive details are chosen for their narrative, thematic, or characterological significance rather than being arbitrary or exhaustive.
**Assumed goal**: Purposeful literary fiction (any tradition from realism to postmodernism) where every detail earns its inclusion—not naturalist fiction (Zola, Dreiser) where exhaustive documentation of environment is part of the aesthetic program.
**Score 0**: Details appear to be chosen arbitrarily—random physical attributes listed with no connection to theme, character, or plot. Description reads as an inventory or "laundry list" of everything visible rather than a curated selection. No detail carries more weight than any other.
**Score 5**: Every detail carries significance beyond its literal content—it reveals character, advances theme, foreshadows plot, or creates atmosphere. The writer has made visible choices about what to include and what to omit. Details that seem incidental on first reading prove meaningful in retrospect. Nothing is described merely because it exists.
**Source**: https://davehood59.wordpress.com/2011/08/26/fictional-truth-and-significant-detail-in-short-fiction/ and https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/common-writing-mistakes-much-description/
**Notes**: A detail "is concrete if it appeals to one of the five senses; it is significant if it also conveys an idea or a judgment or both." Helping Writers Become Authors states that too much description "saps the story of subtext" and recommends: "Does it advance plot? Does it develop character? Does it establish theme? Does it fire reader imagination? If the answer is no, cut it."

### Concrete vs. Abstract Description
**What it measures**: Whether description operates in concrete, sensory terms rather than abstract, conceptual language.
**Assumed goal**: Sensory-grounded literary fiction (Chekhov's dictum: "Don't tell me the moon is shining; show me the glint of light on broken glass") where the concrete precedes the abstract—not philosophical or essayistic fiction (Musil, Bernhard) where abstract language is the medium.
**Score 0**: Description is entirely abstract—"the room was threatening," "she was beautiful," "the landscape was desolate"—with no concrete sensory evidence. The reader must supply all the imagery themselves from abstract labels.
**Score 5**: Description operates in concrete, sensory terms that allow the reader to construct the abstract meaning themselves. "Beautiful" is replaced by the specific features that create beauty. "Threatening" is replaced by the specific details that create threat. The movement is always from specific to universal, never the reverse.
**Source**: https://litreactor.com/columns/big-a-little-a-writing-between-the-concrete-and-abstract and https://writelabel.medium.com/using-concrete-vs-abstract-language-c38487f98ce5
**Notes**: LitReactor argues "Fiction occurs when the particular penetrates the general, when the specific occurs within the abstract" and that writers should "write from the concrete out." Concrete description is "sensory description—what we can touch, see, smell, hear, and taste," while abstract descriptions "have no weight or texture."

### Purple Prose vs. Purposeful Lushness
**What it measures**: Whether ornate, elaborate description serves the narrative or exists merely to display the writer's verbal facility.
**Assumed goal**: Controlled literary aestheticism (Woolf, Morrison, Ondaatje) where lush prose serves emotional and thematic purpose—not austere prose tradition (Hemingway, Carver) where ornamentation is inherently suspect.
**Score 0**: Description is overwrought—excessive adjectives, exaggerated comparisons, multisyllabic words chosen for sound rather than meaning, elaborate inner monologue that adds nothing. The prose draws attention to itself at the expense of the story. "Like a cardboard cutout of a celebrity: from a distance it looks convincing, but there's nothing behind it."
**Score 5**: Even the most elaborate, lush description serves the narrative—creating atmosphere, revealing character, advancing theme, or embodying the story's emotional reality. Ornate prose has "the elegance and cohesion" that distinguishes it from purple prose. The verbal richness is inseparable from the story's meaning rather than layered on top of it.
**Source**: https://reedsy.com/blog/purple-prose/ and https://litreactor.com/columns/storyville-avoiding-purple-prose-in-your-fiction
**Notes**: Reedsy distinguishes purple prose from complex/elaborate writing: "Virginia Woolf's stream-of-consciousness narratives—flowery but serves narrative purpose" and "David Foster Wallace's dense writing—difficult but meaningful, not excessive." Prevention strategies include: write in your own voice, prioritize substance over description, use thesaurus sparingly, adopt reader perspective.

### Synesthesia and Cross-Sensory Imagery
**What it measures**: Whether the text uses cross-sensory descriptions (describing one sense in terms of another) to create vivid, unusual sensory experiences.
**Assumed goal**: Poetic literary fiction (Baudelaire's influence, symbolist tradition, Nabokov, Toni Morrison) where blending sensory modalities creates heightened perception—not transparent realist fiction where sensory description should map cleanly onto ordinary perception.
**Score 0**: No cross-sensory description exists. All senses are described in their own conventional terms. Sensory descriptions are siloed and never interact or blend.
**Score 5**: Cross-sensory imagery is used purposefully and effectively—sounds have colors, textures have temperatures, smells have shapes. The synesthetic descriptions create genuinely new perceptual experiences for the reader that purely single-sense description could not achieve. The blending feels organic rather than forced.
**Source**: https://literaryterms.net/synesthesia/ and https://literarydevices.net/synesthesia/
**Notes**: Synesthesia in literature is "a rhetorical device that describes or associates one sense in terms of another." Popularized by French Symbolists (Baudelaire, Rimbaud, Verlaine), it is used to "heighten and clarify the symbolic imagery" and "create a more immersive reading experience."

### Info-Dump Avoidance in Description
**What it measures**: Whether world-building and setting information is integrated naturally into the narrative or delivered as exposition blocks that break the fictional dream.
**Assumed goal**: Immersive narrative fiction (any genre) where the reader discovers the world through experience rather than lecture—not frame-narrative or essayistic fiction where discursive exposition is part of the form.
**Score 0**: Setting and world-building information is dumped in large blocks of undigested exposition—paragraphs or pages of history, geography, technology, or rules delivered through narrator lecture or "As you know, Bob" dialogue. Description stops the story to teach the reader.
**Score 5**: World-building and setting information is woven into action, dialogue, and character perception so seamlessly that the reader absorbs it without noticing they're being informed. Information is released in the quantity and at the moment when it becomes relevant. Characters never explain things they would already know solely for the reader's benefit.
**Source**: https://mooreattuned.com/four-simple-ways-to-avoid-info-dumping-when-world-building/ and https://www.quillandsteel.com/blogs/writing-tips/info-dumps-fantasy-worldbuilding
**Notes**: Moore Attuned states "building exposition into action makes the lore part of the momentum instead of a speed bump" and "by filtering the world through character perspective, you naturally prioritize what matters to them." Quill & Steel notes "As you know, Bob" dialogue is "anything people would not naturally say, destroying the illusion of a real conversation."

### Setting as Active Element
**What it measures**: Whether the physical environment participates in the narrative as a force that affects characters and plot, rather than serving as a passive backdrop.
**Assumed goal**: Place-driven literary fiction (Hardy, McCarthy, Erdrich, Robinson) where landscape and environment shape characters and events—not character-driven chamber fiction where the setting is genuinely subordinate to interpersonal dynamics.
**Score 0**: The setting is a painted backdrop—it exists but has no effect on characters, plot, or theme. Characters could be anywhere and the story would be identical. The environment never resists, enables, threatens, or transforms.
**Score 5**: The physical environment actively shapes the narrative—climate, geography, architecture, and objects influence character behavior, create obstacles or opportunities, embody thematic concerns, and evolve alongside the plot. The setting is inseparable from the story being told; transplanting the characters to a different environment would produce a different story.
**Source**: https://spines.com/world-building-in-fiction-writing-immersive-storylines/ and https://janefriedman.com/effective-setting/
**Notes**: Spines advises "Make your world's physical environment a key player in your story, not just a backdrop, as it influences characters' actions and the plot." Jane Friedman notes setting can serve pacing, subtext, conflict, and atmosphere simultaneously—a missing photograph "suggests loss or change before it's explained."

### Strong Nouns Over Adjective Padding
**What it measures**: Whether the text relies on precise, evocative nouns (and verbs) rather than modifying weak nouns with multiple adjectives.
**Assumed goal**: Muscular prose tradition (Hemingway, Orwell, Strunk & White) where nouns and verbs do the heavy lifting—not baroque prose tradition (Faulkner, Proust) where modifier-rich sentences are the aesthetic medium.
**Score 0**: Weak, generic nouns are propped up by clusters of adjectives ("the big, old, dark, stone building"). Description depends on modifier stacking rather than precise naming. Adverbs appear wherever the verb is insufficiently specific.
**Score 5**: Nouns are specific enough to carry their own weight without modification ("the brownstone," "the abbey," "the tenement"). When modifiers appear, each one adds essential information that the noun alone cannot convey. Verbs are precise enough to make adverbs unnecessary.
**Source**: https://jerichowriters.com/how-to-write-descriptions-and-create-a-sense-of-place/ and https://quillandbooks.com/2020/02/07/a-deep-dive-into-description-tools-of-the-craft/
**Notes**: Jericho Writers advises "prioritize strong nouns over adjective padding—rather than relying on adjectives to enliven generic nouns, select distinctive nouns themselves that carry inherent character." Quill and Books cites Stephen King: "The road to hell is paved in adverbs." Mark Twain advised: "When you catch an adjective, kill it."

### Imagery-Clarity Balance
**What it measures**: Whether poetic, figurative, or complex description maintains enough clarity for the reader to follow the narrative, or whether beauty comes at the expense of comprehension.
**Assumed goal**: Literary fiction that values both beauty and clarity (Chekhov, Munro, McEwan) where lyrical passages remain grounded—not deliberately obscurantist fiction (late Joyce, some Faulkner) where opacity is part of the aesthetic experience.
**Score 0**: Description is so ornate, abstract, or metaphor-heavy that the reader cannot determine what is literally happening in the scene. Beautiful individual sentences do not cohere into a comprehensible narrative. The reader can admire the language but cannot picture the scene or follow the action.
**Score 5**: Even the most complex, figurative description maintains a clear through-line—the reader always knows what is literally happening in the scene while simultaneously processing the figurative, atmospheric, and thematic dimensions. Beauty and clarity coexist without sacrifice.
**Source**: https://phoebejournal.com/five-tips-for-balancing-imagery-and-clarity-in-fiction/
**Notes**: Phoebe Journal advises: (1) "Punctuate a long, descriptive sentence with a short, active one"; (2) "Identify the strongest lines and let them carry weight" while removing excess; (3) "Ground readers first with clear language before using ambiguous metaphors"; (4) "Draft sentences in boring, plain language initially, then rewrite for vividness in a second pass"; (5) In speculative fiction, ensure "metaphors don't blur with world-building elements" (e.g., "Her face turned to stone" could be literal or figurative).

### Personification Quality
**What it measures**: Whether the attribution of human qualities to non-human things creates vivid imagery or has become a mechanical, meaningless verbal habit.
**Assumed goal**: Literary fiction where figurative language is purposeful (any tradition)—not deliberately anthropomorphic fiction (fairy tales, fables, animist narratives) where objects literally possess agency.
**Score 0**: Personification is mechanical and stock ("the wind whispered," "the sun smiled," "the building loomed menacingly") with no fresh perception or thematic purpose. Objects are given human traits in the most conventional, unexamined way.
**Score 5**: When non-human things are given human attributes, the personification creates a genuinely new perception, serves thematic purpose, or reveals the perceiving character's psychology. The attribution is specific and surprising rather than generic and stock.
**Source**: https://zoemmccarthy.com/writing/personification-giving-inanimate-things-human-traits-for-a-purpose and https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/microsoft-365-life-hacks/writing/what-is-personification-in-writing
**Notes**: Personification "provides a fresh way to describe inanimate things" and "connects a reader with an inanimate entity." It should turn "simple descriptions into vivid scenes that capture readers' imagination" and "make your writing more dynamic." It fails when it defaults to stock formulations that no longer create any perceptual freshness.

### Description Pacing and Proportion
**What it measures**: Whether the amount and timing of description is proportionate to its narrative importance, avoiding both over-description of trivial elements and under-description of crucial ones.
**Assumed goal**: Well-paced literary fiction (any tradition) where the reader's attention is directed proportionally—not deliberately disproportionate fiction (Borges, Perec) where attention asymmetry is a structural device.
**Score 0**: Trivial settings or objects receive exhaustive description while crucial environments are barely sketched. The amount of descriptive attention a scene receives bears no relationship to its narrative importance. High-tension moments are slowed by irrelevant description; pivotal locations are described in a single generic sentence.
**Score 5**: Descriptive attention is proportionate to narrative significance. Important settings, objects, and moments receive fuller description; transitional or familiar spaces receive lighter touches. The pacing of description reinforces the pacing of the story—lingering where the story benefits from immersion, moving quickly where momentum matters.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/common-writing-mistakes-much-description/ and http://blog.janicehardy.com/2011/07/move-along-fixing-problem-pacing.html
**Notes**: Helping Writers Become Authors states "Long descriptions are inevitably boring" when they "lack purpose" and that "readers only engage with details that advance plot, character, or theme." Janice Hardy notes "description slows the pace down, especially in large blocks" and advises against "describing every movement a character makes" since it "takes longer to read than it does for them to actually do it."

### Sensory Detail as Plot Device
**What it measures**: Whether sensory details serve plot functions (foreshadowing, revelation, misdirection) beyond atmospheric decoration.
**Assumed goal**: Craft-conscious literary or genre fiction where everything in the text does double or triple duty—not impressionistic fiction (some Woolf, Sebald) where sensory detail creates mood-texture independent of plot.
**Score 0**: Sensory details are purely decorative—they create atmosphere but never foreshadow, reveal, misdirect, or otherwise participate in the plot mechanics. No sensory detail mentioned early proves significant later.
**Score 5**: Sensory details function simultaneously as atmosphere and as plot architecture. A described smell foreshadows danger. A noted texture becomes evidence. A sound mentioned in passing proves significant. The reader discovers on rereading that descriptive details they absorbed as atmosphere were doing narrative work all along.
**Source**: https://janefriedman.com/effective-setting/ and https://jerichowriters.com/how-to-write-descriptions-and-create-a-sense-of-place/
**Notes**: Jane Friedman identifies foreshadowing through environmental detail—"a missing photograph suggests loss or change before it's explained." Jericho Writers notes "Place descriptions can signal danger, promise, or complexity ahead" and provides examples of "crushed rock" and "poison-stained" terrain hinting at approaching peril.

### Temporal Specificity in Description
**What it measures**: Whether description captures the specific moment in time—time of day, season, weather conditions, stage of an ongoing process—rather than being temporally generic.
**Assumed goal**: Realist and literary fiction where temporal grounding creates verisimilitude (Updike, Robinson, McEwan)—not mythic or timeless fiction (fairy tale, parable) where temporal vagueness is the intended register.
**Score 0**: Description is temporally unanchored—scenes could take place at any time of day, any season, any stage of an ongoing event. Light, temperature, ambient sounds, and other time-dependent sensory details are absent or generic.
**Score 5**: Description is precisely located in time through sensory details that could only exist at that specific moment—the quality of light, the stage of a seasonal process, the ambient sounds of a specific hour. The reader knows not just where they are but when, down to the approximate time of day.
**Source**: https://fictionary.co/journal/three-ways-to-know-if-a-scene-is-anchored/ and https://janefriedman.com/effective-setting/
**Notes**: Fictionary identifies time as one of the three essential anchoring elements (along with POV and setting). Jane Friedman notes the reader "mentally asks: Where are we? What time is it? What's the quality of light?" and that delaying this information causes distraction.

## Sentinels Found

### Sensory Inventory Pattern
**Detection**: Three or more senses catalogued in sequence within a single paragraph using a one-sentence-per-sense structure (e.g., "She could see... She could hear... She could smell... The air tasted of... The surface felt...").
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs internalize writing advice like "use all five senses" from training data and apply it as a checklist rather than letting sensory detail arise organically from the scene. Human writers typically emphasize one sensory modality per narrative moment, making their descriptions more selective and naturalistic.
**Design space cost**: Fiction that uses one-sense-per-paragraph as an intentional structural device (rare but possible in experimental prose) would be falsely flagged.
**Source**: https://www.craftliterary.com/2025/03/26/show-dont-tell-what-ai-cant-do/ and https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction

### "Gentle Breeze" and Stock Atmospheric Phrases
**Detection**: Presence of stock atmospheric phrases such as "gentle breeze," "the air was thick with," "a chill ran down," "the silence was deafening," "hung heavy in the air," "bathed in golden light," or "the weight of [abstract noun]" used as primary scene-setting rather than as part of a character's specific perception.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: These phrases are so common in training data that they are the probabilistically most likely completions for scene-setting contexts. They are generic enough to apply to any scene, which is precisely why LLMs favor them—they function as low-risk, high-probability tokens.
**Design space cost**: Some of these phrases, used once and in specific context, might be acceptable in genre fiction; flagging them eliminates certain conventional usages.
**Source**: https://www.craftliterary.com/2025/03/26/show-dont-tell-what-ai-cant-do/ and https://www.ignorance.ai/p/the-field-guide-to-ai-slop

### "Not X; Y" Descriptive Construction
**Detection**: Two or more instances of the pattern "The [noun] was not [expected quality]; it was [dramatic quality]" or "It wasn't [X]. It was [Y]." used for descriptive emphasis (e.g., "The pull from the deep was no current. It was a summons.").
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: This construction is identified as "universal across models" and functions as a cliché-generation pattern where the negation creates artificial profundity. Human writers use this construction but not as a recurring descriptive default.
**Design space cost**: This is a legitimate rhetorical device (litotes, antithesis) when used sparingly; flagging it penalizes intentional sparse use alongside habitual overuse.
**Source**: https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction

### Triple-Stacked Descriptors
**Detection**: Three or more abstract descriptors stacked in apposition to describe a single object or concept (e.g., "a geometry of madness, a wound in the fabric of what is, older than law and time").
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate lists of three (the "rule of three" internalized from training data) as a default descriptive strategy, often stacking metaphors or abstract descriptors without any one of them being sufficiently developed. Human writers may use this pattern but develop each element rather than stacking undeveloped alternatives.
**Design space cost**: Rhetorical tricolon is a legitimate device with ancient roots (veni, vidi, vici); flagging it catches deliberate as well as mechanical uses.
**Source**: https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction

### Blocky Description-Dialogue-Description Structure
**Detection**: Alternating blocks where a paragraph of pure description/setting is followed by a paragraph of pure dialogue, which is followed by another paragraph of pure description, in a rigid ABAB pattern across multiple exchanges.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs compartmentalize writing modes—generating "clearly defined areas of dialogue, exposition and repetitive narration" in blocks rather than weaving them together as human writers do. Human writing typically interleaves action, dialogue, description, and interiority within the same paragraph.
**Design space cost**: Some genre fiction conventions (screenplay-influenced writing) use blockier structures deliberately.
**Source**: https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction

### Metaphor Spam in Final Sentences
**Detection**: Scene-ending or paragraph-ending sentences that contain two or more metaphors or similes stacked together where the rest of the paragraph contained zero or one (e.g., a plain paragraph ending with "like a forgotten melody, dissolving into the tapestry of night").
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs produce a "poisoned output" effect where metaphors cluster at emphatic positions (sentence-final, paragraph-final) because training data associates these positions with heightened language. The concentration creates "eyeball kicks" that prioritize impressiveness over clarity.
**Design space cost**: Some literary traditions (magical realism, lyric prose) genuinely concentrate figurative language at emphatic positions.
**Source**: https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction

### Em-Dash Overuse in Description
**Detection**: Five or more em-dashes within a single scene's descriptive passages, used to insert parenthetical descriptive details or create artificial dramatic pauses (e.g., "The building—ancient, imposing, forgotten—stood at the edge of the cliff—a sentinel against the storm.").
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Data shows em-dash usage in AI-adjacent text tripled over one year in some contexts. LLMs use em-dashes to create tension and add parenthetical detail because it is a high-probability token in literary text positions. A human writer uses em-dashes sparingly and with intention; LLMs deploy them indiscriminately.
**Design space cost**: Some literary stylists (Emily Dickinson tradition, some contemporary prose) use em-dashes as a signature device; flagging this catches them.
**Source**: https://www.ignorance.ai/p/the-field-guide-to-ai-slop and https://huntingthemuse.net/library/how-to-tell-if-writing-is-ai

### Monotonous Descriptive Rhythm
**Detection**: Five or more consecutive descriptive sentences with the same approximate word count (within 20% variation) and the same grammatical structure (e.g., all Subject-Verb-Object or all "The [noun] [verb] [prepositional phrase]").
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Human writers naturally vary sentence length and structure. LLMs tend toward "uniform sentence length, paragraph rhythm, and cadence" because each sentence is generated with similar probability distributions. The monotony is a byproduct of statistical generation rather than deliberate style.
**Design space cost**: Intentionally hypnotic or liturgical prose (some Gertrude Stein, some Cormac McCarthy) uses repetitive structures deliberately.
**Source**: https://www.ignorance.ai/p/the-field-guide-to-ai-slop

### Awkward/Generic Analogies
**Detection**: Similes or analogies where the vehicle (the thing compared to) is as abstract or unfamiliar as the tenor (the thing being described), or where the comparison adds no new information (e.g., "The city was like a living organism, constantly changing and evolving").
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate analogies that feel "plausible but lack specificity or genuine insight"—they satisfy the structural expectation of a comparison without providing any actual perceptual illumination. The analogy sounds writerly but communicates nothing that the literal statement didn't already convey.
**Design space cost**: Some philosophical or essayistic fiction uses deliberately circular or tautological comparisons as a technique.
**Source**: https://www.ignorance.ai/p/the-field-guide-to-ai-slop and https://countercraft.substack.com/p/mfa-vs-llm-is-openais-metafiction

### "Something Shifted" and Unearned Profundity
**Detection**: Descriptive passages containing phrases like "something shifted," "something changed in the air," "the world seemed to hold its breath," "everything was different now," or similar vague atmospheric markers that assert a change without specifying what actually changed through concrete detail.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: These phrases are high-probability completions at transition points. They simulate atmospheric significance without doing the work of describing what specifically has changed in the character's sensory experience. Human writers either specify the change or omit the explicit marker.
**Design space cost**: Occasional use of "something shifted" as a genuine moment of pre-articulate awareness is legitimate in close-POV fiction.
**Source**: https://www.ignorance.ai/p/the-field-guide-to-ai-slop and https://huntingthemuse.net/library/how-to-tell-if-writing-is-ai

### Description That Defaults to Summary Over Scene
**Detection**: A passage where what should be a rendered scene (characters in a specific place at a specific time) is instead delivered as summary ("The city was a place of contrasts. Rich and poor lived side by side. Markets bustled with activity.") using generic present-tense or habitual descriptions rather than specific narrative moments.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs "excel at summary and exposition" but "fall short in its ability to create detailed, layered scenes." When asked to describe, they default to the generalized summary mode that dominates their training data (encyclopedias, articles, reviews) rather than the specific scene-rendering mode of literary fiction.
**Design space cost**: Some legitimate narrative strategies (epistolary fiction, retrospective narration, historiographic metafiction) use summary descriptions intentionally.
**Source**: https://www.craftliterary.com/2025/03/26/show-dont-tell-what-ai-cant-do/

### Absence of Meaningful Silence or Negative Space
**Detection**: Descriptive passages that fill every moment and every gap with sensory detail, with no deliberate restraint, no strategic gaps, no moments where the absence of description carries meaning.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: "AI is trained to generate phrase patterns; it's not trained to generate silence." LLMs cannot strategically omit—they only generate. Compelling description requires knowing what to leave out, what gaps between lines reveal implicitly, and where restraint creates more impact than elaboration.
**Design space cost**: Some maximalist fiction (Pynchon, Wallace) deliberately fills all available space as part of its aesthetic.
**Source**: https://www.craftliterary.com/2025/03/26/show-dont-tell-what-ai-cant-do/

### Cliché Clustering Under Stress
**Detection**: Three or more clichéd phrases or stock descriptions within a single high-emotional-intensity passage (death scene, love scene, battle scene, revelation)—e.g., "tears streaming down," "heart pounding in chest," "time seemed to stand still," "the world fell away."
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: When generating emotionally intense passages, LLMs reach for the highest-probability emotional language, which is by definition the most clichéd. "Once text reaches a 'local minimum of samey clichés, it won't stop going to the same well.'" Human writers working under emotional intensity often produce their most original language; LLMs produce their most generic.
**Design space cost**: Some deliberately cliché-reclaiming fiction (certain romance sub-genres, some ironic postmodern work) uses stock phrases intentionally.
**Source**: https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction and https://substack.com/@michaeldean9/note/c-136815035

### Formulaic Descriptive Openings
**Detection**: Scene-opening descriptions that begin with "The [time-of-day] [weather/light] [verb-ed] [location]" or "The air was [adjective] with [noun]" as a recurring pattern across multiple scenes.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs have strong priors for scene-opening templates because these patterns appear frequently in training data. Human writers vary their scene openings; LLMs converge on high-probability templates. When multiple scenes in a work all begin with the same structural formula for atmospheric description, statistical generation is indicated.
**Design space cost**: Some literary fiction uses recurring opening formulas as an intentional structural device (Márquez's "Many years later" opening template).
**Source**: https://huntingthemuse.net/library/how-to-tell-if-writing-is-ai and https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction

### Sensory Detail Without POV Integration
**Detection**: Sensory details that describe the environment objectively (as a camera would perceive it) rather than through a specific character's perception, in a narrative that otherwise uses close third or first person.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs produce description from a detached, encyclopedic perspective because their training data includes vast amounts of non-fiction description. The AI "cannot make intentional choices about which details matter thematically or structurally" and defaults to neutral observation rather than character-filtered perception.
**Design space cost**: Some fiction deliberately breaks close-POV for cinematic establishing shots or omniscient passages.
**Source**: https://www.craftliterary.com/2025/03/26/show-dont-tell-what-ai-cant-do/ and https://janefriedman.com/point-of-view/

### Generic Character Description on Introduction
**Detection**: When a character is first introduced, they receive an immediate head-to-toe physical description catalog (hair color, eye color, height, build, clothing) delivered as a block, separate from action or interaction.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs follow the training-data pattern of "character introduction = physical description dump" because this is the most common pattern in amateur fiction and character profiles. Skilled human writers introduce characters through action, dialogue, and selective significant detail rather than through inventory.
**Design space cost**: Some genre fiction conventions (romance, thriller) use more upfront physical description as a reader expectation.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/common-writing-mistakes-much-description/ and https://writers.com/16-common-fiction-writing-mistakes

### "Dance of" / "Tapestry of" / "Symphony of" Stock Metaphor Vehicles
**Detection**: Use of "dance of [abstract noun]," "tapestry of [abstract noun]," "symphony of [abstract noun/sensory noun]," or similar stock metaphor vehicles where a sensory experience is described through an art-form metaphor (e.g., "a tapestry of flavors," "a dance of light and shadow," "a symphony of sounds").
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: These are among the highest-probability metaphor vehicles in LLM outputs because they appear frequently in writing guides, reviews, and mediocre published fiction. They feel literary without being specific to any particular observation. Human writers who use these constructions typically do so once, not as a recurring pattern.
**Design space cost**: Any single use of one of these phrases might be legitimate; the sentinel becomes meaningful when the pattern recurs.
**Source**: https://countercraft.substack.com/p/mfa-vs-llm-is-openais-metafiction and https://www.ignorance.ai/p/the-field-guide-to-ai-slop

### Parallel Structure Overuse in Description
**Detection**: Three or more instances within a single scene of descriptive parallelism following the pattern "No [X]. No [Y]. Just [Z]" or "It was [X]. It was [Y]. It was [Z]" or any similar anaphoric structure used for atmospheric emphasis.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs reflexively deploy parallel structures for emphasis because these patterns score high in training data labeled as "good writing." The construction "No [X]. No [Y]. Just [Z]" is identified as a formulaic AI pattern that "creates artificial tension and drama in 90% of unnecessary cases."
**Design space cost**: Anaphora and parallelism are ancient, legitimate rhetorical devices; flagging them penalizes intentional use alongside mechanical overuse.
**Source**: https://huntingthemuse.net/library/how-to-tell-if-writing-is-ai and https://www.ignorance.ai/p/the-field-guide-to-ai-slop

### Telling-Then-Showing Redundancy
**Detection**: A passage where an emotion or quality is first stated abstractly and then immediately illustrated with sensory detail (e.g., "She was terrified. Her hands trembled as she reached for the door.") creating a tell-then-show redundancy where the showing makes the telling unnecessary.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs frequently produce both the abstract statement and the concrete illustration because both patterns score highly independently. Human writers who have internalized "show don't tell" typically omit the telling once the showing is adequate. The redundancy suggests a system that cannot evaluate whether the abstract statement is made redundant by the concrete detail.
**Design space cost**: Some narrative voices (particularly in YA or accessible literary fiction) deliberately use this pattern for clarity; some close-POV narrators explicitly name their emotions before demonstrating them.
**Source**: https://writers.com/show-dont-tell-writing and https://www.craftliterary.com/2025/03/26/show-dont-tell-what-ai-cant-do/

### Adjective Stacking on Sensory Nouns
**Detection**: Three or more adjectives modifying a single sensory noun (e.g., "the warm, golden, honeyed light" or "the cold, sharp, biting wind") as a recurring pattern across multiple descriptive passages.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs stack adjectives because each modifier independently increases the "literariness" score in the model's probability space. Human writers typically select one precise modifier (or none, choosing a more precise noun instead). The stacking pattern reflects additive probability rather than editorial judgment.
**Design space cost**: Some baroque or maximalist prose styles deliberately use adjective stacking as part of their aesthetic texture.
**Source**: https://reedsy.com/blog/purple-prose/ and https://jerichowriters.com/how-to-write-descriptions-and-create-a-sense-of-place/

### Atmosphere Without Specificity
**Detection**: Descriptive passages that create a mood category (eerie, warm, tense, peaceful) through generic atmosphere words without any specific, concrete details that are unique to this particular scene, location, or moment.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs can generate mood-appropriate vocabulary but struggle to ground that mood in specific observed reality. The result is description that correctly signals "this is a tense scene" through word choice but provides no particular image, sound, smell, or texture that distinguishes this tense scene from any other tense scene ever written.
**Design space cost**: Very short fiction or flash fiction may intentionally use atmospheric shorthand due to word count constraints.
**Source**: https://www.craftliterary.com/2025/03/26/show-dont-tell-what-ai-cant-do/ and https://countercraft.substack.com/p/mfa-vs-llm-is-openais-metafiction
