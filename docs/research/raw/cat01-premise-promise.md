## Criteria Found

### Premise Clarity
**What it measures**: Whether the core story concept can be articulated in a single, clear sentence that communicates protagonist, goal, and central conflict.
**Assumed goal**: Commercial fiction craft (Mary Kole's editorial framework) — assumes stories should be pitchable and agent-ready; literary fiction, experimental fiction, and plotless character studies may not reduce to a single-sentence premise
**Score 0**: The premise cannot be summarized concisely; attempting to describe the story requires multiple paragraphs and still leaves the listener confused about what the story is actually about.
**Score 5**: The premise is expressible as a single sentence that immediately communicates who the protagonist is, what they want, and what stands in their way, generating instant curiosity.
**Source**: https://www.marykole.com/premise
**Notes**: Mary Kole identifies three cornerstone questions: "Who are the main characters?", "What do they want?", "What stands in their way?" She notes: "If you find yourself struggling to come up with something, you might have a story issue, not a premise issue."

### Premise Completeness
**What it measures**: Whether the premise contains all essential structural elements: protagonist, inciting incident, goal, opposition, and stakes.
**Assumed goal**: Goal-oriented plot structure (C.S. Lakin's premise formula) — assumes fiction is driven by a protagonist pursuing a goal against opposition; plotless literary fiction, lyric essays, and ensemble narratives without a single protagonist may lack these elements by design
**Score 0**: The premise is missing multiple essential elements—no clear protagonist, no identifiable goal, no opposition, or no stakes. The story concept is a situation or mood rather than a narrative engine.
**Score 5**: The premise contains a specific protagonist with a passionate drive, a clear inciting incident, central conflict with identifiable opposition, and high personal stakes, all working together as a narrative engine.
**Source**: https://www.livewritethrive.com/2023/01/02/crafting-a-compelling-story-premise/
**Notes**: C.S. Lakin provides the formula: "When [opening conflict] happens to [character(s)], they must [overcome conflict] to [complete their quest]." She emphasizes that without "a protagonist set up to go after a goal that's veered by that inciting incident, you'll have trouble putting a solid novel together."

### Concept Strength (Pre-Premise Appeal)
**What it measures**: Whether the foundational idea underlying the premise is inherently interesting, provocative, or fascinating before any character or plot is attached.
**Assumed goal**: High-concept commercial fiction (Larry Brooks / StoryFix) — assumes the idea itself must sell the book before execution; literary fiction, autofiction, and quiet character-driven novels often succeed on voice and execution rather than concept novelty
**Score 0**: The concept is entirely familiar and unremarkable—a situation readers have encountered many times with no distinguishing element that makes this version worth their attention.
**Score 5**: The concept is inherently fascinating, provocative, or challenging independent of execution—it promises a vicarious experience readers cannot get elsewhere and immediately differentiates itself within its genre.
**Source**: https://storyfix.com/fiction-writers-definition-criteria-concept
**Notes**: Larry Brooks argues that concept must be "inherently, before character or plot, interesting, fascinating, provocative, challenging, engaging, even terrifying." He emphasizes concepts should "promise a vicarious ride for the reader" in situations "not possible, realistic or something tense or horrific, something they would not choose to experience in real life."

### Concept-to-Premise Development
**What it measures**: Whether the story has progressed beyond a bare concept (a "what if") into a fully developed premise with specific characters, goals, and obstacles.
**Assumed goal**: Concept-to-premise pipeline (K.M. Weiland / Larry Brooks) — assumes a story must progress from arena to populated narrative engine; mood pieces, vignettes, and concept-as-atmosphere fiction (e.g., Borges, Calvino) may operate at the concept level deliberately
**Score 0**: The story is built on a concept alone—an interesting "what if" scenario with no specific protagonist, goal, or conflict attached. It reads as a setting or situation, not a story.
**Score 5**: The concept has been fully developed into a premise with specific characters who have clear motivations, defined obstacles, and personal stakes that make the concept into a complete narrative engine.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/story-concept/
**Notes**: K.M. Weiland (citing Larry Brooks) illustrates with: "A story about what it was like to be on the Titanic on the night of its sinking... that is NOT a premise. It is a concept only." A concept isn't a story; a premise is.

### "What If" Question Compellingness
**What it measures**: Whether the speculative or conceptual question at the story's heart is interesting enough to sustain reader engagement through a full narrative.
**Assumed goal**: Speculative fiction tradition (SF/F "what if" premise model) — assumes the story is driven by a speculative conceit whose implications sustain narrative; realistic fiction, memoir-adjacent fiction, and character studies may have no speculative question at all
**Score 0**: The "what if" question is either absent, trivially obvious, or has been so thoroughly explored by prior works that it generates no curiosity.
**Score 5**: The "what if" question is immediately intriguing, easy to articulate, and generates a cascade of follow-up questions about consequences and implications that sustain interest.
**Source**: https://thoughtsonfantasy.com/2014/09/01/the-power-of-what-if-premises-in-science-fiction-and-fantasy/
**Notes**: The source notes that the best speculative premises go beyond the "what if" to ask "and then what would happen?"—exploring social, political, and other consequences. However, it also notes that major successes like Lord of the Rings, Star Wars, and Dune lack easily-distillable premises, suggesting execution can override premise strength.

### High-Concept Pitchability
**What it measures**: Whether the story concept can be communicated in a way that immediately paints a picture in the listener's mind and generates desire to experience the story.
**Assumed goal**: Traditional publishing acquisition model (Writer's Digest / agent-pitch framework) — assumes the story must sell in a query letter; self-published fiction, literary fiction that sells on voice/reputation, and cult-audience work may not need pitchability
**Score 0**: The concept requires extensive explanation to make sense, fails to generate an immediate mental image, and does not produce a "I want to read that" reaction in listeners.
**Score 5**: The concept can be expressed in one to three sentences that immediately paint a vivid picture, feel both simple and unique, and produce an immediate "I want to read that" reaction. It has humor, suspense, conflict, or irony baked in.
**Source**: https://www.writersdigest.com/publishing-faqs/what-is-and-isnt-high-concept-fiction-and-how-do-you-pitch-it
**Notes**: High concept is "an idea that—when you say it—it immediately paints a picture in the listener's mind of what that book is about." The leading character makes up "the vital second half of a high concept" as "the 'and then what' of high concept fiction."

### Premise Originality
**What it measures**: Whether the premise offers something genuinely new—a fresh take, unexpected combination, or novel exploration—rather than retreading familiar territory.
**Assumed goal**: Novelty-driven commercial fiction (Larry Brooks / agent acquisition) — assumes freshness of concept is a primary value; genre fiction often succeeds through excellent execution of familiar premises, and retelling traditions (fairy tale retellings, mythic retellings) treat familiarity as a feature
**Score 0**: The premise is a transparent retread of well-known stories with no distinguishing element in concept, character, setting, or approach. A reader can name the source material immediately.
**Score 5**: The premise feels genuinely novel—either through an unprecedented concept, an unexpected combination of familiar elements, or a fresh angle on known material that reveals something previously unexplored.
**Source**: https://storyfix.com/fiction-writers-definition-criteria-concept
**Notes**: Brooks notes: "When we read that agents and editors are looking for something fresh and new, concept is what they mean." However, other sources note that "the value of a story is about 1% idea and 99% implementation," meaning originality in execution (voice, setting, characters, details) can compensate for a familiar core concept.

### Hook Effectiveness
**What it measures**: Whether the opening lines and pages create immediate engagement through curiosity, tension, voice, or intrigue that compels continued reading.
**Assumed goal**: Commercial opening strategy (Inkshift / agent-submission conventions) — assumes the opening must compete for attention in a slush pile; literary fiction traditions (e.g., Proust, Sebald) may open with slow immersion rather than hooks
**Score 0**: The opening generates no curiosity, poses no questions, establishes no tension, and provides no reason to continue reading. The reader can put the book down without wondering what happens next.
**Score 5**: The opening immediately drops the reader into a moment of interest or tension, establishes a distinct voice, raises compelling questions, and creates an irresistible pull to continue reading.
**Source**: https://inkshift.io/resources/opening-hook
**Notes**: Inkshift identifies seven hook types: the intriguing statement, in medias res, the compelling voice, the mysterious image, the promise of conflict, the dialogue hook, and the setting hook. Critical distinction: a hook "must honestly represent the story to come" and is "not a gimmick."

### Hook Honesty
**What it measures**: Whether the opening hook accurately represents the story that follows, rather than using a disconnected attention-grabber.
**Assumed goal**: Promise-based narrative contract (Inkshift / K.M. Weiland) — assumes the opening is a binding preview of the story's actual content; metafiction and unreliable-frame narratives (e.g., Nabokov's Pale Fire) may deliberately mislead as part of their design
**Score 0**: The hook is a false promise—an exciting or intriguing opening that has no connection to the actual story (e.g., an action scene revealed to be a dream, a provocative statement never explored).
**Score 5**: The hook is an organic entry point into the story's actual narrative—the intrigue it generates is directly connected to the story's core conflict, themes, and character journey.
**Source**: https://inkshift.io/resources/opening-hook
**Notes**: Inkshift specifically warns against "The False Hook" which "creates intrigue disconnected from actual story" and gives the example of "action revealed as dream." A hook is "not unrelated to plot—should be integral to plot or character arc."

### Narrative Promise Establishment
**What it measures**: Whether the opening pages clearly establish what kind of story the reader is entering—genre, tone, style, pacing, and the nature of the central conflict.
**Assumed goal**: Promise-of-the-premise framework (Judy L. Mohr / Blake Snyder) — assumes readers need early genre and tonal orientation to invest correctly; genre-defying fiction and deliberately disorienting openings (e.g., House of Leaves) withhold orientation as a strategy
**Score 0**: After reading the opening, a reader cannot identify the genre, has no sense of tone, does not know what kind of experience to expect, and would be unable to describe what the story is "about."
**Score 5**: The opening pages clearly signal genre, establish tone, introduce the narrative style, and give readers a confident sense of the kind of experience they are signing up for, all without heavy-handed exposition.
**Source**: https://judylmohr.com/2022/06/17/promise-of-the-premise/
**Notes**: Judy L. Mohr specifies the opening must convey "the narrative style you have chosen to use, be that first-person, third-person limited, or omniscient" and whether delivery is "literary...or follow[s] a more commercial genre structure." The reader should be "fully aware of the nature of the story they are reading by the time they hit the 25% mark."

### Genre Contract Adherence
**What it measures**: Whether the story identifies and fulfills the expectations inherent in its chosen genre, including required structural elements, character types, and narrative outcomes.
**Assumed goal**: Story Grid methodology (Shawn Coyne) — assumes genres impose binding structural obligations; genre-blending fiction, slipstream, and New Weird deliberately refuse genre categorization and its attendant obligations
**Score 0**: The story either fails to identify its genre, sets up genre expectations it systematically violates without purpose, or delivers none of the structural elements genre readers expect.
**Score 5**: The story clearly identifies its genre, delivers all required conventions and obligatory scenes, and either fulfills or consciously innovates upon genre expectations in ways that enhance rather than betray the reader experience.
**Source**: https://storygrid.com/editor-roundtable-conventions-obligatory-scenes/
**Notes**: Shawn Coyne's Story Grid framework defines genre conventions as "specific requirements for the story's ALTERNATE WORLD, AVATARS, and catalysts" and obligatory moments as "must-have events, revelations, or decisions and actions that pay off the raised expectations of the Conventions." Each genre has specific selective constraints (setting, conflict levels) and enabling constraints (required character types, catalysts).

### Genre-Specific Obligation Fulfillment
**What it measures**: Whether the story contains the specific obligatory scenes and conventions that its genre demands.
**Assumed goal**: Story Grid obligatory-scenes framework (Shawn Coyne) — assumes each genre has non-negotiable structural requirements; cross-genre fiction, literary fiction marketed as genre, and subversive genre work (e.g., anti-mystery) may omit obligatory scenes deliberately
**Score 0**: The story is missing critical obligatory scenes for its genre—a mystery without a final reveal, a romance without lovers meeting, a thriller without escalating stakes, an action story without a climactic confrontation.
**Score 5**: Every genre-specific obligatory scene is present, well-executed, and the conventions are either fulfilled faithfully or innovated upon in ways that feel fresh rather than deficient.
**Source**: https://storygrid.com/editor-roundtable-conventions-obligatory-scenes/
**Notes**: Examples of genre-specific requirements: Action requires "hero, villain, and victim" plus "speech in praise of the villain" and "set-piece sequences." Crime requires "professional or amateur detective, a criminal 'who makes it personal,' and the victim(s)." Horror requires "unheroic protagonist and a monster that cannot be reasoned with." Love requires "lovers, rivals, helpers, and harmers" plus "opposing forces, secrets, and rituals."

### Tonal Consistency
**What it measures**: Whether the story maintains a coherent emotional register throughout, or intentionally and effectively modulates tone within an established framework.
**Assumed goal**: Unified-tone craft tradition (K.M. Weiland) — assumes tone is a contract that must be maintained; tonal collision as technique (Tarantino, Vonnegut, David Lynch) deliberately juxtaposes incompatible registers for effect
**Score 0**: The story's tone shifts erratically and without purpose—comedy one chapter, horror the next—creating disorientation. Readers cannot determine what kind of emotional experience the story is offering.
**Score 5**: The tone is consistent and unified throughout, serving as a "throughline providing cohesion." Where tonal shifts occur, they are gradual, purposeful, and established within the opening's tonal range.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/story-tone-in-fiction/
**Notes**: K.M. Weiland defines tone as "the narrative's emotional attitude toward the events of the story. It signals to audiences how they should interpret what happens on the page." She provides a test: "Sum your opening chapter's tone in one word, then your closing chapter's tone. If they differ significantly, you may have a problem." The Australia (2008) film is cited as a tonal failure: "every twenty minutes, the story seems to change its mind."

### Tonal Promise Fulfillment
**What it measures**: Whether the tone established in the opening pages is maintained or evolved coherently throughout the story, honoring the reader's initial tonal expectations.
**Assumed goal**: Reader-contract model of tonal consistency (L.G. Estrella) — assumes the opening's tone is a binding promise; genre deconstruction (e.g., Madoka Magica, Spec Ops: The Line) deliberately establishes one tone to subvert it as the central artistic move
**Score 0**: The opening establishes one tone (e.g., light-hearted adventure) but the story abandons it entirely (e.g., becomes grimdark torture) without any foreshadowing or gradual transition, leaving readers feeling the "preceding pages have been invalidated."
**Score 5**: The tonal promise of the opening is honored throughout. Any tonal evolution is gradual, foreshadowed, and feels like a deepening of the original tone rather than a betrayal of it.
**Source**: https://lgestrella.wordpress.com/2020/11/30/how-abrupt-tonal-shifts-can-kill-a-story/
**Notes**: L.G. Estrella documents a case where an abrupt shift from light-hearted to extremely dark over 5,000 words caused reader backlash: "the sudden shift and how they felt it invalidated the preceding 250,000 words." By contrast, Game of Thrones succeeds because "right from the start...you know what sort of story you're reading."

### Reader-Writer Contract Establishment
**What it measures**: Whether the opening establishes a clear implicit contract about genre, plot direction, character focus, style, and point of view that the reader can rely upon.
**Assumed goal**: Reader-writer contract theory (Katherine Cowley / K.M. Weiland) — assumes fiction operates as an implicit agreement between author and audience; postmodern fiction (e.g., Pynchon, DeLillo) may deliberately refuse to establish a stable contract
**Score 0**: The opening establishes no identifiable contract—the reader has no basis for expectations about genre, tone, character, or narrative direction after reading the first chapter.
**Score 5**: The opening clearly and skillfully establishes expectations about genre, tone, character focus, narrative style, and story direction, giving the reader a confident framework for their investment.
**Source**: https://www.katherinecowley.com/jawl/reader-writer-contract/
**Notes**: Cowley states the reader-writer contract should be "established within the first 10-20% of a story." The contract covers "genre, plot, character, style, and point of view." She uses the analogy: "If you order a vegetarian meal, the meal better not have chicken in it."

### Reader-Writer Contract Fulfillment
**What it measures**: Whether the story delivers on the implicit promises made in its opening, resolving in ways that honor the established expectations.
**Assumed goal**: Promise-payoff model (K.M. Weiland's eight promises) — assumes every opening promise must be honored at resolution; subversive fiction and anti-narrative (e.g., Beckett, Kafka) may deliberately frustrate promised resolutions as their core method
**Score 0**: The story systematically violates its own contract—the opening promises one kind of experience but the story delivers something entirely different, with no earned justification for the shift.
**Score 5**: The story fulfills every major promise established in its opening while incorporating surprising elements that feel organic and earned. The reader finishes feeling that the opening's promises were honored and exceeded.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/8-promises-youre-making-to-readersand/
**Notes**: K.M. Weiland identifies eight specific promises: conflict resolution, character consistency, foreshadowing payoff, character continuity, cause and effect, active protagonist in climax, surprise within expectations, and genre convention adherence.

### Conflict Resolution Promise
**What it measures**: Whether every instance of conflict introduced in the story ends with an appropriate climax—confrontation, disaster, or triumph rather than fizzling out.
**Assumed goal**: Scene-level dramatic structure (K.M. Weiland / Swain-Bickham scene-sequel model) — assumes every conflict must climax; literary fiction in the Chekhov/Carver tradition often lets conflicts dissipate or remain unresolved to reflect life's inconclusiveness
**Score 0**: Conflicts are introduced and abandoned without resolution. Story threads fizzle, confrontations are avoided, and tension dissipates without payoff.
**Score 5**: Every conflict introduced is resolved through appropriate confrontation, disaster, or triumph. No thread is dropped, and each resolution feels proportionate to the buildup.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/8-promises-youre-making-to-readersand/
**Notes**: K.M. Weiland: "I promise every instance of conflict will end with an appropriate climax."

### Foreshadowing-to-Payoff Ratio
**What it measures**: Whether significant foreshadowing elements introduced in the story are eventually paid off, and whether major payoffs have adequate foreshadowing.
**Assumed goal**: Chekhov's gun principle (dramatic economy tradition) — assumes every emphasized element must serve the plot; maximalist fiction (e.g., Pynchon, Murakami) and deliberate red-herring mystery traditions introduce elements that intentionally go unpaid
**Score 0**: The story is riddled with unfired Chekhov's guns (foreshadowed elements that never pay off) and/or delivers major plot resolutions that were never foreshadowed (deus ex machina).
**Score 5**: Every significant foreshadowed element pays off meaningfully, and every major resolution has been properly seeded earlier in the narrative. The ratio of setup to payoff is balanced and satisfying.
**Source**: https://www.scribophile.com/academy/what-is-chekhovs-gun
**Notes**: Chekhov's principle: "One must never place a loaded rifle on the stage if it isn't going to go off. It's wrong to make promises you don't mean to keep." Loose ends (accidental setup without payoff) are "the mark of sloppy or poorly-planned writing." Deus ex machina (payoff without setup) breaks the reader agreement.

### Central Dramatic Question Clarity
**What it measures**: Whether the story poses a clear central dramatic question early and answers it by the end.
**Assumed goal**: Dramatic-question-as-spine model (K.M. Weiland / classical dramatic structure) — assumes a single question organizes the narrative from opening to climax; episodic fiction, picaresque novels, and braided narratives may operate without a single central question
**Score 0**: No identifiable central dramatic question exists—the story meanders without a core question driving the reader forward, or the question is so vague that readers cannot articulate what they are waiting to learn.
**Score 5**: A clear, compelling dramatic question is posed early (by implication if not explicitly), drives the entire narrative, and is definitively answered at the climax. The story begins when the question is asked and ends shortly after it is answered.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/storys-most-important-question/
**Notes**: K.M. Weiland: "The moment it is asked, your story begins. The moment it is answered, your story ends." The dramatic question is the story's structural spine—"Rip out that spine, and the whole body crumples."

### Promise-Progress-Payoff Structural Integrity
**What it measures**: Whether the story follows a complete arc of establishing promises, demonstrating progress toward those promises, and delivering satisfying payoffs.
**Assumed goal**: Sanderson's promise-progress-payoff framework (Brandon Sanderson's BYU lectures) — assumes fiction operates as a three-phase delivery system; experimental fiction, circular narratives, and anti-climactic literary fiction (e.g., Waiting for Godot) may deliberately break this arc
**Score 0**: The story makes promises it never progresses toward, shows progress on threads that were never promised, or ends without paying off the promises it established. The three-phase structure is absent or broken.
**Score 5**: Every promise established in the opening is progressed through the middle and paid off at the end. The payoff is "surprising but fulfilling"—it answers the opening promise "in a way that feels earned" and "better than expected."
**Source**: https://www.brandonsanderson.com/blogs/blog/brandon-sandersons-2025-guide-to-plot-lecture-2
**Notes**: Sanderson articulates four types of promises (tone, story, character/conflict, structural) and three types of progress (information-based, relationship, character internal). His ideal payoff: "give the audience the expected thing...but in a way that is better...than expected." He also introduces the "Gandalf Promise" model: promise something early, let danger bury it, make it seem impossible, then fulfill it at the moment hope is lost.

### Signposting Quality
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative provides regular signals to the reader that progress is being made toward the story's promised outcomes.
**Assumed goal**: Sanderson's signposting model (Brandon Sanderson) — assumes readers need regular confirmation of forward momentum; literary fiction that emphasizes uncertainty, ambiguity, or deliberate aimlessness (e.g., Sebald's The Rings of Saturn) may withhold progress signals
**Score 0**: The narrative provides no sense of forward momentum. Readers cannot tell whether the story is advancing toward resolution, and the middle feels aimless or stagnant.
**Score 5**: Regular, well-placed signposts demonstrate that the story is advancing—clues are discovered, relationships deepen, skills develop, stakes escalate. Each section advances something. Readers feel continuous forward momentum.
**Source**: https://www.brandonsanderson.com/blogs/blog/brandon-sandersons-2025-guide-to-plot-lecture-2
**Notes**: Sanderson warns: "A big reason that people drop off a book is that there weren't enough signposts of progress." Signpost types include revealing critical information, introducing new challenges, character making crucial decisions, and shifting story direction.

### Premise Exploitation (Fun and Games)
**What it measures**: Whether the story actively explores and exploits its own premise, delivering scenes that showcase the core concept in action.
**Assumed goal**: Save the Cat! beat-sheet model (Blake Snyder) — assumes the story's middle must deliver "trailer moments" that exploit the premise; literary fiction and character studies may use the premise as backdrop rather than engine, prioritizing interiority over spectacle
**Score 0**: The story's premise is established but never explored. The unique concept that attracted the reader is abandoned in favor of generic plot mechanics. The "trailer moments" are absent.
**Score 5**: The story thoroughly exploits its premise, delivering multiple scenes that showcase the core concept in action. Readers get the specific experiences that the premise promised. The concept is explored from multiple angles.
**Source**: https://savethecat.com/about-the-beats/questioning-the-promise-of-the-premise
**Notes**: Blake Snyder's "promise of the premise" concept: "the premise of a movie, its 'What is it?,' can only be proven to be satisfying when we see it in action." These are "trailer moments" that "deliver on what your book jacket or movie trailer teased." The diagnostic question is: "What scene do you want to see in a movie about [your logline]?"

### Stakes Clarity and Escalation
**What it measures**: Whether the story establishes clear, personal stakes early and escalates them throughout the narrative.
**Assumed goal**: Escalating-stakes model (The Darling Axe / commercial thriller pacing) — assumes tension must increase throughout; slice-of-life fiction, contemplative literary fiction, and picaresque narratives may maintain flat or ambient stakes by design
**Score 0**: Nothing meaningful is at risk for the protagonist. There are no consequences for failure, or the consequences are abstract and impersonal. The reader has no reason to care about the outcome.
**Score 5**: The stakes are specific, personal, and clearly communicated from the opening. They escalate throughout the story, becoming progressively more dire. The consequences of failure are tangible and deeply personal to the protagonist.
**Source**: https://darlingaxe.com/blogs/news/stakes
**Notes**: The Darling Axe notes: "When an editor mentions stakes in their feedback on your novel, it's a polite way of saying, 'who cares?'" Stakes must be "specific, tangible, and measurable." The reader care test: "If they care, the reader will care. If they have a ho-hum attitude, then the reader will yawn."

### Inciting Incident Placement
**What it measures**: Whether the inciting incident—the event that disrupts ordinary life and launches the story—occurs at the right point in the narrative, neither too early nor too late.
**Assumed goal**: Three-act structure timing conventions (C.S. Lakin / Syd Field) — assumes the inciting incident must arrive within the first 10-15% of the narrative; epic fantasy (e.g., Tolkien) and literary fiction with extended world-establishment may deliberately delay the inciting event
**Score 0**: The inciting incident is either missing entirely, buried so deep in the manuscript (50+ pages) that readers have abandoned the story, or occurs so early that readers have no context or investment.
**Score 5**: The inciting incident occurs at the optimal point—early enough to maintain engagement but late enough that readers understand the protagonist's ordinary world and have reason to care about the disruption.
**Source**: https://www.livewritethrive.com/2023/01/02/crafting-a-compelling-story-premise/
**Notes**: C.S. Lakin documents "fifty pages of setup before the premise kicked in, filled with backstory about how characters met, fell in love, and got married." The inciting incident should not be buried. Genre-specific timing varies: mysteries/thrillers often start with the crime on page one; fantasy/SF may delay to establish world rules.

### Opening Orientation
**What it measures**: Whether the opening provides enough information for readers to understand where they are, when they are, and who they are following.
**Assumed goal**: Agent-submission opening craft (Alyssa Matesic / editorial gatekeeping standards) — assumes immediate clarity is mandatory; deliberately disorienting openings (e.g., Faulkner's The Sound and the Fury, McCarthy's Blood Meridian) withhold orientation as a literary strategy
**Score 0**: The opening leaves readers completely disoriented—unable to determine the setting, time period, protagonist identity, or basic situation. Information is either absent or deliberately withheld to the point of confusion.
**Score 5**: The opening efficiently orients readers in space, time, and character while maintaining intrigue. Readers know who the protagonist is, where the story takes place, and what situation they are entering without being subjected to exposition dumps.
**Source**: https://www.alyssamatesic.com/free-writing-resources/mistakes-in-your-first-ten-pages
**Notes**: Matesic identifies "confusing the reader" as the most dangerous opening mistake: "A reader who's confused...is frustrated and likely going to put the book down." Non-negotiable information: who the protagonist is, where we are, what's happening. "Suspense doesn't come from intentionally withholding essential information—it comes from giving them enough information to make them eager to know what happens next."

### Microtension (Page-Level Engagement)
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative maintains curiosity and tension at the page level, even in quiet scenes, through small moments of uncertainty, mismatch, or pressure.
**Assumed goal**: Microtension craft model (Donald Maass / BubbleCow developmental editing) — assumes every page must create forward pull; meditative and contemplative fiction (e.g., Marilynne Robinson's Gilead) may deliberately create pages of reflection without tension
**Score 0**: Pages lack any tension, curiosity, or pull. The reader can stop reading at the end of any paragraph without feeling compelled to continue. Scenes are inert.
**Score 5**: Every page contains some element of microtension—a question in narration, a mismatch between words and body language, a nearby deadline, a test or choice. The reader feels a continuous pull to the next line.
**Source**: https://bubblecow.com/blog/developmental-editing-checklist
**Notes**: BubbleCow states: "You need curiosity on every page, even quiet ones. Microtension is small stress which pulls the eye to the next line." Tools include: a question in narration, mismatch between words and body language, nearby deadline, tests/counterexamples/trade-offs forcing choices.

### Seed-Planting Integrity
**What it measures**: Whether elements introduced with emphasis early in the story play correspondingly important roles throughout and at the climax.
**Assumed goal**: Seed-and-harvest narrative design (Mary Kole / Kidlit editorial tradition) — assumes early emphasis creates binding obligations to pay off later; picaresque fiction and episodic narratives may introduce elements that serve a single chapter without recurrence
**Score 0**: Elements given prominent attention in the opening are abandoned or never appear again. The story's opening focus bears no relationship to the story's actual concerns.
**Score 5**: Every element planted prominently in the opening grows in importance throughout the narrative. Seeds planted early bear fruit at the climax. Nothing introduced with emphasis is wasted.
**Source**: https://kidlit.com/the-promise-of-the-novel/
**Notes**: Mary Kole: "In fiction, you plant seeds from the very beginning and they grow in importance as you hurtle toward the climax." She warns: "If any element plays a strong role in your opening, let it play a strong role throughout. No spending 10 pages focusing on a school story if school does not show up ever again."

### Resolution Earned-ness
**What it measures**: Whether the story's resolution is achieved through means established within the narrative—through character agency, foreshadowed resources, and logical consequences—rather than through external, unearned intervention.
**Assumed goal**: Anti-deus-ex-machina tradition (Aristotelian dramatic theory / Chapter Blog) — assumes resolution must arise from within the story's established causal framework; absurdist fiction (e.g., Ionesco), magical realism, and mythological traditions may use external or divine intervention as a legitimate narrative device
**Score 0**: The resolution is a deus ex machina—the conflict is resolved by an external force, previously unknown resource, or unearned coincidence that was never set up or foreshadowed. The characters' struggles are rendered meaningless.
**Score 5**: The resolution is achieved through the protagonist's own agency using skills, relationships, or resources established earlier in the narrative. The victory costs something. All the pieces were visible in retrospect.
**Source**: https://blog.chapter.pub/deus-ex-machina/
**Notes**: Three criteria for an "earned miracle": the possibility is established early, the outcome remains uncertain until the last moment, and the resolution costs something. "If the solution was always going to fall from the sky, then every obstacle the character faced was meaningless. Every sacrifice was theater. The tension was a lie."

### Protagonist Active Agency in Climax
**What it measures**: Whether the protagonist plays an appropriately active role in resolving the central conflict, rather than being rescued or having the problem solved for them.
**Assumed goal**: Active-protagonist model (K.M. Weiland / Western hero's journey tradition) — assumes the protagonist must be the decisive agent of resolution; tragedy, naturalist fiction (e.g., Zola, Hardy), and stories about systemic powerlessness may feature protagonists who are acted upon rather than acting
**Score 0**: The protagonist is passive in the climax—the conflict is resolved by other characters, external events, or coincidence while the protagonist watches or benefits without acting.
**Score 5**: The protagonist directly and actively resolves the central conflict through their own choices, using skills or relationships developed throughout the story. Their agency is the decisive factor.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/8-promises-youre-making-to-readersand/
**Notes**: K.M. Weiland: "I promise my protagonist(s) will play an appropriately active role in the Climax." Deus ex machina (external resolution) breaks this promise.

### Surprise-Within-Expectations Balance
**What it measures**: Whether the story contains genuine surprises and avoids pure predictability while still operating within the framework of its established promises.
**Assumed goal**: "Surprising but inevitable" payoff ideal (Brandon Sanderson / K.M. Weiland) — assumes the best fiction satisfies expectations through unexpected means; comfort-read genres (cozy mystery, category romance) may deliver precisely predictable outcomes as a feature, and avant-garde fiction may pursue pure surprise without expectation-management
**Score 0**: The story is entirely predictable—every scene plays out exactly as genre templates suggest, with no surprises, subversions, or unexpected developments. OR the story is so unpredictable as to feel random.
**Score 5**: The story consistently surprises within its established framework. Developments feel both unexpected and inevitable in retrospect. Genre expectations are fulfilled but through fresh, non-obvious means.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/8-promises-youre-making-to-readersand/
**Notes**: K.M. Weiland: "I promise not every scene will play out exactly as readers expect." Sanderson's ideal payoff: "A surprising but fulfilling answer to your promise." Balance expectation with surprise—deliver the expected thing "but in a way that is better...than expected."

### Safety-to-Threat Spectrum Calibration
**What it measures**: Whether the story correctly positions itself on the genre-appropriate spectrum between "reader will feel safe" and "reader will feel threatened" and maintains that position.
**Assumed goal**: Genre-as-emotional-contract model (CrimeReads / genre fiction conventions) — assumes each genre promises a specific emotional safety level; genre-transgressive fiction (e.g., Gillian Flynn subverting romance, Joe Hill subverting cozy) deliberately violates the expected safety level
**Score 0**: The story is incorrectly calibrated—it promises safety (romance) but delivers threat (horror), or vice versa, without purpose or warning. The reader's emotional experience is mismatched with their expectations.
**Score 5**: The story accurately positions itself on the safety-to-threat spectrum for its genre and delivers an experience consistent with that positioning throughout.
**Source**: https://crimereads.com/genre-communicates-a-contract-with-the-reader/
**Notes**: CrimeReads establishes that genre determines where on the Safety-to-Threat line "your reader can expect to be left." Romance promises "no matter how perilous the waters, you will get them back to port." Psychological horror "promised misery" and "expects that to be honored with suffering." Murder mystery promises "I will trick you, there will be death, but there will also be resolution. It is a game the reader can win."

### Difficulty/Challenge Calibration
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative challenges facing the reader (puzzles, mysteries, ambiguity) are calibrated to be engaging without being either trivially easy or impossibly opaque.
**Assumed goal**: Fair-play mystery tradition (CrimeReads / golden age detective fiction conventions) — assumes the reader should be able to solve the puzzle in retrospect; deliberately unsolvable mysteries (e.g., Robbe-Grillet's The Erasers) and fiction that prioritizes atmosphere over puzzle (e.g., Tana French) may reject this calibration
**Score 0**: The story's challenges are either so obvious that readers feel patronized or so opaque that readers feel excluded. Mysteries are either unsolvable or transparent; ambiguity is either absent or impenetrable.
**Score 5**: Challenges are perfectly calibrated—the reader feels engaged and tested without being excluded. Mysteries are solvable in retrospect. Ambiguity enriches rather than confuses.
**Source**: https://crimereads.com/genre-communicates-a-contract-with-the-reader/
**Notes**: The source warns: make mysteries "impossible to figure out and the reader will feel spurned by an unearned ending" or "too easy" and readers dismiss it immediately.

### Suspension of Disbelief Maintenance
**What it measures**: Whether the story establishes its premises convincingly enough that readers accept and operate within the story's logic, even when it departs from reality.
**Assumed goal**: Internal-consistency worldbuilding model (Tolkien's "secondary belief" / Coleridge) — assumes fiction must establish and maintain a coherent internal logic; surrealist fiction (e.g., Kafka, Bulgakov) and dream-logic narratives may deliberately undermine consistent world-rules
**Score 0**: The story makes claims about its world that it fails to support. Rules are inconsistent, character reactions are implausible, and the reader constantly questions the logic of events rather than engaging with the story.
**Score 5**: The story's internal logic is consistent and convincing. Whatever premises the story establishes are supported throughout. Readers accept even fantastical elements because they are presented with internal consistency and appropriate character reactions.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suspension_of_disbelief
**Notes**: Internal consistency is fundamental: "SF/F readers will generally be willing to accept bizarre and impossible world constructions, as long as they are internally consistent." Character reactions serve as credibility anchors—shock, denial, explanation, and acceptance stages build plausibility. When suspension breaks, "doubt creeps in, disbelief isn't far behind."

### Technology/Magic Consistency
**What it measures**: Whether the story's speculative elements (technology, magic, supernatural) maintain a generally consistent level and are properly introduced.
**Assumed goal**: Hard magic/hard SF consistency tradition (Sanderson's Laws of Magic / Kelly McCullough's F&SF contract) — assumes speculative elements must follow discoverable, consistent rules; soft magic systems (e.g., Tolkien, Le Guin) and New Weird fiction may treat magic as irreducibly mysterious
**Score 0**: The story's speculative elements fluctuate wildly in capability and rules, with new powers or technologies appearing whenever the plot requires them. Previously established rules are contradicted without explanation.
**Score 5**: Speculative elements maintain consistent rules throughout. Any expansion of capabilities is properly foreshadowed and logically grounded. Readers can predict what is and isn't possible within the story's framework.
**Source**: https://kellymccullough.com/the-implicit-contract-between-writer-and-reader/
**Notes**: McCullough states that technology should maintain "a generally consistent level" with deviations requiring explanation "either at the time of the introduction of the deviation, or at least noted as being a deviation." SF with magical elements needs magic "shown to exist very very early in the story."

### Problem Statement Establishment
**What it measures**: Whether the story establishes its core problem early enough for readers to orient themselves and invest in the narrative.
**Assumed goal**: F&SF reader-contract model (Kelly McCullough) — assumes genre fiction must state its problem in the first few pages to anchor reader investment; literary fiction and experimental narratives may defer or obscure the central problem as part of their design (e.g., the problem emerges gradually in Kazuo Ishiguro's work)
**Score 0**: The core problem driving the narrative is either never clearly established or appears so late that readers have disengaged before understanding what the story is about.
**Score 5**: The core problem is established early—ideally in the first couple of pages—giving readers immediate orientation and reason to invest. The resolution follows logically from this established problem.
**Source**: https://kellymccullough.com/the-implicit-contract-between-writer-and-reader/
**Notes**: McCullough states authors must establish the core problem "ideally in the first couple of pages." The resolution need not match character expectations but "should be logical and follow naturally from the flow of the story."

### Character Continuity
**What it measures**: Whether characters who are prominent in the opening remain significant throughout, or have their diminished role adequately explained.
**Assumed goal**: Promise-based character contract (K.M. Weiland's eight promises) — assumes early prominence creates an obligation to sustain that character; ensemble fiction with rotating casts (e.g., Game of Thrones, The Wire) and literary fiction with deliberately transient characters may cycle characters in and out
**Score 0**: Characters introduced as important in the opening vanish from the story without explanation, leaving readers wondering what happened to them and feeling their investment was wasted.
**Score 5**: Every character given significant screen time in the opening either remains important throughout the story or has their departure meaningfully explained. No character introduced as important is silently abandoned.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/8-promises-youre-making-to-readersand/
**Notes**: K.M. Weiland: "I promise characters who are important in the beginning of the story will not be forgotten about by the end."

### Cause-and-Effect Chain Integrity
**What it measures**: Whether every cause in the story leads to an appropriate effect, and every significant effect has a proportionate cause.
**Assumed goal**: Causal-chain dramatic structure (K.M. Weiland / Aristotelian plot logic) — assumes narrative events must form an unbroken chain of cause and effect; absurdist fiction (e.g., Ionesco, Heller's Catch-22) and surrealist narratives deliberately sever causal links
**Score 0**: Events happen without logical cause, consequences do not follow actions, and the story's chain of causality is broken or arbitrary.
**Score 5**: Every action leads to logical, proportionate consequences. Every major event has identifiable causes established earlier in the narrative. The causal chain is unbroken and satisfying.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/8-promises-youre-making-to-readersand/
**Notes**: K.M. Weiland: "I promise every cause will end in an appropriate effect—and vice versa."

### Opening Pacing Balance
**What it measures**: Whether the opening achieves the right balance between action/engagement and context/orientation—neither drowning the reader in backstory nor throwing them into incomprehensible action.
**Assumed goal**: In-medias-res opening tradition (SouthWest Writers / commercial fiction pacing) — assumes openings must balance immediate action with minimal context; epic fiction (e.g., Tolkien, Tad Williams) and literary fiction in the 19th-century tradition may front-load extensive world-establishment
**Score 0**: The opening is either a wall of backstory/exposition that delays the story for dozens of pages, or it is so fast and contextless that readers are confused and disoriented.
**Score 5**: The opening enters the story at the right moment—"fast, but not too fast"—providing enough context for reader orientation while maintaining momentum and engagement. Backstory is "seasoning, not the meal."
**Source**: https://www.southwestwriters.com/writing-fiction-the-promise-of-the-first-chapter/
**Notes**: Eboch writes about finding balance: "Fast, but not too fast. How do you find the balance?" The answer: "Start in a moment of action, where something is changing, and cut the background" while still allowing readers time to understand and care.

### B-Plot Integration
**What it measures**: Whether subplots and secondary narrative threads feel like organic extensions of the main story rather than distractions or diversions.
**Assumed goal**: Unified-plot craft tradition (CrimeReads / genre-fiction subplot conventions) — assumes subplots must serve the main narrative; maximalist fiction (e.g., Dickens, David Mitchell's Cloud Atlas) and picaresque novels may embrace digressions as their own reward
**Score 0**: Subplots feel disconnected from the main narrative, pulling readers away from the core story without enriching it. They "feel like derailments" and waste the reader's time.
**Score 5**: Subplots feel like natural extensions of the main narrative, enriching the central conflict and themes. They "feel like side quests, not derailments."
**Source**: https://crimereads.com/genre-communicates-a-contract-with-the-reader/
**Notes**: CrimeReads states B-plots should "feel like side quests, not derailments, so the reader doesn't feel you've wasted their time."

### Narrative Coherence
**What it measures**: Whether all elements of the story—characters, plot events, themes, settings—align logically and consistently to create an understandable, connected whole.
**Assumed goal**: Formal story evaluation (arxiv computational narrative assessment) — assumes coherence is a measurable, universal quality of good fiction; fragmented narratives (e.g., Burroughs' cut-up technique, Danielewski's House of Leaves), stream-of-consciousness fiction, and deliberately incoherent postmodern work reject coherence as a primary value
**Score 0**: The narrative is incoherent—events contradict each other, character motivations are inconsistent, themes are scattered, and the reader cannot construct a unified understanding of what the story is about.
**Score 5**: All narrative elements align seamlessly. Events follow logically from prior events, character actions are motivated and consistent, themes are unified, and the story reads as a coherent whole.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2408.14622v1
**Notes**: The survey identifies coherence as a primary evaluation dimension: "Whether all sentences and plots are well structured, with the context organized and connected logically." Sub-aspects include cohesion, consistency, implicit relevance, completeness, ending, and clarity.

### Interestingness/Engagement
**What it measures**: Whether the story is enjoyable, entertaining, and engaging to read, with rich details that engage the reader's imagination.
**Assumed goal**: Computational narrative evaluation (arxiv story quality metrics) — assumes entertainment and sensory richness are measurable proxies for quality; minimalist fiction (e.g., Carver, Hemingway) deliberately strips sensory detail, and challenging literary fiction (e.g., Bernhard, Jelinek) may prioritize discomfort over enjoyment
**Score 0**: The story is boring, flat, and fails to engage the reader's imagination or emotions at any point. It reads as a summary of events rather than a lived experience.
**Score 5**: The story is highly enjoyable and entertaining, with rich details and descriptions that engage the reader's senses and imagination. Reading it is a compelling, immersive experience.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2408.14622v1
**Notes**: The survey defines this as "Whether the story is highly enjoyable or entertaining to read, with rich details and descriptions that engage the readers' senses and imagination."

### Surprise/Suspense Generation
**What it measures**: Whether the story creates genuine suspense and surprise, particularly through unpredictable-yet-logical developments.
**Assumed goal**: Suspense-driven narrative model (arxiv story evaluation / commercial genre pacing) — assumes unpredictability is a core value; literary fiction that foregrounds inevitability (e.g., Chronicle of a Death Foretold) and character-study fiction where outcomes are known from the start may not prioritize surprise
**Score 0**: The story is entirely predictable from beginning to end. No scene produces genuine surprise or suspense. The reader always knows what will happen next.
**Score 5**: The story generates genuine suspense throughout and delivers surprising developments that, in retrospect, feel inevitable. Tension is maintained and surprises are both unexpected and logical.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2408.14622v1
**Notes**: The survey identifies surprise as: "Whether the story creates suspense and surprise, especially in mystery fictions."

### Empathic Engagement
**What it measures**: Whether the story arouses the reader's emotional experience, passion, and empathy.
**Assumed goal**: Empathy-centered fiction theory (arxiv story evaluation / Martha Nussbaum's narrative ethics) — assumes fiction's value lies partly in generating emotional identification; intellectually driven fiction (e.g., Borges, Lem), satirical fiction (e.g., Swift, Voltaire), and Brechtian alienation-effect narratives deliberately suppress empathic identification
**Score 0**: The story fails to evoke any emotional response. Characters feel flat, events feel inconsequential, and the reader remains emotionally detached throughout.
**Score 5**: The story creates deep emotional engagement—readers feel the characters' emotions, experience genuine empathy, and are emotionally affected by the story's events and outcomes.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2408.14622v1
**Notes**: The survey defines empathy as: "Whether the story arouses the readers' emotional experience, passion, and empathy."

### Point of View Consistency
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative maintains a consistent, deliberately chosen point of view that serves as an effective filter for the story's information and experience.
**Assumed goal**: POV-as-organizing-principle model (Jane Friedman / contemporary commercial fiction craft) — assumes a single, rigorously maintained POV per scene is the standard; omniscient narration (e.g., Tolstoy, Pratchett), free indirect discourse, and experimental POV-shifting (e.g., Woolf's The Waves) operate under different conventions
**Score 0**: The point of view is inconsistent, accidental, or absent—shifting between characters within scenes without purpose, mixing omniscient and limited perspectives, or providing information the viewpoint character could not know.
**Score 5**: The point of view is deliberately chosen, rigorously maintained, and serves as an effective subjective filter that shapes the reader's experience. Any shifts are purposeful and clearly signaled.
**Source**: https://janefriedman.com/deadliest-first-page-sin/
**Notes**: Jane Friedman identifies "default omniscience" as the deadliest first-page sin: "NO POINT OF VIEW = NO STORY." Point of view is "the organizing principle" and "the rock on which fiction is built." Without it, readers receive "information" rather than "experience."

### Commonsense Plausibility
**What it measures**: Whether the story adheres to commonsense knowledge about physical entities, social interactions, and human behavior within its established framework.
**Assumed goal**: Verisimilitude tradition (arxiv story evaluation / realist fiction conventions) — assumes characters and events must behave in psychologically and physically recognizable ways; absurdist fiction (e.g., Kafka's Metamorphosis, Ionesco), fabulism, and surrealist narratives deliberately violate commonsense expectations
**Score 0**: Characters behave in ways that violate basic human psychology, physical events defy established in-world physics, and social interactions follow no recognizable human pattern.
**Score 5**: All character behavior, physical events, and social interactions are plausible within the story's established framework. Even fantastic elements are grounded in recognizable human experience and logic.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2408.14622v1
**Notes**: The survey defines commonsense as: "Whether the story adheres to commonsense knowledge, such as physical entities and social interactions."

## Sentinels Found

### Premise Inexpressible in One Sentence
**Detection**: After reading the complete work, it is impossible to express the premise in a single sentence that communicates protagonist, goal, and central conflict.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs frequently generate narratives that meander through scenarios without a unifying narrative spine, because token-by-token generation optimizes for local coherence rather than global structural integrity. A story that cannot be reduced to a premise sentence likely has no premise.
**Design space cost**: Excludes deliberately fragmented or experimental literary fiction that intentionally resists summary (e.g., some modernist/postmodernist works).
**Source**: https://www.marykole.com/premise

### Inciting Incident Buried Past 15% Mark
**Detection**: The event that disrupts ordinary life and launches the central conflict does not occur until after the first 15% of the manuscript.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs tend to generate extensive "throat clearing"—backstory, worldbuilding dumps, and character descriptions—before reaching any narrative action, because their training data contains many such openings and they optimize for completeness of context rather than narrative urgency.
**Design space cost**: Excludes deliberate slow-burn openings in literary fiction or epic fantasy that use extended world-establishment before the inciting event.
**Source**: https://www.livewritethrive.com/2023/01/02/crafting-a-compelling-story-premise/

### Genre Unidentifiable After First Chapter
**Detection**: After reading the first chapter, it is impossible to confidently identify the story's genre.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs trained on diverse corpora often produce genre-ambiguous openings because they average across many styles rather than committing to one, resulting in a "gray goo" that reads as technically competent but generically unclassifiable.
**Design space cost**: Excludes intentionally genre-blending or genre-defying literary fiction.
**Source**: https://judylmohr.com/2022/06/17/promise-of-the-premise/

### False Hook (Opening Action Disconnected from Story)
**Detection**: The opening scene's primary tension or action (first 1-3 pages) has no causal, thematic, or character connection to the story's actual central conflict.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs frequently generate exciting openings because they are statistically common in training data, then shift to an unrelated story because they lack global narrative planning. The hook and the story are generated as separate probability streams.
**Design space cost**: Excludes deliberately misleading openings that serve a later thematic purpose (rare in practice).
**Source**: https://inkshift.io/resources/opening-hook

### Opening Revealed as Dream/Simulation/Fantasy
**Detection**: The opening scene is revealed within the first few chapters to have been a dream, simulation, virtual reality, or fantasy sequence.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: This is a known LLM failure mode—generating exciting opening content then needing to reconcile it with a calmer actual story, leading to the "it was all a dream" escape hatch. It also represents one of the most commonly cited examples of a betrayed narrative promise.
**Design space cost**: Excludes the rare case where dream-frame openings serve a genuine structural purpose (e.g., prophetic dreams that establish thematic concerns).
**Source**: https://inkshift.io/resources/opening-hook

### Tonal Whiplash Without Foreshadowing
**Detection**: The story's emotional register shifts abruptly (e.g., from comedic to horrific, or from light adventure to grimdark) without any prior establishment that such a range exists within the story's world.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs can shift tone unpredictably because each generated passage optimizes for local context rather than maintaining a consistent global emotional register. The model may encounter a prompt or preceding passage that triggers a different tonal mode without "remembering" the established tone.
**Design space cost**: Excludes deliberately disorienting tonal shifts used for artistic effect (e.g., Tarantino-style tonal collisions).
**Source**: https://lgestrella.wordpress.com/2020/11/30/how-abrupt-tonal-shifts-can-kill-a-story/

### Every Chapter Ends on Upbeat Forward-Looking Note
**Detection**: More than 75% of chapter endings contain optimistic, forward-looking, or resolution-affirming final paragraphs.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs trained with RLHF are biased toward positive, helpful, conflict-resolving outputs. This manifests in fiction as "nauseatingly upbeat forward-looking rosy glow" chapter endings rather than organic tension, ambiguity, or dread.
**Design space cost**: Excludes genuinely optimistic fiction where upbeat chapter endings are thematically appropriate.
**Source**: https://www.creativindie.com/how-to-humanize-chatgpt-written-content-for-better-fiction-and-to-pass-ai-detection/

### Protagonist Returns to Small Town and Resolves Conflict Through Community
**Detection**: The primary plot follows a protagonist who returns to or lives in a small town and resolves their central conflict by reconnecting with tradition, community, or local values.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Analysis of 11,800 AI-generated stories found they "overwhelmingly conform to a single narrative plot structure across countries: a protagonist lives in or returns home to a small town and resolves a minor conflict by reconnecting with tradition and organising community events."
**Design space cost**: Excludes the many genuine human-written stories with this structure (it is a legitimate and popular genre). This sentinel has a high false-positive rate and should be used only in conjunction with other indicators.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2603.13545v1

### Conflict-Free or Sanitized Central Tension
**Detection**: The story's central conflict involves no genuine threat, loss, cost, or irrecoverable consequence. All tensions are resolved easily and without sacrifice.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: AI stories systematically "sanitise real-world conflicts" and "downplay narrative tension in favour of nostalgia and reconciliation" due to RLHF training that penalizes harmful/negative content. Constitutional AI approaches emphasizing "helpful, harmless, and honest" suppress the conflict and tension essential for narrative.
**Design space cost**: Excludes slice-of-life fiction and certain literary fiction where low-stakes observation is the point.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2603.13545v1

### Emotionally Flat Narrative Despite Dramatic Events
**Detection**: Events that should produce strong emotional responses (death, betrayal, loss, triumph) are narrated with an "eerie placidity"—the prose registers no emotional intensity proportionate to the events described.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs lack genuine emotional experience and often produce prose that is "emotionally flat and boring" despite technically competent description of dramatic events. The emotional register remains uniform regardless of what is happening in the plot.
**Design space cost**: Excludes deliberately flat-affect narration used for artistic effect (e.g., Camus's The Stranger, certain minimalist fiction).
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2603.13545v1

### Details Feel Random Rather Than Chosen
**Detection**: Specific details in the narrative (objects, settings, character descriptions, metaphors) do not resonate with themes, deepen character, or serve any identifiable narrative purpose. They could be swapped with other details without affecting meaning.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Lincoln Michel observes that in AI fiction "the specific details in the story feel random. Not drawn to deepen characters or resonate with themes." Good writers "pick details to work together to create specific effects and meanings." LLMs select details based on statistical probability rather than narrative intent.
**Design space cost**: Minimal—randomly chosen details are almost never a deliberate artistic choice.
**Source**: https://countercraft.substack.com/p/mfa-vs-llm-is-openais-metafiction

### Metaphor Pile-Up Without Coherent Vehicle
**Detection**: The narrative deploys multiple extended metaphors for the same concept that draw from incompatible source domains (e.g., construction imagery, tailoring imagery, and magical imagery all describing the same thing within a few paragraphs).
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Michel identifies the "five-car metaphor pile-up" where AI mixes construction (nails, scaffolding), tailoring (cloth, dyed, draped), and magic without coherent purpose. LLMs generate metaphors based on local probability without maintaining a coherent metaphorical framework.
**Design space cost**: Excludes deliberately surrealist or maximalist prose that intentionally mixes metaphorical registers.
**Source**: https://countercraft.substack.com/p/mfa-vs-llm-is-openais-metafiction

### Premise Stated But Never Explored
**Detection**: The story's core speculative, dramatic, or thematic premise is established but the narrative never meaningfully explores its implications, consequences, or variations. The premise serves as set dressing rather than the engine of the story.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs can generate compelling premises from their training data but lack the ability to systematically explore implications. Michel notes AI fiction has "underexplored premise"—the concept is "serviceable" but remains "thematically underexplored."
**Design space cost**: Minimal—a premise that exists only as decoration is a structural flaw in nearly all cases.
**Source**: https://countercraft.substack.com/p/mfa-vs-llm-is-openais-metafiction

### Flat Narrative Arc (Story "Doesn't Go Anywhere")
**Detection**: The story lacks meaningful progression—the protagonist's situation, understanding, relationships, or capabilities at the end are substantially similar to the beginning. Events occur but nothing fundamentally changes.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Michel identifies the fundamental AI fiction problem: "it doesn't really go anywhere. It's flat." LLMs generate sequences of events but lack the ability to construct genuine narrative arcs. "The central idea is just repeated with different flowery but incoherent metaphors."
**Design space cost**: Excludes certain literary fiction that deliberately eschews traditional arc structure (e.g., some slice-of-life, Beckettian work).
**Source**: https://countercraft.substack.com/p/mfa-vs-llm-is-openais-metafiction

### Deus Ex Machina Resolution
**Detection**: The central conflict is resolved by an external force, previously unknown resource, or unearned coincidence that was never set up or foreshadowed in the narrative.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generating stories token-by-token can paint themselves into narrative corners and then resolve conflicts through sudden, unearned means because they lack the ability to plan backward from climax. The resolution is generated based on what token comes next, not what was established before.
**Design space cost**: Excludes intentional deus ex machina used for thematic purposes (e.g., absurdist literature, divine intervention in mythology/magical realism where it is part of the established world).
**Source**: https://blog.chapter.pub/deus-ex-machina/

### Unfired Chekhov's Guns
**Detection**: The narrative introduces elements with heightened emphasis (unusual details, lengthy descriptions, pointed attention) that are never revisited or paid off.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate interesting-sounding details for local effect without tracking whether they need to be resolved. Each passage is optimized for immediate engagement rather than long-range narrative integrity, resulting in "loose ends" that are "the mark of sloppy or poorly-planned writing."
**Design space cost**: Excludes deliberate red herrings in mystery/thriller genres and deliberately ambiguous literary fiction.
**Source**: https://www.scribophile.com/academy/what-is-chekhovs-gun

### Opening Saturated with Backstory/Exposition
**Detection**: The first chapter or first 10% of the manuscript is primarily devoted to backstory, worldbuilding exposition, or character history rather than present-tense narrative action.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs are trained on corpora that include prologues, appendices, and expository passages. When generating openings, they tend to "front-load the story with excessive backstory, worldbuilding, or character descriptions" because this pattern is statistically common in their training data. They "treat the reader like a student who needs to study before the story can begin."
**Design space cost**: Excludes frame narratives, epistolary openings, and certain literary fiction traditions that open with extended exposition.
**Source**: https://inkshift.io/resources/opening-hook

### Protagonist Absent from Opening
**Detection**: The first chapter focuses primarily on characters who are not the story's protagonist, or the protagonist does not appear until the second chapter or later.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs can begin generating narrative without a clear sense of which character is the protagonist, resulting in openings that feature whichever characters the model generates first. Human writing craft dictates that "readers will assume that whoever is prominent in the opening pages is the MC."
**Design space cost**: Excludes stories that deliberately open with non-protagonist perspectives for structural reasons (e.g., prologues from the villain's POV, ensemble narratives).
**Source**: https://www.southwestwriters.com/writing-fiction-the-promise-of-the-first-chapter/

### Misdirected Promise (Opening Genre ≠ Story Genre)
**Detection**: The genre signaled by the opening chapter (based on setting, tone, conflict type, and character focus) is different from the genre the story actually occupies for the majority of its length.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs may generate an opening in one genre mode and shift to another as generation continues, because they lack global awareness of the story's genre classification. Mary Kole identifies "misdirected promise" as the most common opening error she observes—openings that "take place in school or with family or during a sports game" that don't reflect the actual story.
**Design space cost**: Excludes deliberate genre-bending that is established as the story's identity from the opening.
**Source**: https://kidlit.com/the-promise-of-the-novel/

### Homogeneously Positive Emotional Register
**Detection**: The narrative's emotional arc remains consistently positive or neutral throughout, with no sustained periods of tension, dread, grief, anger, or other negative emotional states proportionate to the story's events.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Research confirms LLM-generated narratives are "homogeneously positive," producing "flatter arousal curves and less tension than human-written fiction." This results from RLHF training biases that penalize negative emotional content.
**Design space cost**: Excludes genuinely uplifting fiction (cozy mysteries, feel-good romances) where sustained positive register is genre-appropriate.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2603.13545v1

### Repetitive Syntactic Patterns
**Detection**: The prose exhibits the same sentence structures repeated across multiple paragraphs—particularly the "Not X; Y" construction, lists of three, or rigid topic-sentence/evidence/summary paragraph structure.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: AI-generated prose relies on "formulaic sentence structures" with "common templates including the 'From X to Y' construction and heavy use of present participial phrases" at "two to five times the rate found in human-written text." Human writers instinctively vary their constructions; LLMs default to predictable patterns.
**Design space cost**: Excludes deliberately repetitive prose used for rhetorical or rhythmic effect.
**Source**: https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction

### Characters Repeat Same Words/Phrases
**Detection**: Different characters in the story use the same distinctive phrases, vocabulary patterns, or speech rhythms, making them sound interchangeable.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs "tend to choose the same character names, the same words ad nauseum" and "even have different characters repeat the exact same words." The model generates dialogue from a single voice distribution rather than maintaining distinct character voices.
**Design space cost**: Minimal—distinct character voices are a near-universal craft standard.
**Source**: https://www.creativindie.com/how-to-humanize-chatgpt-written-content-for-better-fiction-and-to-pass-ai-detection/

### Flowery-But-Meaningless Prose
**Detection**: The narrative contains sentences that sound profound or literary but convey no specific meaning when analyzed—"flowery but nonsensical sentences" that seem impressive on first reading but dissolve under scrutiny.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Michel identifies "womb of oblivion" style lines—sentences that sound profound but "don't mean anything." LLMs optimize for prose that patterns-match to "literary" writing without the underlying semantic content. The result is "an algorithm assembling probable language without thought."
**Design space cost**: Excludes deliberately opaque or sound-driven literary prose (e.g., Gertrude Stein, some poetry-influenced fiction).
**Source**: https://countercraft.substack.com/p/mfa-vs-llm-is-openais-metafiction

### Story Reads Like Plot Summary Rather Than Lived Experience
**Detection**: The narrative tells what happened rather than rendering scenes—it summarizes events, reports emotions, and explains motivations rather than dramatizing them through action, dialogue, and sensory detail.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs "summarize or describe the premise rather than launching into a narrative" and struggle with "show, don't tell"—"failing to convey character emotion and plot advancement through action and dialogue alone, instead relying on exposition and telling."
**Design space cost**: Excludes summary passages that are a deliberate craft choice for pacing (e.g., transitional passages in epic fiction).
**Source**: https://www.creativindie.com/how-to-humanize-chatgpt-written-content-for-better-fiction-and-to-pass-ai-detection/

### No Character Has a Distinct Voice
**Detection**: The narrative voice and all character dialogue share the same register, vocabulary level, and speech patterns. The narrator and all characters sound like the same person.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate all text from a single probability distribution, meaning narrator voice and character voices tend to converge. The result is a story where "the LLM has no character or voice" and characters are "even less of a character" than the narrator.
**Design space cost**: Excludes first-person narration where the narrator's voice deliberately colors all reported speech.
**Source**: https://countercraft.substack.com/p/mfa-vs-llm-is-openais-metafiction

### White Room Syndrome
**Detection**: Scenes take place in ungrounded space—readers cannot determine whether they are indoors or outdoors, day or night, or in any identifiable location because setting details are absent.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs can generate dialogue and action without anchoring it in physical space, producing scenes that float in a contextless void. Human writers ground scenes in sensory detail; LLMs may omit this when optimizing for plot advancement.
**Design space cost**: Minimal—setting grounding is a near-universal craft requirement.
**Source**: https://www.alyssamatesic.com/free-writing-resources/mistakes-in-your-first-ten-pages

### Stakes Never Articulated or Escalated
**Detection**: It is impossible to identify what the protagonist stands to lose at any point in the story, or the stakes remain at the same level from beginning to end without escalation.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs can generate sequences of events without establishing why those events matter. Stakes require intentional planning—what is at risk and how that risk grows. Without backward planning from climax, LLMs produce narratives where "nothing is at risk" and "the reader has no reason to care about what happens."
**Design space cost**: Excludes certain experimental or meditative fiction where absence of stakes is a deliberate artistic choice.
**Source**: https://darlingaxe.com/blogs/news/stakes

### Subversion Without Foundation (Bait-and-Switch)
**Detection**: The story dramatically shifts its genre, tone, or central conflict in a way that contradicts the opening's established promises without any foreshadowing or setup in the preceding narrative.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generating long-form fiction can shift modes mid-generation as their context window fills with new content, producing genre shifts or tonal pivots that were never foreshadowed because the model lacks global narrative awareness.
**Design space cost**: Excludes intentional, well-foreshadowed genre pivots where clues are planted throughout (e.g., From Dusk Till Dawn, which signals its shift through earlier genre tension).
**Source**: https://www.richellebraswell.com/blog/subverting-reader-expectations

### Resolution Arrives Without Character Struggle
**Detection**: The story's central conflict is resolved without the protagonist having made meaningful choices, taken decisive action, or incurred personal cost.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs can generate plot resolution by introducing convenient solutions rather than constructing resolution through character agency. The result: "every obstacle the character faced was meaningless. Every sacrifice was theater."
**Design space cost**: Excludes tragic or absurdist fiction where the point is that human agency is insufficient (but even these typically show the struggle).
**Source**: https://blog.chapter.pub/deus-ex-machina/

### Opening Paragraph Uses Stock Attention-Grabbing Formula
**Detection**: The opening uses a formulaic attention-grabbing construction such as "Have you ever wondered...," "What if I told you...," "It was a [adjective] [time] when...," or other patterns commonly found in blog posts and marketing copy.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: "AI writing tools consistently rely on the same attention-grabbing formulae" with openings like "Have you ever wondered," "Are you struggling with," or "What if I told you," because "AI models learn from countless blog posts and marketing copy using these exact patterns."
**Design space cost**: Minimal—these openings are widely recognized as weak in published fiction regardless of authorship.
**Source**: https://www.theaugmentededucator.com/p/the-ten-telltale-signs-of-ai-generated

### Consistent Repetition of Specific Names/Locations/Themes Across Works
**Detection**: When evaluating multiple works from the same source, the same character names, location names, occupations, and thematic concerns appear repeatedly across different stories.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Brigham's cross-model study documented "consistent repetition of specific names, locations, occupations, and themes" regardless of which AI system generated the text. "Stereotypical patterns persisted even with prompting to avoid them."
**Design space cost**: Only applicable when evaluating a corpus rather than a single work. Not applicable to single-work evaluation.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2603.13545v1

### Emdash and Semicolon Overuse
**Detection**: The prose uses emdashes, semicolons, or specific punctuation marks at a rate significantly higher than comparable human-written fiction in the same genre.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: AI-generated fiction shows "emdash overuse" as a stylistic marker, with newer models substituting semicolons/commas while "maintaining predictable structures." This reflects the model's training distribution favoring certain punctuation patterns.
**Design space cost**: Excludes authors who deliberately favor emdash-heavy or semicolon-heavy prose styles (e.g., Emily Dickinson's descendants).
**Source**: https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction

### Blocky Prose Rhythm
**Detection**: Dialogue, exposition, and narration appear in clearly demarcated, separate blocks rather than flowing naturally into each other. Scenes follow a rigid structure of narration block, dialogue block, narration block.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: AI-generated prose shows "dialogue, exposition, and narration appear in clearly demarcated sections rather than flowing naturally." Human writers intersperse these elements; LLMs tend to generate them in discrete chunks.
**Design space cost**: Excludes deliberately structured or experimental prose formats.
**Source**: https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction

### "Not X; Y" Construction Overuse
**Detection**: The prose frequently uses the construction "It wasn't X; it was Y" or "Not X, but Y" as a rhetorical device, appearing multiple times per chapter.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: This construction is identified as "nearly universal in AI output" in fiction. It represents a statistically common pattern in the training data that LLMs over-index on.
**Design space cost**: Excludes prose that deliberately uses this construction as a recurring stylistic choice.
**Source**: https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction

### Swings Between Ponderous Similes and Cliche Short Lines
**Detection**: The prose alternates rhythmically between "long, heavy comparisons" (extended similes/metaphors) and "short lines that sound like stock phrases" in a predictable cadence.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: AI-generated fiction "often swings between two modes: long, heavy comparisons, then short lines that sound like stock phrases." This reflects the model's attempt to pattern-match to "literary" and "punchy" prose styles without integrating them organically.
**Design space cost**: Excludes prose that deliberately employs rhythmic alternation as a stylistic device.
**Source**: https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction
