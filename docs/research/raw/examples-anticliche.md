# Cliche Avoidance and Restrained Prose: Exemplar Passages

Three short excerpts from published novels and stories demonstrating how skilled writers handle familiar situations (grief, transcendence, vision) without purple prose, stock phrases, or received language.

---

## 1. Grief Rendered as a Practical Problem

**Joan Didion, *The Year of Magical Thinking* (2005)**

> I could not give away the rest of his shoes. I stood there for a moment, then realized why: he would need shoes if he was to return. The recognition of this thought by no means eradicated the thought. I have still not tried to determine (say, by giving away the shoes) if the thought has lost its power.

**What makes this work:** A lesser writer describing grief after a spouse's death would reach for tears, emptiness metaphors, or the weight-on-the-chest cliche. Didion instead locates grief in a logistical absurdity: she cannot donate shoes because the dead man will need them. The prose is flat, declarative, almost clinical -- "The recognition of this thought by no means eradicated the thought" reads like a case note, not a confession. This restraint is what makes it devastating. The irrational belief is reported without dramatization or self-pity, and the final sentence admits the magical thinking persists without resolving it. The reader supplies the emotional weight precisely because the prose refuses to.

## 2. Transcendence Described Through Negation

**Raymond Carver, "Cathedral" (1981)**

> His fingers rode my fingers as my hand went over the paper. It was like nothing else in my life up to now. Then he said, "I think that's it. I think you got it," he said. "Take a look. What do you think?" But I had my eyes closed. I thought I'd keep them that way for a little longer. I thought it was something I ought to do. "Well?" he said. "Are you looking?" My eyes were still closed. I was in my house. I knew that. But I didn't feel like I was inside anything. "It's really something," I said.

**What makes this work:** The narrator has just experienced a moment of genuine human connection and spiritual openness -- drawing a cathedral with a blind man while his own eyes are closed. The stock approach would be epiphany language: revelation, warmth flooding through him, scales falling from eyes. Carver does the opposite. The passage is built entirely from negation and understatement: "like nothing else," "I didn't feel like I was inside anything," "It's really something." The narrator lacks the vocabulary for what has happened to him, so the prose enacts that inadequacy rather than compensating for it. The flat, monosyllabic sentences refuse to elevate the moment, which is exactly what makes it feel earned rather than imposed.

## 3. Vision Without Purple Prose

**Denis Johnson, "Emergency" from *Jesus' Son* (1992)**

> On the farther side of the field, just beyond the curtains of snow, the sky was torn away and the angels were descending out of a brilliant blue summer, their huge faces streaked with light and full of pity. The sight of them cut through my heart and down the knuckles of my spine, and if there'd been anything in my bowels I would have messed my pants from fear.
>
> Georgie opened his arms and cried out, "It's the drive-in, man!"

**What makes this work:** This is a vision scene -- angels descending from a torn sky -- which in most hands would become the worst kind of overwrought prose. Johnson prevents that through two moves. First, the physical response is not elevated but degrading: the narrator nearly soils himself. This grounds the transcendence in the body rather than floating it into abstraction. Second, the vision is immediately punctured by Georgie's identification of its source -- a drive-in movie screen playing through a snowstorm. The passage holds both registers simultaneously: the narrator's drugged, genuine awe and the banal mechanical explanation. Neither cancels the other. The prose itself is simple and declarative even when describing the impossible, which is what prevents "angels descending out of a brilliant blue summer" from tipping into purple -- the sentence earns its one lyrical phrase by surrounding it with plainness.
