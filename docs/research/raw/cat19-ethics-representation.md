## Criteria Found

### Stereotype Avoidance (Racial/Ethnic)
**What it measures**: Whether characters from marginalized racial/ethnic groups are depicted as complex individuals rather than through reductive stereotypes.
**Assumed goal**: Social justice-informed fiction criticism (sensitivity reading tradition) where representation must avoid reducing individuals to group stereotypes and respect cultural specificity -- transgressive fiction may deliberately deploy stereotypes for satirical or deconstructive purposes, and literary realism may depict characters who hold stereotypical views to critique those views.
**Score 0**: Characters of color exist only as stereotypes (the "magical negro" who exists to help white protagonists, the "model minority" Asian who is studious and submissive, the "fiery Latina," the "angry Black woman," the "wise elder" indigenous person). Racial identity is reduced to single traits. Characters have no inner life, goals, or agency independent of serving the protagonist's arc.
**Score 5**: Characters from all racial/ethnic backgrounds are fully realized individuals with their own goals, flaws, histories, and inner lives. Cultural specificity is present and researched but does not reduce characters to cultural ambassadors. Stereotypes, if present, are interrogated by the narrative rather than reinforced. Characters of color have agency and arcs independent of white characters.
**Source**: https://writershelpingwriters.net/2022/06/avoiding-stereotypes-in-fiction-people-of-color/ and https://www.readinmag.com/fantasyblog/diversity-vs-tokenism
**Notes**: Writing With Color (writingwithcolor.com) emphasizes that readers default to white unless ethnicity is explicitly stated, making intentional description necessary for meaningful representation. The Council on Interracial Books for Children's "Ten Quick Ways" checklist asks whether standards for success require "white behavior" and whether people of color are considered "the problem."

### Tokenism vs. Integrated Diversity
**What it measures**: Whether diverse characters are present as decoration/quota-filling or are meaningfully integrated into the narrative.
**Assumed goal**: Social justice-informed criticism (diversity as substantive, not cosmetic) where marginalized characters must have full narrative integration -- some literary traditions may include characters of various backgrounds without foregrounding identity, and commercial fiction may include diversity for market appeal without deep integration.
**Score 0**: Diverse characters are present in name only. They have minimal backstory, vague physical descriptions with no cultural anchoring, no personal goals outside advancing the protagonist's arc, and are dispensable to the plot. Diversity appears only during designated cultural moments. A character of color is killed off conveniently once their "usefulness" ends.
**Score 5**: Diverse characters possess their own motivations, relationships, and arcs. Their identities are specific and culturally grounded rather than racially vague. They matter to the plot in ways that removing them would damage the narrative. Cultural elements are woven throughout rather than appearing in isolated moments. Multiple characters from any given background show within-group diversity.
**Source**: https://www.readinmag.com/fantasyblog/diversity-vs-tokenism and https://writingwithcolor.com/post/733471425183285248/faq-what-is-tokenism
**Notes**: Readin' Magazine identifies specific markers of tokenism including racially vague descriptions ("tawny," "olive skin"), culturally inappropriate naming, and "convenient deaths." The academic literature (PMC 10837049) finds that perceived tokenism thwarts consumer acceptance and can backfire on diversity goals.

### Cultural Appropriation vs. Respectful Engagement
**What it measures**: Whether the work engages with cultures outside the author's own through respectful research and engagement, or through extractive cherry-picking.
**Assumed goal**: Social justice-informed criticism (community consent and benefit tradition) where cross-cultural fiction requires humility, research, and community engagement -- the philosophical tradition (APA Blog, Miller) holds that stories intertwine across cultures and the key criterion is whether the use is "constructive and creative or destructive," while the transgressive tradition rejects gatekeeping based on identity entirely.
**Score 0**: Cultural elements are plucked from their context as decoration ("the salad bar approach"). Sacred or significant practices are trivialized or treated as fantasy. No evidence of research or community engagement. The work profits from cultural elements while erasing or demeaning their source communities. Colonial "everything is up for grabs" mentality.
**Score 5**: Cross-cultural elements reflect deep research, ideally including consultation with community members or sensitivity readers. Cultural elements retain their significance and context. The work acknowledges its sources and treats them with specificity rather than generalizing. The author demonstrates awareness of their positionality. Cultural traditions are depicted with internal logic and respect.
**Source**: https://everydayfeminism.com/2018/04/cultural-appropriation-fiction/ and https://blog.apaonline.org/2024/09/12/someone-elses-story-cultural-appropriation-in-fiction/
**Notes**: Everyday Feminism recommends reading 100 books by authors from the culture being depicted; if you cannot find 100 books, reconsider writing about that culture. The APA Blog (Miller) argues the key evaluative criterion is "the nature of the 'use'" -- constructive/creative vs. destructive. Jeannette Ng and others emphasize that sensitivity readers should be compensated fairly.

### Research Integrity for Cross-Cultural Depiction
**What it measures**: The depth and quality of research underpinning depictions of cultures, communities, or experiences outside the author's own.
**Assumed goal**: Social justice-informed fiction criticism (authenticity tradition) where depiction of marginalized communities requires extensive primary research -- literary formalism may evaluate only the internal coherence of the fictional world regardless of real-world accuracy, and postmodern fiction may deliberately play with inauthenticity.
**Score 0**: No evidence of research. Cultural practices, language, geography, or social dynamics are clearly invented or drawn from other fiction/media rather than reality. Anachronisms and inaccuracies pervade. The work reflects internalized stereotypes absorbed from pop culture rather than lived experience or primary sources.
**Score 5**: Cultural details are specific, accurate, and grounded in primary sources or lived experience. The work reflects understanding of internal diversity within depicted communities (not treating them as monolithic). Historical and social context is accurate. Language, naming, customs, and social dynamics reflect genuine knowledge. The work has been reviewed by people with relevant lived experience.
**Source**: https://www.writersdigest.com/write-better-fiction/representation-in-fiction-how-to-write-characters-whose-experiences-are-outside-of-your-own and https://www.sensitivityreader.com/about
**Notes**: Writer's Digest notes that writers need to achieve the status of "guest" -- someone with insider knowledge and trust -- and that this status must be given by the community, not self-assigned. Authenticity in literary representation requires "embracing a multiplicity of narratives of marginalized identities, written by authors from those communities."

### Depiction vs. Endorsement Clarity
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative framework makes clear the distinction between depicting morally reprehensible attitudes/behaviors and endorsing them.
**Assumed goal**: Social justice-informed criticism (narrative accountability tradition) where depiction without clear critique risks normalizing harm AND transgressive fiction tradition (Palahniuk, Burroughs, Ellis) where fiction should be free to depict the monstrous without moralizing, and readers should distinguish character views from authorial views -- these are genuinely opposed schools of thought.
**Score 0**: The narrative presents prejudiced, violent, or exploitative behavior through a lens that rewards, romanticizes, or normalizes it. Plot events confirm the prejudiced character's worldview. There are no "hints" (Achebe's term) or alternative frameworks suggesting the author recognizes the depicted behavior as problematic. The implied author's perspective is indistinguishable from the bigoted character's perspective.
**Score 5**: The work maintains clear separation between what is depicted and what is endorsed, through any combination of: narrative consequences, contrasting perspectives, ironic framing, structural critique, or the moral complexity of the depicted situation. Even unreliable narrators operate within a framework where the implied author's perspective is distinguishable from the narrator's. Alternatively, in the transgressive tradition, the work is so clearly operating in a space of deliberate provocation that the depiction-endorsement question is part of the work's artistic project.
**Source**: https://electricliterature.com/moral-craft-issues-of-plot-and-prejudice/ and https://atitagain.substack.com/p/depiction-is-not-endorsement
**Notes**: Matthew Salesses (Electric Literature) identifies the "plot-endorsement problem": when narrative events validate a biased protagonist's worldview, the story endorses that bias regardless of stated intentions. He uses the example of an Irish fiddler whose racist views about an Asian violinist are confirmed by the plot's resolution. Chinua Achebe's critique of Conrad demonstrates how scarcity of characterization/dialogue for African characters combined with absence of alternative frameworks produces racist fiction despite possible authorial intent. The counter-tradition (J.D. Riley, Substack) argues that villains must exist and Nabokov writing Lolita does not endorse pedophilia.

### Authorial Endorsement Through Narrative Structure
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative's structural elements (plot resolution, character reward/punishment, framing) implicitly endorse particular ideological positions about marginalized groups.
**Assumed goal**: Social justice-informed criticism (structural analysis tradition, drawing on Salesses, Achebe) where narrative structure itself carries ideology -- formalist criticism may evaluate structure purely for narrative coherence, and transgressive fiction deliberately refuses clear moral framing.
**Score 0**: The plot resolves in ways that confirm stereotypes or prejudiced worldviews. Characters who hold bigoted views are rewarded by the narrative. Marginalized characters serve as "vessels" for the protagonist's development. The narrative's framing, at every level (primary narrator, implied author), shares the same uncritiqued prejudiced perspective.
**Score 5**: Narrative structure actively interrogates power and prejudice. Plot resolutions challenge rather than confirm stereotypes. Multiple narrative layers create ironic distance between character views and implied authorial perspective. Marginalized characters' experiences are depicted on their own terms, not instrumentalized for the protagonist's growth.
**Source**: https://electricliterature.com/moral-craft-issues-of-plot-and-prejudice/
**Notes**: Salesses distinguishes between the "real author" and "implied author" (following Chatman): a real author's non-racist beliefs don't guarantee non-racist fiction. Flannery O'Connor is cited as a counterexample who centralized prejudiced white characters while creating Black characters who were "not symbols defined in opposition to whiteness." The key test: does the implied author's perspective differ from the prejudiced character's?

### Trauma Depiction Ethics
**What it measures**: Whether traumatic experiences are depicted with psychological realism, respect for survivors, and narrative purpose, rather than for shock value or exploitation.
**Assumed goal**: Social justice-informed criticism (trauma-informed writing tradition, SFWA) where depiction of trauma must center healing and avoid gratuitous suffering -- the transgressive fiction tradition (Palahniuk's Haunted, Selby's Requiem for a Dream) deliberately subjects readers to extreme discomfort as artistic strategy, and literary realism may depict trauma unflinchingly without redemptive framing.
**Score 0**: Trauma is depicted as a temporary inconvenience that characters quickly recover from. Or: trauma is depicted with gratuitous, lingering detail that serves no purpose beyond shock. Characters are defined solely by their trauma with no other dimensions. Trauma is used as a plot device to motivate other characters (especially male characters motivated by women's suffering). The depiction sensationalizes or eroticizes violence. No evidence of research into psychological responses.
**Score 5**: Trauma depiction reflects research into genuine psychological responses. Characters experience ongoing, realistic consequences. Trauma is one facet of a character's identity, not their entire being. The narrative purpose of depicting trauma is clear (character development, thematic exploration, bearing witness). The work treats survivors' experiences with dignity. Content warnings are provided where appropriate.
**Source**: https://www.sfwa.org/2021/11/23/on-trauma-informed-writing/ and https://coloradoreview.colostate.edu/2017/10/ethics-and-aesthetics-trauma-narratives/
**Notes**: SFWA states that "stories depicting trauma as a temporary inconvenience reinforce the idea that ongoing trauma-responses are unusual or weak." The Colorado Review notes that "subject matter is insufficient" -- being "striking because of their subject matter" doesn't guarantee quality; the narrative craft matters more than what trauma is depicted. Parul Sehgal's "Case Against the Trauma Plot" argues that in contemporary literature, the "trauma plot" has become a dominant and limiting narrative structure.

### Trauma Exploitation / "Misery Porn" Avoidance
**What it measures**: Whether the work exploits suffering for entertainment, titillation, or emotional manipulation rather than engaging with it honestly.
**Assumed goal**: Social justice-informed criticism (anti-exploitation tradition) where depicting suffering must serve the depicted community rather than the consumer -- critics like Anne Rothe analyze how trauma has become "cultural capital" in a consumption economy, while defenders argue that unflinching depiction of suffering has literary value (Les Miserables tradition).
**Score 0**: The work dwells on suffering with prurient detail, treating it as spectacle. Pain exists primarily to create an emotional reaction in the reader rather than to illuminate the experience of those suffering. The work combines "moral outrage and titillation." Marginalized characters exist primarily to suffer. The narrative mines real-world tragedies for dramatic effect without understanding or respect.
**Score 5**: When suffering is depicted, it serves the story's thematic and character purposes. The work maintains the dignity of those who suffer. Painful experiences lead to genuine insight, change, or understanding (not necessarily redemption). The narrative does not linger on graphic details beyond what is necessary for understanding. The work resists treating pain as entertainment or consumption.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Misery_porn and https://lareviewofbooks.org/article/negative-space-close-reading-trauma-porn/
**Notes**: Anne Rothe's "Popular Trauma Culture" (2011) analyzes how trauma has become cultural capital that publishers, media, and readers all participate in commodifying. Parul Sehgal argues the "trauma plot" reduces characters to their worst experiences. "Poverty porn" is a related concept where depictions of poverty "exploit the poor's condition in order to generate the necessary sympathy."

### Violence Depiction: Purpose vs. Gratuitousness
**What it measures**: Whether violence in the narrative serves clear narrative purposes or exists primarily for shock value.
**Assumed goal**: Social justice-informed criticism (consequentialist tradition) where violence must show consequences and serve the depicted community -- the transgressive/horror tradition deliberately uses extreme violence for aesthetic effect, and literary realism may depict violence as banal or meaningless because that reflects reality.
**Score 0**: Violence is depicted in graphic, lingering detail that serves no narrative purpose beyond shocking the reader. There are no consequences for violence -- it is treated as spectacle. Violence is eroticized or glamorized. Characters who commit violence are rewarded by the plot. The narrative desensitizes rather than creates empathy. Violence against marginalized characters is disproportionate and instrumentalized.
**Score 5**: Every violent scene has a clear narrative purpose (advancing plot, revealing character, creating meaningful tension). The consequences of violence are depicted -- physical, psychological, social. Violence is neither glamorized nor gratuitous. The human cost is visible. If violence is extreme, the extremity serves the work's thematic project. Violence against marginalized characters is not disproportionate or instrumentalized.
**Source**: https://www.sistersincrimecentralvirginia.com/single-post/2017/08/27/gratuitous-vs-meaningful-violence-in-crime-fiction and https://www.inkitt.com/writersblog/the-point-of-violence-in-fiction-writing/
**Notes**: Don Winslow (TIME) argues that writers "must never be glib" about violence and that the ethical responsibility lies in "depicting the results of violence, the real human pain of the victims and their loved ones." The key distinction: gratuitous violence "promotes desensitization rather than empathy," while purposeful violence integrates with character development and thematic exploration.

### Sexual Violence Depiction Ethics
**What it measures**: Whether depictions of sexual violence are handled with appropriate gravity, consequences, and purpose rather than being eroticized or trivialized.
**Assumed goal**: Social justice-informed criticism (feminist critique tradition) where sexual violence depiction must center survivors and avoid titillation -- the transgressive tradition (e.g., Despentes' Baise-moi) deliberately refuses conventional framings of sexual violence, and some literary fiction depicts sexual violence as part of unflinching realism.
**Score 0**: Sexual violence is eroticized or depicted through a lens that conflates it with consensual sex. Victims are blamed or their suffering is minimized. Sexual violence is used as a convenient backstory motivation (especially "rape as backstory" for female characters to explain their toughness). The perpetrator's perspective is centered sympathetically. No consequences are depicted. Sexual violence against marginalized characters is disproportionate.
**Score 5**: Sexual violence, when depicted, is clearly framed as violence. The survivor's perspective and experience are centered. Consequences (psychological, social, legal) are depicted realistically. The depiction serves clear narrative purpose beyond shock. The work distinguishes clearly between the act and its aftermath. Consent and its absence are depicted with nuance and clarity.
**Source**: https://www.thefandomentals.com/ethics-sexual-violence-media/ and https://electricliterature.com/who-gets-to-write-about-sexual-abuse-and-what-do-we-let-them-say/
**Notes**: Critics challenge the notion that sexual violence depiction is "inevitable," arguing that authorial choices to include it are deliberate. Recent research shows schools in young adult romance novels are portrayed as "failing girls, treating sexual harassment as misunderstandings." Some representations (e.g., I May Destroy You) are praised for being "carried out from a feminist perspective, far from stereotypes."

### Sex Scene Purpose and Ethics
**What it measures**: Whether sex scenes serve narrative/character functions or exist purely as titillation, and whether they handle consent and power dynamics responsibly.
**Assumed goal**: Social justice-informed criticism (feminist/consent-aware tradition) where sex scenes must handle consent explicitly and avoid romanticizing coercion -- the romance genre tradition may use fantasy scenarios involving power imbalances as legitimate fantasy, and literary fiction may depict problematic sexual dynamics to critique them.
**Score 0**: Sex scenes exist purely for titillation with no character or narrative purpose. Consent is absent or ambiguous and this is romanticized. Power imbalances (boss/employee, teacher/student) are eroticized without acknowledgment. Characters' bodily responses are conflated with consent. The "she said no but meant yes" dynamic is present. Non-consensual encounters are framed as romantic.
**Score 5**: Sex scenes reveal character, develop relationships, or advance themes. Consent is modeled clearly (even in "dark romance," the framing makes clear what is fantasy vs. endorsement). Power dynamics are acknowledged when present. Characters have agency. The work distinguishes between depicting problematic dynamics and endorsing them. Sex serves the story rather than existing as spectacle.
**Source**: https://jerichowriters.com/complete-guide-to-writing-sex-in-fiction/ and https://longriverreview.com/blog/2024/consent-and-the-romance-genre-why-isnt-there-more-of-it/
**Notes**: Jericho Writers argues sex scenes should reveal character and "move the story on"; if the story would be equally effective without the scene, it may be gratuitous. The romance genre has specific ongoing debates about consent depiction, with critics noting that "just because someone accepts what is happening or has a bodily response does not mean that they consent."

### Gender Representation Depth
**What it measures**: Whether female and non-binary characters are fully realized individuals with agency, complexity, and independent arcs, or are reduced to supporting functions for male characters.
**Assumed goal**: Feminist literary criticism (post-second-wave tradition) where female characters must have agency, depth, and arcs independent of male characters -- traditional genre fiction may use archetypal roles for all characters regardless of gender, and some literary traditions deliberately interrogate gender roles through their violation.
**Score 0**: Female characters exist only in relation to male characters (love interests, mothers, victims). Women's achievements are attributed to their relationships with men rather than their own initiative. Female characters lack independent motivations, goals, or arcs. The "strong female character" trap: women are physically powerful but emotionally flat, rejecting femininity as a synonym for weakness. Women are fridged (killed, maimed, or assaulted to motivate male characters). Women serve as Manic Pixie Dream Girls -- quirky love interests with no inner life whose purpose is transforming the male protagonist.
**Score 5**: Female characters possess the same depth, complexity, and range as male characters. Women have independent arcs, motivations, and agency. The work passes the Bechdel test (two named women discuss something other than a man) AND the Mako Mori test (a female character has her own arc not supporting a male character's arc). Femininity is not equated with weakness. Women's strengths include emotional depth, flaws, realistic motivations -- not just physical prowess.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bechdel_test and https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mako_Mori_test and https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_refrigerators and https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manic_Pixie_Dream_Girl
**Notes**: Research (PMC 11260095) shows female characters are "persistently portrayed as more passive, especially by male authors" -- male and female characters are responsible for 53.6% and 46.4% of actions in cross-gender relationships. Nathan Rabin (who coined "Manic Pixie Dream Girl") later acknowledged the term itself became weaponized against complex female characters. The "strong female character" critique argues that "a strong character has strong characterisation. They are flawed, complex, varied, fallible, and realistic."

### LGBTQ+ Representation Quality
**What it measures**: Whether LGBTQ+ characters are depicted as multidimensional people whose identity is one facet of their characterization, or are reduced to stereotypes, tragedy, or decoration.
**Assumed goal**: Social justice-informed criticism (GLAAD/Vito Russo tradition) where LGBTQ+ characters must be multidimensional, plot-relevant, and not defined solely by identity -- the queer transgressive tradition (Burroughs, Dennis Cooper, Virginie Despentes) may deliberately use extreme or stereotypical depictions for subversive purposes, and literary realism may depict homophobia/transphobia as part of unflinching social portrayal.
**Score 0**: LGBTQ+ characters are defined solely by their sexuality or gender identity. They exist only to "provide colorful commentary, paint urban authenticity, or set up a punchline" (GLAAD). Queer characters are disproportionately killed (Bury Your Gays) especially after moments of happiness. LGBTQ+ characters exist to educate or transform straight characters (Magical Queer). Queer identity is coded through villain traits without explicit acknowledgment (queer coding as villainy). Queerbaiting: hints at queerness without commitment.
**Score 5**: LGBTQ+ characters pass the Vito Russo Test: (1) identifiably LGBTQ+, (2) not solely defined by orientation/identity, (3) tied to the plot such that removal would significantly affect the story, (4) story is not outwardly offensive with well-known tropes. Queer characters experience joy, success, and complexity alongside challenges. Their arcs are their own, not instrumentalized for straight characters' development. If the work depicts homophobia/transphobia, it does so with clear critique.
**Source**: https://glaad.org/sri/2025/vito-russo-test/ and https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bury_your_gays and https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Queer_coding
**Notes**: Statistics from Wikipedia's Bury Your Gays entry: of 1,779 scripted US TV series (1976-2016), among shows featuring lesbian/bisexual characters, 35% killed off such characters while only 16% provided happy endings. The Lexa Pledge (2016) commits writers to providing meaningful LGBTQ storylines. Dan Levy (Schitt's Creek) and ND Stevenson (She-Ra) are cited as creators who deliberately avoided tragic queer romance. Historical context: the Hays Code forced queer coding as the only available representation, establishing the villain-as-queer association.

### Disability Representation Quality
**What it measures**: Whether disabled characters are depicted as full human beings with agency, or reduced to ableist tropes.
**Assumed goal**: Disability justice criticism where disabled characters must have agency, inner lives, and stories not reducible to their disability -- the literary realism tradition may depict societal ableism unflinchingly, and some disability narratives written by disabled authors may deliberately center disability experience in ways that sensitivity criticism might flag.
**Score 0**: Disabled characters are "inspiration porn" -- existing to inspire able-bodied characters/readers. Disability is a plot device for other characters' development (the Me Before You problem). The "magical cure" resolves disability as if it were a narrative problem. Disability is used as metaphor for moral failing. Mental illness equals violence. Physical disability is played for comedy. Disabled characters are one-dimensional -- defined entirely by their disability with no other traits.
**Score 5**: Disabled characters have full inner lives, goals, and arcs independent of their disability. Disability is one facet of their characterization. Assistive devices and accommodations are depicted matter-of-factly. The narrative does not treat disability as a problem requiring resolution. If disability is central to the story, it is depicted with accuracy and respect. Characters with disabilities show within-group diversity (intersecting with race, gender, sexuality). The work resists both "supercrip" narratives and pity narratives.
**Source**: https://bookriot.com/ableist-tropes-in-fiction/ and https://mythcreants.com/blog/five-signs-your-story-is-ableist/
**Notes**: Mythcreants identifies five signs of ableism: bodies/disabilities used for jokes, mental illness portrayed as threatening, disability as plot device, magical cure, and disability as metaphor. Book Riot notes that only 0.5% of TV characters with disabilities have speaking roles despite 20% of Americans being disabled. The disability community emphasizes that "the magical cure trope implies that we're inferior and denies an important part of our identities."

### Mental Illness Representation
**What it measures**: Whether mental illness is depicted with clinical accuracy, humanity, and narrative purpose rather than through stigmatizing tropes.
**Assumed goal**: Social justice-informed criticism (destigmatization tradition) where mental illness depiction must be accurate and avoid reinforcing stigma -- the horror tradition may use madness as a narrative device, and literary fiction may depict distorted perception as artistic technique (unreliable narrator).
**Score 0**: Mental illness equals violence (only ~4% of US violence is attributable to mental illness). Villains are scary specifically because of their mental illness. The "tortured artist" trope romanticizes unmedicated mental illness as source of creativity. Suicide attempts are used as inciting events with sensationalized treatment that risks glorifying suicide. Characters "overcome" mental illness through willpower alone, implying it's their fault. Mental illness is played for comedy. OCD, PTSD, bipolar disorder, etc. are used as casual adjectives divorced from clinical reality.
**Score 5**: Symptoms are generally consistent with the diagnosis. Characters with mental illness are not reduced to their condition. Treatment (therapy, medication) is depicted without stigma. The narrative acknowledges that mental illness is a chronic condition, not a temporary plot obstacle. Characters maintain autonomy and personhood. If mental illness drives conflict, it does so with accuracy and empathy rather than sensationalism.
**Source**: https://selinajeckert.com/2019/01/18/10-mental-health-tropes-i-hate-in-fiction/comment-page-1/ and https://www.rabbitwitharedpen.com/blog/mental-illness-in-fiction-1
**Notes**: Psychology Today notes that the "super OCD" trope trivializes the condition. Selina Eckert identifies 10 harmful mental health tropes including the "split personality killer," "beautiful sadness" depression, and the idea that love cures mental illness. SFWA guidelines emphasize working with sensitivity readers who have "personal or professional experience" with the conditions being depicted.

### Racial Stereotype Patterns (Specific Named Tropes)
**What it measures**: Whether the work reproduces specific, named harmful racial tropes that have been extensively documented in criticism.
**Assumed goal**: Social justice-informed criticism (anti-racist literary analysis tradition) where specific named tropes indicate failure of representation -- literary realism may depict characters who hold these views as part of social critique, and satirical fiction may invoke these tropes to deconstruct them.
**Score 0**: The work contains one or more of the following uninterrogated tropes: Magical Negro (Black character with mystical powers existing to help white protagonist, with no personal history or arc); White Savior (white character rescuing people of color who cannot help themselves); Noble Savage (indigenous character who is pure/spiritual by virtue of being "uncivilized"); Model Minority (Asian character who is studious, quiet, and successful through hard work); Mammy/Jezebel/Sapphire (controlling images of Black women as nurturing servant, hypersexual temptress, or angry emasculator); Orientalist depictions (Eastern cultures as exotic, mysterious, backward, or threatening).
**Score 5**: The work avoids or actively deconstructs these tropes. If they appear, they are subjected to narrative critique. Characters from marginalized racial groups have complex, individualized characterization that resists reduction to known stereotypes. The work demonstrates awareness of these tropes' histories and deliberately works against them.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magical_Negro and https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_savior and https://scholarsarchive.byu.edu/criterion/vol11/iss1/5/
**Notes**: Spike Lee popularized "Magical Negro" in 2001, criticizing The Green Mile and The Legend of Bagger Vance. The White Savior is described as a "modern-day version of 'The White Man's Burden.'" The Mammy/Jezebel/Sapphire triad functions as "controlling images" that "justify exploitation, regulate sexuality, and silence resistance." Edward Said's Orientalism (1978) established the framework for understanding how Western fiction systematically constructed the East as inferior.

### Indigenous Representation Quality
**What it measures**: Whether indigenous peoples are depicted as contemporary, complex communities or reduced to historical stereotypes.
**Assumed goal**: Indigenous sovereignty-informed criticism where indigenous peoples must be depicted as contemporary, diverse, self-determining communities -- the Western genre tradition historically depicts indigenous peoples as obstacles or props, and some literary traditions use "indigenous wisdom" as spiritual metaphor.
**Score 0**: Indigenous characters are relegated to the past (ignoring that "#WeAreStillHere"). They speak in "Tonto-speak" (broken English implying inferiority). Non-Native characters abandon modern life to live with indigenous people and "appear more Native than the Natives" (Mohican Syndrome). Indigenous peoples are depicted as needing white rescue (White Savior). Indigenous cultures are treated as monolithic rather than reflecting the diversity of hundreds of distinct nations. Sacred practices are used as fantasy elements.
**Score 5**: Indigenous characters exist in the present as well as the past. They speak normally. Their cultural practices are specific to actual nations rather than generalized "Native" culture. Indigenous characters have agency and self-determination. The work recognizes internal diversity among indigenous communities. If historical settings are used, indigenous perspectives are centered rather than filtered through colonial viewpoints.
**Source**: https://indigenousreadsrising.com/resources-for-teachers-librarians-and-readers-tropes-and-stereotypes/ and https://www.sapiens.org/culture/native-american-stereotypes/
**Notes**: Indigenous Reads Rising identifies four prevalent tropes: Relegation to the Past, Tonto-Speak, Mohican Syndrome, and White Savior. The resource states that "there is no such thing as a good stereotype" -- even the Noble Savage and Ecological Indian are harmful. J.K. Rowling's "History of Magic in North America" is cited as an example of equating Native traditions with fantasy.

### Intersectional Representation
**What it measures**: Whether the work recognizes that characters hold multiple, intersecting identities rather than treating identity along a single axis.
**Assumed goal**: Intersectional feminist criticism (Crenshaw tradition) where characters must reflect the complexity of intersecting identities -- literary traditions that focus on universal human experience may deliberately de-emphasize identity categories, and some critics argue intersectional checklisting can become reductive.
**Score 0**: Characters are defined by a single identity axis (a character is "the Black one" or "the gay one" with no further complexity). Intersecting oppressions are invisible -- a Black woman's experience is treated as interchangeable with a Black man's or a white woman's. Specific intersectional tropes are present: the "fiery Latina," "submissive Asian woman," "angry Black woman." Marginalized characters with multiple identities are absent entirely.
**Score 5**: Characters reflect realistic combinations of identities that interact in complex ways. A Black trans woman's experience is not reducible to "Black" + "trans" + "woman." The work recognizes that intersection of identities creates unique experiences. Within-group diversity is visible. Characters are not forced to represent an entire identity category.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intersectionality and https://library.fiveable.me/literary-theory-criticism/unit-6/intersectionality/study-guide/xltCfBdv6UTWOLOW
**Notes**: Crenshaw (1989) argued that "the intersection of multiple identities creates a unique experience that cannot be understood by examining a single identity in isolation." bell hooks notes women of color are frequently reduced to compound stereotypes ("fiery Latina," "submissive Asian," "angry Black woman") that "erase the full complexity of their intersecting identities." The disability community particularly emphasizes the need for representation of "disabled people of color, queer and trans people with disabilities."

### Power Dynamic Awareness
**What it measures**: Whether the work demonstrates awareness of how power operates along axes of race, gender, class, sexuality, ability, and other identity categories, and whether it reproduces or interrogates those dynamics.
**Assumed goal**: Critical theory-informed criticism (Marxist, feminist, postcolonial, Foucauldian traditions) where fiction must acknowledge and interrogate structural power -- libertarian literary criticism may evaluate fiction purely on individual character merit, and commercial fiction may avoid explicit engagement with power for marketability.
**Score 0**: The narrative naturalizes existing power hierarchies without examination. Characters from dominant groups are centered without acknowledgment. Systemic oppression is invisible -- individual characters succeed or fail based solely on personal merit. The work reproduces colonial, patriarchal, or ableist perspectives as default "reality." Characters from marginalized groups who resist oppression are depicted as irrational or ungrateful.
**Score 5**: The work demonstrates awareness of structural power dynamics without reducing characters to political positions. Systems of oppression are visible in the story's world when relevant. Characters navigate power structures in realistic ways. The narrative does not naturalize inequality. When power dynamics are central, the work reflects understanding of how multiple systems (racism, sexism, classism, ableism) interact.
**Source**: https://www.byarcadia.org/post/literary-theory-101-power-structures-and-cultural-studies-in-literary-analysis and https://www.numberanalytics.com/blog/ideology-power-dynamics-literary-works
**Notes**: Foucault's analysis emphasizes that power is relational and strategic, not simply possessed. Postcolonial criticism (after Said) highlights how fiction has historically "unwittingly but systematically helped to gain consent for imperialist policies." Feminist criticism asks "who has the power to act (agency)" and whether all characters have autonomy.

### Moral Imagination and Ethical Complexity
**What it measures**: Whether the work exercises genuine moral imagination -- prompting readers to see moral situations from new perspectives -- or reduces ethics to simple binaries.
**Assumed goal**: Philosophical/literary ethics tradition (Eileen John, Mark Johnson, Martha Nussbaum) where fiction's purpose includes expanding moral perception and empathetic capacity -- genre fiction that uses clear good/evil binaries serves different purposes (escapism, moral clarity), and some postmodern fiction deliberately refuses moral positioning.
**Score 0**: Moral situations are depicted in black-and-white terms with clear heroes and villains. Characters face no genuine ethical dilemmas. The work confirms readers' existing moral assumptions rather than challenging them. Ethical complexity is avoided in favor of easy answers. Characters from marginalized groups are either saints or villains with no moral nuance.
**Score 5**: The work exercises what Eileen John calls "accessible openness" -- prompting readers to see familiar moral situations anew. Characters face genuine ethical dilemmas without easy answers. The work activates readers' "own role as sustainer and maker of meaning." Moral complexity extends to all characters regardless of identity. The work prompts questions about complicity, responsibility, and the difficulty of moral life.
**Source**: https://junkyardofthemind.com/blog/2020/9/27/moral-imagination-fiction-and-meaning-making and https://www.britannica.com/topic/moral-imagination
**Notes**: Eileen John characterizes moral imagination as "activity that goes beyond argument and direct experience, somehow concerns the moral dimension of life." Mark Johnson argues that moral understanding relies on "metaphorical concepts embedded in larger narratives." The key criterion: fiction should be "more demanding of a reader's moral resources" to qualify as genuinely morally imaginative.

### #OwnVoices / Authorial Identity Considerations
**What it measures**: Whether the author's relationship to the identity being depicted is acknowledged and whether this relationship affects the quality of the depiction.
**Assumed goal**: Social justice-informed criticism (#OwnVoices tradition, now retired as a formal label) where marginalized communities' own stories should be prioritized -- universalist literary tradition holds that any author can write any character with sufficient skill and research, and the retirement of #OwnVoices as a label reflects concern that the framework was being used to gatekeep and invade privacy.
**Score 0**: The work depicts marginalized experiences with obvious outsider ignorance. The author has not engaged with the communities being depicted. The work takes up narrative space that could amplify marginalized voices without adding genuine insight. The work profits from marginalized experiences while reproducing harmful stereotypes.
**Score 5**: The work reflects genuine understanding of the experiences being depicted, whether through lived experience or thorough, respectful engagement. If the author writes outside their experience, evidence of deep research, community consultation, and sensitivity reading is apparent in the quality of the depiction. The work enriches rather than diminishes understanding of the depicted experience. If own-voices, the author's lived experience brings authenticity without being reduced to autobiographical assumption.
**Source**: https://bookriot.com/what-happened-to-the-own-voices-label/ and https://www.pragmaticmom.com/2018/06/ownvoices-controversy/
**Notes**: Corinne Duyvis created #OwnVoices in 2015 to highlight books where authors share marginalized identities with protagonists. We Need Diverse Books retired the term in 2021 because it was "misused to gatekeep identities and invade authors' privacy." The debate reflects tension between wanting to amplify marginalized voices and avoiding limiting what any author (including marginalized ones) can write. The APA Blog (Miller) asks whether the author's use is "constructive and creative or destructive."

### Unconscious/Implicit Bias in Characterization
**What it measures**: Whether the work reflects the author's unexamined implicit biases in how characters are described, developed, and treated by the narrative.
**Assumed goal**: Social justice-informed criticism (implicit bias awareness tradition) where unconscious bias must be identified and corrected -- formalist criticism evaluates character based on internal coherence regardless of real-world bias, and some argue that "bias hunting" in fiction can become reductive.
**Score 0**: Characters conform to the author's unconscious assumptions about their demographic. Women are described by appearance first; men by action. Characters of color are described through food metaphors ("chocolate skin," "caramel complexion"). Default characters (those without specified identity) are implicitly white, male, straight, able-bodied. Characters from marginalized groups are defined by their marginalization. The author's assumptions about "normal" are visible in who gets backstory and who doesn't.
**Score 5**: The work reflects self-awareness about implicit bias. All characters receive comparable depth of description and development regardless of demographic. Physical descriptions avoid racialized food metaphors and gendered patterns. Default assumptions are challenged -- not every unspecified character reads as white/male/straight. The work has been reviewed for unconscious bias (through sensitivity reading or self-reflection).
**Source**: https://www.writermag.com/improve-your-writing/fiction/unconscious-bias/ and https://writingwithcolor.com/
**Notes**: The Writer magazine notes that "when writers fall prey to unconscious bias, we can only see the characters we expect to see." Literary fiction's advantage: it "disrupts reader expectations, undermining prejudices and stereotypes." Writing With Color specifically addresses how food-based skin tone descriptions reduce characters to consumable objects.

### Content Warning / Reader Safety
**What it measures**: Whether the work provides appropriate signals about potentially harmful content, enabling informed reader consent.
**Assumed goal**: Trauma-informed writing tradition where readers deserve informed consent about potentially triggering content -- literary purists argue that content warnings constitute spoilers and that fiction should be encountered without preconceptions, and some argue this creates a culture of avoidance rather than engagement.
**Score 0**: The work contains graphic depictions of sexual violence, self-harm, child abuse, or other potentially triggering content with no warning. Marketing and packaging give no indication of the content's nature. Readers are ambushed by extreme content.
**Score 5**: The work provides appropriate content warnings without spoiling the narrative. Potentially triggering content is signaled through genre conventions, packaging, author notes, or explicit content warnings. The reader can make an informed decision about whether to engage. The work respects readers' right to consent to their reading experience.
**Source**: https://www.pagesandplatforms.com/blog/content-warnings and https://www.ooliganpress.com/content-warnings-who-should-use-them-and-why-they-really-matter/
**Notes**: The principle: "at their core, content warnings are about consent -- about giving readers all the information they need to decide whether a book is for them." The counter-argument: "an author can't be responsible for the triggers of a reader they've never met." The compromise position: provide warnings while respecting that fiction has always depicted difficult content.

### Harm Prevention (Do No Harm Standard)
**What it measures**: Whether the work avoids content that could actively cause real-world harm through imitation, instruction, or normalization.
**Assumed goal**: Ethics-of-fiction tradition (Ron Hansen, Markkula Center) where fiction has a duty to avoid instructing readers in harmful activities -- the free expression tradition holds that fiction should never be constrained by anticipated reader response, and transgressive fiction deliberately courts the possibility of offense.
**Score 0**: The work provides detailed instructions for violence, self-harm, or other dangerous activities. Real living persons are defamed. The work normalizes abuse patterns in ways likely to be imitated by vulnerable readers (especially in YA). Suicide is depicted in ways that violate media guidelines on contagion (detailed method, romanticization).
**Score 5**: The work takes care not to function as an instruction manual for harm. Real persons are depicted fairly or fictionalized sufficiently. When depicting dangerous behavior, the narrative context makes clear the consequences. Depictions of suicide follow established media guidelines. The work exercises what Ron Hansen calls motivation from "truth and beauty" rather than "vengeance and cruelty."
**Source**: https://www.scu.edu/ethics/focus-areas/more-focus-areas/resources/the-ethics-of-fiction-writing/
**Notes**: Ron Hansen (Markkula Center) identifies the golden rule as foundational: "do unto others as you would have them do unto you." Most major publishers require a guarantee that manuscript contents will not cause injury. The tension: fiction necessarily depicts dangerous, immoral, and harmful behavior -- the question is whether the depiction serves artistic purposes or functions primarily as harm instruction.

### Consent Modeling in Relationships
**What it measures**: Whether romantic and sexual relationships in the work model consent or normalize coercion, and whether the narrative framework makes the distinction clear.
**Assumed goal**: Feminist/consent-aware criticism where fiction should model or at least acknowledge consent dynamics -- the dark romance tradition deliberately depicts non-consensual scenarios as fantasy, and literary realism may depict coercive relationships as social critique.
**Score 0**: Romantic pursuit involves persistent pressure after refusal. Physical "no" signals (pushing away, freezing) are interpreted as "playing hard to get." Power imbalances (boss/employee, teacher/student, captor/captive) are romanticized without acknowledgment. "She said no but meant yes" is treated as romantic. Bodily responses are equated with consent. The narrative rewards coercive behavior with romantic success.
**Score 5**: Consent is present and visible in romantic/sexual interactions. When coercive dynamics exist, the narrative framework makes clear they are problematic (even if characters don't recognize this). Power imbalances are acknowledged. The work distinguishes between fantasy (clearly framed) and normalized behavior. Characters who respect boundaries are not punished for doing so.
**Source**: https://longriverreview.com/blog/2024/consent-and-the-romance-genre-why-isnt-there-more-of-it/ and https://www.feministbookclub.com/toxic-or-titillating-the-romance-tropes/
**Notes**: Long River Review notes "a lack of consent between characters in romance novels, whether it's sexual consent or consent in everyday life." The specific problem: "just because someone accepts what is happening or has a bodily response does not mean that they consent." Feminist Book Club identifies the persistent trope that women "like to be chased" as normalizing harassment.

### Orientalism Avoidance
**What it measures**: Whether the work reproduces Orientalist frameworks that construct Eastern/non-Western cultures as exotic, inferior, mysterious, or threatening.
**Assumed goal**: Postcolonial criticism (Said tradition) where Western fiction must avoid constructing the East through imperial lenses -- some argue Said's framework is itself reductive and that fiction can engage with cultural difference without falling into Orientalism, and fantasy/science fiction may create fictional cultures inspired by real ones.
**Score 0**: Non-Western cultures are depicted as monolithically exotic, mysterious, or backward. Eastern characters lack interiority and serve as backdrop for Western protagonists' journeys. Cultural details are exoticized (harems, snake charmers, mystical wisdom). Non-Western settings exist only as sites of danger or spiritual revelation for Western characters. The work reproduces colonial knowledge frameworks.
**Score 5**: Non-Western cultures are depicted with specificity, internal diversity, and complexity. Characters from these cultures have full interiority and agency. Cultural elements are contextualized rather than exoticized. Western perspectives on non-Western cultures are not treated as neutral or objective. The work demonstrates awareness of how colonial frameworks have shaped Western perceptions.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientalism_(book))
**Notes**: Said (1978) argues that "Orientalism, in the sense of the Western scholarship about the Eastern world, is inextricably tied to the imperialist societies that produced it." The greatest intellectual impact of Orientalism was on literary theory, cultural studies, history, and human geography, generating the field of postcolonial studies.

### Black Women's Representation (Controlling Images)
**What it measures**: Whether Black women characters are depicted beyond the "controlling images" (Patricia Hill Collins' term) that have historically regulated their representation.
**Assumed goal**: Black feminist criticism (Collins, hooks tradition) where Black women's representation must transcend the Mammy/Jezebel/Sapphire triad -- some literary traditions reclaim or subvert these images (e.g., Jesmyn Ward's reclamation of the Jezebel in Salvage the Bones), and others argue the framework can itself become reductive.
**Score 0**: Black women characters map onto one or more controlling images: Mammy (nurturing servant more devoted to white charges than her own family), Jezebel (hypersexualized temptress), Sapphire (angry, emasculating). Black women exist to serve, seduce, or scold. Their experiences are not centered on their own terms. Intersecting oppressions of race and gender are invisible.
**Score 5**: Black women characters transcend or deliberately deconstruct controlling images. They have full inner lives, complex motivations, and arcs centered on their own experiences. If elements of these archetypes appear, they are interrogated rather than reproduced uncritically. The work acknowledges the intersection of race and gender in Black women's experience. Multiple Black women characters show within-group diversity.
**Source**: https://scholarsarchive.byu.edu/criterion/vol11/iss1/5/ and https://drcarolynwest.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/06/2008-Mammy-Jezebel-Sapphire-and-their-homegirls-Developing-an-oppositional-gaze-toward-the-images-of-Black-women.pdf
**Notes**: West (2008) documents how these archetypes function as "controlling images" that "justify exploitation, regulate sexuality, and silence resistance." Ward's work is cited as an example of reclaiming these images: showing "why Esch becomes sexually active and her value and humanity" rather than simply reproducing the Jezebel stereotype.

### Asian Representation (Model Minority and Beyond)
**What it measures**: Whether Asian characters are depicted beyond the model minority stereotype and other reductive tropes.
**Assumed goal**: Asian American criticism where Asian characters must reflect the diversity of 40+ ethnic groups rather than the monolithic "model minority" -- some literary traditions use Asian cultural traditions as aesthetic resource, and some Asian American authors deliberately engage with or subvert these stereotypes.
**Score 0**: Asian characters are studious, quiet, and successful through hard work (model minority). Asian women are submissive or hypersexualized ("lotus blossom" or "dragon lady"). Asian men are desexualized or cast as martial arts experts. The 40+ ethnic groups under "Asian" are treated as monolithic. Asian characters exist primarily in STEM roles. Cultural specificity is absent -- "Asian" is treated as a single culture.
**Score 5**: Asian characters are complex individuals whose ethnicity is specific (Chinese, Korean, Vietnamese, etc., not generically "Asian"). They have full range of personality traits, professions, and relationship dynamics. The model minority myth is not reproduced. Both Asian men and women have full romantic/sexual agency. Cultural specificity reflects research into specific communities.
**Source**: https://www.pewresearch.org/race-and-ethnicity/2023/11/30/asian-americans-and-the-model-minority-stereotype/ and https://www.readinmag.com/fantasyblog/diversity-vs-tokenism
**Notes**: Pew Research documents that the model minority myth "erases disparities between the 40+ ethnic groups that fall under the 'Asian American' umbrella." The myth is "anti-Black at its core, weaponized against Black and brown communities." Readin' Magazine cites Cho Chang in Harry Potter as tokenism: "Asian character with generic naming, written as intelligent but submissive."

### Transgressive Fiction Ethics (Deliberate Provocation)
**What it measures**: Whether works that deliberately transgress moral norms do so with artistic purpose and self-awareness, or whether transgression is purely gratuitous.
**Assumed goal**: Transgressive fiction criticism (Burroughs/Palahniuk/Ellis/de Sade tradition) where fiction should be free to explore the most extreme human experiences without moral constraint, providing "incisive social commentary" -- the sensitivity reading tradition would evaluate such works by their potential for harm, and mainstream literary criticism may question whether shock constitutes artistic merit.
**Score 0**: The work transgresses norms purely for shock value with no artistic purpose. Extreme content (violence, sexual deviance, taboo violation) is present but serves no function beyond provocation. The work mistakes offensiveness for profundity. Transgression targets already-marginalized groups (punching down). The work provides no framework for processing its extreme content.
**Score 5**: Transgression serves clear artistic purposes: social critique, exploration of human extremity, deconstruction of moral complacency. The work, like Palahniuk's definition, uses antisocial behavior to "interrogate sociopolitical norms." Extreme content functions within a coherent artistic vision. The transgression challenges the comfortable rather than further marginalizing the vulnerable. The work is self-aware about its provocations.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transgressive_fiction
**Notes**: Palahniuk defined transgressive fiction as "fiction in which characters misbehave and act badly as a way of either feeling alive, or as political acts of civil disobedience." Key tension: Burroughs is "heralded as a moral writer actually promoting western ideals when safely placed in a tradition, and the transgressive is defanged." The genre differs from exploitation in that protagonists "pursue means to better themselves and their surroundings -- albeit unusual and extreme ones."


## Sentinels Found

### Uniform Default Identity
**Detection**: All or nearly all characters without explicit identity markers read as white, male, straight, cisgender, and able-bodied. No character of any marginalized identity appears unless specifically prompted.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs default to majority-representation training data. Research shows AI-generated fiction defaults to "white, straight, and gender-conforming characters -- even when prompts never mentioned identity." White names dominate 71-84% of generated output. This represents the model's statistical prior, not intentional characterization.
**Design space cost**: Forecloses the vast majority of human experience and perspective. Creates fiction that reflects only a narrow demographic slice. Reproduces the historical underrepresentation that marginalized communities have fought against.
**Source**: https://www.earth.com/news/ai-writing-tools-have-hidden-bias-in-character-creation/ and https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/00043125.2024.2330340

### Magical Negro / Mystical Minority Pattern
**Detection**: A character from a marginalized group (especially Black) possesses vague mystical wisdom or powers, has no personal history or independent arc, exists solely to guide or save a white/dominant-group protagonist, and may sacrifice themselves for the protagonist.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs trained on decades of fiction where this trope is extremely common will reproduce it as a default pattern for cross-racial character dynamics. The pattern is statistically prevalent in training data and requires no creative thought to generate.
**Design space cost**: Reduces marginalized characters to plot devices. Denies them agency and full humanity. Reproduces one of the most documented and criticized racial tropes in American fiction.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magical_Negro

### Bury Your Gays
**Detection**: The only LGBTQ+ character dies, often immediately after a moment of romantic happiness or self-acceptance; or LGBTQ+ characters die at disproportionate rates compared to straight characters.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Training data contains decades of fiction produced under the Hays Code and its cultural aftermath, where queer characters were systematically killed. The statistical pattern in the training data skews toward tragic queer outcomes. An LLM generating "what comes next" for a queer character will gravitate toward tragedy because that's what its training data shows.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates the possibility of joyful, complex queer narratives. Reinforces the association between queerness and death/suffering. Removes a broad swath of human experience from positive fictional depiction.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bury_your_gays

### Fridging (Women's Suffering as Male Motivation)
**Detection**: A female character is killed, maimed, depowered, or sexually assaulted primarily to provide motivation, emotional development, or backstory for a male character. The female character has no independent arc or her arc exists only to intersect with the male character's.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: This is one of the most common plot structures in action, thriller, and superhero fiction -- exactly the kinds of genre fiction overrepresented in training data. An LLM generating conflict for a male protagonist will statistically gravitate toward harming female characters because this pattern appears thousands of times in the training data.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates female characters' own narrative potential. Reduces women to instruments of male character development. Forecloses the possibility of female characters driving their own arcs.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_refrigerators

### White Savior Narrative
**Detection**: A white character rescues, liberates, or morally redeems people of color who are depicted as unable to help themselves. The white character's journey of self-discovery is centered; the people of color are backdrop.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: This structure is overwhelmingly common in training data (The Help, Dangerous Minds, Freedom Writers, The Blind Side, Green Book, etc.). An LLM generating a cross-racial narrative will gravitate toward centering the white character because that's the statistically dominant pattern.
**Design space cost**: Denies people of color agency in their own liberation. Centers whiteness even in stories ostensibly about racial justice. Reproduces colonial power dynamics. Forecloses narratives where communities of color are their own agents of change.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_savior

### Monoculture Characters (Homogenized Speech and Behavior)
**Detection**: All characters regardless of background, culture, age, class, or region speak in the same register, use the same vocabulary, share the same cultural references, and react to situations in identical ways.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate text that clusters in a "shared region of narrative space" with less diversity than human-authored stories. Research shows AI "homogenizing speech patterns across different social contexts." The model's tendency toward its statistical mean erases the cultural, regional, and class-specific variations that distinguish real human voices.
**Design space cost**: Destroys the possibility of authentic cultural voice. Makes all characters interchangeable regardless of their stated backgrounds. Eliminates the texture that comes from genuine diversity of experience and expression.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.03136 and https://janefriedman.com/how-creativity-survives-in-an-ai-monoculture/

### Trauma as Temporary Inconvenience
**Detection**: A character experiences severe trauma (assault, combat, loss of a loved one, torture) and returns to baseline functioning within one or two scenes with no lasting psychological effects.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs are trained to generate narrative resolution. The statistical pattern in training data is for characters to "overcome" adversity and return to the plot. The model lacks understanding of actual psychological responses to trauma and defaults to quick recovery because that's what keeps the plot moving.
**Design space cost**: Erases the reality of trauma's lasting effects. Reinforces the idea that ongoing trauma responses are "unusual or weak" (SFWA). Denies readers who have experienced trauma the validation of seeing realistic depictions.
**Source**: https://www.sfwa.org/2021/11/23/on-trauma-informed-writing/

### Diversity as Costume (Surface-Level Identity)
**Detection**: A character's marginalized identity is mentioned in their introduction (name, physical description) but never affects their experience, worldview, relationships, or narrative arc. Their identity is purely cosmetic.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs can generate diverse character descriptions (names, skin tones) because these are surface-level text features. But integrating identity into lived experience, worldview, and narrative consequence requires understanding that statistical text generation cannot provide. The result is characters who are "diverse" in the character sheet but whose actual stories are indistinguishable from any other character's.
**Design space cost**: Creates an illusion of diversity that actually erases the experiences it claims to represent. A character whose Blackness never affects their life in a recognizably American setting is not representation -- it's decoration.
**Source**: https://www.readinmag.com/fantasyblog/diversity-vs-tokenism and https://medium.com/@alessiagebauer/moving-beyond-tokenism-the-importance-of-treatment-diversity-in-media-157da21d5714

### Sanitized Moral Universe
**Detection**: All moral choices are clearly binary (obviously right vs. obviously wrong). No character faces a genuine ethical dilemma. Prejudice, systemic oppression, and moral complexity are absent or resolved within a single scene. Every conflict has a clean, positive resolution.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs, particularly those trained with RLHF, are biased toward agreeable, positive, and resolution-oriented output. "AI-generated stories cluster in a shared region of narrative space" favoring "stability over change" and "tidy, single-track plots." The model's sycophantic tendencies extend to narrative: it generates the resolution users "want" rather than the complexity the story needs.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates moral imagination. Fiction's unique power is to place readers in situations of genuine moral difficulty. A sanitized moral universe teaches nothing and challenges no one. It forecloses the entire tradition of morally complex fiction from Dostoevsky to Morrison.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.03136 and https://junkyardofthemind.com/blog/2020/9/27/moral-imagination-fiction-and-meaning-making

### Manic Pixie Dream Girl Pattern
**Detection**: A female character exists primarily to be quirky, eccentric, and emotionally transformative for a male protagonist. She has no independent goals, history, or inner life. She appears to teach the male character to "live" and then disappears from the narrative once her function is served.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: The MPDG pattern is extremely common in romantic comedies and coming-of-age stories in the training data (Garden State, Elizabethtown, 500 Days of Summer). An LLM generating a male protagonist's love interest will gravitate toward this pattern because it appears frequently and provides a clear narrative function.
**Design space cost**: Reduces female characters to instruments of male transformation. Eliminates the possibility of reciprocal romantic relationships where both characters grow. Reinforces the idea that women's purpose is to serve male emotional development.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manic_Pixie_Dream_Girl

### Queer Coding as Villainy
**Detection**: Villains or antagonists display stereotypically queer-associated traits (flamboyance, gender nonconformity, aesthetic refinement, effeminacy) while no explicitly LGBTQ+ characters appear. Queerness is implied through negative coding rather than explicit, positive representation.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Decades of Hays Code-era fiction coded villains as queer because positive queer representation was banned. This pattern is deeply embedded in training data, especially Disney villains (Ursula, Scar, Jafar, Captain Hook) and Bond villains. An LLM generating a villain will draw on these patterns.
**Design space cost**: Perpetuates the association of queerness with evil. Denies positive LGBTQ+ representation. Reproduces a historical artifact of censorship as if it were natural characterization.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Queer_coding

### Noble Savage / Ecological Indian
**Detection**: Indigenous characters are depicted as inherently spiritual, environmentally wise, and simple. They exist in an idealized pre-modern state. They speak in aphorisms about nature. They have no modern concerns, technology, or complex political life.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: The Noble Savage is one of the oldest and most pervasive tropes in Western literature, from Rousseau through James Fenimore Cooper to contemporary media. It is massively overrepresented in training data compared to accurate indigenous representation. An LLM generating indigenous characters will gravitate toward this well-worn pattern.
**Design space cost**: Denies indigenous peoples modernity, complexity, and full humanity. Erases the reality of contemporary indigenous life. Forces indigenous characters into a "primitive" mold that serves non-indigenous fantasies about "natural" living.
**Source**: https://indigenousreadsrising.com/resources-for-teachers-librarians-and-readers-tropes-and-stereotypes/

### Rape as Backstory
**Detection**: A female character's entire motivation, personality, or "toughness" is explained by a past sexual assault that appears in her backstory. The assault is not explored on its own terms but functions purely as an origin story for her current characterization.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: "Rape as backstory" is one of the most common shortcuts in genre fiction for generating a "damaged but strong" female character. It appears with extreme frequency in training data for action, thriller, and urban fantasy genres. An LLM reaching for a female character's motivation will statistically encounter this pattern often.
**Design space cost**: Reduces sexual violence to a character-building exercise. Eliminates the vast range of experiences that shape women's lives. Instrumentalizes survivors' trauma. Creates a false equivalence between victimization and character depth.
**Source**: https://www.thefandomentals.com/ethics-sexual-violence-media/

### Model Minority Default for Asian Characters
**Detection**: Asian characters are automatically placed in STEM careers, depicted as studious and quiet, and their emotional/romantic lives are minimized or absent. No cultural specificity distinguishes different Asian ethnicities.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: The model minority stereotype is pervasive in American media training data. LLMs will reproduce it because it represents the statistical majority of Asian representation in their training corpus. The flattening of 40+ distinct ethnic groups into "Asian" reflects the training data's own lack of specificity.
**Design space cost**: Erases the diversity of Asian American experience. Reinforces a stereotype that was "anti-Black at its core" and designed to pit minorities against each other. Denies Asian characters emotional depth and romantic agency.
**Source**: https://www.pewresearch.org/race-and-ethnicity/2023/11/30/asian-americans-and-the-model-minority-stereotype/

### Emotion-Telling Over Showing in Cross-Cultural Moments
**Detection**: When characters from different cultural backgrounds interact, the narrative tells the reader what to feel about the interaction ("she felt deeply moved by his culture") rather than showing the interaction's texture and complexity through action, dialogue, and specific detail.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs default to "stating emotions directly rather than showing through physical reactions" (StoryScope research). This is especially pronounced in cross-cultural interactions where the model lacks the specific cultural knowledge needed to render the encounter in concrete, embodied detail and falls back on emotional summary.
**Design space cost**: Replaces genuine cross-cultural encounter with platitude. Eliminates the reader's opportunity to experience cultural difference through specificity. Produces fiction that gestures at diversity without achieving it.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.03136

### Redemption Through Proximity to Marginalized People
**Detection**: A protagonist from a dominant group achieves moral growth or redemption specifically through contact with marginalized characters, who exist primarily as catalysts for the protagonist's transformation rather than as subjects of their own stories.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: This pattern encompasses White Savior, Magical Negro, and similar tropes. It is the dominant cross-racial narrative structure in training data. An LLM generating a story involving characters from different racial backgrounds will default to this structure because it is the most common one in its training corpus.
**Design space cost**: Centers dominant-group characters even in stories ostensibly about marginalized experience. Reduces marginalized characters to therapeutic instruments. Forecloses narratives centered on marginalized characters' own goals and growth.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_savior and https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magical_Negro
