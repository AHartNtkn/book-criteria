## Criteria Found

### Central Conflict Clarity
**What it measures**: Whether the story has a clearly identifiable central conflict that drives the narrative forward.
**Assumed goal**: Story Grid's central-conflict doctrine (Coyne, Shawn), which holds that every story must be organized around a single identifiable core conflict. Violated by mosaic narratives (e.g., Cloud Atlas), plotless literary fiction (e.g., Woolf's The Waves), and meditative fiction where atmosphere or consciousness replaces conflict as the organizing principle.
**Score 0**: No identifiable central conflict; events happen without meaningful opposition; the story is a sequence of occurrences without a unifying struggle.
**Score 5**: A single, clearly defined central conflict anchors the story, is introduced early, escalates progressively, and is resolved (or deliberately left unresolved) in a way that feels complete.
**Source**: https://storygrid.com/writing-strong-character-conflict/
**Notes**: Story Grid emphasizes that conflict is "the lifeblood of storytelling" and that without it, even interesting characters feel flat. The central conflict should be expressible in a single sentence.

### Conflict Organicism
**What it measures**: Whether conflicts arise naturally from character traits, desires, and circumstances rather than being imposed artificially by the author.
**Assumed goal**: The character-driven conflict school (Write It Scared, Weiland), which holds that all conflict must emerge from established character psychology — desires, flaws, and false beliefs — rather than from external plot imposition. Violated by plot-driven genre fiction (e.g., Clancy-style techno-thrillers) where situational conflict is the engine, and by absurdist fiction (e.g., Kafka) where conflict is imposed by inexplicable external forces as a deliberate artistic choice.
**Score 0**: Conflicts feel manufactured, imposed from outside the story's logic, or exist solely because the author needs something to happen. Characters act out of character to create conflict.
**Score 5**: Every conflict emerges organically from established character desires, flaws, relationships, and worldbuilding. Opposition feels inevitable given who the characters are and what they want.
**Source**: https://www.writeitscared.co/blog-3/howtocraftmeaningfulconflict
**Notes**: "Conflict is the force that makes the character change." Authentic conflict stems from character work: understanding external desires, internal needs, false beliefs, and values. "It's not about the external magnitude of the disturbance; what's important is how it affects the character internally."

### Conflict as Character Revelation
**What it measures**: Whether conflict functions to reveal who characters truly are under pressure, stripping away pretense to expose authentic nature.
**Assumed goal**: The pressure-as-revelation doctrine (McKee's Story, Egri's The Art of Dramatic Writing), which holds that the primary function of conflict is to strip away social masks and expose authentic character. Violated by action-adventure fiction where conflict exists to create spectacle, and by plot-puzzle fiction (e.g., Agatha Christie) where conflict serves the mystery mechanism rather than character exposure.
**Score 0**: Conflict exists as pure plot machinery; characters respond to obstacles without revealing anything new about themselves; behavior under pressure is indistinguishable from behavior in comfort.
**Score 5**: Each conflict systematically strips away layers of pretense, forcing characters to reveal who they are when comfort and safety are gone; choices under pressure expose values, fears, and authentic nature that were previously hidden.
**Source**: https://www.inkdroplit.com/blog/2024/5/10/crafting-conflict-in-fiction
**Notes**: "Conflict is not something that happens to characters. It is something that reveals them. Every obstacle strips away another layer of pretense and forces the character to show who they are when comfort and safety are gone."

### Stakes Clarity
**What it measures**: Whether the reader understands what characters stand to lose or gain, and why it matters.
**Assumed goal**: Kevin T. Johns's stakes-clarity principle, which holds that the emotional impact of stakes is a function of specificity and pre-establishment — the reader must know what's at risk before it's endangered. Violated by mystery and horror fiction where ambiguous or unknown stakes are a deliberate tension device (e.g., Shirley Jackson's "The Lottery" withholds stakes until the final reveal).
**Score 0**: No discernible stakes; the reader cannot articulate what happens if the protagonist fails; consequences of failure are vague or absent.
**Score 5**: Stakes are specific, concrete, and clearly communicated; the reader understands exactly what is at risk before it is endangered; consequences of failure are viscerally felt.
**Source**: https://www.kevintjohns.com/are-you-killing-the-tension-high-stakes-writing-mistakes/
**Notes**: "Clarity makes stakes hit harder. Make sure the reader knows what matters before it's in danger." The magnitude of stakes matters less than clarity about them. A small consequence becomes emotionally powerful when readers understand exactly what's at risk.

### Stakes Escalation
**What it measures**: Whether stakes progressively increase throughout the narrative, with the highest stakes reserved for the climax.
**Assumed goal**: The progressive-escalation model (Writing Mastery, Darling Axe, Maass), which requires stakes to ratchet upward at each turning point, with the climax representing maximum risk. Violated by episodic fiction (e.g., picaresque novels) where stakes reset between adventures, and by literary fiction structured around diminishment or entropy rather than escalation (e.g., Kazuo Ishiguro's The Remains of the Day).
**Score 0**: Stakes remain flat throughout; the situation at the midpoint is no more dire than at the beginning; or stakes decrease after the midpoint. Alternatively, stakes start at maximum and have nowhere to go.
**Score 5**: Stakes escalate in a clear, logical progression; each act or major section raises the bar; the climax represents the moment of maximum risk; earlier stakes are dwarfed by later ones.
**Source**: https://www.writingmastery.com/blog/tips-for-raising-the-stakes
**Notes**: "If the protagonist's stakes aren't rising with each turning point, the reader will check out of the story." Escalation can happen by broadening (affecting more people) or deepening (affecting the character more personally). Save biggest stakes for the climax and work backward.

### Three Levels of Stakes Integration
**What it measures**: Whether the story operates simultaneously on public, personal, and philosophical stakes.
**Assumed goal**: ProWritingAid's three-stakes framework (public/personal/philosophical), which requires simultaneous external, relational, and thematic threat dimensions operating in concert. Violated by genre fiction operating on a single stakes axis (e.g., pure action-adventure with only external stakes, or pure literary introspection with only philosophical stakes).
**Score 0**: Only one level of stakes exists (typically only public/external stakes with no personal or philosophical dimension).
**Score 5**: All three levels are present, clearly developed, and intertwined: public stakes drive plot significance, personal stakes drive emotional investment, philosophical stakes drive thematic depth.
**Source**: https://prowritingaid.com/three-levels-of-stakes
**Notes**: "The public stakes drive plot and make the protagonist's story goals feel significant. The personal stakes drive character and make us care about the protagonist. The philosophical stakes drive theme and add deeper thematic meaning." When one level is missing, the story feels overly simplistic.

### Personal Stakes Grounding
**What it measures**: Whether large-scale or abstract stakes are grounded in specific, personal consequences for characters the reader cares about.
**Assumed goal**: The personal-grounding doctrine (Darling Axe, Maass), which holds that abstract or global stakes only function when filtered through specific personal consequences for characters the reader already cares about. Violated by epic/mythic fiction where impersonal cosmic stakes are the point (e.g., Tolkien's Silmarillion), and by hard science fiction where stakes are civilizational rather than personal.
**Score 0**: Stakes are entirely abstract or global with no personal dimension; saving the world but no individual person the reader cares about is at risk; the protagonist has no personal skin in the game.
**Score 5**: Every major stakes escalation is anchored in specific personal consequences; global stakes are filtered through their impact on individual relationships and losses the reader has been made to care about.
**Source**: https://darlingaxe.com/blogs/news/stakes
**Notes**: "It's the internal conflicts of your characters that make the reader care." External pyrotechnics fail without character investment. In Die Hard, the building explosion matters because John McClane's estranged wife is inside. For big stakes to be effective, they need to be tied to deeply personal, emotional stakes.

### Tension vs. Conflict Distinction
**What it measures**: Whether the author demonstrates mastery of both tension (anticipation of conflict) and conflict (active collision), deploying them as distinct tools.
**Assumed goal**: Weiland's tension-conflict distinction model (Helping Writers Become Authors), which treats tension (anticipation of conflict) and conflict (active collision) as separate craft tools that must be deployed independently and in complement. Violated by action-first genre fiction (e.g., thriller set-pieces) where sustained conflict without anticipatory tension is the norm, and by literary minimalism (e.g., Carver) where tension exists without conflict ever fully materializing.
**Score 0**: The author treats tension and conflict as identical; scenes are either all-action or all-inert; there is no anticipatory dread, only direct confrontation or nothing.
**Score 5**: Tension and conflict operate as complementary tools—"pistons working in concert"; quiet scenes drip with dread and unspoken danger; action scenes are earned by prior tension; the reader experiences both the threat of conflict and conflict itself.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/conflict-and-tension/
**Notes**: "Conflict is a defined obstruction between the character and the goal. Tension is the threat of conflict." Every scene should feature either conflict or tension. Understanding their distinction allows writers to balance and enhance each. Characters in a bunker awaiting artillery bombardment experience significant tension despite no active conflict.

### Microtension (Line-Level Tension)
**What it measures**: Whether tension operates at the sentence and paragraph level through conflicting emotions, subtext, and contradictory impulses, not just at the plot level.
**Assumed goal**: Donald Maass's microtension doctrine (The Fire in Fiction, Writing the Breakout Novel), which holds that tension must operate at the sentence level through conflicting emotions and contradictory impulses — not just at the plot level. Violated by spare prose traditions (e.g., Hemingway, Cormac McCarthy) where flat declarative sentences create tension through accumulation and absence rather than line-level emotional contradiction.
**Score 0**: Tension exists only at the macro level (will the hero survive?); individual paragraphs, dialogue exchanges, and descriptions are emotionally flat; reader can skim without losing engagement.
**Score 5**: Every page contains microtension through conflicting emotions, unexpected word choices, subtext, and inner contradictions; the reader feels unease, curiosity, or emotional pull in individual lines, not just in plot structure.
**Source**: https://www.livewritethrive.com/2014/10/08/infusing-microtension-in-your-novel/
**Notes**: Donald Maass's concept: microtension "is the moment-by-moment tension that keeps the reader in a constant state of suspense over what will happen, not in the story but in the next few seconds." It comes from "conflicting emotions" — identifying the driving emotion in a passage, then introducing its opposite. Real people experience contradictory emotions simultaneously (crying at weddings, laughing at funerals). Maass: "tension is not a function of plot. This type of tension does not come from high stakes or the circumstances of a scene."

### Subtext as Tension Vehicle
**What it measures**: Whether tension is created through what goes unsaid — the gap between surface dialogue/action and underlying emotional reality.
**Assumed goal**: The Hemingway iceberg theory and its codification in modern subtext pedagogy (Writer's Digest, McKee), which holds that the most important meaning in a scene must be communicated below the surface of dialogue and action. Violated by direct-address narrative traditions (e.g., Dostoevsky's confessional narrators, Brecht's epic theatre) where characters state their internal reality explicitly as an artistic strategy.
**Score 0**: Everything is stated explicitly; characters say exactly what they mean; there is no gap between surface and depth; dialogue functions purely as information delivery.
**Score 5**: Tension lies between the saying, thinking, and doing; characters are riddled with conflict between what they express and what they feel; scenes function on multiple levels simultaneously; the most important information is communicated through implication.
**Source**: https://www.writersdigest.com/write-better-fiction/how-to-use-subtext-and-the-art-of-dramatic-tension-in-fiction
**Notes**: "Subtext is the unconscious implicit desire that motivates a character to behave explicitly." Think of scenes as "layer cakes" where dialogue/behavior form the top layer and unconscious desire forms the bottom. Hemingway's "Hills Like White Elephants" is the canonical example. Characters should be "riddled with conflict" between surface and depth.

### Scene-Level Conflict/Tension Presence
**What it measures**: Whether individual scenes contain their own source of conflict or tension, independent of the global story conflict.
**Assumed goal**: The scene-as-story-unit doctrine (Johns, Story Grid, Swain), which requires every scene to contain its own internal conflict or tension source with specific goals and consequences for failure. Violated by transitional and atmospheric fiction (e.g., Marilynne Robinson's Housekeeping) where scenes exist for contemplative, tonal, or thematic purposes without scene-level opposition.
**Score 0**: Many scenes lack any source of conflict or tension; characters have conversations without opposing goals; scenes exist only to deliver information or move characters between locations.
**Score 5**: Nearly every scene contains either active conflict (opposing goals colliding) or tension (threat of conflict, unresolved questions, anticipatory dread); even quiet scenes maintain engagement through emotional undercurrents.
**Source**: https://www.kevintjohns.com/are-you-killing-the-tension-high-stakes-writing-mistakes/
**Notes**: "Every scene requires its own stakes—a specific goal and real consequences for failure." Each scene should answer: "What does the character want right now, and what happens if they don't get it?" Without scene-level stakes, conversations become filler. Build tension through "dozens of smaller scenes with clear goals and real consequences."

### Scene-Level Value Shift
**What it measures**: Whether each scene produces a measurable change in the character's situation, moving from one emotional or circumstantial state to another.
**Assumed goal**: Story Grid's value-shift methodology (Coyne, Gilbo), which requires every scene to produce a measurable change in polarity (positive to negative or vice versa) along at least one value axis. Violated by slice-of-life fiction (e.g., iyashikei, Chekhov's short stories) where scenes capture states of being rather than state changes, and by stream-of-consciousness fiction where scene boundaries are deliberately dissolved.
**Score 0**: Scenes begin and end in the same state; nothing has changed for any character; the scene could be removed without affecting the story.
**Score 5**: Every scene shifts at least one value (safety to danger, trust to betrayal, hope to despair, ignorance to knowledge); the character's situation is measurably different at the end than at the beginning; turning points are identifiable.
**Source**: https://storygrid.com/value-shift-101/
**Notes**: Story Grid methodology: "A story value is simply a human experience that can change from positive to negative or negative to positive." If your analysis shows no turning point, you have exposition, not a scene. Characters should rarely flip between absolute extremes — incremental shifts are more credible. The Foolscap documents 15 core scenes tracking external and internal charges.

### Character Agency in Conflict
**What it measures**: Whether the protagonist actively drives the story through choices rather than passively receiving conflict.
**Assumed goal**: The protagonist-as-causal-agent model (Weiland, McKee, Swain), which requires the protagonist to drive story events through active choices rather than passively receiving them. Violated by naturalist fiction (e.g., Zola, Dreiser) where characters are subject to deterministic forces, witness narratives (e.g., The Great Gatsby), and existentialist fiction (e.g., Camus's The Stranger) where deliberate passivity is thematically constitutive.
**Score 0**: The protagonist is a passive recipient of events; things happen to them but they make no meaningful choices; they are moved around by the plot like a chess piece; their actions have no causal relationship to outcomes.
**Score 5**: The protagonist drives story events through active choices; conflict arises from their decisions and their consequences; even when constrained by overwhelming forces, they find ways to act with agency; the 3C cycle (Conflict-Choice-Consequence) operates in every scene.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/character-agency/
**Notes**: "Character agency is the character driving story events through choices." Without it, "it's not really the character's story." Even with overwhelming antagonist power (Lord of the Rings, the Fellowship trapped on a mountain), characters must make meaningful decisions. A protagonist who is "just a pinball bouncing from event to event" creates "no stakes — only randomness."

### Antagonistic Force Credibility
**What it measures**: Whether the opposition is believable, motivated, and poses a genuine threat that the protagonist might not overcome.
**Assumed goal**: Jane Friedman's credible-opposition principle, which holds that the antagonistic force must be at least as capable as the protagonist to generate genuine uncertainty about the outcome. Violated by power-fantasy fiction (e.g., xianxia, certain superhero stories) where protagonist superiority is the genre contract, and by comedy where obviously outmatched antagonists are a source of humor.
**Score 0**: The antagonist is a cardboard villain with no motivation; opposition is trivially weak; or the antagonist opposes the protagonist for no discernible reason; there is no credible threat.
**Score 5**: The antagonist is at least as clever, powerful, and determined as the protagonist; their motivations are comprehensible and even sympathetic; they have clear justifications for their actions; the reader genuinely worries the protagonist might lose.
**Source**: https://janefriedman.com/adversaries-in-fiction-who-is-standing-in-your-characters-way/
**Notes**: "Without a strong opponent, most novels lack that crucial emotional experience for the reader: worry." The antagonist should be "as clever, powerful, and determined as your protagonist" — preferably stronger. "When readers can clearly line up the character's motivation to their goals and needs, it gives credibility to their actions." Adversarial conflict "always requires a reason for existing."

### Antagonist Dimensionality
**What it measures**: Whether opposing forces have their own coherent worldview, motivations, and internal logic rather than being purely evil or obstructive.
**Assumed goal**: The morally complex antagonist tradition (modern literary thriller, prestige TV writing, George R.R. Martin), which requires antagonists to have coherent internal justifications and function as protagonists of their own stories. Violated by mythic fiction, fairy tales, and allegorical fiction (e.g., Tolkien's Sauron, Lewis's White Witch) where the antagonist represents an abstract moral force rather than a psychologically individuated character.
**Score 0**: The antagonist exists solely to oppose the protagonist with no independent motivation; they are evil for evil's sake; or the opposing force is a generic obstacle with no internal logic.
**Score 5**: The antagonist has a coherent worldview and believes their actions are justified; they care intensely about something; their opposition to the protagonist arises from genuine philosophical, personal, or practical differences; they are the hero of their own story.
**Source**: https://killzoneblog.com/2015/03/create-fascinating-believable.html
**Notes**: "Credible villains become evil for sympathetic reasons. They care intensely, obsessively about something, or someone." Today's readers want antagonists who are "more complex, realistic, and believable." The opposition should have clear justifications that are "the driving force behind their story."

### Internal-External Conflict Integration
**What it measures**: Whether internal psychological conflict and external plot conflict are linked, with each influencing and complicating the other.
**Assumed goal**: The dual-conflict integration model (Scribophile, Weiland, Bell), which requires internal psychological conflict and external plot conflict to be causally linked — each fueling and complicating the other. Violated by pure action fiction where external conflict operates without an internal dimension, and by introspective literary fiction (e.g., Proust, Woolf) where internal conflict operates largely independent of external events.
**Score 0**: Internal and external conflicts exist in isolation; the character's psychological struggle has no bearing on plot events; or the story has only external conflict with no internal dimension (or vice versa).
**Score 5**: Internal conflict directly fuels and complicates external conflicts; character flaws create or worsen external obstacles; external challenges force confrontation with internal demons; resolution of one requires transformation in the other.
**Source**: https://www.scribophile.com/academy/internal-vs-external-conflict
**Notes**: "External conflict is what makes the reader turn the page to find out what happens next. Internal conflict makes the reader care." When writers focus only on solving external problems without showing emotional growth, stories feel flat. Let internal struggles "bleed into dialogue, choices, mistakes, and relationships."

### Multi-Layered Conflict Structure
**What it measures**: Whether the story operates on multiple simultaneous conflict layers (internal, interpersonal, societal) that influence each other.
**Assumed goal**: The layered-conflict model (Author's Pathway, Bell, Maass), which requires simultaneous operation of internal, interpersonal, and societal conflict layers that causally influence each other. Violated by focused genre fiction that deliberately operates on a single conflict plane (e.g., survival fiction with purely external conflict, or confessional fiction with purely internal conflict), and by allegory where conflict layers are symbolic rather than causal.
**Score 0**: Only one type of conflict exists (typically external/interpersonal with no internal or societal dimension); conflict operates on a single plane.
**Score 5**: Multiple conflict layers operate simultaneously and intersect: internal psychological struggles affect interpersonal relationships which connect to broader societal forces; resolving one layer impacts others; the narrative weaves these layers into a unified whole.
**Source**: https://authorspathway.com/crafting-your-story/plot-development/conflict-in-fiction-creating-tension-and-drama-in-your-story/
**Notes**: "The most engaging novels often feature multiple layers of conflict working in tandem." Layering conflicts so they influence each other creates more realistic characters since "in real life, our struggles are often interrelated." Internal conflict might affect relationships, leading to interpersonal conflict, which connects to societal friction.

### Dilemma Quality
**What it measures**: Whether characters face genuine dilemmas (choices between two bad options or two goods that cannot coexist) rather than merely obstacles to overcome.
**Assumed goal**: The dilemma-over-obstacle school (The Write Practice, Egri, McKee), which holds that the highest form of conflict is a forced choice between two valued things — not merely a barrier to overcome. Violated by adventure fiction where obstacles (walls, enemies, puzzles) are the primary mode of opposition, and by survival fiction where the conflict is endurance rather than choice.
**Score 0**: Characters face only obstacles (things in the way) without genuine dilemmas (choices with costs); every problem has an obvious correct solution; the protagonist never has to sacrifice anything important.
**Score 5**: Characters regularly face genuine dilemmas where both options carry real costs; there is no clearly correct choice; decisions test character values and beliefs; the reader agonizes alongside the character over which path to take.
**Source**: https://thewritepractice.com/dilemma-choice/
**Notes**: "A dilemma is a conflict with two possible solutions, where a person must choose between two desirable options, or two undesirable options." The dilemma is "the moment where the protagonist must choose, and that decision carries so much weight that there is no turning back." The best conflict combines realistic action with genuine thoughts and feelings, with internal dilemma bridging action and decision.

### Impossible Choice Presence
**What it measures**: Whether the story includes at least one choice where the protagonist must sacrifice something deeply valued to achieve their goal.
**Assumed goal**: The sacrifice-as-meaning tradition (Ackerman & Puglisi's Writers Helping Writers, Hardy), which holds that a story must include at least one moment where the protagonist must give up something deeply valued to achieve their goal — a "collision of imperatives" with no safe option. Violated by comedy and wish-fulfillment fiction where the protagonist gets what they want without meaningful sacrifice, and by coming-of-age fiction where growth itself is the resolution rather than a costly trade-off.
**Score 0**: The protagonist never has to sacrifice anything important; every victory is free; goals are achieved without meaningful cost; or apparent costs are immediately negated.
**Score 5**: The protagonist faces at least one "Sophie's Choice" moment where they must decide between two equally devastating options; the choice tests their deepest values; the consequences of the decision are permanent and painful; the reader feels the weight of what was given up.
**Source**: https://writershelpingwriters.net/2023/01/force-your-character-to-make-hard-choices/
**Notes**: "The most heart-wrenching times for characters are when they must make a decision in which someone will pay regardless of their choice." A moral or ethical dilemma is "a collision of imperatives: follow one, and you sacrifice another, with no safe option or loophole." Aim for at least one impossible choice per story.

### Try-Fail Cycle Effectiveness
**What it measures**: Whether the protagonist makes multiple genuine attempts to solve their problem, failing productively (learning, changing, escalating) before succeeding.
**Assumed goal**: Genre fiction craft tradition (Sanderson, Fawkes, Kowal), which requires protagonists to attempt and fail multiple times before succeeding — each failure productive and each attempt qualitatively different. Violated by literary fiction where problems are endured or accepted rather than solved (e.g., Chekhov), by tragedy where the protagonist never overcomes, and by thriller pacing where compression demands fewer cycles.
**Score 0**: The protagonist succeeds on the first attempt; or fails repeatedly without learning anything; or success arrives from external intervention rather than character effort; each attempt is the same approach repeated.
**Score 5**: The protagonist attempts progressively more difficult and costly approaches; each failure teaches something or forces adaptation; the character tries simpler solutions first and resorts to costlier ones; final success requires combining lessons from prior failures and is qualitatively different from initial attempts.
**Source**: https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2025/12/writing-try-fail-cycles-strengths-vs-weaknesses.html
**Notes**: "The easy road rarely reveals true character. The hard road does." The Yes-but/No-and technique creates partial success or partial failure. Three try-fail cycles is the minimum. Each cycle should incrementally step up the conflict and/or stakes. A weakness of rigid try-fail structure is risk of repetitive monotony if applied too literally.

### Escalation Pattern
**What it measures**: Whether complications, obstacles, and consequences intensify progressively throughout the narrative rather than remaining flat or random.
**Assumed goal**: Storm Writing School's escalation model and Freytag's pyramid descendants, which require complications to intensify progressively — each obstacle more severe, more personal, or more costly than the last — with the climax as absolute peak. Violated by picaresque fiction where complication intensity is random, by circular/cyclical narratives (e.g., Finnegans Wake), and by stories structured around emotional deflation rather than escalation.
**Score 0**: Complications are randomly distributed without escalation; the middle is as intense as the beginning; scenes could be rearranged without affecting tension trajectory; the climax is no more intense than earlier conflicts.
**Score 5**: Each new complication is more severe, more personal, or more costly than the last; the escalation follows a clear logic (broadening scope, deepening personal stakes, removing resources, tightening time constraints); the climax represents the absolute peak of all escalating pressures.
**Source**: https://stormwritingschool.com/escalating-complications/
**Notes**: "Escalating complications must escalate." The proximity effect matters: "If you're in a race and you fall within the first three meters, it's a lot less dramatic than if you fall within the last three meters." Failure becomes more tragic when the finish line is visible. Escalation mechanisms include increasing antagonistic forces, raising emotional stakes, making success more difficult, and introducing greater desperation.

### Conflict-Driven Escalation vs. Forced Escalation
**What it measures**: Whether escalation emerges from character actions and their consequences (organic) or from the author arbitrarily imposing worse situations (forced).
**Assumed goal**: The organic-escalation principle (About Writing, Weiland), which holds that every escalation must be traceable to the protagonist's prior choices and their consequences — not imposed by authorial fiat. Violated by disaster fiction and invasion narratives where escalation is legitimately external, and by cosmic horror (e.g., Lovecraft) where escalating threat is deliberately disconnected from character agency.
**Score 0**: Escalation is imposed from outside the story's logic; new obstacles appear without causal connection to character actions; the author simply makes things worse by fiat; events don't connect logically.
**Score 5**: Each escalation is a direct consequence of the protagonist's previous choices or failures; the character's attempts to solve problems create new, larger problems; the reader can trace the causal chain from inciting incident to climax through character decisions.
**Source**: https://www.aboutwriting.org/how-to-use-rising-action-escalation-and-switches/
**Notes**: "Forced escalation is the illogical progression of conflict where events don't seem connected and fail to align in the end." For genuine rising action, "everything must be driven by the character." Rising action can be bad when "your desire for rising action has the characters doing things they simply would not do."

### Tension-Release Rhythm
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative alternates between tension-building and release/recovery, creating a sustainable emotional rhythm rather than unrelenting intensity or flatness.
**Assumed goal**: The tension-release rhythm model (Nail Your Novel, Maass, Campbell's "campfire scene"), which requires alternation between intensity and recovery to sustain engagement across novel length. Violated by relentless-pacing genre fiction (e.g., Lee Child's Reacher novels) that deliberately maintains a single high-tension register, and by meditative fiction that deliberately maintains a single low-tension register.
**Score 0**: Either relentless conflict without breathing room (reader exhaustion) or extended periods without tension (reader boredom); the narrative operates at a single emotional intensity throughout.
**Score 5**: Tension and release alternate in a rhythm that builds overall while providing necessary recovery; quiet scenes deepen character investment before the next escalation; the "campfire scene" strategy is employed before climactic sequences; the rhythm mirrors a human heartbeat with crests and troughs.
**Source**: https://nailyournovel.wordpress.com/2016/07/03/conflict-in-every-scene-disasters-in-every-act-yes-and-no/
**Notes**: "Even the fastest-paced thriller or suspense novel needs downtime scenes or you'll wear the reader out. Relentless conflict is exhausting after a while." Quiet moments deepen stakes without action by allowing personal bonding, adding moral dimensions, and strengthening reader investment. Each scene must still contain forward movement and change, even if not overt conflict.

### Scene-Sequel Structure
**What it measures**: Whether action scenes (goal-conflict-disaster) are followed by reaction scenes (reaction-dilemma-decision) that process events and set up the next action.
**Assumed goal**: Dwight V. Swain's Scene-Sequel framework (Techniques of the Selling Writer), which requires action scenes (goal-conflict-disaster) to alternate with reaction scenes (reaction-dilemma-decision) in a structured rhythm. Violated by action-thriller pacing that compresses or eliminates sequels, and by literary fiction structured around extended interior reflection without alternating action beats (e.g., Mrs. Dalloway).
**Score 0**: Only action without reaction (characters never process what happens to them) or only reaction without action (characters only think and feel without doing); no alternation between modes.
**Score 5**: Action scenes (goal, conflict, disaster) are followed by sequel scenes (reaction, dilemma, decision) that allow characters to process events emotionally, consider options, and commit to new plans; the pattern creates a natural rhythm of doing and thinking.
**Source**: https://www.advancedfictionwriting.com/articles/writing-the-perfect-scene/
**Notes**: Dwight V. Swain's framework: Scene = goal + conflict + disaster; Sequel = reaction + dilemma + decision. The sequel's dilemma is "a situation with no good options" where the character must find the "least-bad path forward." The decision restores proactivity and generates a new goal. This pattern cycles naturally, creating compulsive forward momentum.

### Urgency and Time Pressure
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative creates a sense that time matters — that delay has consequences and the protagonist cannot afford to wait.
**Assumed goal**: The urgency-as-tension-engine model (Story Grid's clock principle, Aristotle's unity of time), which holds that compressing the time available for action increases narrative tension. Violated by expansive literary fiction where temporal spaciousness is the point (e.g., Proust's In Search of Lost Time), and by contemplative fiction where the absence of urgency is thematically necessary.
**Score 0**: There is no time pressure; the protagonist could take forever to act with no consequences; delays and detours carry no cost; the pace feels leisurely when it should feel urgent.
**Score 5**: Time pressure is palpable, whether through literal deadlines (ticking clocks, countdowns) or figurative urgency (deteriorating situations, narrowing windows of opportunity, irreversible changes); delays have clear costs; the reader feels the acceleration.
**Source**: https://storygrid.com/mastering-the-clock/
**Notes**: Aristotle recognized that compressing time increases tension. A ticking clock "feels less like a TV trope and more like a tension driving engine." You don't need a literal clock — "the internal clock comes in—the sense that something is building, shifting, and something important might be lost—not necessarily in minutes or hours, but in emotion, opportunity, or identity." A flat tire is ten times worse when you have a plane to catch.

### Conflict Resolution Earnedness
**What it measures**: Whether resolutions arise from within the story's established logic through character effort, or arrive through convenience and contrivance.
**Assumed goal**: The earned-resolution principle (Aristotle's Poetics through modern craft — Fawkes, McKee, Sanderson's First Law), which requires that conflict resolution emerge from within the story's established logic through character effort, not through coincidence or previously unestablished elements. Violated by classical Greek drama (where divine intervention is conventional), magical realism (where synchronicity is a structural force), and comedy/farce where lucky timing is a genre convention.
**Score 0**: Resolution arrives through deus ex machina, coincidence, or previously unknown abilities/resources; the protagonist does not earn their victory; conflicts dissolve rather than being solved; or resolution is trivially easy.
**Score 5**: Resolution emerges from the protagonist's choices, growth, and effort; every tool used in the resolution was established earlier in the narrative; the resolution feels simultaneously surprising and inevitable; character transformation was necessary to achieve it.
**Source**: https://www.literautas.com/en/blog/post-1009/deus-ex-machina-what-is-it-and-how-to-avoid-it/
**Notes**: "The resolution of any conflict should arise internally, from the action, objects, and events that have already been introduced in a story." The reader needs to understand whatever solves the conflict before it solves the conflict. Deus ex machina "undermines the tension and stakes you have been building."

### Consequences Permanence
**What it measures**: Whether character choices and conflict outcomes have lasting consequences that alter the story's trajectory.
**Assumed goal**: Consequentialist plotting (Darling Axe, Story Grid, Martin), which holds that character choices must produce permanent, irreversible state changes that accumulate across the narrative. Violated by episodic fiction and sitcom-structure narratives where reset-to-status-quo is a genre convention, and by Groundhog Day / time-loop structures where impermanence is the premise.
**Score 0**: Choices have no lasting consequences; damage is immediately reversed; status quo is restored after each conflict; the story operates on a reset button.
**Score 5**: Every significant choice produces permanent consequences that alter the story's trajectory; victories come at costs that cannot be undone; the world and relationships are permanently changed by conflict outcomes; there is no returning to how things were.
**Source**: https://darlingaxe.com/blogs/news/stakes
**Notes**: "Everything your character does should have consequences." Each consequence should create larger obstacles to the goal. George R.R. Martin kills major characters to prove failure is possible. "Let your characters do stupid things, make bad decisions, take risks. Let things go wrong for them."

### Dramatic Irony Deployment
**What it measures**: Whether information asymmetry between reader and characters is used strategically to create tension, dread, or anticipation.
**Assumed goal**: The information-asymmetry-as-tension model (Irvine, Hitchcock's "bomb under the table" principle), which holds that strategic control of what the reader knows versus what characters know is a primary tension-generation tool. Violated by strict first-person-limited narratives where the reader can never know more than the POV character, and by surprise-dependent fiction (e.g., twist endings) where keeping the reader ignorant is the primary strategy.
**Score 0**: Reader and characters always share the same information; no deliberate information gaps exist; opportunities for dramatic irony are missed.
**Score 5**: The author strategically controls what the reader knows vs. what characters know; the reader sometimes knows danger the character doesn't see (creating dread) and sometimes knows less than the character (creating mystery); these gaps are managed deliberately and resolved at optimal moments.
**Source**: https://www.ian-irvine.com/for-writers/article-5-how-to-create-conflict/
**Notes**: "Readers know something characters don't; killer outside the window while hero relaxes; hero's mistakes obvious to reader." Both directions of information asymmetry create tension: reader knows more (dread) or less (suspense). The gap between what is known and unknown sustains engagement.

### Anticipation and Dread Construction
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative builds anticipation of coming conflict or consequences, creating emotional investment before events occur.
**Assumed goal**: The suspense-psychology model (CrimeReads, Hitchcock, de Suspense's neurochemical engagement theory), which holds that anticipation of future events — not present action — is the primary driver of reader engagement. Violated by in-the-moment experiential fiction (e.g., stream-of-consciousness) that creates engagement through present-tense immersion, and by literary fiction that eschews anticipatory structures in favor of retrospective meaning-making.
**Score 0**: Events arrive without anticipatory buildup; there is no foreshadowing of coming conflict; the reader is neither hoping nor fearing about future events; everything is reactive rather than anticipatory.
**Score 5**: The reader is in a constant state of anticipation about what will happen next; threats are established before they materialize; promises of conflict are set up and paid off; the reader's brain is activated by uncertainty about outcomes they care about.
**Source**: https://crimereads.com/the-psychology-of-suspense/
**Notes**: Suspense triggers the amygdala and adrenal glands, producing a contained neurochemical rush. Dopamine is released by anticipation. The narrative completion drive compels readers to pursue unresolved threads. Fiction provides "vicarious experience while maintaining psychological safety." Readers experience satisfaction from problem-solving engagement with story events.

### Conflict Variety
**What it measures**: Whether the story uses multiple types of conflict (character vs. character, self, society, nature, fate, technology, supernatural) rather than relying on a single mode.
**Assumed goal**: The multi-mode conflict doctrine (F(r)iction's six-type taxonomy), which holds that compelling stories layer multiple conflict types (self, character, society, nature, fate, technology) to create varied emotional registers. Violated by genre fiction that deliberately operates in a single conflict mode (e.g., survival fiction focusing exclusively on person vs. nature, or psychological fiction focusing exclusively on person vs. self).
**Score 0**: Only one type of conflict exists throughout (typically character vs. character through argument/fight); the same mode of opposition repeats without variation.
**Score 5**: Multiple conflict types operate throughout the narrative, each appropriate to the scene and story moment; the protagonist faces different kinds of opposition (interpersonal, internal, environmental, societal) that require different skills and create different emotional registers.
**Source**: https://frictionlit.org/crafting-compelling-conflict-the-heartbeat-of-storytelling/
**Notes**: Six types identified: character vs. self, character, society, technology, nature, supernatural. The most compelling stories "layer multiple conflict types—overarching conflicts plus scene-specific tensions." Each type engages different emotional registers and requires different character responses.

### Character Desire Specificity
**What it measures**: Whether character desires are concrete and specific enough that success or failure is unambiguous by the end of each scene and the story.
**Assumed goal**: Story Grid's S.M.A.R.T. desire framework (Coyne, McKee's "object of desire"), which requires character goals to be concrete and measurable enough that success or failure is unambiguous at every scene boundary. Violated by literary fiction where desires are deliberately inchoate or shifting (e.g., characters in Chekhov who don't know what they want), and by existentialist fiction where the absence of clear desire is thematically central.
**Score 0**: Character desires are vague ("wants to be happy," "wants things to be better"); it's impossible to tell whether the character got what they wanted; goals are abstract and unmeasurable.
**Score 5**: Character desires are specific, concrete, and measurable; "wants to get their estranged brother to attend their wedding" rather than "wants family reconciliation"; every scene has a clear objective whose success or failure is identifiable.
**Source**: https://storygrid.com/writing-strong-character-conflict/
**Notes**: Story Grid's S.M.A.R.T. system: desires should be Specific (concrete enough for unambiguous success/failure), Measurable (clear indicators), Achievable (possible within story rules), Relevant (connected to central conflict), Time-Bound (includes urgency). The formula: "The character wants [A] without having to [B]."

### Conflict Evolution
**What it measures**: Whether conflicts transform over the course of the narrative rather than remaining static, reflecting character growth and changing circumstances.
**Assumed goal**: The transformative-conflict model (Write It Scared, Weiland), which holds that conflicts must evolve, deepen, and reveal new dimensions as characters grow — not remain static obstacles to be repeatedly attacked. Violated by episodic genre fiction (e.g., serialized detective fiction) where a consistent conflict type recurs by design, and by Sisyphean literary fiction (e.g., Beckett) where static, unchanging conflict is the thematic point.
**Score 0**: The conflict at the end is the same as the conflict at the beginning; the nature of opposition never changes; the protagonist is fighting the same battle in the same way throughout.
**Score 5**: Conflicts evolve, transform, and reveal new dimensions as the story progresses; what seemed like the central conflict gives way to deeper conflicts; character growth reframes the nature of the opposition; the protagonist's understanding of what they're fighting shifts.
**Source**: https://www.writeitscared.co/blog-3/howtocraftmeaningfulconflict
**Notes**: "Conflicts shouldn't remain static; as your story progresses, let the conflict evolve, intensify, or even transform into something entirely different." Characters initially pursue goals through the path of least resistance. Escalating conflict reveals deeper layers until characters must embrace internal transformation.

### Threat Proximity and Imminence
**What it measures**: Whether threats feel close and immediate rather than distant and theoretical.
**Assumed goal**: Weiland's threat-proximity principle (Helping Writers Become Authors), which holds that temporal and physical closeness of danger is a primary determinant of tension effectiveness — immediate threats create stronger tension than distant ones. Violated by dystopian and speculative fiction where looming systemic threats (surveillance, ecological collapse) operate on long time horizons as the primary tension source, and by literary fiction about slow-motion catastrophe (e.g., Jenny Offill's Weather).
**Score 0**: All threats are distant, theoretical, or abstract; danger never feels imminent; the protagonist is never in immediate peril; threats are discussed but never materialized nearby.
**Score 5**: Threats are proximate and immediate; danger is present in the scene, not just discussed; the reader feels that something bad could happen at any moment; the temporal and physical distance between the character and danger is small.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/most-common-mistakes-series-how-you-may/
**Notes**: When danger "feels remote or unlikely to materialize soon, urgency diminishes. Temporal proximity matters—immediate threats create stronger tension than distant ones." One of the three primary causes of inadequate tension is threats positioned too distantly.

### Conflict Hierarchy
**What it measures**: Whether the story maintains a clear hierarchy of conflicts with one dominant conflict providing structure while smaller conflicts create scene-level texture.
**Assumed goal**: F(r)iction's conflict-hierarchy model, which requires one dominant overarching conflict to provide structural spine while smaller scene-level conflicts branch beneath it in a clear hierarchy. Violated by ensemble fiction with deliberately co-equal conflict threads (e.g., Robert Altman-style narratives), and by experimental fiction that resists hierarchical organization of its conflicts (e.g., David Mitchell's Cloud Atlas).
**Score 0**: Conflicts are all the same magnitude, creating a flat landscape; or too many equally important conflicts compete for attention, creating confusion; or scene-level conflicts are disconnected from the central conflict.
**Score 5**: One dominant overarching conflict provides structural spine; smaller conflicts branch from character relationships and actions; scene-level conflicts serve the central conflict while maintaining their own integrity; the hierarchy is clear without being rigid.
**Source**: https://frictionlit.org/crafting-compelling-conflict-the-heartbeat-of-storytelling/
**Notes**: "Multiple conflicts function best with clear hierarchies: one dominant overarching conflict provides structure, while smaller scene-level conflicts branch from character relationships and actions." Too many simultaneous conflicts of equal weight create reader confusion.

### Obstacle Difficulty Calibration
**What it measures**: Whether obstacles are appropriately challenging — not so easy as to be trivial, not so hard as to require authorial intervention.
**Assumed goal**: Irvine's difficulty-calibration principle, which requires obstacles to be at the edge of the protagonist's capabilities — neither trivially easy nor impossibly hard — so that success demands genuine extension of skill, growth, or sacrifice. Violated by power-fantasy fiction (e.g., xianxia, certain superhero stories) where effortless dominance is the genre promise, and by literary tragedy where impossibly overwhelming obstacles are thematically necessary.
**Score 0**: Obstacles are either trivially easy (protagonist breezes through) or impossibly hard (requiring deus ex machina or coincidence to overcome); difficulty is not calibrated to the protagonist's abilities.
**Score 5**: Obstacles are at the edge of the protagonist's capabilities, requiring full use of their skills, growth, or sacrifice; each obstacle is genuinely difficult but not impossibly so within the story's logic; the protagonist must extend themselves to succeed.
**Source**: https://www.ian-irvine.com/for-writers/article-5-how-to-create-conflict/
**Notes**: "A strong protagonist requires a stronger antagonist if the risk is to be believable." Give the hero disadvantages at the start: they don't understand the problem, lack necessary skills, carry personality flaws that blind them. The antagonist should be at least as strong as the hero, preferably stronger. Block success at every turn but grant small victories to prevent reader resignation.

### Four Throughline Completeness
**What it measures**: Whether the story addresses conflict through multiple perspectives (objective story, main character, obstacle character, relationship) rather than a single viewpoint.
**Assumed goal**: Dramatica theory's four-throughline model (Phillips & Huntley), which requires every complete story to address conflict through four distinct perspectives: overall story (They), main character (I), impact character (You), and relationship (We). Violated by single-POV intimate fiction that deliberately restricts perspective, by minimalist fiction that operates on fewer structural layers, and by experimental fiction that rejects the premise that completeness requires multiple viewpoints.
**Score 0**: Conflict is presented from only one perspective; the story lacks relationship conflict or obstacle character perspective; the audience only sees the protagonist's experience of the conflict.
**Score 5**: The story addresses conflict through multiple complementary perspectives: the overall story conflict (They), the protagonist's personal conflict (I), the impact character's challenge (You), and the central relationship's conflict (We); each throughline operates in a distinct domain.
**Source**: https://narrativefirst.com/articles/naturally-structuring-a-story-for-conflict/
**Notes**: Dramatica theory: four throughlines match "the Four Perspectives through which the human mind can appreciate conflict: They, I, You, and We." Dynamic pairs (items diagonally across from one another) offer the best opportunity for conflict as they appear as opposites. Each throughline operates in a distinct conflict domain (Universe, Mind, Physics, Psychology).

### Unresolved Questions as Engagement
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative maintains a steady supply of unanswered questions that pull the reader forward.
**Assumed goal**: Storm Writing School's engagement-through-uncertainty model, which holds that the fundamental driver of reader engagement is unresolved questions — the "potential for change" that compels readers to continue because humans cannot tolerate unresolved problems. Violated by contemplative fiction where the reader is invited to sit with ambiguity rather than pursue resolution (e.g., Sebald's The Rings of Saturn), and by fiction that provides full transparency and creates engagement through other means (e.g., dramatic irony in Greek tragedy).
**Score 0**: All questions are answered as soon as they arise; there is no mystery, no withholding, no curiosity driving the reader forward; the reader is never left wondering about anything.
**Score 5**: The narrative always has multiple unresolved questions operating simultaneously; as some are answered, new ones emerge; the reader is never in a state where all threads are resolved (until the ending); information is revealed at exactly the rate that maximizes curiosity.
**Source**: https://stormwritingschool.com/the-key-to-reader-engagement/
**Notes**: "Humans don't like unresolved problems and want everything sorted out before they can relax." Writers leverage this by never letting everything get completely sorted out. Each sentence "can add details but also leave questions unanswered." The fundamental driver of engagement is "the potential for change" — the basic question: "What's going to happen next?"

### Showing vs. Telling Conflict
**What it measures**: Whether pivotal conflict moments are dramatized in real-time scenes rather than summarized in exposition.
**Assumed goal**: The dramatization-over-summary principle (Weiland, Bickham, Swain), which requires pivotal conflict moments to be rendered in real-time scene rather than compressed into narrative summary. Violated by compressed literary forms (e.g., Borges's ficciones) where summary is the primary mode, and by epistolary and frame narratives where key events are deliberately reported rather than dramatized.
**Score 0**: Major conflicts are summarized after the fact ("They argued about X"); confrontations happen offscreen; pivotal moments are recounted in summary; the reader is told about tension rather than experiencing it.
**Score 5**: All major conflict moments are dramatized in real-time scenes; the reader is present for confrontations, decisions, and turning points; pivotal moments are experienced, not reported; only minor conflicts are summarized.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/most-common-mistakes-series-how-you-may/
**Notes**: Writers "spend many pages telling summarized versions of exciting elements. All in summary. Tension diminishes when pivotal moments are merely recounted rather than dramatized." Excessive backstory and detailed descriptions bog down pacing and diminish immediacy.

### Backstory-Tension Balance
**What it measures**: Whether backstory and exposition are distributed so as not to interrupt or deflate moments of tension.
**Assumed goal**: The momentum-preservation principle (Weiland, Maass), which holds that backstory and exposition must never interrupt active tension sequences — context should arrive when the reader needs it, not when the author finds it convenient. Violated by epic fantasy and historical fiction where extensive worldbuilding exposition is a genre expectation (e.g., Tolkien's genealogical digressions), and by literary fiction where temporal digression is a deliberate structural choice (e.g., Faulkner).
**Score 0**: Backstory dumps interrupt tense sequences; exposition arrives at the worst possible moments; flashbacks or info-dumps kill momentum at critical junctures; the reader is pulled out of engagement by untimely context.
**Score 5**: Backstory is woven into the narrative without breaking tension; exposition arrives when the reader needs it, not when it's convenient for the author; tense moments are never interrupted by flashbacks or information delivery; context enriches rather than deflates engagement.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/most-common-mistakes-series-how-you-may/
**Notes**: "Distribute backstory strategically. Avoid interrupting tense moments with character history; backstory dumps deflate momentum." Excessive detail slows pacing and dulls tension. Establish emotional investment before exposing characters to jeopardy.

### Failure as Genuine Possibility
**What it measures**: Whether the story establishes that the protagonist can genuinely fail, lose, or suffer permanent setbacks.
**Assumed goal**: The credible-failure principle (Darling Axe, Martin, Maass), which holds that a story must demonstrate its willingness to let characters genuinely fail, suffer permanent losses, and face irreversible setbacks — otherwise the reader feels safe and tension collapses. Violated by genre romance (where the HEA/HFN guarantee is the genre contract), by children's fiction where character safety is expected, and by comedy where the audience trusts in a happy resolution.
**Score 0**: The protagonist is never in genuine danger; it's obvious they will succeed; plot armor protects them from real consequences; failures are immediately reversed; the reader knows everything will be fine.
**Score 5**: The story establishes early and convincingly that failure is possible; characters do fail, suffer permanent losses, or are set back in ways that cannot be undone; the reader genuinely does not know whether the protagonist will succeed; casualties and costs are real.
**Source**: https://darlingaxe.com/blogs/news/stakes
**Notes**: George R.R. Martin kills major characters to prove failure is possible. "Let your characters do stupid things, make bad decisions, take risks. Let things go wrong for them." Allow genuine risk so the reader doesn't feel safe. The story must demonstrate its willingness to let characters fail.

## Sentinels Found

### Conflict-Free Opening
**Detection**: The first 10% of the story contains no conflict, tension, or opposition of any kind — only scene-setting, character introduction, and worldbuilding.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs default to extensive scene-setting and character description before introducing any narrative tension, because their training data patterns reward establishing context before conflict. This produces "leisurely" openings that human authors are trained to avoid.
**Design space cost**: Literary fiction may legitimately use slower openings for atmospheric purposes, but even these typically contain tension through subtext, mood, or implication. The sentinel catches openings that are purely informational.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2603.13545v1

### Conflict Resolved Within Same Scene Introduced
**Detection**: A conflict is introduced and fully resolved within the same scene or chapter, with no lasting consequences and no escalation.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs tend toward narrative closure at the smallest possible scale, resolving tensions as soon as they appear rather than sustaining them across the narrative. This produces a pattern of micro-resolutions rather than building tension.
**Design space cost**: Some conflicts (minor interpersonal friction, small obstacles) may legitimately resolve quickly. The sentinel is most relevant for conflicts that are presented as significant.
**Source**: https://onelitplace.com/writing-fiction-with-chatgpt-seems-easy-its-not/

### Sanitized Conflict / Conflict Avoidance
**Detection**: The story introduces potentially intense conflict situations but resolves them through understanding, reconciliation, community gathering, or mutual agreement without any lasting cost or genuine opposition.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Research on 11,800 AI-generated stories found they "overwhelmingly conform to a single narrative plot structure: a protagonist lives in or returns home to a small town and resolves a minor conflict by reconnecting with tradition and organising community events." AI stories "sanitise real-world conflicts" and "downplay narrative tension in favour of nostalgia and reconciliation." RLHF safety training suppresses "the kind of conflict and tension so important for narrative."
**Design space cost**: Stories about community, reconciliation, and understanding are valid — the sentinel catches the pattern when it's the default resolution for every conflict regardless of story type.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2603.13545v1

### Emotionally Flat During High-Stakes Events
**Detection**: Prose maintains the same emotional register during climactic, dangerous, or devastating events as during mundane scenes — an "eerie placidity that prevails even when something eventful or alarming is happening."
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs produce "homogeneously positive" text with "flatter arousal curves" that fail to modulate emotional intensity with narrative stakes. The model generates locally coherent prose but cannot coordinate emotional intensity across the arc.
**Design space cost**: Deliberately flat affect during extreme events is a valid literary technique (Camus, McCarthy) — but it is an intentional choice that creates its own form of tension through contrast. The sentinel catches accidental flatness rather than deliberate restraint.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2603.13545v1

### No-Cost Victory
**Detection**: The protagonist achieves their goal without sacrificing anything meaningful, without paying a price, and without permanent consequences from the conflict.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs default to satisfying narrative closure where protagonists get what they want and keep what they have. The model avoids generating text where characters genuinely lose things, because positive outcomes dominate training data patterns.
**Design space cost**: Comedies and feel-good stories may legitimately feature low-cost victories. The sentinel is most relevant for stories that have established serious stakes.
**Source**: https://darlingaxe.com/blogs/news/stakes

### Deus Ex Machina Resolution
**Detection**: The central conflict is resolved by a character, ability, object, or event that was not established in the story prior to the resolution.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate conflict and resolution semi-independently. The model may introduce a conflict in early tokens that it cannot resolve coherently in later tokens, leading to the introduction of unestablished solutions. This is a failure of long-range narrative planning.
**Design space cost**: Legitimate surprise twists involve elements that were subtly foreshadowed — the distinction is between "surprising but inevitable in retrospect" and "completely unearned."
**Source**: https://www.literautas.com/en/blog/post-1009/deus-ex-machina-what-is-it-and-how-to-avoid-it/

### Idiot Plot
**Detection**: The conflict could be immediately resolved if characters shared information they have no reason to withhold, or if they took the obvious action that any reasonable person would take.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs may generate conflict by withholding information between characters without establishing character-specific reasons for the withholding. The model knows what all characters know and must actively suppress this to create information asymmetry — a task it performs through pattern-matching rather than character modeling.
**Design space cost**: Miscommunication-based conflict is a legitimate literary device when characters have established, specific reasons for not sharing information (pride, shame, fear, distrust). The sentinel catches withholding that exists only to sustain the plot.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idiot_plot

### Passive Protagonist
**Detection**: The protagonist does not make any meaningful choices that drive the conflict forward; events happen to them but they do not cause events; they react but never act.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs tend to generate protagonists who observe and react because generating proactive character decisions requires modeling character psychology, predicting consequences, and maintaining consistency — all tasks where local token prediction struggles. It's easier to generate "then X happened" than "she decided to X because Y."
**Design space cost**: Deliberately passive protagonists exist in literary fiction (e.g., Meursault in The Stranger), but this is an intentional artistic choice that creates meaning through the passivity itself, not an accidental absence of agency.
**Source**: https://www.kevintjohns.com/are-you-killing-the-tension-high-stakes-writing-mistakes/

### Uniform Conflict Mode
**Detection**: Every conflict in the story is the same type (all interpersonal arguments, all action sequences, all internal monologue) with no variation in the mode of opposition.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs tend to repeat successful patterns. If the model generates an effective argument scene early, it will generate more argument scenes. This creates a monotonous conflict texture where the same mode of opposition repeats because the model optimizes for local coherence rather than global variety.
**Design space cost**: Genre fiction may legitimately emphasize one conflict mode (action thrillers emphasize physical conflict, literary fiction emphasizes internal conflict), but even within genres, variation is expected.
**Source**: https://frictionlit.org/crafting-compelling-conflict-the-heartbeat-of-storytelling/

### Therapy-Speak Resolution
**Detection**: Characters resolve conflicts by articulating their feelings directly, validating each other's emotions, and reaching mutual understanding through explicit emotional processing language ("I hear you," "I feel seen," "That must have been hard for you").
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs trained on internet text (including therapy content, self-help, and relationship advice) default to therapeutic communication patterns when resolving interpersonal conflict. This produces resolutions that feel like therapy sessions rather than dramatic scenes.
**Design space cost**: Stories specifically about therapy, emotional growth, or communication are legitimate. The sentinel catches the pattern when it appears as the default resolution style regardless of context or character.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2603.13545v1

### Stakes Stated But Not Demonstrated
**Detection**: The narrative tells the reader the stakes are high (characters say "the fate of the world depends on this") but never shows consequences, near-misses, or evidence that the stakes are real.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs can generate declarations of stakes without generating the narrative evidence that makes stakes feel real. "The world will end" is easy to generate; showing a character losing something irreplaceable as proof that stakes are real requires sustained narrative coherence.
**Design space cost**: None significant. This is a craft failure regardless of genre.
**Source**: https://darlingaxe.com/blogs/news/stakes

### Flat Escalation (No Rising Action)
**Detection**: Conflict intensity at the midpoint is not measurably higher than at the 20% mark; obstacles do not become more severe, personal, or costly as the story progresses.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate locally coherent scenes without tracking global narrative trajectory. Each scene may contain adequate conflict, but the overall escalation curve is flat because the model doesn't maintain a "tension level" variable that must increase.
**Design space cost**: Picaresque and episodic fiction may have intentionally flat escalation. The sentinel is most relevant for fiction with a single protagonist pursuing a specific goal.
**Source**: https://stormwritingschool.com/escalating-complications/

### Immediate Forgiveness After Betrayal
**Detection**: A character betrays, harms, or deeply wrongs another character, and the victim forgives or reconciles with the betrayer within the same scene or the next scene without a process of rebuilding trust.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs compress emotional arcs to minimize sustained negative affect. Betrayal-to-forgiveness is a pattern in training data, and the model shortcuts to the resolution without generating the intermediate stages of anger, distrust, testing, and gradual rebuilding.
**Design space cost**: Flash fiction and stories specifically about radical forgiveness may compress this arc. The sentinel is most relevant for novel-length fiction where emotional consequences should be sustained.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2603.13545v1

### Tension Without Payoff (Dangling Threat)
**Detection**: The narrative builds tension around a specific threat or danger that is then abandoned — never addressed, resolved, or paid off.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs can generate tension-building passages without maintaining a narrative ledger of what has been promised. A threat established in earlier context may fall outside the effective attention window for resolution, leaving it dangling.
**Design space cost**: Deliberately unresolved threats can create realistic messiness or thematic ambiguity. The sentinel catches accidental abandonment, not deliberate ambiguity.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/conflict-in-fiction-what-it-really-is-and-why-its-important-to-plot-2/

### All Conflict Is External
**Detection**: The protagonist faces only external opposition (fights, chases, obstacles) with no internal psychological conflict, self-doubt, or values-based struggle.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: External conflict is easier to generate because it's action-based and follows clear patterns. Internal conflict requires modeling psychology, generating authentic emotional ambivalence, and sustaining contradictory impulses — tasks where LLMs default to simpler external action.
**Design space cost**: Action-forward genre fiction may legitimately emphasize external conflict, but even the best action stories include some internal dimension.
**Source**: https://www.autocrit.com/internal-vs-external-conflict/

### Vague Antagonist Motivation
**Detection**: The primary antagonist opposes the protagonist without any established reason, goal, or worldview that explains why they oppose them.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate antagonists as structural functions ("someone who opposes the protagonist") rather than as characters with independent psychology. This produces opposition that exists to serve the plot rather than emerging from character logic.
**Design space cost**: Impersonal antagonistic forces (nature, disease, systemic oppression) may not have "motivation" in the character sense. The sentinel applies to character antagonists.
**Source**: https://janefriedman.com/adversaries-in-fiction-who-is-standing-in-your-characters-way/

### Conflict Through Misunderstanding Only
**Detection**: The primary driver of conflict is characters misunderstanding each other, where a single honest conversation would resolve everything.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Misunderstanding-based conflict is a simple pattern: keep characters from sharing information, then resolve by revealing it. LLMs default to this because it requires minimal psychological modeling — the conflict is mechanical (information withheld) rather than characterological (values in opposition).
**Design space cost**: Misunderstanding can drive plot when characters have established reasons for miscommunication. The sentinel catches it as the sole or primary conflict driver.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idiot_plot

### Escalation by External Event Only
**Detection**: Every escalation in the story comes from external events (new villain appears, natural disaster, discovery of information) rather than from consequences of the protagonist's own choices.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs can escalate by introducing new elements more easily than by tracing causal consequences of character decisions. Generating "then a new threat appeared" is simpler than generating "because she chose X, Y was now inevitable."
**Design space cost**: Some genres (disaster stories, invasion narratives) legitimately escalate through external events. The sentinel is most relevant for character-driven fiction.
**Source**: https://www.aboutwriting.org/how-to-use-rising-action-escalation-and-switches/

### No Dilemmas (Only Obstacles)
**Detection**: The protagonist faces only obstacles (things in the way that must be overcome) without ever facing genuine dilemmas (choices between two goods or two evils where something must be sacrificed).
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Obstacles are simpler to generate than dilemmas. "A wall blocks the path" requires the protagonist to find a way over/around/through. "Choose which of two people to save" requires modeling character values, generating internal deliberation, and producing lasting emotional consequences. LLMs default to the simpler structure.
**Design space cost**: Action-heavy genre fiction may emphasize obstacles over dilemmas. The sentinel catches the complete absence of dilemmas in fiction that aspires to emotional depth.
**Source**: https://lindasclare.com/2021/10/writing-conflict-obstacles-vs-dilemmas/

### Telescoped Emotional Processing
**Detection**: After major traumatic events (death, betrayal, devastating loss), characters process their grief or shock in a single paragraph or brief scene before moving on to the next plot event.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs compress the sequel (reaction-dilemma-decision) into minimal text because generating extended emotional processing requires sustained engagement with a single emotional state, which the model's token prediction optimizes away from in favor of advancing to the next event.
**Design space cost**: Fast-paced genre fiction may legitimately compress emotional processing. The sentinel catches the pattern when events that should be devastating receive minimal emotional weight.
**Source**: https://www.advancedfictionwriting.com/articles/writing-the-perfect-scene/

### Abstract Stakes Only
**Detection**: All stakes are abstract or global (the fate of the world, the future of humanity, the survival of the kingdom) with no specific, personal, concrete consequences for individual characters.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs can generate grand abstract stakes easily ("the world will end") because these are common training data patterns. Generating specific personal stakes ("she will lose the only person who ever believed in her") requires deeper character modeling.
**Design space cost**: Epic fiction may have legitimately global stakes, but even these require personal grounding to create reader investment.
**Source**: https://prowritingaid.com/three-levels-of-stakes

### Consequence-Free Action Scenes
**Detection**: Action sequences (fights, chases, confrontations) end with all participants essentially undamaged and unchanged — no injuries persist, no relationships are altered, no resources are lost.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate action as spectacle rather than as narrative event. The model can produce exciting moment-to-moment action but doesn't maintain a persistent world state where damage accumulates and consequences persist.
**Design space cost**: Certain genres (comedy, farce) may deliberately feature consequence-free action. The sentinel catches it in fiction that has established serious stakes.
**Source**: https://darlingaxe.com/blogs/news/stakes

### Single Emotion per Character per Scene
**Detection**: Each character in a scene experiences only one emotion throughout, with no conflicting feelings, ambivalence, or emotional complexity.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs assign an emotional tag to characters and maintain it through a scene. Generating conflicting simultaneous emotions requires the model to hold two contradictory states and express both through behavior — a task that conflicts with local coherence optimization.
**Design space cost**: Simple emotional states are appropriate for minor characters in brief scenes. The sentinel catches it in major characters during significant scenes where emotional complexity would be expected.
**Source**: https://www.livewritethrive.com/2014/10/08/infusing-microtension-in-your-novel/

### Homogeneous Narrative Structure
**Detection**: The story follows a single predictable pattern: protagonist faces problem, tries to solve it, encounters brief setback, ultimately succeeds, learns a lesson.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Analysis of AI stories found "overwhelming conformity to a single narrative plot structure." LLMs default to the most common narrative template in training data. This produces stories that feel interchangeable regardless of ostensible genre, setting, or character differences.
**Design space cost**: Simple narrative structures are valid for certain forms (fables, parables, children's stories). The sentinel catches the pattern in fiction that should have structural sophistication.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2603.13545v1

### Conflict Resolved by Realization
**Detection**: The climactic conflict is resolved because the protagonist "realizes" something (their true feelings, the real problem, what really matters) rather than through action, sacrifice, or confrontation.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Realization is the cheapest possible resolution — it requires no action, no sacrifice, no external change. LLMs default to epiphany-based resolution because generating an insight is simpler than generating a dramatically complex resolution involving multiple characters, consequences, and trade-offs.
**Design space cost**: Literary fiction may legitimately climax with moments of recognition or understanding, but even these typically require action or change as consequence of the realization.
**Source**: https://onelitplace.com/writing-fiction-with-chatgpt-seems-easy-its-not/

### Symmetrical Conflict (Neat Opposition)
**Detection**: Conflicts are structured as clean binaries (good vs. evil, truth vs. lies, love vs. hate) with no moral ambiguity, mixed motivations, or asymmetric power dynamics.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate conflict through pattern-matched opposition. If A wants X, the antagonist wants not-X. This produces neat symmetry rather than the messy, asymmetric conflicts that characterize real human experience. Training data is rich with simplified moral frameworks.
**Design space cost**: Fairy tales, myths, and certain genre fiction legitimately use binary opposition. The sentinel catches it in fiction aspiring to psychological or moral complexity.
**Source**: https://narrativefirst.com/articles/naturally-structuring-a-story-for-conflict/

### Melodramatic Stakes Inflation
**Detection**: The story immediately establishes world-ending or life-threatening stakes without earning them through character investment, starting at maximum intensity with nowhere to escalate.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs interpret "high stakes" literally and set them to maximum from the first scene. The model lacks the craft understanding that stakes need to be built, not declared, and that starting at maximum leaves no room for escalation.
**Design space cost**: In medias res openings and certain genre conventions (espionage, thriller) may legitimately begin with high stakes. The sentinel catches the pattern when high stakes are declared but not felt.
**Source**: https://www.writingmastery.com/blog/tips-for-raising-the-stakes

### Character Wound Never Activated
**Detection**: A character's backstory establishes a psychological wound, trauma, or vulnerability that is never meaningfully tested, exploited, or addressed by the story's conflicts.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate character backstory and plot conflict somewhat independently. The model may establish a character wound (dead parent, past failure, trust issues) without later generating conflicts that specifically target that wound, because the two elements are generated in different context windows.
**Design space cost**: Minor characters may have wounds that aren't central to the plot. The sentinel applies to the protagonist's primary wound.
**Source**: https://www.ian-irvine.com/for-writers/article-5-how-to-create-conflict/

### Conflict Contradicts Established Character
**Detection**: A character behaves in ways that directly contradict their established personality, values, or capabilities solely to create conflict — acting stupid, cruel, or reckless when they have been established as thoughtful, kind, or careful.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate scenes semi-independently, and a character's established traits in earlier scenes may not be strongly activated when generating later scenes that need conflict. The model may generate conflict by forcing characters to behave out of character because conflict is the local optimization target.
**Design space cost**: Character behavior that seems contradictory can be a legitimate sign of hidden depths, pressure, or development — but only if the change is explained or explored. The sentinel catches unexplained contradiction.
**Source**: https://www.aboutwriting.org/how-to-use-rising-action-escalation-and-switches/

### Every Scene Is a Fight
**Detection**: Every scene involves direct confrontation (argument, physical fight, shouting match) with no scenes of tension, anticipation, dread, planning, or emotional processing.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs equate "conflict" with "confrontation" and generate continuous direct opposition. This conflates conflict with tension, missing the entire repertoire of quiet dread, anticipatory anxiety, subtext, and emotional pressure that creates sustainable dramatic engagement.
**Design space cost**: Action-heavy genre fiction may have a high proportion of confrontation scenes, but even these need variety in mode and intensity.
**Source**: https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2017/03/tension-vs-conflict-hint-they-arent.html

### No Subtext in Dialogue
**Detection**: Characters in conflict always say exactly what they mean, feel, and want; there is no gap between surface meaning and underlying intention; no character ever conceals, deflects, or implies.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Subtext requires the model to generate text that means something different from what it literally says — a dual-signal task where the surface text and underlying meaning must diverge. LLMs optimize for clear communication, making subtext architecturally difficult.
**Design space cost**: Some characters in some contexts speak directly. The sentinel catches the complete absence of subtext across all characters and all scenes.
**Source**: https://www.writersdigest.com/write-better-fiction/how-to-use-subtext-and-the-art-of-dramatic-tension-in-fiction

### Too-Neat Ending
**Detection**: Every conflict thread is resolved, every character gets what they need, loose ends are tied up, and the ending produces complete closure with no lingering questions, costs, or ambiguity.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs trained on fiction patterns resolve toward closure because most training data stories end with resolution. The model maximizes narrative completion at the cost of realistic messiness. AI-generated stories "tend to provide too neat of an ending with melodramatic emotions."
**Design space cost**: Genre fiction (romance, mystery) may legitimately provide clean closure. The sentinel catches the pattern when the story has established complex, layered conflicts that realistically would leave residue.
**Source**: https://onelitplace.com/writing-fiction-with-chatgpt-seems-easy-its-not/
