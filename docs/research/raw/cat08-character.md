## Criteria Found

### Want/Need Tension
**What it measures**: Whether the protagonist has a conscious external desire (Want) in productive tension with an unconscious internal need (Need), and whether the story forces confrontation between them.
**Assumed goal**: Character-arc-driven fiction in the K.M. Weiland / John Truby tradition, where internal transformation is the engine of meaning -- plot-first genre fiction (e.g., procedurals, hard SF) may subordinate internal need to external problem-solving without loss of quality.
**Score 0**: Characters have no distinguishable want or need; or want and need are identical, producing no internal tension; or the need is never confronted, leaving the character arc inert.
**Score 5**: The Want emerges organically from a specific Lie the character believes; the Need directly opposes it; the plot repeatedly forces choices between them; the climax demands sacrifice of one for the other; resolution feels both surprising and inevitable.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/character-arcs-3/
**Notes**: Weiland's framework: Want = conscious desire from limited perspective (the Lie); Need = the Truth that heals the root cause. The Want is often a "false solution" addressing symptoms. Common pitfall: Want and Need too similar, eliminating the fulcrum of inner change. The want/need framework has four explanation methods per September C. Fawkes: Ghost vs Ghostbuster, External vs Internal, Flawed vs Accurate Worldview, Anti-Theme vs Theme.

### Character Agency
**What it measures**: Whether the protagonist's choices and actions drive the plot, as opposed to events happening to or around a passive character.
**Assumed goal**: Western narrative tradition (Aristotle through McKee) where protagonist agency is considered essential to dramatic engagement -- some literary traditions (e.g., naturalism, certain postmodern fiction) deliberately subvert agency to make thematic points about powerlessness.
**Score 0**: The protagonist is a witness or bystander; events would unfold identically without them; other characters solve problems on their behalf; the character makes no meaningful choices.
**Score 5**: The protagonist initiates action, makes choices with real consequences, and directly causes the story's turning points; even moments of apparent passivity are revealed as deliberate choices; the reader can trace causal lines from character decisions to plot outcomes.
**Source**: https://janefriedman.com/who-makes-it-happen-giving-your-characters-agency/
**Notes**: Four agency failure modes identified: The Witness (present but observing), The Bystander (plot happens adjacent), The Recipient (benefits from others' actions), The Victim (endures hardship without seizing control). Key test: "Would events have happened the same way if your character hadn't been there?" Agency requires both clear goals (overarching and scene-level) and demonstrated will/intention.

### Character Arc Execution
**What it measures**: Whether the character's internal transformation (or deliberate stasis, in flat arcs) is structured, paced, and earned across the full narrative.
**Assumed goal**: Transformative arc fiction (the dominant Western storytelling paradigm from Aristotle to Save the Cat) -- episodic fiction, picaresque, and some literary minimalism may deliberately avoid transformation arcs.
**Score 0**: Character undergoes no change, or changes abruptly without groundwork; arc is crammed into the climax; growth contradicts established characterization; transformation feels unearned because obstacles were trivial.
**Score 5**: Arc follows a traceable progression with setbacks, regressions, and partial victories; change emerges from repeated challenge to the character's core belief; the climax forces definitive choice; transformation is costly and hard-won; even a flat arc demonstrates consistent testing of the character's truth.
**Source**: https://rivereditor.com/blogs/character-arc-guide-transformation-2026
**Notes**: Three primary arc types: Positive/Growth (Lie to Truth), Negative/Corruption (stability to ruin), Flat/Steadfast (character transforms the world). Key criterion for "earned" transformation: "Growth cannot be easy or it feels unearned -- make change costly and difficult, achieved through failure and forced reckoning." Characters cannot be cowardly for 200 pages then spontaneously brave in the final chapter. Arc consists of: Initial State, Challenges, Transformation, Final State.

### Ghost/Wound/Lie/Weakness Architecture
**What it measures**: Whether the character's backstory trauma (Ghost), resulting psychological damage (Wound), protective false belief (Lie), and behavioral expression (Weakness) form a coherent, integrated system that drives both character and plot.
**Assumed goal**: Psychologically grounded fiction (literary fiction, character-driven genre fiction) where behavior must be motivated by interior logic -- fabulist, allegorical, or purely plot-driven fiction may treat characters as functions rather than psychologies.
**Score 0**: Character has no discernible backstory motivation; or backstory exists but doesn't connect to present behavior; or the Lie is stated but never tested; or weakness is cosmetic rather than consequential.
**Score 5**: A specific Ghost event produces a identifiable Wound that generates a coherent Lie the character uses as coping mechanism; this Lie manifests as observable Weakness that creates real consequences in the story; the plot systematically challenges the Lie until the character must either abandon it (positive arc) or double down (negative arc).
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/whats-the-difference-your-characters-ghost-vs-wound-vs-lie-vs-weakness/
**Notes**: Ghost = the incident (car crash, divorce); Wound = the impact (belief of unworthiness); Lie = coping mechanism/false worldview; Weakness = behavioral expression. Three categories of Ghost: guilt, grief, grievance. The Lie initially served a protective function but becomes increasingly ineffective. Multiple weaknesses may exist, but the central one should connect organically to the Ghost-Wound-Lie structure.

### Character Complexity Through Contradiction
**What it measures**: Whether characters contain structured internal oppositions that generate narrative energy, rather than being defined by a single trait or role.
**Assumed goal**: Literary fiction and prestige television (Dostoevsky, Henry James, The Sopranos) where psychological complexity is the primary subject -- genre fiction with archetypal characters (fairy tales, certain adventure fiction) may deliberately use non-contradictory characters as functional elements.
**Score 0**: Characters are defined by a single trait; all behavior is predictable from one quality; characters never surprise the reader; no tension exists between what characters want and what they do.
**Score 5**: Characters embody credible, structured contradictions (between desires and behavior, between stated principles and enacted choices, between public persona and private self); contradictions are consistent and fundamental, not random; the reader can trace every surprise back to an established tension; characters function as "thematic operators" testing propositions about belief and action.
**Source**: https://bookishbay.com/character-complexity-in-literary-fiction/
**Notes**: Robert McKee: "Dimension means contradiction: either within deep character (guilt-ridden ambition) or between characterization and deep character (a charming thief)." Contradictions must be consistent -- a nice guy who randomly kicks a cat in one scene doesn't add dimension. Key examples: Raskolnikov (intellectual ambition vs psychological collapse), Isabel Archer (independence ideals vs marital submission), Stevens (professional persona vs emotional emptiness). McKee notes TV characters like Tony Soprano can be "12-dimensional" through accumulated contradiction across episodes.

### Ensemble Differentiation
**What it measures**: Whether each member of a multi-character cast is psychologically distinct, functionally non-redundant, and individually indispensable to the narrative.
**Assumed goal**: Multi-POV or large-cast fiction (epic fantasy, literary ensemble novels, television) where reader investment must be distributed across many characters -- single-protagonist fiction evaluates this differently, focusing on supporting cast serving the protagonist's arc.
**Score 0**: Characters are interchangeable; multiple characters serve the same function; all characters sound alike in dialogue; removing a character would not change the story; cast is too large for the story's length.
**Score 5**: Each character has a unique psychology, voice, goal, and thematic perspective; no two characters could swap roles; each character's presence is justified by a contribution only they can make; the cast creates a web of distinct, conflicting perspectives that illuminates the central conflict from multiple angles.
**Source**: https://thenovelsmithy.com/ensemble-cast/
**Notes**: Four ensemble types identified: The Mystery Machine (unified group), The Character Web (converging separate storylines), Polar Perspectives (opposing sides with glue characters), The Thematic Cast (connected by theme only). Key test for each ensemble member: "Does this character warrant their own POV?" Each member needs: strong connection to core conflict, unique problem-solving capacity, clear motivations, thematic relevance, distinctive perspective unavailable elsewhere, stakes and potential losses, conflict generation with other characters. Common pitfalls: oversized casts, character redundancy, lost focus, weak introductions, insufficient story length for development.

### Character Voice Distinctiveness
**What it measures**: Whether each character speaks, thinks, and expresses themselves in ways unique to their personality, background, and emotional state, such that characters are identifiable without dialogue tags.
**Assumed goal**: Fiction where dialogue carries significant narrative weight (literary realism, drama-heavy genre fiction) -- highly stylized or narrator-dominated fiction (e.g., Nabokov, some first-person literary fiction) may deliberately filter all voices through the narrator's voice.
**Score 0**: All characters speak in the same register, vocabulary, and rhythm; dialogue is interchangeable between characters; the "cover the name" test fails completely; characters sound like the author rather than themselves.
**Score 5**: Each character has distinct vocabulary, syntax, rhythm, and speech patterns reflecting their background, education, psychology, and emotional state; readers can identify speakers without tags; voice reveals character through what is said, what is avoided, and how things are phrased; internal monologue is as distinctive as spoken dialogue.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/character-voices/
**Notes**: The "Cover the Name" test (from screenwriting): cover character names above dialogue and try to identify who is speaking. If you can't tell, voices need more distinction. Voice goes beyond word choice to encompass rhythm, syntax, vocabulary, and underlying attitudes. A medieval history professor should use different vocabulary than a street criminal. Key elements: word choice, speech patterns, pacing, sentence structure, personality traits expressed through language.

### Backstory Integration
**What it measures**: Whether character history is revealed through organic narrative techniques rather than expository dumps, and whether revealed backstory serves present-story purposes.
**Assumed goal**: Modern fiction craft (post-Hemingway "iceberg theory") where information is earned through story engagement -- 19th-century omniscient narration and some epic fantasy traditions may use more direct exposition without penalty.
**Score 0**: Backstory delivered in large expository blocks, especially in opening chapters; backstory info-dumped through "as you know, Bob" dialogue; backstory present but disconnected from present-story tension; too much backstory too soon destroying forward momentum.
**Score 5**: Backstory revealed through the "drip" technique -- small, strategically placed revelations throughout the narrative; each revelation is triggered by present-story need; backstory emerges through dialogue, physical discovery, character behavior, and triggered memories; revelations build suspense and deepen emotional stakes; the reader learns history just when they most need it.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/backstory-drip-pt-3-of-3/
**Notes**: The "backstory drip" combines several techniques: dialogue-based revelation, physical/visual discovery, plot-driven revelation, and behavioral implication. Key principle: "Hold these details back as long as you can. Reveal them gradually throughout your novel in clever, imaginative ways." The biggest mistake: delivering too much information too soon because writers want readers to understand everything before the story proper begins.

### Interiority Balance
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative provides appropriate access to the POV character's internal experience (thoughts, feelings, memories, reactions) without overwhelming the story's forward momentum or underserving the character's emotional life.
**Assumed goal**: Close-POV fiction (most modern literary and commercial fiction) where the reader occupies the character's consciousness -- distant-POV, cinematic, or behaviorist fiction (Hemingway, Hammett, some thriller writing) deliberately restricts interiority as a stylistic choice.
**Score 0**: Either no interiority (reads like a screenplay with only dialogue and action) leaving the reader disconnected from the character; or excessive interiority (continuous internal commentary) that bogs down pacing and makes external action feel like slow motion.
**Score 5**: Interiority and exteriority are balanced according to genre and story needs (not necessarily 50/50 -- may be 70/30 or 80/20); internal processing occurs at dramatically appropriate moments; interiority reveals what external action cannot; thoughts contradict or complicate dialogue, creating subtext; the character's unique way of processing the world is itself characterizing.
**Source**: https://ascribewriters.com/finding-the-sweet-balance-between-interiority-and-exteriority-in-fiction/
**Notes**: Genre affects expected balance: romance skews toward more interiority; action/adventure skews toward exteriority; psychological thrillers require deep interiority. Signs of too much: character repeatedly interrupts action with thoughts within a single page; pacing becomes sluggish. Signs of too little: reader confusion, lack of emotional stakes, characters feel observed from the outside even in close POV. Best added when: a word or sentence triggers something important; character senses deception; internal thoughts contradict dialogue.

### Emotional Authenticity
**What it measures**: Whether characters' emotional responses are specific to their individual psychology, history, and circumstances rather than generic or formulaic.
**Assumed goal**: Fiction prioritizing psychological realism (literary fiction, psychological thriller, character-driven romance) -- genre fiction with more externalized emotional conventions (action, certain SF) may rely on broader emotional strokes without penalty.
**Score 0**: Characters express emotions through generic, interchangeable reactions (e.g., "her heart pounded," "tears streamed down her face"); emotional responses don't reflect the specific character's personality or history; emotions are named/told rather than demonstrated; all characters react identically to similar stimuli.
**Score 5**: Each character's emotional response is tailored to their specific psychology, wound, and history; emotions are layered (multiple simultaneous feelings including contradictory ones); emotional arcs are nonlinear with regressions and backtracking; physical/behavioral manifestations of emotion are character-specific and surprising yet credible; the writer has clearly explored what each emotion means to this particular character.
**Source**: https://fictionflourish.com/character-crafting/authentic-emotions-in-character-writing
**Notes**: Donald Maass's "third-level emotions" technique: identify primary emotion, ask what else the character feels, ask again to deepen complexity. True emotional arcs are "messy, nonlinear, and complex, often involving backtracking." Writers must know their characters' important relationships and impactful experiences to create tailored emotional responses. The biggest reason emotion fails: "the writer hasn't gotten to know their character well enough to create tailored responses."

### Subtext in Character Expression
**What it measures**: Whether characters communicate through implication, withholding, and behavioral contradiction rather than stating their thoughts and feelings directly.
**Assumed goal**: Fiction in the realist tradition (Chekhov, Hemingway, contemporary literary fiction) where the gap between what is said and what is meant generates dramatic power -- didactic fiction, children's literature, or certain comedic traditions may use more direct expression.
**Score 0**: All dialogue is on-the-nose (characters say exactly what they mean); characters answer every question directly; motivations are explicitly stated rather than revealed through behavior; no gap exists between spoken word and underlying desire; readers are told everything.
**Score 5**: Characters habitually communicate through indirection, deflection, and implication; dialogue carries layered meaning below the surface; body language and behavior contradict or complicate spoken words; what characters don't say is as revealing as what they do say; the reader participates actively in interpreting character meaning.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/on-the-nose-dialogue/
**Notes**: Subtext defined as "the unconscious implicit desire that motivates a character to behave explicitly." The "layer cake" model: top layer = dialogue/behavior; bottom layer = conscious/unconscious desire; middle = the murky unspoken material. Four techniques for amplifying subtext: limit POV count, limit POV character's insight, create dimensional characters with hidden depths, trust your characters (don't explain their behavior). On-the-nose dialogue is "a credibility killer" -- when readers see it, they assume the author lacks subtlety throughout.

### Reader-Character Identification Bond
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative establishes sufficient emotional connection between reader and character to sustain investment across the full story.
**Assumed goal**: Commercial and literary fiction where reader engagement depends on caring about the protagonist -- experimental fiction, satire, or Brechtian distancing may deliberately prevent identification to force critical distance.
**Score 0**: Reader has no reason to worry about, root for, or be fascinated by the character; no empathy-building techniques employed in opening; character is neither sympathetic, fascinating, nor relatably flawed; bond is attempted through description rather than demonstrated emotion.
**Score 5**: Multiple identification techniques are deployed early and layered throughout: sympathy (undeserved misfortune), jeopardy (physical/emotional/reputational danger), likability (demonstrated kindness, humor, competence), fascination (mastery, courage, contradiction), wish-fulfillment, familiarity, relatable goals, and sensory-emotional detail; bond is established before flaws are revealed; even unsympathetic protagonists are compelling through fascination or moral code.
**Source**: https://writeswithtools.com/2018/01/29/the-character-identification-bond/
**Notes**: H.R. D'Costa's "imprinting" concept: once bonded, readers metaphorically follow a character anywhere. Critical timing: approximately 10 pages for novels, 5 pages for agent submissions. Sequencing rule: establish bonds first, then reveal flaws. Nine methods ranked: universal emotion, sympathetic circumstances, strategic jeopardy, likability, fascination, wish-fulfillment, familiarity, relatable goals, empathy through sensory detail. For anti-heroes: establish a personal code of honor, use emotional withholding, surround with worse characters.

### Relationship Dynamics
**What it measures**: Whether character relationships are varied, evolving, and revelatory -- each relationship drawing out different facets of the characters involved.
**Assumed goal**: Fiction where interpersonal dynamics carry narrative weight (literary fiction, romance, family saga, ensemble drama) -- solo survival narratives or philosophical fiction with minimal interaction may have legitimately sparse relationship dynamics.
**Score 0**: All relationships feel the same; characters interact identically with everyone; no power dynamics, history, or tension between characters; relationships are static and do not evolve; the protagonist shows the same face to all characters.
**Score 5**: Every relationship dynamic is distinct; characters show different facets depending on who they're with; relationships have their own arcs with evolution over time; power dynamics are present and shift; characters bring out contradictions in each other; conflict exists (large or small) in every pairing; the protagonist's behavior changes depending on the relationship context.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/9-ways-to-approach-relationship-dynamics-in-fiction/
**Notes**: Truby's principle: every character should be in some form of conflict with every other character. Six key polarities: Friend/Enemy, Superior/Subordinate, Attack/Defend/Withdraw, Passive/Aggressive, Positive/Negative, Confident/Insecure. Three primary character types: Protagonist, Antagonist, Relationship Character (inspires/impacts protagonist's growth). Each relationship should differ from others; characters shouldn't all react identically to the protagonist. "The relationship IS a character" -- it has its own arc, needs, and trajectory.

### Character Credibility
**What it measures**: Whether characters' actions, thoughts, and dialogue remain logically consistent with their established traits, the story's internal logic, and the specific moment in the narrative.
**Assumed goal**: Fiction operating within any mimetic tradition (realism, genre realism, secondary-world realism) where the reader's willingness to accept the character as "real within this world" is essential -- absurdist, surrealist, or allegorical fiction may deliberately violate credibility for artistic effect.
**Score 0**: Characters act in ways that contradict their established traits without explanation; emotional responses are mismatched to situations; characters suddenly acquire skills or knowledge they shouldn't have; the reader frequently thinks "this person would never do that."
**Score 5**: Characters' actions remain consistent with their established psychology even as they change; emotional responses match the specific moment and the character's history; surprises come from the revelation of hidden depth, not arbitrary behavior; the reader experiences even shocking character actions as retrospectively inevitable; credibility is maintained within the story world's own rules.
**Source**: https://www.storyinliteraryfiction.com/essays-on-writing/credibility/
**Notes**: "Credibility is judged by the story world created by the author and told by the narrator." Important distinction: characters can evolve and change, but even as they do, their behavior should remain recognizable as them. Common failure modes: lazy plot choices, inconsistent characterization, mismatched emotional responses, illogical progressions. Emotional scenes work best with "terse, clear, concrete language" rather than overwrought intensity.

### Character-Driven Stakes
**What it measures**: Whether what's at risk in the story is rooted in what the character specifically and intensely cares about, creating personal investment that generates reader investment.
**Assumed goal**: Fiction where emotional engagement is the primary reader experience (literary fiction, romance, thriller, drama) -- puzzle fiction or idea-driven SF may root stakes in intellectual rather than personal-emotional investment.
**Score 0**: Stakes are abstract or disconnected from what the character values; the character shows no emotional investment in outcomes; stakes exist only at the plot level with no personal dimension; the character faces no meaningful dilemmas.
**Score 5**: Stakes operate on multiple levels simultaneously (public/personal/philosophical); what's at risk is deeply tied to the character's specific values, relationships, and identity; the character faces genuine dilemmas where all choices carry cost; stakes escalate through both external pressure and deepening personal investment; even small-scale stakes feel enormous because the character cares enormously.
**Source**: https://tiffanyyatesmartin.medium.com/high-stakes-fiction-making-your-reader-care-2130e00c8611
**Notes**: Three levels of stakes: public (drives plot), personal (drives character), philosophical (drives theme). "Readers don't care what happens until they care whom it's happening to." Key technique: conflicting desires create a "crucible" (Sol Stein's term). Stakes don't need to be dramatic or dangerous -- tonally appropriate stakes in a quiet novel can be as effective. Common failure: making choices based on ambivalence rather than conflicted desire. Stakes are "the fulcrum on which everything else balances."

### Meaningful vs Cosmetic Flaws
**What it measures**: Whether character flaws actively create consequences and drive conflict rather than existing as decorative traits that never cost the character anything.
**Assumed goal**: Fiction where character flaws serve narrative and thematic function (most craft traditions from Aristotle's hamartia onward) -- picaresque or comedic traditions may use flaws for entertainment value without requiring consequence.
**Score 0**: Character has no flaws (Mary Sue/Gary Stu); or flaws are purely cosmetic ("clumsy but it's cute"); flaws never create real consequences; flaws are stated but never demonstrated in action; flaws don't connect to the character's arc or the story's conflicts.
**Score 5**: Flaws are deeply connected to the character's wound and Lie; they actively create problems at the worst possible moments; flaws cost the character things they can't easily recover (relationships, trust, opportunities, safety); flaws fuel both internal and external conflict; flaws are the engine of the character's arc (what must be overcome or what destroys them).
**Source**: https://www.well-storied.com/blog/how-to-craft-impactful-character-flaws
**Notes**: "One of the quickest ways to weaken a character is to give them flaws that never matter." The most compelling flaws connect directly to the growth arc and central conflicts. A flaw should "show up at the worst possible moment and make things harder, not easier." Cosmetic flaws (quirky clumsiness, endearing awkwardness) don't create tension. The Mary Sue problem is essentially the absence of meaningful flaws -- characters who face no real consequences and have answers for every problem.

### Foil and Mirror Cast Architecture
**What it measures**: Whether supporting characters are designed to illuminate the protagonist through contrast (foils) and parallel (mirrors), serving architectural rather than merely decorative functions.
**Assumed goal**: Thematically structured fiction (literary fiction, prestige drama) where the cast is designed as a system of perspectives on the central question -- adventure fiction or procedurals may use supporting characters primarily as functional roles (the expert, the comic relief) without thematic architecture.
**Score 0**: Supporting characters exist solely for plot convenience; no character reflects or contrasts the protagonist's journey; supporting cast could be removed without thematic loss; all characters represent the same perspective.
**Score 5**: Mirror characters share the protagonist's wound or flaw but diverge in resolution, forcing the protagonist to confront possible futures; foil characters operate within the same institutional/goal framework but challenge the protagonist's ethics, strategy, or reasoning; together, the cast creates a thematic argument where every character represents a different answer to the story's central question.
**Source**: https://www.notmfa.com/craft/form-not-formula-mirrors-and-foils
**Notes**: Mirror characters serve resonance (internal) -- they force protagonists to confront possible futures. Foils serve resistance (external) -- they defend alternative worldviews. Key distinction from antagonists: foils illuminate through juxtaposition, not opposition. Failure modes: flattening mirrors into copies, mislabeling any disagreement as foiling, using contrast as aesthetic rather than architectural. Scene-level test: "Mirrors keep protagonists emotionally honest; foils force ideological defense."

### Antagonist Complexity
**What it measures**: Whether the antagonistic force has its own coherent motivation, internal logic, and psychological depth rather than being evil for its own sake.
**Assumed goal**: Fiction where the antagonist is a developed character rather than a force of nature (literary fiction, character-driven thriller, complex fantasy) -- horror, fairy tale, and some adventure fiction may use purely archetypal or elemental antagonists effectively.
**Score 0**: Antagonist is evil for evil's sake with no coherent motivation; villain has no internal logic or perspective; antagonist exists solely as an obstacle with no psychology; villain's actions make no sense from their own perspective.
**Score 5**: The antagonist believes they are the hero of their own story; their goals make sense from their perspective even if their methods are wrong; they have conflicting desires, moral ambiguity, or internal struggle; their complexity creates unpredictability; they are consistent in actions, language, and motivations within the logic of their own personality and background.
**Source**: https://atmospherepress.com/what-makes-a-good-villain/
**Notes**: "The biggest mistake in villain writing is creating antagonists who are evil for evil's sake." Compelling antagonists may act out of fear, desperation, or genuine belief they serve the greater good. A complex, multifaceted antagonist creates more tension because knowing they aren't fully evil introduces unpredictability. The antagonist should have internal logic that the reader can follow even while disagreeing with.

### Character-as-Theme
**What it measures**: Whether the character's journey, psychology, and transformation embody and investigate the story's thematic questions rather than merely existing alongside them.
**Assumed goal**: Literary fiction and thematically ambitious genre fiction where character and theme are inseparable (Dostoevsky, Morrison, Le Guin) -- entertainment-first fiction may treat theme as emergent rather than architecturally integrated with character.
**Score 0**: Character's journey has no connection to the story's thematic concerns; theme is stated through dialogue or narration rather than demonstrated through character experience; character arc and thematic arc are disconnected; supporting characters don't represent different perspectives on the theme.
**Score 5**: The character's internal struggle (Lie vs Truth) directly embodies the story's thematic question; the character's arc IS the theme being tested through action; supporting characters represent different answers to the same thematic question; the character's transformation (or failure to transform) constitutes the story's thematic argument; plot events force the character to confront the theme through personal experience rather than abstraction.
**Source**: https://quickcreator.io/writing/character-development-theme-fiction-data-analysis/
**Notes**: "Character arc reveals and proves theme, while plot structure creates and unfolds character arc." Minor characters serve thematic architecture by representing alternative positions. The want typically embodies the anti-thematic statement (the Lie); the need aligns with the thematic statement (the Truth). Complex characters function as "thematic operators, transforming plot into a structured investigation of motive, belief, and action."

### Flat vs Round Character Deployment
**What it measures**: Whether the author makes deliberate, effective choices about which characters receive full psychological development (round) versus serving as functional types (flat), deploying each appropriately.
**Assumed goal**: Novels requiring both types per E.M. Forster's framework -- short fiction may use primarily flat or primarily round characters effectively given space constraints.
**Score 0**: Protagonist is flat (definable by a single trait, incapable of surprise) when roundness is needed; or every character is equally developed, causing narrative bloat; or flat characters are so thin they break believability; no intentional deployment strategy is visible.
**Score 5**: Protagonist and key characters are round (capable of surprising the reader without losing credibility); supporting characters are deliberately flat where appropriate (immediately recognizable, never need reintroducing, serve clear narrative functions); the collision between flat and round characters "parallels life more accurately" (Forster); the author shows awareness that flatness and roundness are tools, not qualities.
**Source**: https://fictioneditorsopinions.com/2018/flat-and-round-characters/
**Notes**: E.M. Forster (Aspects of the Novel, 1927): flat characters "constructed around a single idea or quality," round characters "sufficiently complex to be able to surprise the reader without losing credibility." Flat characters are useful: "never need reintroducing, never run away, have not to be watched for development." Dickens excels at flat characters (Miss Havisham). "The complexity of the novel often requires flat people as well as round, and the outcome of their collisions parallels life more accurately."

### Character Self-Revelation
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative includes moments where characters discover truths about themselves that the reader has been prepared to understand, creating sudden emotional and thematic clarity.
**Assumed goal**: Transformative-arc fiction in the John Truby / Robert McKee tradition where self-revelation is the climactic payoff -- picaresque, slice-of-life, or deliberately anti-epiphanic fiction (e.g., Carver) may resist revelation.
**Score 0**: Characters never gain insight into their own behavior or beliefs; or revelation is told through narration rather than enacted; or the character "realizes" something without any preceding evidence of the internal struggle.
**Score 5**: The moment of self-revelation is sudden for maximum dramatic force; it creates a burst of shared emotion between character and reader; it presents new information showing the character has been living a lie; it triggers immediate new moral action; the revelation has been carefully prepared through preceding events so it feels both surprising and inevitable.
**Source**: https://www.thestorydepartment.com/character-change-vs-revelation/
**Notes**: Two types: Character Change (fundamental transformation through events) vs Character Revelation (hidden complexity revealed through events). Truby's technique: begin at the end of the change with the self-revelation, then determine the starting point, then figure out steps between. "Revelation is perhaps the single most powerful element of storytelling -- without revelation there can be no realization and therefore no learning experience."

### Emotional Mode Deployment
**What it measures**: Whether the author uses appropriate techniques to generate reader emotion rather than merely describing character emotion (the distinction between character feeling and reader feeling).
**Assumed goal**: Commercial and literary fiction per Donald Maass's framework where the reader's emotional experience is the measure of success -- minimalist fiction may deliberately withhold emotional access as technique.
**Score 0**: Emotions are exclusively told ("She felt angry"); no technique beyond naming emotions is employed; the author assumes that describing a character's emotion will make the reader feel it; emotional scenes rely on charged language rather than dramatic technique.
**Score 5**: The author deploys multiple emotional modes: Inner Mode (stated emotions used creatively, not just named), Outer Mode (action and behavior that provoke reader emotions matching characters'), and Other Mode (a dialogue between reader and writer through description, dialogue, and detail); emotional scenes use the "third-level emotions" technique (layering primary emotion with secondary and tertiary feelings); character-specific sensory details ground emotions in scene.
**Source**: https://litreactor.com/columns/donald-mass-and-the-emotional-craft-of-fiction-writing
**Notes**: Maass's key insight: "Characters on page aren't real; reader emotions are real." The most useful question is not "how to show what characters are going through" but "how to get readers to go on an emotional journey of their own." Technique for deepening: identify the primary emotion, ask what else the character feels, ask again. Then evaluate through objectification/analogy, moral judgment, emotional alternatives, and justification.

### Character Consistency Under Change
**What it measures**: Whether characters remain recognizably themselves even as they grow, change, and evolve throughout the narrative.
**Assumed goal**: Fiction of any genre where characters are meant to be experienced as persistent identities across narrative time -- some experimental or postmodern fiction deliberately fragments character identity.
**Score 0**: Characters change personality without explanation; established traits disappear randomly; the character in chapter 20 is unrecognizable from chapter 1 without any traceable development; goals and motivations shift without cause.
**Score 5**: Characters evolve along logical trajectories connected to theme and plot; even after significant transformation, core personality traits remain recognizable; changes in behavior can be traced back through the chain of events that caused them; the reader experiences the character as a continuous identity who has grown rather than been replaced.
**Source**: https://sparks1524.com/2022/04/27/the-writers-craft-keeping-characters-consistent/
**Notes**: "Characters don't need to be static -- they can evolve, grow, change, and mature -- but even as they do, they and their behavior should remain recognizable as them." Track characters' traits, backstories, and development to avoid inconsistencies. Consistent goals make actions believable; when motivations change unexpectedly without reason, it creates disconnection.

### Three-Level Stakes Architecture
**What it measures**: Whether stakes operate at public (plot), personal (character), and philosophical (theme) levels simultaneously, creating layered investment.
**Assumed goal**: Ambitious fiction (literary or genre) where multiple levels of meaning operate simultaneously -- simpler genre fiction may effectively operate on one or two levels.
**Score 0**: Stakes exist at only one level (usually plot); personal stakes are absent or disconnected from plot stakes; no philosophical dimension; stakes feel external and arbitrary rather than rooted in character.
**Score 5**: Public stakes drive the plot and make the protagonist's goals feel significant; personal stakes drive the character and make readers care about the protagonist and their relationships; philosophical stakes drive the theme and add deeper meaning; all three levels reinforce each other and escalate together; the character's personal investment makes abstract stakes concrete.
**Source**: https://prowritingaid.com/three-levels-of-stakes
**Notes**: Public stakes = what happens to the world/community; personal stakes = what happens to the character's relationships and identity; philosophical stakes = what the outcome means about the human condition. The most effective fiction operates on all three simultaneously. Even world-ending scenarios succeed because of personal-level stakes. The character's personal investment is what makes the reader care about any of it.

### Idiot Plot Avoidance
**What it measures**: Whether the plot avoids depending on characters acting with implausible stupidity, withholding obvious information, or making decisions that no person of their established intelligence would make.
**Assumed goal**: Fiction where characters are meant to be taken seriously as intelligent agents (most fiction outside of farce or satire) -- comedy of errors or absurdist fiction may deliberately use characters acting foolishly.
**Score 0**: The entire plot depends on characters not sharing critical information that any reasonable person would share; characters make decisions that contradict their established intelligence for no reason other than plot convenience; the story would end in five minutes if anyone behaved rationally.
**Score 5**: Characters act consistently with their established intelligence and knowledge; when characters withhold information, there are psychologically credible reasons (fear, shame, distrust, protection); plot complications arise from genuine dilemmas, conflicting goals, or insufficient information rather than artificial stupidity; even character mistakes feel organic to their psychology.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idiot_plot
**Notes**: Coined by James Blish, popularized by Roger Ebert: "A plot which is kept in motion solely by virtue of the fact that everybody involved is an idiot." Ebert: "When characters have depth and their decisions have consequences, I grow restless when their misunderstandings could be ended by words that the screenplay refuses to allow them to utter." Second-Order Idiot Plot: the entire world population must lose 50 IQ points for the plot to work.

### Reaction Beat Pacing
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative provides appropriate emotional processing time between significant events, allowing characters to absorb and react in ways that create reader understanding without stalling momentum.
**Assumed goal**: Close-POV fiction where the reader experiences events through the character's consciousness -- fast-paced thriller or action fiction may deliberately compress reaction time to maintain momentum.
**Score 0**: Characters experience major events with no internal processing, leaving readers emotionally disconnected; or characters process every minor event at length, destroying pacing; reaction beats are generic rather than character-specific; the narrative oscillates between all-action and all-thought without integration.
**Score 5**: Reaction beats are calibrated to the significance of the event; major turning points receive appropriate processing time; minor events are processed efficiently; the full toolkit of interiority is deployed (bodily reactions, thought processes, memory, denial, imagination, philosophical weight); reaction beats reveal character rather than merely acknowledging the event.
**Source**: https://www.writersdigest.com/once-more-with-feeling-writing-interiority-that-resonates-in-fiction
**Notes**: "Adding a thought or emotional beat before or after action or dialogue can help readers understand what's driving a character's behavior." Emotional beats work best when layered with bodily reactions, thought processes, memory, denial, imagination, and philosophical weight. Balance is essential: too much interiority makes external action feel like slow motion; too little reads like a "fictionalized screenplay." Genre affects expectation: romance requires more processing, action requires less.

## Sentinels Found

### Uniform Character Voice
**Detection**: Apply the "cover the name" test to dialogue passages: remove all dialogue attribution and check if speakers can be identified by voice alone. If three or more characters' dialogue lines could be freely swapped without detection, the sentinel fires.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs produce text from a single statistical voice distribution. Without extremely specific prompting, all characters converge on the model's default register -- polite, competent, inoffensive. The "default output of any LLM is an average of its training data -- competent, inoffensive, and utterly without personality." This is a fundamental architectural limitation: the model has one voice, not many.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates character voice as a source of meaning. In human-written fiction, HOW a character speaks is as characterizing as WHAT they say. Flattening all voices to one register removes an entire dimension of characterization, relationship dynamics, and subtext.
**Source**: https://www.junia.ai/blog/llm-default-voice-ai-writing

### Absence of Want/Need Tension
**Detection**: The protagonist has no identifiable internal need that conflicts with their external goal; or the protagonist achieves their goal without confronting any internal limitation or false belief; or the Want and Need are identical.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate goal-oriented plot progression (character wants X, pursues X, gets/doesn't get X) because that's the most common narrative pattern in training data. The subtler architecture of Want vs Need -- where pursuing the Want actually reinforces the character's dysfunction -- requires understanding of human psychology that autocomplete cannot model. LLMs resolve conflicts through action rather than internal transformation.
**Design space cost**: Stories without Want/Need tension lack the "fulcrum of inner change" that creates thematic depth. Characters feel like plot-advancement devices rather than psychologically real people. Victories feel hollow because nothing internal was at stake.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/character-arcs-3/

### Passive Protagonist
**Detection**: The protagonist makes no decisions that alter the story's direction; other characters or external events resolve the central conflict; the protagonist could be removed from the story without changing the outcome; the climax features someone other than the protagonist acting.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs struggle with causal chains connecting character decisions to plot outcomes. They default to generating events that happen TO characters rather than events CAUSED BY characters, because statistical prediction prioritizes what typically comes next in a narrative rather than what a specific character would do. Analysis of AI-generated novels shows "the actual climaxes feature mysterious cloaked figures or sorcerers resolving conflicts, removing protagonist involvement entirely."
**Design space cost**: Eliminates the fundamental dramatic contract: a protagonist who does not act is not a protagonist. Readers invest in characters who struggle and choose; watching things happen to someone generates pity at best, boredom at worst.
**Source**: https://coyotetracks.org/blog/ai-writing/

### All-Positive Emotional Palette
**Detection**: Characters experience only positive emotions or rapidly resolve negative emotions into positive ones; no sustained grief, anger, resentment, jealousy, or bitterness; emotional conflicts are resolved within the same scene they arise; the narrative's emotional trajectory is monotonically positive.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: RLHF training optimizes LLMs for "helpful, harmless, and honest" outputs, which in fiction manifests as systematic emotional sanitization. Studies confirm AI stories are "homogeneously positive," producing "flatter arousal curves and less narrative tension than human-written fiction." LLMs "sanitise real-world conflicts" and "downplay narrative tension in favour of nostalgia and reconciliation."
**Design space cost**: Eliminates the full range of human emotional experience from the story. Fiction that avoids sustained negative emotion cannot explore grief, moral complexity, the cost of choices, or the ambiguity of human connection. It produces what critics call "eerie placidity."
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2603.13545v1

### Stereotypical Character Details
**Detection**: Characters have names, occupations, or background details that appear across multiple AI-generated stories with high frequency (e.g., "Elara," "Thorne," "Anya" for SF; immigrant stories defaulting to Chicago/New York; Black experience narratives repeating identical names; Indigenous characters appearing exclusively as environmental stewards).
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs sample from learned distributions, and for specialized genres, the training data pool is limited, causing convergence on the most statistically common character names and details. Large-scale analysis of 11,800 AI-generated stories found "consistent repetition of specific names, locations, occupations, and themes" across different AI systems. This is a direct consequence of predicting "the most likely next token."
**Design space cost**: Eliminates specificity -- the quality that makes characters feel like individuals rather than types. When a character's name, occupation, and background are statistically generic, they feel assembled from parts rather than imagined into being. The character becomes a composite photograph rather than a portrait.
**Source**: https://glaforge.dev/posts/2025/07/22/the-sci-fi-naming-problem-are-llms-less-creative-than-we-think/

### Cosmetic Flaws Only
**Detection**: The protagonist's stated flaws (clumsiness, awkwardness, stubbornness, "too caring") never create genuine consequences in the plot; flaws are mentioned but never demonstrated in action; flaws are endearing rather than costly; the character never loses anything due to their flaws.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs can generate the concept of "flawed character" (it's a common writing instruction) but struggle to sustain the causal chain where a flaw creates real damage. The model defaults to surface-level imperfections that don't disrupt the positive emotional trajectory it's been trained to produce. Mary Sue detection criteria: "possess multiple skills that conveniently pop up without explanation," "rarely struggle," and have "an answer for every problem."
**Design space cost**: Characters without meaningful flaws have no dramatic tension. The character's struggle against their own worst tendencies is often more compelling than the external plot. Cosmetic flaws produce characters who feel engineered to be liked rather than designed to be human.
**Source**: https://industrialscripts.com/mary-sue-character/

### No Subtext in Dialogue
**Detection**: Characters consistently say exactly what they mean; no gap exists between what is said and what is felt; characters answer every question directly; no deflection, evasion, or indirect communication; emotional states are narrated through dialogue rather than implied through behavior.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Subtext requires modeling two simultaneous layers of meaning -- what the character says and what they actually want/feel -- which requires understanding human psychology, social dynamics, and the specific relational history between characters. LLMs predict the most probable next response, which is typically the direct answer. AI fiction "typically lacks the implicit layers of meaning found in human writing. Characters state their needs directly rather than revealing them through action, dialogue tension, and unspoken desires."
**Design space cost**: On-the-nose dialogue eliminates one of fiction's most powerful tools for creating depth, tension, and reader engagement. When characters say exactly what they mean, the reader has no interpretive work to do, no tension to feel, and no insight into the gap between public and private self.
**Source**: https://writershelpingwriters.net/2025/09/how-to-differentiate-stories-from-ai-slop/

### Rapid Conflict Resolution
**Detection**: Conflicts between characters are introduced and resolved within the same scene or chapter; no conflict persists across multiple scenes; misunderstandings are cleared up immediately through direct conversation; no relationship remains strained for extended narrative time.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs are "designed to go with you and let you lead -- they want to provide the best response" and don't maintain adversarial dynamics naturally. In game narrative contexts: "there's no struggle, no progression, no loss -- LLMs are trained to do what you say." The sycophantic tendency that makes LLMs agreeable in conversation extends to fiction: characters become agreeable too, resolving tensions rather than sustaining them.
**Design space cost**: Sustained conflict is the engine of narrative tension. Stories where every conflict resolves immediately have no dramatic architecture -- they become a series of minor disturbances that never accumulate into anything meaningful. The reader never worries because there's nothing to worry about.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2603.13545v1

### Flat Character Arc Where Round Arc Is Required
**Detection**: The protagonist begins the story with no false belief, wound, or internal limitation; they face no internal challenge; they end the story unchanged; yet the narrative is structured as a transformative journey (not a flat-arc steadfast narrative). The character was meant to change but didn't.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs can generate plot events (quests, conflicts, resolutions) without modeling the internal transformation those events should produce in the character. "Elara has no character arc beyond moving from unsure she's ready to be a queen to being confident she can be." The model generates the external structure of transformation without the internal substance.
**Design space cost**: A character who goes on a journey but doesn't change is a tourist, not a protagonist. The reader experiences the events as a sequence rather than a story -- things happen, but they don't mean anything because nobody is transformed by them.
**Source**: https://coyotetracks.org/blog/ai-writing/

### Cliche Physical Reactions
**Detection**: Characters' emotional states are rendered through stock physical responses: "heart pounded," "blood ran cold," "stomach churned," "breath caught," "tears streamed," "jaw clenched," "hands trembled." Three or more such cliches in a single chapter trigger the sentinel.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: These phrases are the most statistically common way to render emotion in the training corpus. LLMs default to them because they are the highest-probability completions for "she felt scared and her..." The model cannot generate novel, character-specific physical manifestations of emotion because it doesn't understand what emotions feel like -- it only knows what words typically follow emotion-describing contexts.
**Design space cost**: Stock physical reactions are the "telling" that masquerades as "showing." They provide the syntax of embodied emotion without its substance. Character-specific emotional reactions (a character who goes very still when scared, or who laughs inappropriately when grieving) are powerfully characterizing. Generic reactions characterize nobody.
**Source**: https://coyotetracks.org/blog/ai-writing/

### Memory/Continuity Failures Across Scenes
**Detection**: Characters forget established information, relationships, or events from earlier in the narrative; characters reintroduce themselves to people they've already met; character traits established in one chapter disappear in another; backstory details contradict between scenes.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs have limited context windows and process text sequentially. As narrative length increases, earlier character information falls out of effective context. User reports from AI storytelling platforms: "the AI forgets established character traits as stories grow longer"; "eventually, your character cards are ignored"; bots "tend to forget previous messages causing inconsistencies." NoCha benchmark: accuracy drops from 59.8% on sentence-level to 41.6% on tasks requiring synthesis across narrative distances.
**Design space cost**: Character consistency is the foundation of reader trust. When a character forgets their own history, the reader's investment collapses. Every continuity failure reminds the reader they're consuming generated text rather than inhabiting a fictional world.
**Source**: https://cuckoo.network/blog/2025/04/17/negative-feedback-on-llm-powered-storytelling-and-roleplay-apps

### Single-Trait Character Repetition
**Detection**: A character's defining trait (determined, brave, kind, mysterious) is stated or demonstrated more than 5 times without variation, nuance, or complication. The narrative repeatedly tells the reader the same thing about the character.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs maintain character consistency through repetition rather than through accumulated complexity. When a character is established as "determined," the model generates additional instances of determination because that's the highest-probability completion for that character context. Analysis found all AI protagonists "share identical traits: they're 'determined' to the point of obsession, with this single characteristic repeated relentlessly throughout their narratives."
**Design space cost**: Repetition of a single trait is the opposite of character development. Human-written characters demonstrate a trait once, then complicate, contradict, and deepen it. The reader learns new things about the character as the story progresses. AI characters reconfirm what the reader already knows, creating the illusion of characterization through accumulation of the same data point.
**Source**: https://coyotetracks.org/blog/ai-writing/

### Narrative Tells Emotions Instead of Enacting Them
**Detection**: The predominant method of conveying character emotion is through direct naming ("She felt angry," "He was devastated," "Joy filled her heart") rather than through behavior, dialogue, physical sensation, or dramatic situation. More than half of emotional moments are rendered through telling.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Telling emotion is the shortest path between the concept of emotion and its expression in text. LLMs, optimized for fluent next-token prediction, default to the most direct rendering. Showing emotion requires understanding how a specific emotion manifests in a specific person in a specific situation -- a multi-layered inference the model cannot perform. "We're told everything and shown nothing. Beyond that, note the utter lack of concrete imagery."
**Design space cost**: Told emotions don't make readers feel anything. As C.S. Lakin notes: "Telling an emotion doesn't make readers feel or experience the emotion." The reader is informed about the character's state but excluded from experiencing it. This is the fundamental difference between fiction that moves readers and fiction that merely describes events.
**Source**: https://coyotetracks.org/blog/ai-writing/

### Backstory Info-Dump in Opening
**Detection**: The first chapter or opening pages contain extended passages of character history, background, or biography delivered through narration, internal monologue, or "as you know, Bob" dialogue rather than through present-tense dramatic action.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generating character introductions follow the most common pattern in their training data: establish who the character is before putting them in motion. The model generates context-setting information because that's what statistically follows character introduction. Human writers learn to withhold backstory and reveal it through the story's progression; this restraint requires narrative judgment that autocomplete lacks.
**Design space cost**: Backstory dumps in the opening destroy forward momentum at the moment the reader most needs it. The reader hasn't yet bonded with the character, so they have no reason to care about the character's history. The opening should establish the character in action; history should emerge gradually as the reader's investment deepens.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/backstory-drip-pt-3-of-3/

### Homogeneous Ensemble (All Characters Sound/Act Alike)
**Detection**: In a multi-character narrative, apply differentiation checks: (1) dialogue voice is interchangeable between characters, (2) characters react to the same event in the same way, (3) characters share the same emotional register, (4) characters have similar backgrounds or perspectives, (5) removing any one character would not change the thematic landscape.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: The LLM has one voice model, not multiple. When generating ensemble fiction, all characters converge on the same linguistic patterns, emotional responses, and decision-making frameworks. "AI Dungeon outputs drift toward a neutral voice despite customization options"; "different characters feel less distinct due to similar phrasing and roleplay tropes." The model cannot maintain multiple simultaneous character models because it doesn't have separate representations for different characters.
**Design space cost**: An ensemble whose members are interchangeable has no ensemble value. The entire purpose of multiple characters is to offer different perspectives, voices, and responses to the same circumstances. When everyone sounds and acts the same, you have one character with multiple names.
**Source**: https://cuckoo.network/blog/2025/04/17/negative-feedback-on-llm-powered-storytelling-and-roleplay-apps

### Reconciliation Without Reckoning
**Detection**: Characters who are in conflict reach resolution without either party confronting the genuine source of their disagreement; conflicts end through generic expressions of understanding ("I see what you mean now," "I'm sorry," "Let's move forward together") without specific acknowledgment of what went wrong or what changed.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs optimize for resolution. The training process rewards positive, helpful outcomes. In fiction, this manifests as conflicts that dissolve rather than resolve -- characters skip the painful, specific work of reckoning with their differences and jump to generic reconciliation language. AI-generated narratives "overwhelmingly conform to a single narrative plot structure where protagonists resolve minor conflicts through reconnecting with tradition and organizing community events."
**Design space cost**: Reconciliation without reckoning feels false. Real resolution requires characters to confront specific failures, acknowledge specific harms, and make specific changes. Generic reconciliation teaches the reader nothing about these specific people and their specific relationship. It's the emotional equivalent of a form letter.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2603.13545v1

### Missing Character Wound
**Detection**: The protagonist has no identifiable past trauma, formative loss, or psychological injury that explains their present behavior and drives their arc. They appear to have no history that matters.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs can generate characters with goals and surface-level personality traits but struggle to create the deep psychological architecture (Ghost/Wound/Lie/Weakness) that makes characters feel psychologically real. AI-generated character wounds are "shallow" -- the model can name a wound ("her mother died") without understanding how that wound specifically shapes behavior, relationships, and worldview. Analysis found: "Kira's mother's death -- supposedly central to her motivation -- receives no exploration of what the mother believed in or how her death specifically shaped the protagonist."
**Design space cost**: Characters without wounds are characters without depth. The wound is the origin point of the character's internal struggle, the engine of their relationships, and the foundation of their thematic significance. Without it, the character is an assembly of traits rather than a psychology.
**Source**: https://coyotetracks.org/blog/ai-writing/

### Verbatim Scene/Paragraph Repetition
**Detection**: Entire paragraphs, sentences, or descriptive passages appear verbatim or near-verbatim in multiple locations in the text. Characters restate their own dialogue from earlier scenes. Descriptions of recurring settings are copy-pasted rather than varied.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs can fall into "repetition loops" where previously generated text becomes the highest-probability completion for similar contexts. The "Repeat Curse" in LLMs results in "restating earlier text almost verbatim" and "when hitting continue it tends to repeat literally everything." This is a direct consequence of autoregressive generation: text that already exists in the context has elevated probability of being generated again.
**Design space cost**: Repetition shatters the illusion of authorial intentionality. In human-written fiction, repetition is a deliberate craft choice (motif, refrain, echo). In AI-generated fiction, it's a mechanical failure that reminds the reader the text is generated rather than composed. It also represents wasted narrative space where development, variation, or progression should occur.
**Source**: https://cuckoo.network/blog/2025/04/17/negative-feedback-on-llm-powered-storytelling-and-roleplay-apps

### Protagonist Described as Determined/Brave/Kind Without Demonstration
**Detection**: The narrative or other characters describe the protagonist using positive abstract adjectives (determined, brave, kind, clever, compassionate) that are not supported by specific on-page actions demonstrating those qualities.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs can generate character descriptions (telling) more easily than the specific dramatic situations that demonstrate those qualities (showing). The model knows that protagonists are typically described as brave or determined and generates those descriptors as part of character establishment, but fails to create the challenging situations where bravery or determination would be meaningfully tested and displayed.
**Design space cost**: Told character traits carry no weight. "She was brave" means nothing if we never see her face something that terrifies her. Demonstration is the only way character traits become real to the reader. Abstract description without demonstration is marketing copy, not characterization.
**Source**: https://coyotetracks.org/blog/ai-writing/
