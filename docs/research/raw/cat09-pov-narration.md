## Criteria Found

### POV Selection Appropriateness
**What it measures**: Whether the chosen point of view serves the story's needs in terms of intimacy, scope, information control, and reader experience.
**Assumed goal**: Reader-response tradition where POV is a functional storytelling tool selected to maximize emotional and informational effects — experimental fiction (Calvino, Danielewski) may deliberately subvert optimal POV for metafictional purposes
**Score 0**: POV chosen arbitrarily or by default, actively working against the story's needs (e.g., first person used for a sprawling multi-character epic requiring simultaneous access to distant events; omniscient used for a claustrophobic psychological thriller needing deep interiority).
**Score 5**: POV chosen with clear intent that serves story goals — the limitations of the chosen perspective create productive narrative tension (suspense, dramatic irony, unreliable narration) rather than fighting the material. Every advantage of the chosen POV is exploited.
**Source**: https://janefriedman.com/point-of-view/
**Notes**: Friedman identifies four evaluation questions: (1) Is the narrator participating, observing, or reconstructing? (2) Does the narrator announce itself or remain invisible? (3) Is the narrator detached or invested? (4) Is the narrator qualified and trustworthy? Different POVs suit different story types — first person for character-driven internal journeys, omniscient for epic scope with large casts, limited third for mystery/suspense.

### POV Consistency
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative maintains its chosen POV type, person, depth, and tense consistently throughout, with any shifts being deliberate and clearly signaled.
**Assumed goal**: Contemporary commercial and literary fiction tradition where POV consistency is a baseline craft expectation — postmodern and experimental fiction (e.g., Faulkner's The Sound and the Fury) may deliberately break consistency for disorientation effects
**Score 0**: POV shifts randomly between types (limited suddenly becomes omniscient), person (third drifts into first), depth (deep POV suddenly pulls to distant), or tense (past becomes present) without signals, markers, or apparent purpose. Readers cannot tell whose head they are in.
**Score 5**: Every shift in POV type, person, depth, or tense is deliberate, clearly marked, and serves a narrative purpose. Within scenes, the chosen POV is maintained flawlessly. Readers always know whose consciousness they inhabit and when a transition occurs.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/pov-problems/
**Notes**: "Inconsistency in POV is one of the quickest tip-offs of an amateur narrative." The article identifies POV inconsistency as the foundational problem from which other POV issues flow. This includes mixing narrative perspectives (tight third suddenly becomes omniscient) and changing tense unexpectedly.

### Head-Hopping Control
**What it measures**: Whether POV shifts between characters within scenes are deliberate and controlled versus accidental and disorienting.
**Assumed goal**: Close-third-person immersive tradition where the reader experiences the story through one consciousness at a time per scene — omniscient narration (Tolstoy, Pratchett) deliberately operates differently, using a distinct narrator voice to access multiple minds
**Score 0**: The narrative jumps between multiple characters' thoughts and perceptions within single scenes or paragraphs without transition markers, creating a "scattergun effect" that disorients readers. No consistent focal character per scene. Characters' internal thoughts mingle indiscriminately.
**Score 5**: Each scene has one clear focal character whose consciousness the reader inhabits. Other characters' states are conveyed through observed behavior, dialogue, and inference rather than direct thought access. POV transitions between scenes use clear structural markers (scene breaks, chapter breaks).
**Source**: https://darlingaxe.com/blogs/news/head-hopping
**Notes**: Head-hopping is distinct from omniscient narration: omniscient uses a distinct narrator voice that is NOT the protagonist, while head-hopping jumps between deep-third perspectives. The Darling Axe notes contemporary readers have "refined expectations for POV stability." Advanced Fiction Writing notes romance genre historically accepted head-hopping though modern romance publishers increasingly reject it.

### Psychic/Narrative Distance Control
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative effectively manages the distance between reader and character consciousness, using the full spectrum from distant/objective to intimate/stream-of-consciousness.
**Assumed goal**: John Gardner's psychic distance framework and its descendants in the close-third-person tradition — Brechtian alienation techniques and meta-fiction deliberately disrupt psychic closeness for intellectual engagement over emotional immersion
**Score 0**: Distance is static (stuck at one level throughout) or shifts jarringly without transition (jumping from distant overview directly to stream of consciousness). No awareness of the distance spectrum. Emotional moments are narrated at a distance; mundane moments are narrated with excessive closeness.
**Score 5**: Distance shifts fluidly along the spectrum, matched to narrative need. Emotional peaks use close distance; scene-setting uses far distance. Transitions between levels are gradual (moving one level at a time). The writer demonstrates command of all five levels: (1) far/objective, (2) distant/character-named, (3) moderate/character-felt, (4) close/free indirect, (5) intimate/stream of consciousness.
**Source**: https://jerichowriters.com/narrative-distance/
**Notes**: Jericho Writers identifies five levels of narrative distance. Key principle: mainstream fiction spends most time in levels 3-4. Moving between characters requires traveling outward through levels (4 to 3 to 2 to neutral) before traveling inward into another character. Gardner's The Art of Fiction defines psychic distance as "the distance the reader feels between himself and the events in the story."

### Filter Word Management
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative minimizes unnecessary filter words (saw, heard, felt, noticed, wondered, realized, etc.) that create distance between reader and character experience.
**Assumed goal**: Deep/close POV tradition dominant in contemporary commercial fiction — omniscient narration and deliberately distanced styles (Ishiguro's Stevens in Remains of the Day) may use filter words purposefully to create narrative distance as a character trait
**Score 0**: Narration is saturated with filter words ("she saw the door open," "he felt angry," "she noticed the blood on the floor") creating constant reminder that the reader is observing through a character rather than experiencing directly. Every sensation is mediated through explicit perception verbs.
**Score 5**: Sensory experiences and perceptions are rendered directly ("the door swung open," "heat flooded his chest," "blood pooled on the tile"). Filter words are used only when the act of perceiving is itself significant or when deliberate distance serves the scene. The reader experiences the story rather than being told about a character's experience of the story.
**Source**: https://darlingaxe.com/blogs/news/filter-words
**Notes**: Sensory filter words include: see, hear, feel, touch, taste, smell, notice, observe, look, listen, perceive, sense, seem, recognize, watch, glance, gaze, peek, stare, spot, gawk. Cognitive filter words include: think, remember, realize, wonder, consider, decide, believe, imagine, worry, know, contemplate, reflect, understand, assume, learn, ponder, conclude, doubt, expect. Core removal technique: replace filtered constructions with direct sensory language.

### Free Indirect Discourse Skill
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative effectively blends character consciousness into third-person narration without attribution tags, allowing the narrator to temporarily recede while character voice emerges.
**Assumed goal**: Post-Flaubert literary fiction tradition where free indirect discourse is the primary tool for psychological depth in third person — minimalist/behaviorist fiction (Hemingway, Carver) deliberately restricts interiority, preferring external focalization
**Score 0**: The narrative never blends character voice into third-person narration, relying entirely on explicit thought tags ("she thought," "he wondered") or maintaining a flat narrator voice that never takes on character coloring. Alternatively, attempts at FID are confusing — the reader cannot distinguish narrator commentary from character thought.
**Score 5**: Character thoughts, vocabulary, speech patterns, and emotional coloring flow seamlessly into third-person narration without attribution. The reader experiences a "two-brain" effect — narrator and character perspectives merge and separate fluidly. The technique is applied consistently from the beginning and enhances both characterization and narrative authority.
**Source**: https://litreactor.com/columns/the-benefits-of-free-indirect-discourse
**Notes**: FID "embeds a character's speech or thoughts into an otherwise third-person narrative" without denoting "which thought belongs to whom," creating a narrative with "two 'brains': one belonging to the narrator, the other belonging to the character." Combines "the biggest asset a first-person POV has (access)" with "the single best benefit of a third-person narrative (reliability)." Progenitors include Joyce, Austen, James, Woolf.

### Character Voice Distinctiveness in Narration
**What it measures**: Whether POV characters' narrative sections are voiced distinctively — reflecting their unique vocabulary, syntax, worldview, knowledge, and personality — or whether all POV sections sound interchangeable.
**Assumed goal**: Multi-POV fiction tradition where each perspective character must be immediately identifiable by voice alone — single-POV fiction with a transparent narrator style (many thrillers) may prioritize readability over distinctive voice
**Score 0**: All POV characters narrate in the same voice — same vocabulary, same sentence structure, same metaphor sources, same level of diction. Readers cannot identify whose section they are in without checking chapter headers. The narration sounds like one person regardless of whose perspective is active.
**Score 5**: Each POV character's sections are immediately recognizable through distinctive vocabulary, syntax, metaphorical range, focus of attention, and attitude. A chef notices food preparation details; a soldier notices tactical terrain. The voice reflects education, culture, emotional state, and personality without caricature.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/pov-problems/
**Notes**: "What is one sentence that will tell readers exactly who she is?" — if you cannot answer this for each POV character, voice is weak. Comparing "I was doing graffiti" (bland) to Whitney Gardner's You're Welcome, Universe: "Six stencils in and it's gone...I'm making art here. I'm creating. I'm on fire" (distinctive personality). Multi-POV novels require each character to have their own narrative goal and stakes and form a complete, satisfying story thread when read sequentially.

### Author/Narrator Voice Separation
**What it measures**: Whether the three levels of voice — author voice, narrator voice, and character voice — are handled with appropriate distinction and consistency.
**Assumed goal**: Fiction craft tradition distinguishing the implied author from the narrator from the characters — autofiction and confessional narratives deliberately blur these boundaries
**Score 0**: The author's voice bleeds through all characters and the narrator indiscriminately. Every character sounds like the author. The narrator's vocabulary and concerns are clearly the author's rather than emerging from the narrative world. No separation between these layers.
**Score 5**: Author voice is detectable as a cumulative pattern across works but does not intrude on individual character voices. The narrator voice is appropriate to the story and distinct from the author's personal voice. Character voices are distinct from both narrator and author, reflecting their own personalities and worldviews.
**Source**: https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2022/02/author-voice-vs-narrator-voice-vs.html
**Notes**: Voice hierarchy is a nesting structure: author voice (outermost) contains narrator voice (middle) which contains character voices (innermost). "Tom Sawyer can't put on the voice of the narrator nor the voice of Mark Twain." Despite different narrators sounding distinct, "the writing still rings of Mark Twain" — authorial fingerprints remain. Formula: "What a Person Thinks or Talks about + How She Says It = Voice."

### Author Intrusion Avoidance
**What it measures**: Whether the author's knowledge, opinions, and expository needs are kept separate from the POV character's consciousness, or whether the author "breaks through" the character to address the reader directly.
**Assumed goal**: Immersive close-POV tradition where the author's hand should be invisible — Victorian and satirical traditions (Austen, Thackeray, Snicket) use deliberate authorial intrusion as a stylistic feature
**Score 0**: The narrative frequently breaks character consciousness to explain things the character already knows, describe things the character cannot perceive, insert vocabulary inappropriate to the character, or deliver exposition the author wants the reader to have. The author's hand is constantly visible.
**Score 5**: Every line of narration is filtered through the POV character's consciousness, knowledge, vocabulary, and perception. Information reaches the reader only through channels the character would naturally access. When exposition is needed, it emerges organically from character thought, dialogue, or action.
**Source**: https://writersinthestormblog.com/2023/02/10-examples-of-author-intrusion-in-deep-pov-and-how-to-fix-it/
**Notes**: Ten types of author intrusion identified: (1) Unnecessary character identification of known people, (2) Self-explanation of objects the character already understands, (3) Physical self-description without reason, (4) Describing one's own face/expressions one cannot see, (5) World-building exposition dumps, (6) Observing events the character cannot perceive, (7) Over-specific identification beyond character knowledge, (8) Narrator voice judgment replacing emotional depth, (9) Author summary skipping character experience, (10) Failure to vary depth strategically.

### POV Information Discipline
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative respects the knowledge boundaries of the POV character — only conveying information the character would plausibly know, notice, or think about.
**Assumed goal**: Limited/close POV tradition where maintaining character knowledge boundaries creates suspense and reader trust — omniscient narration deliberately transcends these boundaries through a narrator who is not bound by character knowledge
**Score 0**: The POV character knows things they have no way of knowing — names of strangers, events happening elsewhere, technical details outside their expertise, other characters' internal states presented as fact. The narrative leaks omniscient information into limited-POV scenes.
**Score 5**: Every piece of information in the narrative is something the POV character could plausibly know, perceive, or infer. Unknown characters are described physically until identified. Technical details match the character's expertise. Other characters' emotions are inferred from observable behavior. Gaps in the character's knowledge create productive narrative tension.
**Source**: https://www.livewritethrive.com/2024/08/31/common-pov-violations-and-how-to-catch-them/
**Notes**: Five specific violations: (1) Head-hopping — jumping between character perspectives, (2) Character knowing information they have not learned, (3) Narrator intruding with omniscient knowledge, (4) Not showing phone conversations the POV character hears, (5) Using names for characters the POV character has not identified. Detection: "Ask yourself: Does my character know this?"

### Tense Consistency and Purposefulness
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative maintains consistent verb tense within its primary timeline, with any tense shifts serving clear narrative purposes (flashback, commentary, habitual action) rather than occurring accidentally.
**Assumed goal**: Transparent prose tradition where tense consistency aids fluent reading — experimental works (e.g., Jennifer Egan's A Visit from the Goon Squad) may shift tense deliberately between sections for structural effect
**Score 0**: Tense shifts randomly within paragraphs and sentences without purpose. Past tense drifts into present and back without signaling timeline changes. Dialogue tags inconsistently use "said" versus "says." Flashbacks and present-timeline narration are indistinguishable by tense.
**Score 5**: One primary tense is maintained consistently throughout the main narrative. Shifts to other tenses serve clear purposes (recounting past events, future anticipation, habitual actions) and are smoothly signaled. Shifts are rare, brief, and return cleanly to the primary tense.
**Source**: https://www.scribophile.com/academy/choosing-your-tense-and-keeping-it
**Notes**: Common shift triggers: writers becoming emotionally absorbed and drifting into present tense; mixing present-tense dialogue with past-tense narrative; the verbs "go/went" being a common error site. Strategy: do a "tense pass" during revision.

### Tense Selection Appropriateness
**What it measures**: Whether the chosen narrative tense (past, present, or mixed) serves the story's needs for temporal flexibility, immediacy, and character complexity.
**Assumed goal**: Story-first craft tradition where tense is a functional choice serving narrative goals — literary fiction may value present tense's immediacy for philosophical and psychological works (e.g., Coetzee's Disgrace)
**Score 0**: Tense chosen by default or convention without considering its effects on the narrative. Present tense used for a story requiring complex timeline manipulation; past tense used for a story whose power depends on moment-to-moment immediacy.
**Score 5**: Tense is chosen deliberately for its effects on the narrative. Present tense creates genuine immediacy for character-driven, compressed-timeline stories. Past tense provides temporal flexibility for complex multi-timeline or retrospective narratives. The tense choice enhances rather than constrains the storytelling.
**Source**: https://www.writersdigest.com/write-better-fiction/the-pros-and-cons-of-writing-a-novel-in-present-tense
**Notes**: Present tense advantages: immediacy, urgency, simplified grammar, alignment with character psychology "boxed in the present" (Joyce Cary). Present tense disadvantages: restricted time manipulation, limited character complexity, diminished retrospective suspense, temptation toward trivial detail. Past tense advantages: access to all twelve verb tenses, flexible timeline order, comfortable retrospection. Past tense: better for long-form, multi-POV, shifting timelines, epic scope.

### Focalization Clarity
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative clearly distinguishes who sees (the focalizer) from who speaks (the narrator), maintaining consistent focalization within scenes.
**Assumed goal**: Genettian narratological tradition distinguishing focalization from voice — pre-Genette traditions often conflated these, and some popular fiction still treats "POV" as a single undifferentiated concept
**Score 0**: The narrative confuses who is perceiving with who is speaking. The narrator describes things no character could perceive while maintaining a "limited" label. Internal focalization is violated when the narrative shifts to external observation of the focal character (describing their own face) or to another character's perception without transition.
**Score 5**: The focalizer (whose perception orients the narrative) is clearly established and maintained within scenes. Zero focalization (omniscient) is distinct from internal focalization (character-bound) — the narrator does not accidentally slip between them. External focalization (camera-eye) is used deliberately for effect.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Focalisation
**Notes**: Genette's three types: zero (omniscient, narrator knows more than characters), internal (filtered through character consciousness — fixed, variable, or multiple), external (narrator knows less than characters, reporting only observable). Bal refined this by distinguishing the focalizer (subject of perception) from the focalized (object of perception). Genette emphasizes "any single formula of focalization does not always bear on an entire work, but rather on a definite narrative section."

### Multi-POV Management
**What it measures**: Whether a multi-POV narrative distributes perspectives effectively, with each POV character earning their perspective through narrative necessity, distinctive voice, and consistent presence.
**Assumed goal**: Structurally disciplined multi-POV tradition (e.g., George R.R. Martin's rotating chapters) where each perspective serves the whole — mosaic/collage fiction (Cloud Atlas, A Visit from the Goon Squad) may use deliberate structural asymmetry
**Score 0**: Too many POV characters dilute reader investment. POV characters appear sporadically or are introduced late without establishment. Some POVs could be removed without affecting the plot. POV characters' voices are interchangeable. Distribution is random rather than purposeful.
**Score 5**: Every POV character is essential to the story — each has unique access to information, stakes, and thematic resonance that cannot be obtained through another character. POVs are established early and distributed consistently. Each POV forms a satisfying thread when read sequentially. The number of POVs is the minimum needed for the story.
**Source**: https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/pov-problems/
**Notes**: "The Fewer POVs the Better" as a rule of thumb. Test: "Look at your Climactic Moment. Which characters are involved? These are the most important characters." Most successful multi-POV novels use two to five narrators. Random POV distribution — a character appearing only once or introduced only in the third act — is jarring and usually extraneous. Each POV character should appear at least once per quarter of the book.

### POV Character Selection Per Scene
**What it measures**: Whether each scene is told from the perspective of the character with the most at stake, the most to lose, or the most interesting vantage point on the events.
**Assumed goal**: Scene-craft tradition where POV character selection is a strategic narrative decision — some experimental structures deliberately choose the least obvious POV character for defamiliarization
**Score 0**: Scenes are told from arbitrary or default characters' perspectives. Crucial confrontations are narrated by bystanders rather than participants. The character experiencing the most interesting internal conflict is sidelined while a less engaged character narrates.
**Score 5**: Each scene's POV character is the person with the most at stake, the most to learn, or the most compelling internal experience of the events. When multiple characters could narrate, the chosen perspective maximizes tension, information control, and emotional impact.
**Source**: https://www.alyssamatesic.com/free-writing-resources/third-person-pov
**Notes**: "Ensure that every POV in your story earns its place." A good rule of thumb is to focus the POV on the character with the most to lose in that scene. If you can remove a POV and the plot still functions, it probably wasn't necessary.

### Narrative Depth Variation
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative varies its depth of interiority strategically — going deep during emotionally significant moments and pulling back during transitions and lower-stakes passages.
**Assumed goal**: Contemporary fiction pacing tradition where narrative depth maps to dramatic weight — minimalist fiction (Hemingway) maintains relatively consistent surface-level depth, trusting subtext
**Score 0**: The narrative maintains a flat, unchanging depth — either always deep (creating exhausting interiority even during mundane moments) or always shallow (failing to exploit emotional peaks). No modulation between action and reflection.
**Score 5**: Depth varies dynamically: deep interiority during moments of decision, revelation, or emotional extremity; moderate depth during dialogue and interaction; shallow/distant narration during transitions, time-jumps, and scene-setting. The variation creates rhythm and emphasis.
**Source**: https://writersinthestormblog.com/2023/02/10-examples-of-author-intrusion-in-deep-pov-and-how-to-fix-it/
**Notes**: "Consider whether details matter to story goals; unnecessary depth slows pacing." Match depth to importance — if contract details are not crucial to the scene's purpose, go shallow. The BubbleCow checklist states: "Thoughts must serve decisions, not drain momentum through aimless rumination."

### Omniscient Narrator Voice Distinctiveness
**What it measures**: For omniscient narration specifically — whether the omniscient narrator has a distinctive, consistent voice that is clearly separate from any character's voice, providing its own personality, attitude, and commentary.
**Assumed goal**: Successful omniscient narration tradition (Austen, Pratchett, Tolkien) where the narrator is effectively a character — transparent/effaced omniscient narration deliberately minimizes narrator personality for panoramic scope
**Score 0**: The omniscient narrator has no distinctive voice, functioning as a generic information conduit. Or the narrator's voice is inconsistent, sometimes sounding like one character, sometimes another, sometimes the author. The narrator provides access to multiple minds without the coherent personality that distinguishes omniscient from head-hopping.
**Score 5**: The omniscient narrator is a compelling presence with consistent voice, tone, and attitude. The narrator's commentary, word choice, and perspective are distinct from every character's voice. The narrator's personality creates irony, humor, or authority that enriches the story beyond what any character could provide.
**Source**: https://emilylanewrites.com/austens-voice-what-is-the-omniscient-narrator-with-examples/
**Notes**: Austen's omniscient narrator in Pride and Prejudice employs a witty voice commenting on social conventions while remaining detached. Pratchett's Discworld uses a folksy historian-like narrator. "The key to a successful folksy narrator is the voice — it must be a compelling character." An omniscient narrator without a distinct voice risks being mistaken for head-hopping.

### Unreliable Narrator Craft
**What it measures**: When unreliable narration is used, whether the unreliability is purposeful, detectable by attentive readers, and serves the story's themes rather than being accidental or meaningless.
**Assumed goal**: Post-Booth literary tradition where narrator unreliability is a deliberate craft tool revealing character and theme — straightforward commercial fiction typically relies on reliable narration for reader trust
**Score 0**: The narrator is unreliable accidentally — the author intended reliable narration but created contradictions, impossibilities, or knowledge gaps through carelessness. Alternatively, deliberate unreliability has no payoff — the reader detects the lies but they reveal nothing about the character or world.
**Score 5**: Unreliability is constructed with intent. Attentive readers can detect discrepancies between the narrator's account and the implied truth. The gap between what the narrator says and what happened reveals character psychology, thematic meaning, or both. The unreliability has structural consequences — the reader's understanding of events transforms upon recognizing it.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unreliable_narrator
**Notes**: Booth: narrator is "reliable when he speaks for or acts in accordance with the norms of the work (the implied author's norms), unreliable when he does not." Phelan identifies six types across three axes: misreporting/underreporting (facts), misinterpreting/underinterpreting (knowledge), misevaluating/underevaluating (values). Riggan's categories: picaros, madmen, naifs, clowns, liars. Nanning's signals: intratextual (self-contradiction, memory gaps), extratextual (contradicting world knowledge), reader competence (genre recognition).

### Dramatic Irony Through POV
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative uses POV limitations and multi-perspective structures to create productive gaps between what readers know and what characters know, generating tension, suspense, or thematic resonance.
**Assumed goal**: Reader-engagement tradition where information asymmetry between reader and character creates anticipation and emotional investment — some postmodern fiction prefers information symmetry or mutual confusion between reader and character
**Score 0**: No productive information asymmetry exists between reader and characters. In multi-POV stories, information from one perspective never meaningfully interacts with another. In single-POV stories, no gap exists between reader understanding and character understanding. Alternatively, the author withholds information the POV character has, creating false mystery rather than dramatic irony.
**Score 5**: POV limitations create powerful information asymmetry. In multi-POV stories, readers see two or more threads converging before characters do. In single-POV stories, readers recognize narrator biases, blind spots, or deceptions that the narrator does not. The gap between reader knowledge and character knowledge generates genuine suspense, empathy, or thematic depth.
**Source**: http://blog.janicehardy.com/2010/06/oh-irony.html
**Notes**: "Dramatic irony is quite effective in multiple POV stories where events happening in one POV affect another, as readers can see two (or more) points racing toward each other." In first person, dramatic irony comes from the audience realizing something about the character that the character does not realize. Omniscient narrators have "great opportunity to play with dramatic irony" because they can share information with readers that characters lack.

### Information Management Through POV
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative uses POV strategically to control the flow of information to the reader — revealing what's needed when it's needed, creating suspense through withholding, and building understanding through progressive disclosure.
**Assumed goal**: Plot-driven fiction tradition where information flow drives reader engagement — literary fiction may prioritize emotional or thematic resonance over information management
**Score 0**: Information flow is uncontrolled — the reader learns everything at once through an omniscient narrator who reveals too much too soon, or the reader is kept artificially ignorant by a narrator who withholds information the POV character possesses (false mystery). No strategic timing of revelations.
**Score 5**: POV is used as a precision tool for information control. Limitations of the chosen POV create natural suspense (the character genuinely does not know). Multi-POV structures reveal different facets of events at carefully timed intervals. No false withholding — when information is hidden from the reader, it is because the POV character genuinely lacks it.
**Source**: https://sanjidakay.substack.com/p/understanding-point-of-view
**Notes**: "POV determines who is telling the story and how much information the reader has access to." First person: reader only knows what character knows, and character can lie. Third limited: author controls what is revealed and when, enabling dramatic irony. Omniscient: risk of "giving away too much too soon" — requires careful withholding.

### Interior Monologue Integration
**What it measures**: Whether internal thoughts are woven into the narrative seamlessly and purposefully, serving character development and plot advancement rather than creating static rumination.
**Assumed goal**: Action-and-decision-driven fiction tradition where interiority serves forward momentum — literary and psychological fiction (Woolf, Proust) may value sustained interior exploration as the primary narrative mode
**Score 0**: Internal monologue is absent (creating emotional blankness), excessive (creating static navel-gazing that halts plot momentum), or poorly integrated (jarringly separated from action by italics and thought tags when the narrative could use free indirect discourse). Thoughts do not serve decisions or character development.
**Score 5**: Interior monologue is seamlessly woven into action, dialogue, and description. Thoughts serve decisions and reveal character. Deep POV uses free indirect discourse to blend interiority with narration. Direct thought (italicized) is used sparingly for emphasis. Interior monologue creates emotional resonance without halting narrative momentum.
**Source**: https://www.advancedfictionwriting.com/blog/2012/04/18/how-to-do-interior-monologue-in-fiction-writing/
**Notes**: Two types: direct interior monologue (exact character thoughts) and indirect interior monologue (character thoughts embedded in narration through rhetorical questions, exclamations, fragments, associational leaps). BubbleCow checklist: "Thoughts must serve decisions, not drain momentum through aimless rumination."

### Second Person Narration Craft
**What it measures**: When second person ("you") narration is used, whether it serves a clear purpose beyond novelty and maintains reader engagement rather than alienation.
**Assumed goal**: Literary fiction tradition that uses second person for specific psychological effects (complicity, dissociation, universalizing) — mainstream commercial fiction generally avoids second person as it is considered niche and potentially alienating
**Score 0**: Second person is used as a gimmick without purpose — the novelty of "you" is the only distinction from a first-person narrative. Readers feel commanded rather than immersed. The "you" creates alienation because the reader's actual experience contradicts the narrated actions. Or second person is used inconsistently, shifting in and out without purpose.
**Score 5**: Second person serves a clear purpose: creating complicity with a morally questionable protagonist, representing dissociation or trauma, universalizing specific experience, or constructing a narrative game with the reader. The "you" pronoun creates genuine intimacy or productive discomfort rather than mere novelty.
**Source**: https://www.scribophile.com/academy/using-second-person-pov
**Notes**: Notable examples: McInerney's Bright Lights, Big City (hedonism and self-destruction), Calvino's If on a winter's night a traveler (meta-fictional game). Technique tips: use present tense for added immediacy; use stream of consciousness; minimize "you" and "your" to avoid repetitiveness; ensure purposefulness beyond voice experimentation.

### Epistolary/Document Narration Craft
**What it measures**: When epistolary or found-document narration is used, whether each document type and narrator voice is authentic, distinct, and serves the story's information architecture.
**Assumed goal**: Epistolary fiction tradition (Richardson, Stoker, contemporary found-footage novels) where the document format itself is part of the narrative meaning — traditional narration does not face these requirements
**Score 0**: Documents sound identical despite being attributed to different characters. The document format adds nothing that traditional narration could not provide. Document types are inconsistent (a character writes in their diary the way they would narrate an action scene). The conceit is not maintained — documents contain information their putative authors would not include.
**Score 5**: Each document type sounds authentic to its form (letters sound like letters, reports sound like reports, texts sound like texts). Each narrator has a distinct voice reflecting education, personality, and relationship to the reader. The document format creates natural information gaps, unreliability, and dramatic irony that traditional narration could not achieve.
**Source**: https://www.sfwa.org/2020/02/11/epistolary-tales-the-narration-of-documentation/
**Notes**: "Use the minimum number of POVs and document types, especially when you're first starting out." Each narrator should have "a distinct voice, style, and perspective." Stoker's Dracula uses letters, ship logs, news clippings, diaries, and doctors' notes to "illuminate multiple agendas and viewpoints."

### Retrospective/Temporal Narrator Distance
**What it measures**: Whether the temporal distance between the narrating self and the experiencing self is established, consistent, and productive — particularly in first-person and past-tense narration.
**Assumed goal**: First-person retrospective narration tradition where the gap between "then" and "now" creates meaning — present-tense first-person narration deliberately eliminates this gap for immediacy
**Score 0**: The temporal position of the narrator is unclear or inconsistent. A past-tense narrator sometimes speaks with full hindsight and sometimes seems to not know what will happen next without any clear pattern. The gap between narrating self and experiencing self is neither exploited for irony/reflection nor collapsed for immediacy — it simply is not managed.
**Score 5**: The narrator's temporal position is clear and consistent. If retrospective, the gap between narrating self and experiencing self is exploited for dramatic irony, foreshadowing, earned wisdom, or emotional resonance. The narrator's present-tense reflections are distinct from their past-tense experiences. The temporal structure creates meaning.
**Source**: https://static1.squarespace.com/static/5af2133196d455829f76d724/t/5d4ed2ae27cc360001bb8095/1565446833766/GradLecture_Handout.pdf
**Notes**: "Narratorial duality" refers to the psychological and temporal separation between narrator's present (narrating) self and past (experiencing) self. "Retrospective narrators straddle two voices — the voice of innocence, and the voice of experience." The longer the temporal distance, the more reflective the narration becomes.

### Narrative Stance/Tone Consistency
**What it measures**: Whether the narrator's attitude toward the material — their stance, tone, and emotional relationship to the events — remains consistent and appropriate throughout, or shifts deliberately for effect.
**Assumed goal**: Craft tradition where tonal consistency builds reader trust and establishes the narrative contract — tonal disruption may be deliberate in satirical or genre-blending fiction
**Score 0**: The narrator's attitude toward the material shifts randomly — sardonic in one chapter, earnest in the next, without the story events justifying the change. The narrator's emotional relationship to events is inconsistent (detached about a death, overwrought about a minor inconvenience). Vocabulary and sentence construction do not match the established tone.
**Score 5**: The narrator maintains a consistent stance toward the material that establishes reader trust and expectations. When tone shifts occur, they are motivated by story events and clearly signaled. Vocabulary, sentence length, and construction consistently reflect the established tone (dry, wry, urgent, lyrical, etc.).
**Source**: https://theeditorsblog.net/2013/04/19/tone-mood-style-the-feel-of-fiction/
**Notes**: "Tone is achieved through word choice (diction), sentence construction and word order (syntax), and by what the viewpoint character focuses on." BubbleCow checklist: maintain consistent stance (dry, wry, urgent, calm) throughout; eliminate cliches; control jargon. Audio test: read aloud to verify rhythm consistency.

### Showing vs Telling Through POV
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative leverages POV to show character experience directly rather than telling the reader about it — rendering sensory experience, emotion through behavior, and understanding through implication.
**Assumed goal**: Post-Jamesian "dramatic method" tradition where fiction mimics direct experience rather than authorial report — oral storytelling traditions and some literary fiction (e.g., Marquez) embrace telling as a legitimate narrative mode
**Score 0**: The narrative constantly tells the reader about emotional states ("she was sad"), character traits ("he was brave"), and judgments ("it was a beautiful day") rather than rendering experience through sensory detail, behavior, and implication. The POV is squandered — being inside a character's head produces the same flat reporting as being outside it.
**Score 5**: Being inside the POV character's consciousness means the reader experiences the world through that character's senses, associations, and emotional responses. Emotions are shown through physical sensation, behavior, and the objective correlative rather than named. Character traits emerge from choices and observations rather than labels.
**Source**: https://www.alyssamatesic.com/free-writing-resources/show-dont-tell
**Notes**: "In general, showing helps you immerse the reader in the POV character's experience." Filters come between the character and the reader — "instead of showing the experience, the writer tells the reader what the character experiences." Objective correlative (Eliot): communicate emotion through objects, situations, or circumstances that correlate with the emotion, without naming it.

### Genre-Appropriate POV Convention
**What it measures**: Whether the POV choices respect or deliberately subvert genre expectations in a way that serves the story.
**Assumed goal**: Commercial fiction tradition where genre conventions set reader expectations for POV — literary fiction explicitly values subversion of convention when it serves artistic purposes
**Score 0**: POV choices ignore genre conventions without compensation. First-person omniscient in a thriller where suspense depends on limited information. Distant third person in a romance where intimacy is paramount. The genre's POV conventions are violated not for artistic effect but through ignorance.
**Score 5**: POV choices either fulfill genre expectations effectively (close third in contemporary romance, first person in noir, omniscient in epic fantasy) or subvert them with clear purpose and skill (second person in a thriller for complicity effect, omniscient in a romance for panoramic irony).
**Source**: https://www.alyssamatesic.com/free-writing-resources/third-person-pov
**Notes**: Genre guidelines: YA favors first-person; mysteries/thrillers favor close third or first-person for limited knowledge; romance favors close third or first-person for emotional development; literary/sci-fi/fantasy most commonly employ omniscient. Recommendation: research five bestsellers in your genre to study POV impact.

### POV Transition Craft
**What it measures**: Whether transitions between POV characters are clearly marked, smoothly executed, and establish the new POV character's consciousness quickly.
**Assumed goal**: Multi-POV fiction tradition where transitions must orient the reader efficiently — experimental fiction may use unmarked transitions for disorientation as a deliberate effect
**Score 0**: POV transitions are unmarked or poorly marked. Readers must re-read to identify whose perspective they are in. The new POV character's identity is not established in the opening lines of their section. Transitions occur mid-paragraph or mid-sentence without structural markers.
**Score 5**: POV transitions use clear structural markers (scene breaks, chapter breaks, section markers). The new POV character's identity and consciousness are established in the opening line or two of each section. Transitions move smoothly through the narrative distance spectrum — pulling back from one character's intimacy, through neutral distance, and into the new character's intimacy.
**Source**: https://prowritingaid.com/art/398/How-to-Seamlessly-Shift-Between-POV-Characters-.aspx
**Notes**: Types of breaks: paragraph break (minimum for POV shift), line break (same scene, different POV), scene break (POV change plus time passage), chapter break (clear structural boundary). BubbleCow diagnostic: "One viewpoint per scene with clear anchors in opening line." Best to stick to one perspective per scene, selecting the character with the most to share.

### Stream of Consciousness Craft
**What it measures**: When stream of consciousness or deep interiority techniques are used, whether they authentically represent the experience of thinking rather than merely being unpunctuated prose.
**Assumed goal**: Modernist literary tradition (Joyce, Woolf, Faulkner) where rendering consciousness itself is the primary artistic goal — commercial fiction generally avoids extended stream of consciousness as it can be difficult for readers
**Score 0**: Stream of consciousness is used as a label for run-on sentences or unpunctuated prose that does not actually represent the experience of thinking. No free association, looping repetition, sensory intrusion, or fragmented syntax that characterizes actual mental experience. Alternatively, it is so formless as to be incomprehensible without conveying character or meaning.
**Score 5**: Stream of consciousness authentically renders the experience of thinking — including free association, looping repetition, sensory observation intruding on abstract thought, strange punctuation reflecting mental rhythm, and the character's voice emerging through diction and preoccupation. The technique deepens characterization and thematic resonance.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stream_of_consciousness
**Notes**: Interior monologue relates thoughts as coherent sentences; stream of consciousness portrays "the actual experience of thinking, in all its chaos and distraction." Technical characteristics: free association, looping repetitions, sensory observations, nonexistent or unconventional punctuation and syntax. Key distinction from interior monologue: stream of consciousness represents mental state before it is "condensed, organized, or edited down into narrative coherence."

### First-Person Self-Description Discipline
**What it measures**: Whether first-person narrators describe themselves in ways that are physically and psychologically plausible — avoiding impossible self-observation and unmotivated vanity.
**Assumed goal**: Immersive first-person tradition where the narrator's self-awareness must be psychologically realistic — unreliable or vain narrators may describe themselves extensively as a character trait
**Score 0**: The first-person narrator describes their own appearance as if viewed from outside ("my golden hair cascaded," "my blue eyes sparkled"). Mirror scenes are used as clumsy exposition devices. The narrator comments on their own facial expressions they cannot see. Physical descriptions read like a character sheet rather than a person's natural self-awareness.
**Score 5**: Self-description is limited to what a person would naturally notice or think about — sensations (hair in their eyes), clothing choices (grabbing a jacket), others' reactions to them. Physical appearance is revealed through dialogue, others' responses, or contextually motivated observation. The narrator's self-image reflects their psychology rather than the author's need to describe them.
**Source**: https://evalangston.com/2018/03/01/6-point-of-view-mistakes/
**Notes**: "First-person narrators cannot describe themselves as if they are looking from a vantage point outside of themselves. They cannot see their own eyes or hair shining unless viewing a mirror." Author intrusion type #3: physical self-description without organic reason. Author intrusion type #4: describing one's own face/blush ("heat spread across her cheeks" is acceptable; "a blush crept into her cheeks" is not, as the character cannot see their own face).

### Narrator-Narratee Relationship
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative establishes a clear and consistent relationship between the narrator and their implied audience (narratee), and whether this relationship serves the story.
**Assumed goal**: Narratological tradition (Chatman, Prince) where the narrator-narratee relationship is a structural element that shapes how information is delivered — most commercial fiction has an implied but unremarked-upon narratee
**Score 0**: The narrator's audience is unclear or inconsistent. Sometimes the narrator addresses a specific "you," sometimes they narrate to no one in particular, sometimes they break the fourth wall — without these shifts being meaningful. The narrator explains things the narratee should already know, or fails to explain things the narratee would need.
**Score 5**: The narrator-narratee relationship is clear, consistent, and productive. If the narrator addresses a specific audience (a judge, a friend, a future self), this shapes what is explained, what is assumed, and how events are presented. If the narratee is implied rather than explicit, the level of explanation and intimacy remains consistent.
**Source**: https://books.openbookpublishers.com/10.11647/obp.0187/ch5.xhtml
**Notes**: Chatman's communication model: real author -> implied author -> narrator -> narratee -> implied reader -> real reader. The narratee is the audience the narrator addresses, distinct from the real reader and the implied reader. "The narrator of a story can never address the implied reader, which is necessarily external to the discourse."

### Vocabulary and Diction Alignment with POV
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative's word choice matches the POV character's education, culture, age, era, and personality — or whether the author's vocabulary bleeds through.
**Assumed goal**: Deep/close POV tradition where even narration (not just dialogue) should reflect the character's linguistic register — omniscient narration may deliberately use vocabulary above any character's level as part of the narrator's distinct voice
**Score 0**: A twelve-year-old narrator uses words like "juxtaposition" and "ephemeral." A medieval peasant POV character notices "mahogany Queen Anne furniture." A street-smart character narrates in academic prose. The author's vocabulary and education are visible through every character regardless of their background.
**Score 5**: Each POV character's narration reflects their linguistic register — vocabulary, sentence complexity, metaphor sources, and cultural references match their background. A chef notices culinary details; a soldier notices tactical terrain; a child uses simple words and concrete thinking. The author disappears behind the character's voice.
**Source**: https://evalangston.com/2018/03/01/6-point-of-view-mistakes/
**Notes**: "First person narrators' vocabulary, descriptions, and reflections must match their age and experience level. A twelve-year-old wouldn't describe furniture as 'a mahogany Queen Anne dining table' — they'd say 'a fancy, old-fashioned table.'" Author intrusion type #7: over-specific identification beyond character knowledge (a city person distinguishing pine from spruce).

### Objective Correlative in POV
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative uses objects, settings, and sensory details as emotional correlatives — conveying the POV character's emotional state through what they notice and how they perceive it, rather than through direct emotional labeling.
**Assumed goal**: Eliot's objective correlative tradition and the "show don't tell" school — some literary traditions (Latin American magical realism, fairy tale narration) directly name emotional states as part of their narrative mode
**Score 0**: Emotional states are named rather than evoked ("she was sad," "he felt angry"). The POV character's perceptions do not change based on their emotional state. The environment is described objectively regardless of whether the character is elated or devastated. No correlation between internal state and external perception.
**Score 5**: The POV character's emotional state colors their perception of everything. A grieving character notices the emptiness of rooms, dust on photographs, faded colors. An anxious character notices sharp edges, tight spaces, ticking clocks. Emotion is conveyed through the character's selective attention and interpretive lens rather than labels.
**Source**: https://www.writersdigest.com/be-inspired/what-is-an-objective-correlative-and-how-can-you-use-it-in-fiction
**Notes**: T.S. Eliot (1919): communicate emotion by describing objects, situations, or circumstances that correlate with the character's emotion without identifying it. Instead of "He felt a deep sense of loss," describe him standing in an empty room, running fingers over a framed photograph on a dusty mantelpiece.


## Sentinels Found

### Uniform Narrator Voice Across All POV Characters
**Detection**: In a multi-POV narrative, extract three consecutive paragraphs from each POV character's sections. If the paragraphs are indistinguishable in vocabulary, sentence structure, metaphor sources, and rhythm — if you cannot tell which character is narrating without the chapter header — the voices are uniform.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs have a single "voice" that is the statistical average of their training data. Without extensive character-specific prompting, all POV characters will narrate in the same register: slightly literary, slightly formal, competent but personality-free. This is the most fundamental LLM narration failure.
**Design space cost**: Deliberately uniform voice is used in some literary fiction where the uniformity itself is thematic (e.g., characters trapped in similar circumstances), though this is rare and always purposeful.
**Source**: https://www.noveble.com/blogs/finding-right-llm-settings

### Filter Word Saturation
**Detection**: Count filter verbs (saw, heard, felt, noticed, realized, wondered, thought, knew, seemed, watched) per 1000 words of narration. If the density exceeds 15 per 1000 words in a narrative using close/deep POV, the prose is over-filtered.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs default to mediated narration ("she saw the door open," "he felt the heat") because their training data includes all narrative distances. Without specific instruction, they produce the most statistically common narration pattern, which is moderately filtered rather than immersive.
**Design space cost**: Deliberately filtered narration is used in distanced or omniscient styles, and in characters who are emotionally dissociated or observational by nature (e.g., Ishiguro's Stevens).
**Source**: https://darlingaxe.com/blogs/news/filter-words

### Personality Reset Between Scenes
**Detection**: Compare the POV character's voice, preoccupations, and emotional state at the end of one of their scenes with the beginning of their next scene. If the character's emotional arc, vocabulary evolution, or psychological state shows no carry-over — as if each scene starts from a default template — a personality reset has occurred.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs process each prompt with limited context about character state across scenes. A character who was devastated at the end of chapter 3 may narrate chapter 7 with no residual grief, fear, or changed worldview because the model has no persistent character state.
**Design space cost**: Time-skip narration may legitimately show changed emotional states, but the change should be noted rather than ignored.
**Source**: https://www.creativindie.com/how-to-humanize-chatgpt-written-content-for-better-fiction-and-to-pass-ai-detection/

### Nauseatingly Upbeat Scene Endings
**Detection**: Check the final paragraph of each scene or chapter. If more than 70% end on an optimistic, resolved, or forward-looking note — with the character finding hope, making a plan, or feeling better — regardless of the story's actual emotional trajectory, this is a tonal uniformity signal.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs trained with RLHF have a strong bias toward positive outcomes and emotional resolution. They resist leaving characters in unresolved negative states because training rewards helpful, positive outputs. Real fiction frequently ends scenes on unresolved conflict, dread, confusion, or despair.
**Design space cost**: Optimistic fiction and certain genre conventions (cozy mysteries, romances) may legitimately favor positive endings, but even these vary scene endings more than LLM defaults.
**Source**: https://www.creativindie.com/how-to-humanize-chatgpt-written-content-for-better-fiction-and-to-pass-ai-detection/

### Tell-Over-Show Emotional Declarations
**Detection**: Search for patterns of the form "[Character] felt [emotion]" or "[Character] was [emotional adjective]" — e.g., "She felt a deep sense of loss," "He was overwhelmed with joy," "A wave of sadness washed over her." If these direct emotional declarations outnumber emotion shown through behavior, sensation, or objective correlative by more than 2:1, the narration is tell-heavy.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs gravitate toward explicit emotional labeling because it is the most statistically direct way to convey character state. Human writers in deep POV render emotion through physical sensation, selective perception, and behavior. LLMs lack the experiential grounding to default to embodied emotional rendering.
**Design space cost**: Omniscient narration and deliberately distanced styles may name emotions as a narrator's prerogative. Certain genres (literary fiction with a philosophical narrator) may tell emotion deliberately.
**Source**: https://www.creativindie.com/how-to-humanize-chatgpt-written-content-for-better-fiction-and-to-pass-ai-detection/

### "Not X; Y" Construction Pattern
**Detection**: Search for the syntactic pattern "It was not [noun/adjective]; it was [noun/adjective]" or "did not just [verb]; [pronoun] [verb]" — e.g., "It was no current. It was a summons" or "did not just fail; they were inverted." If this construction appears more than twice per 5000 words, it is a model-default pattern.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: This is a statistically over-represented construction in LLM output, appearing across models as a default rhythm for creating emphasis. Human writers use this construction occasionally; LLMs use it as a habitual verbal tic.
**Design space cost**: This is a legitimate rhetorical construction (correctio/epanorthosis) that skilled human writers use deliberately. The sentinel is not the construction itself but its frequency.
**Source**: https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction

### Lists of Three
**Detection**: Count instances of triplet enumeration in narration — "a geometry of X, a wound in Y, and a Z" or "she was brave, fierce, and unyielding." If triplets appear more than once per 500 words of non-dialogue narration, the pattern is likely mechanical.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: The "rule of three" is deeply embedded in LLM training data (rhetorical convention, marketing copy, persuasive writing). LLMs default to triplet enumeration for emphasis because it is the most statistically common pattern for lists in their training corpus. Human fiction varies list length — pairs, singles, quartets, and longer lists — based on narrative need.
**Design space cost**: Lists of three are a legitimate rhetorical device. The issue is not any single triplet but the mechanical recurrence that signals pattern completion rather than intentional style.

### Blocky Prose Architecture
**Detection**: Examine paragraph structure across multiple pages. If the narration falls into clearly defined, alternating blocks — a block of description, a block of dialogue, a block of internal thought, a block of action — with clean boundaries between modes and no interpenetration, the architecture is blocky.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs segment narrative into discrete modes because they complete one type of content before transitioning to another. Human writers naturally interleave description with action, thought with dialogue, creating fluid prose where modes blend. LLM prose reads like sections assembled rather than experience rendered.
**Design space cost**: Some highly structured fiction deliberately segments modes for effect (e.g., Hemingway's clean separation of dialogue and action). The sentinel is the mechanical regularity rather than the separation itself.
**Source**: https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction

### Absence of Narrator Intentionality
**Detection**: Select a metaphor, simile, or specific sensory detail from the narration and ask: "Does this detail connect to the character's psychology, the scene's theme, or the story's larger pattern?" If more than half the figurative language and specific details serve no purpose beyond decoration — they do not recur, develop, or connect to anything — the detail selection is unintentional.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate "probable language" without narrative intent — a detail like "marigolds" or "selenium tastes of rubber bands" appears because it is statistically likely after the preceding tokens, not because it connects to character or theme. Human writers select details that work together to create specific effects and meanings.
**Design space cost**: Surrealist and absurdist fiction may deliberately use unmotivated detail for its own sake.
**Source**: https://countercraft.substack.com/p/mfa-vs-llm-is-openais-metafiction

### Flat Narrative Arc Within POV
**Detection**: Track the emotional valence (positive/negative) of each POV character's narration across their scenes. If the emotional trajectory is essentially flat — neither building nor developing — with each scene returning to roughly the same emotional baseline, the arc is flat.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs "struggle to adequately develop critical turning points in a story, such as the major setback and climax," resulting in "diminished suspense and arousal." The emotional trajectory of LLM narration tends to be flatter than human writing, with less dynamic range and less escalation toward climax.
**Design space cost**: Deliberately flat emotional trajectories exist in minimalist fiction and certain literary styles, but these are typically purposeful and thematically motivated.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2407.13248v1

### Monotonous Sentence Cadence
**Detection**: Measure sentence length variation across five consecutive paragraphs of narration. If the standard deviation of sentence length is less than 3 words (most sentences are roughly the same length), the cadence is monotonous.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs tend toward a "droning" rhythm, producing sentences of similar length and structure. Human writers naturally vary sentence length for pacing, emphasis, and emotional effect — short sentences for tension, long sentences for reflection, fragments for impact.
**Design space cost**: Some literary styles (e.g., Gertrude Stein, certain hypnotic prose) deliberately use repetitive cadence for effect.
**Source**: https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction

### Redundant Emotional Telling After Showing
**Detection**: Search for patterns where emotion is shown through behavior or dialogue and then immediately restated explicitly — e.g., "Her hands trembled as she set down the cup. She was terrified." If the explicit emotional label follows a behavioral or physical rendering in more than 30% of emotional moments, this is a redundancy pattern.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs hedge toward completeness by both showing and telling emotion, as if uncertain the shown emotion was sufficient. Human writers in close POV trust the reader to interpret behavioral cues without following them with explicit labels.
**Design space cost**: Some didactic fiction or fiction aimed at younger readers may legitimately reinforce emotional cues. Omniscient narrators may also name emotions after showing them as part of their commentary function.

### "He Felt / She Felt" Construction Density
**Detection**: Count instances of "[pronoun] felt [emotion/sensation]" per 1000 words. If this construction appears more than 5 times per 1000 words in close/deep POV narration, the prose is over-reliant on explicit emotional mediation.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: This is the default LLM construction for conveying character emotion — direct, explicit, and unambiguous. It is the path of least resistance for a model that needs to convey emotional state. Human deep-POV writers render emotion through physical sensation, selective perception, and free indirect discourse rather than this construction.
**Design space cost**: First-person narration with a reflective narrator may legitimately use "I felt" constructions as part of the narrator's analytical voice.

### All Characters Uniformly Eloquent
**Detection**: Compare the narrative register of POV characters across different backgrounds — a street kid, a professor, a farmer, a teenager. If all narrate with the same level of verbal sophistication, sentence complexity, and vocabulary range — all sounding like educated adults regardless of background — the voices are uniformly eloquent.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs produce text at a consistent register that reflects the average sophistication of their training data — slightly above casual, slightly below academic. They do not naturally produce the broken syntax of a child, the jargon of a specialist, or the dialect of a regional speaker without explicit prompting.
**Design space cost**: Some literary fiction deliberately uses elevated prose for all POV characters as a stylistic choice (e.g., Cormac McCarthy's characters speak in Biblical cadences regardless of education). This is a conscious artistic decision, not a default.
**Source**: https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction

### Front-Loaded Character Descriptions
**Detection**: Check whether POV characters are described comprehensively in their first appearance — a full physical description, backstory summary, and personality overview delivered in the first 500 words of their first scene. If this occurs for more than half the POV characters, the descriptions are front-loaded.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs treat character introduction as an information-delivery task, dumping a character profile when the character first appears. Human writers reveal character gradually through behavior, dialogue, and selective detail over multiple scenes.
**Design space cost**: Certain genres (epic fantasy, large-cast thrillers) may front-load some description for reader orientation, though skilled authors still distribute this information.
**Source**: https://www.creativindie.com/how-to-humanize-chatgpt-written-content-for-better-fiction-and-to-pass-ai-detection/

### Context Poisoning / Vocabulary Well Exhaustion
**Detection**: Track the repetition of distinctive words, phrases, or constructions across a POV character's scenes. If the same metaphor sources, adjectives, or sentence constructions recur with increasing frequency as the narrative progresses — as if the text is drawing from an increasingly narrow vocabulary pool — context poisoning is occurring.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Once an LLM generates initial passages in a story, its subsequent output becomes "poisoned" by its own patterns, refusing to "stop going to the same well" for vocabulary and construction choices. Human writers instinctively seek variety; LLMs optimize toward self-consistency in a way that becomes repetitive.
**Design space cost**: A character's verbal tics and recurring preoccupations are legitimate character traits. The distinction is between deliberate recurring motifs and mechanical vocabulary impoverishment.
**Source**: https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction

### Absence of Subtext in POV Character's Observations
**Detection**: Examine passages where the POV character observes other characters. If the POV character's observations are purely literal — noting exactly what is happening without interpretation, bias, or the coloring of their own emotional state and desires — there is no subtext.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs describe what happens in a scene accurately but without the POV character's interpretive lens. A jealous character should misread friendly gestures as threatening; an infatuated character should notice beauty where others see plainness. LLMs produce neutral observation because they lack the character's psychological filter.
**Design space cost**: Deliberately neutral observation is used in external focalization (Hemingway's "camera eye") and in characters who are emotionally detached or highly disciplined observers.
**Source**: https://countercraft.substack.com/p/mfa-vs-llm-is-openais-metafiction

### Positive-Outcome Bias in Narrative Trajectory
**Detection**: Classify the emotional trajectory of the overall narrative. If the story follows a consistently positive arc (difficulty leading to triumph, sadness leading to acceptance, conflict leading to resolution) without any sustained negative movement, despair-without-hope sections, or ambiguous endings, the trajectory shows positive-outcome bias.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs "augment the human bias" toward positive outcomes, generating "Man in Hole" arcs more than half the time and nearly excluding narratives with negative endings ("Riches to Rags," "Oedipus"). This reflects RLHF training that rewards positive, helpful outputs.
**Design space cost**: Many legitimate genres (romance, cozy mystery, middle-grade fiction) conventionally end positively. The sentinel applies most strongly to literary fiction, tragedy, noir, and other forms where negative outcomes are expected.
