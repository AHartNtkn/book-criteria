# Character Voice and Register Variety: Prose Exemplars

Three passages from published fiction demonstrating distinct character voices, register shifts, and voice that reveals personality and background through how characters speak.

---

## 1. Zora Neale Hurston, *Their Eyes Were Watching God* (1937)

The narrator opens the novel:

> Ships at a distance have every man's wish on board. For some they come in with the tide. For others they sail forever on the horizon, never out of sight, never landing until the Watcher turns his eyes away in resignation, his dreams mocked to death by Time. That is the life of men.

Then Janie speaks to her friend Pheoby in dialect:

> "Ah don't mean to bother wid tellin' you somethin' unless Ah say so. You can tell 'em what Ah say if you wants to. Dat's just de same as me 'cause mah tongue is in mah friend's mouf."

And later, Tea Cake courts Janie:

> "Ah didn't aim tuh let on tuh yuh 'bout it, leastways not right away, but Ah ruther be shot wid tacks than fuh you tuh act wid me lak you is right now."

**What makes the voice work:** Hurston operates a double-register narrative throughout the entire novel. The omniscient narrator speaks in high literary English -- balanced clauses, abstract imagery, philosophical compression ("his dreams mocked to death by Time"). The moment any character opens their mouth, the prose shifts entirely into deep Southern Black vernacular: "Ah" for "I," elided consonants ("somethin'," "tuh"), rhythmic spoken phrasing ("ruther be shot wid tacks"). The two registers never contaminate each other. The narrator never condescends to the dialect; the dialect never strains toward the narrator's diction. The effect is that folk speech occupies the same plane of literary dignity as formal prose, without either register being subordinated to the other.

Source: Zora Neale Hurston, *Their Eyes Were Watching God* (J.B. Lippincott, 1937), Chapters 1 and 11. Opening passage confirmed via [SparkNotes](https://www.sparknotes.com/lit/eyes/section1/); dialect analysis via [CliffsNotes](https://www.cliffsnotes.com/literature/t/their-eyes-were-watching-god/critical-essays/use-of-dialect-in-their-eyes-were-watching-god) and [PBS](https://www.pbs.org/speak/seatosea/powerprose/hurston/).

## 2. Charles Portis, *True Grit* (1968)

Mattie Ross negotiates with the horse trader Colonel Stonehill:

> "I will tell you what I will do. I will take back those ponies and the gray horse that my father bought, and I will keep the price paid on the ponies and the saddle and you can keep the gray horse and the difference of twenty dollars."
>
> "I would not entertain such a proposition."
>
> "Then I will take it to law. I have a good lawyer in J. Noble Daggett, and he has never lost a case before Judge Parker."

Later, Rooster Cogburn testifies in court under cross-examination:

> "Well, if it ain't loaded and cocked it don't shoot."

And when Mattie refuses his whiskey:

> "I would not put a thief in my mouth to steal my brains."

**What makes the voice work:** Mattie Ross, a fourteen-year-old girl, speaks in archaic, lawyerly, King James Bible cadences -- formal syntax, no contractions, legalistic precision ("I will tell you what I will do," "entertain such a proposition"). Rooster Cogburn, a rough federal marshal, speaks in terse frontier vernacular with dry wit ("if it ain't loaded and cocked it don't shoot"). Portis never breaks the consistency of either voice: Mattie never drops into slang, Rooster never rises to formality. The comedy and the character emerge entirely from the collision of these two registers within the same conversation, and the reader can always tell who is speaking without a dialogue tag.

Source: Charles Portis, *True Grit* (Simon & Schuster, 1968), Chapters 1 and 3. Passages confirmed via [Wikiquote](https://en.wikiquote.org/wiki/True_Grit_(2010_film)), [LitCharts](https://www.litcharts.com/lit/true-grit/chapter-3), [Bookroo](https://bookroo.com/quotes/true-grit), and [American Rhetoric](https://www.americanrhetoric.com/MovieSpeeches/moviespeechtruegritcogburntestimony.htm).

## 3. Irvine Welsh, *Trainspotting* (1993)

Mark Renton narrates in his natural Leith dialect:

> "They won't let ye dae it. They won't let ye dae it, because its seen as a sign ay thir ain failure. The fact that ye jist simply choose tae reject whit they huv to offer. Choose us. Choose life. Choose mortgage payments; choose washing machines; choose sitting oan a couch watching mind-numbing and spirit-crushing game shows... Well, ah choose no tae choose life."

Then, in the "Speedy Recruitment" chapter, Renton code-switches into Standard English during a job interview:

> "Right... ah, those halcyon school days. It seems like a long time ago now."

While Spud, in the same interview, cannot code-switch and stays in broad Scots:

> "Actually man, ah've goat tae come clean here. Ah went tae Augie's, St. Augestine, likesay, then Craigy, eh Craigroyston, ken."

**What makes the voice work:** Welsh differentiates characters not through what they talk about but through their relationship to language itself. Renton is intelligent enough to code-switch -- he can put on Standard English when a situation demands it, which reveals his education and his conscious choice to reject the system he understands. Spud cannot code-switch; his dialect stays thick regardless of context, with verbal tics ("likesay," "ken") that mark him as trapped in an idiom he cannot escape. The entire novel is written in phonetic Scots ("dae" for "do," "ye" for "you," "oan" for "on"), so every character's voice is rendered as speech-on-the-page rather than literary prose, but within that shared dialect, each narrator's degree of fluency and self-awareness creates a distinct personality.

Source: Irvine Welsh, *Trainspotting* (Secker & Warburg, 1993), "Speedy Recruitment" chapter and opening monologue.
