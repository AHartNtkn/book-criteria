# Exemplar Passages: Description

Three short excerpts from published novels demonstrating sensory detail filtered through character POV, economical description that does narrative work, and specificity of detail that creates presence.

---

## 1. Sensory Detail Filtered Through Character POV

**Raymond Chandler, *The Big Sleep* (1939)**

> The air was thick, wet, steamy and larded with the cloying smell of tropical orchids in bloom. The glass walls and roof were heavily misted and big drops of moisture splashed down on the plants. The light had an unreal greenish color, like light filtered through an aquarium tank. The plants filled the place, a forest of them, with nasty meaty leaves and stalks like the newly washed fingers of dead men. They smelled as overpowering as boiling alcohol under a blanket.

**What makes this work:** Every sensory channel -- heat, humidity, smell, light, texture -- is active, but none of it is neutral observation. The details are filtered through Marlowe's hardboiled consciousness and arrive already carrying attitude: orchids are "cloying," leaves are "nasty" and "meaty," stalks resemble "the newly washed fingers of dead men." The greenhouse is objectively just hot and full of plants, but Marlowe's POV transforms it into something suffocating and sinister, foreshadowing the corruption he will find in the Sternwood family. The similes do double duty -- "light filtered through an aquarium tank" makes the reader feel trapped in an enclosed, alien space, while "boiling alcohol under a blanket" suggests something illicit being concealed. Description and characterization are inseparable: we learn what Marlowe sees and simultaneously how his mind works.

## 2. Economical Description Doing Narrative Work

**Joan Didion, *Play It As It Lays* (1970)**

> She drove it as a riverman runs a river, every day more attuned to its currents, its deceptions, and just as a riverman feels the pull of the rapids in the lull between sleeping and waking, so Maria lay at night in the still of Beverly Hills and saw the great signs soar overhead at seventy miles an hour. Agoura, Calabasas, King City, J 101. Again and again she returned to an intricate stretch just south of the interchange where successful passage from the Hollywood onto the Harbor required a diagonal move across four lanes of traffic.

**What makes this work:** Didion describes the Los Angeles freeway system but is actually describing Maria's psychological state. The passage never names what is wrong -- depression, dissociation, a crumbling marriage -- yet the reader understands that a woman who has memorized freeway currents and dreams of highway signs at night is someone compulsively numbing herself through motion. The concrete proper nouns (Agoura, Calabasas, King City, J 101) create specificity and presence, but they also convey emptiness: these are not destinations, just names that flash past. The single extended simile -- riverman to river -- does all the emotional work, equating mastery with compulsion. The final sentence, with its clinical language ("successful passage," "diagonal move," "four lanes"), makes driving sound like a problem to solve, which is exactly Maria's strategy for not feeling. Every word of description advances character.

## 3. Specificity of Detail Creating Presence

**Cormac McCarthy, *Blood Meridian* (1985)**

> They rode on and the sun in the east flushed pale streaks of light and then a deeper run of color like blood seeping up in sudden reaches flaring planewise and where the earth drained up into the sky at the edge of creation the top of the sun rose out of nothing like the head of a great red phallus until it cleared the unseen rim and sat squat and pulsing and malevolent behind them. The shadows of the smallest stones lay like pencil lines across the sand and the shapes of the men and their mounts advanced elongate before them like strands of the night from which they'd ridden, like tentacles to bind them to the darkness yet to come.

**What makes this work:** McCarthy achieves presence through relentless physical specificity -- not "the sun rose" but a precise sequence of light stages rendered in concrete terms (streaks, runs, reaches, flaring planewise). The absence of punctuation within the sentence forces the reader forward at the pace of the riders, unable to pause or look away. The similes escalate in violence and strangeness: "blood seeping up," "great red phallus," "squat and pulsing and malevolent" -- the sunrise itself becomes a hostile living thing. Then the second sentence contracts to the intimate and precise: shadows of the smallest stones lying like pencil lines. That scalar shift -- from cosmic to granular -- is what creates the sensation of being physically present in the landscape. The final image, shadows as "tentacles to bind them to the darkness yet to come," converts description into prophecy without breaking the sensory surface. The passage never leaves the visible world, yet everything it shows carries narrative weight.
