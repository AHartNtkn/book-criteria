## Criteria Found

### Exposition Integration Quality
**What it measures**: Whether background information (character history, world mechanics, setting context) is woven into the narrative through action, dialogue, and scene work rather than delivered in standalone blocks.
**Assumed goal**: Immersive fiction tradition (Gardner's "fictional dream") where readers experience the story world rather than receive lectures about it -- pulp adventure and some hard SF traditions may accept more direct exposition by genre contract.
**Score 0**: Exposition is delivered in multi-paragraph blocks that halt narrative momentum. Background information is presented as standalone passages disconnected from present-moment action. Characters stop what they're doing so the narrator can explain things. The reader must process abstract information with no emotional stakes or dramatic context.
**Score 5**: Background information emerges naturally through character action, dialogue subtext, environmental detail, and plot necessity. Readers absorb world and character information while remaining engaged with present-moment drama. Exposition serves double duty -- revealing character, advancing plot, or building tension simultaneously. Information appears at the moment it becomes relevant to the unfolding story.
**Source**: [Helping Writers Become Authors](https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/common-writing-mistakes-pt-50/), [The Darling Axe](https://darlingaxe.com/blogs/news/infodumps), [Ellen Brock](https://ellenbrockediting.com/2014/07/03/novel-boot-camp-lecture-3-how-to-avoid-info-dumping/)
**Notes**: The "weave and wait" method from K.M. Weiland: introduce details only when they become necessary, then integrate them subtly. The Darling Axe's "film test": if the action has stopped so a character can explain what's going on, it's an infodump. Ellen Brock's five-step elimination: assess necessity, identify minimum viable content, integrate into existing scenes, create new scenes to reveal information, avoid laziness.

### Backstory Distribution
**What it measures**: Whether character and world history is revealed gradually throughout the narrative at points of relevance, or concentrated in early chapters or single passages.
**Assumed goal**: Modern commercial fiction convention (post-Hemingway) favoring progressive disclosure -- epic fantasy and literary fiction with strong narrative voice may front-load more deliberately as part of their contract with the reader.
**Score 0**: Backstory is concentrated in the opening chapters or delivered in large blocks whenever a new character or element is introduced. Character histories are explained before the reader has reason to care. The narrative pauses to deliver biography.
**Score 5**: Backstory is distributed across the full narrative, appearing in sentence-length fragments at moments of emotional or plot relevance. The reader learns about character pasts through triggered memories, contextual details, dialogue implications, and behavioral patterns. Each piece of backstory illuminates the present scene.
**Source**: [The Novelry](https://www.thenovelry.com/blog/exposition-in-a-story), [Black Anvil Books](https://www.black-anvil-books.com/blog/avoiding-top-heavy-stories-the-problem-with-front-loaded-descriptions), [Writers Digest](https://www.writersdigest.com/write-better-fiction/writing-mistakes-writers-make-using-too-much-exposition-too-early)
**Notes**: The Novelry: "The level of exposition directly relates to a character's significance" -- minor characters warrant minimal backstory. Black Anvil: opening is "valuable real estate" that should be used strategically, not for encyclopedia entries. Writers Digest: "Avoid huge data dumps of everything that's ever happened" and make backstory relevant to the current scene.

### Information Delivery Timing (Rate of Revelation)
**What it measures**: Whether information is released at the optimal moment in the narrative -- not too early (losing tension), not too late (causing confusion), and at a pace that maintains reader engagement.
**Assumed goal**: Suspense-oriented storytelling tradition (Hitchcock, thriller conventions) where information timing is the primary mechanism for emotional engagement -- literary fiction may deliberately violate this for thematic reasons (e.g., Morrison's fragmented chronology in Beloved).
**Score 0**: Information is either frontloaded before the reader has context to appreciate it, or withheld so long the reader becomes confused or frustrated. Key details arrive at arbitrary moments unconnected to dramatic need. The rate of revelation is uniform -- no acceleration during high-stakes moments, no deceleration during breathing room.
**Score 5**: Information arrives at the moment it becomes narratively necessary and emotionally resonant. The rate of revelation accelerates and decelerates in rhythm with tension. Early chapters prioritize mystery and engagement over comprehensive explanation. Two to three open questions exist at any given time, with answers and new questions arriving in controlled alternation.
**Source**: [Self Pub Action Club: Rate of Revelation](https://selfpubactionclub.substack.com/p/rate-of-revelation-aka-the-best-writing), [Jane Friedman: Curiosity and Tension](https://janefriedman.com/the-importance-of-curiosity-and-tension-to-storytelling/), [Gilliam Writers Group](https://www.gilliamwritersgroup.com/blog/when-to-tell-and-when-to-withhold-mastering-the-release-of-information)
**Notes**: Jane Friedman's question architecture: maintain 2-3 open questions simultaneously operating at story, act, chapter, and scene levels. Pattern: raise two questions, answer one; raise two new, answer one. Gilliam Writers Group: "If too much is revealed too early, the story may lose tension and momentum. If too little is offered for too long, the reader may become frustrated or disengaged."

### Dialogue Exposition Naturalness
**What it measures**: Whether information delivered through dialogue emerges from legitimate character motivations and dynamics, or whether characters explain things they already know for the reader's benefit.
**Assumed goal**: Realist fiction tradition and contemporary commercial fiction convention where dialogue must sound like people actually talk -- stylized or theatrical traditions (Stoppard, Brecht) may deliberately use artificial dialogue exposition.
**Score 0**: Characters routinely explain things both parties already know ("As you know, Bob..."). Dialogue sounds like a textbook or lecture. Characters deliver information in ways no human would naturally speak. Conversations exist solely to inform the reader, with no authentic interpersonal dynamics.
**Score 5**: Every piece of information delivered through dialogue has a legitimate character motivation (persuading, arguing, revealing under pressure, teaching a genuine newcomer). Dialogue reveals character personality while conveying information. What characters don't say is as informative as what they do say. Conversations feel like authentic interpersonal exchanges that happen to illuminate the reader.
**Source**: [Helping Writers Become Authors: As You Know Bob](https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/as-you-know-bob/), [Andy Dibble: Defense of Maid and Butler](https://andydibble.com/2021/12/14/when-does-dialog-that-says-whats-already-known-work/), [F(r)iction: Exposition 101](https://frictionlit.org/the-writers-downfall-exposition-101/)
**Notes**: F(r)iction's EKT principle: "Everybody (in the world of the story) Knows That" -- don't explain information characters already possess. Andy Dibble identifies three situations where saying-what's-already-known works: rhetorical purposes (justifying emotions, making a point), reassurance/caution ("Careful, that's an oscillating phaser"), and ritual/ceremony. The "As You Know" test: if you can literally place "as you know" before a dialogue statement, it's problematic exposition.

### Show vs Tell Calibration
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative appropriately selects between dramatization (showing through scene, action, sensory detail) and narration (telling through summary, exposition) based on the importance and type of information being conveyed.
**Assumed goal**: The Ann Leckie school of nuanced show-don't-tell, where character development and emotional arc should be shown, but some worldbuilding exposition can be told efficiently -- rejecting both the purist "never tell" position and the undiscriminating "tell everything" position.
**Score 0**: The narrative tells when it should show (character emotions stated directly, character traits asserted rather than demonstrated, relationships described rather than dramatized) AND/OR shows when it should tell (minor transitional information dramatized into full scenes when a sentence of summary would suffice, causing pacing bloat).
**Score 5**: Character development, emotional states, and key relationships are demonstrated through action, behavior, dialogue, and sensory detail. Transitional information, time-jumps, and minor context are efficiently summarized. The balance shifts based on narrative importance: high-stakes moments are shown; low-stakes connective tissue is told. Worldbuilding uses the approach that best serves the immediate narrative need.
**Source**: [Ann Leckie: Show Don't Tell](https://annleckie.com/2015/05/25/writing-rules-show-dont-tell/), [Hidden Gems: Show Not Tell with Emotions](https://www.hiddengemsbooks.com/show-not-tell-with-emotions/), [Writers Helping Writers: When Telling Emotion is Right](https://writershelpingwriters.net/2019/08/when-telling-not-showing-emotion-is-the-right-choice/)
**Notes**: Leckie's key distinction: "For character and motivation, especially when affecting plot, showing is nearly always better. For non-characterization exposition (worldbuilding, background), a good balance of showing and telling is appropriate." She critiques "fetishizing" all-show exposition and argues "a beautifully done paragraph of exposition can sometimes do the job better." The question is not "should I show or tell?" but "is this the right choice for this specific moment?"

### Incluing and Worldbuilding Integration
**What it measures**: Whether unfamiliar world elements (technology, magic systems, social structures, terminology) are integrated seamlessly through contextual clues, or explained through direct exposition blocks.
**Assumed goal**: Jo Walton's SF reading protocol tradition where "worldbuilding is something the reader does, building the world from the clues" -- hard SF exposition may require more explicit technical detail, and secondary-world fantasy may need more overt cultural context.
**Score 0**: World mechanics are explained in lecture-format passages. Invented terminology is defined in parenthetical asides or footnotes that break narrative flow. Characters explain their own world as if they are strangers to it. The narrative stops to teach the reader about the setting.
**Score 5**: World elements emerge through character interaction with them. Invented terms are contextualized by usage, allowing readers to infer meaning. Characters demonstrate familiarity with their world rather than explaining it. Information about world mechanics appears at the moment of narrative relevance. Readers build their understanding incrementally from scattered clues, and the gap between knowing and not-knowing creates engagement rather than confusion.
**Source**: [Tomeworks: What is Incluing](https://www.tomeworksedits.com/blog/what-is-incluing-and-why-should-you-be-doing-it), [Futurismic: Jo Walton SF Reading Protocols](http://futurismic.com/2010/01/20/jo-walton-on-the-protocols-of-reading-science-fiction/), [Mythcreants: How to Teach World Terms](https://mythcreants.com/blog/how-to-teach-world-terms-without-confusing-readers/)
**Notes**: Jo Walton coined "incluing" as "the process of scattering information seamlessly through the text." She describes SF as "like a mystery where the world and the history of the world is what's mysterious." Mythcreants identifies three tiers of term clarity: terms already in the dictionary, terms that evoke real-world associations (e.g., "trolls"), and fully invented terms that need the most contextual support.

### RUE (Resist the Urge to Explain)
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative trusts readers to draw their own conclusions from dramatized evidence, or whether it explains the significance of its own scenes, actions, and emotional moments.
**Assumed goal**: The immersive fiction tradition (Gardner's fictional dream, Bickham's scene-and-sequel structure) where readers participate in meaning-making -- explicitly didactic fiction (allegory, parable, some literary fiction) may intentionally explain for thematic purposes.
**Score 0**: The narrative routinely explains the emotional significance of scenes it has just dramatized. Characters articulate their own feelings explicitly. The narrator tells the reader what to think about events. After showing a character's behavior, the text explains what that behavior means. Subtext is immediately surfaced as text.
**Score 5**: Dramatized scenes stand on their own without subsequent explanation. Characters' internal states are shown through behavior, dialogue, and physical response without explicit labeling. Readers are trusted to interpret implications, emotional undercurrents, and thematic resonances. Subtext remains subtext. The narrative creates questions rather than answers.
**Source**: [Linda S. Clare: RUE](https://lindasclare.com/2013/03/rue-resist-the-urge-to-explain/), [Terry Odell: R.U.E.](https://terryodell.com/r-u-e-resist-the-urge-to-explain/), [Linda S. Clare: Writing Without Explanations](https://lindasclare.com/2022/07/writing-without-explanations-rue/)
**Notes**: RUE was popularized by Browne and King in "Self-Editing for Fiction Writers." Key principle: "leak out bits and pieces in the same way you learn about people in real life: through things they say and do, their body language and their emotions." The goal is to immerse readers and keep them "walking around in your fictional dream." Explanations "put the brakes on the pace of your story and can do exactly the opposite of what the author intended."

### Subtext Quality
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative creates meaning through what is implied, unsaid, contradicted, or demonstrated through behavior -- the depth beneath the surface of what is explicitly stated.
**Assumed goal**: Literary fiction and sophisticated commercial fiction traditions (Hemingway's iceberg theory, Carver's minimalism) where subtext carries the emotional weight -- genre fiction with strong plot focus may prioritize clarity over subtlety in certain passages.
**Score 0**: Characters say exactly what they mean. Emotions are named rather than demonstrated. Thematic concerns are stated explicitly. No gap exists between what characters say and what they feel. Dialogue functions purely as information transfer. The narrative has no depth beneath its surface.
**Score 5**: What characters say and what they mean are productively different. Physical behavior contradicts or complicates spoken words. Repeated motifs accumulate meaning across the narrative. Silence and omission carry emotional weight. The reader senses complexity, hidden motivations, and unspoken dynamics. Thematic resonance emerges from pattern rather than statement.
**Source**: [Helping Writers Become Authors: 5 Ingredients for Subtext](https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/5-ingredients-need-story-subtext/), [Helping Writers Become Authors: Subtext in Dialogue](https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/subtext-in-dialogue/), [Hemingway Iceberg Theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iceberg_theory)
**Notes**: K.M. Weiland's five ingredients: subtext arises from space between fixed points, must explicitly exist beneath the surface in the author's mind, must remain under the surface in the text, is created by dichotomy between interior and exterior, and exists in silent spaces. Hemingway: "If you leave out important things or events that you know about, the story is strengthened. If you leave or skip something because you do not know it, the story will be worthless."

### Foreshadowing and Payoff Balance
**What it measures**: Whether significant plot developments, character changes, and revelations are properly prepared through earlier planted details, and whether those planted details pay off satisfactorily.
**Assumed goal**: Structuralist story craft (Sanderson's promise-progress-payoff, Chekhov's economy principle) where every element introduced must serve a purpose and major developments must feel "surprising yet inevitable" -- experimental and literary fiction may deliberately subvert payoff expectations.
**Score 0**: Major plot developments arrive without preparation, feeling arbitrary or deus ex machina. Alternatively, extensive setup details never pay off (unfired Chekhov's guns). Twists depend on information the reader never had fair access to. Foreshadowing is either absent (readers feel blindsided) or so heavy-handed that every development is predictable.
**Score 5**: Key developments are supported by earlier planted details that readers register subconsciously on first reading and recognize consciously on rereading. The balance between surprise and inevitability is maintained. Setup details all pay off, though sometimes in unexpected ways. Red herrings are intentional and distinguishable from broken promises. Readers experience the "aha" moment of realizing how earlier breadcrumbs connect.
**Source**: [The Darling Axe: Foreshadowing](https://darlingaxe.com/blogs/news/foreshadowing), [Helping Writers Become Authors: Setup and Payoff](https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/setup-and-payoff-the-two-equally-important-halves-of-story-foreshadowing/), [Backstage: Plant and Payoff](https://www.backstage.com/magazine/article/chekhovs-gun-explained-examples-75529/)
**Notes**: The Darling Axe: best twists are "inevitable but unexpected." Over-foreshadowing makes plots predictable; under-foreshadowing creates confusion. Backstage distinguishes red herrings (intentional misdirection serving the story) from broken promises (accidental setup without payoff). Setup and payoff must function as a unified system; a payoff without setup creates fragmented storytelling.

### Dramatic Irony and Information Asymmetry Use
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative deliberately creates productive gaps between what the reader knows and what characters know, generating tension, suspense, dread, or humor through that asymmetry.
**Assumed goal**: Hitchcock's suspense tradition where "giving the audience information the characters don't have" creates the deepest engagement -- mystery genre convention may instead favor keeping reader and detective at the same knowledge level.
**Score 0**: The reader and all characters possess identical information at all times, missing opportunities for tension. Alternatively, information asymmetry is created accidentally (reader confused about what characters know) rather than deliberately. No strategic use of knowledge gaps to generate emotion.
**Score 5**: The narrative deliberately controls who knows what and when. Some scenes gain power from the reader knowing something a character doesn't (dramatic irony creating dread or suspense). Other scenes gain power from the reader knowing less than the characters (mystery creating curiosity). The knowledge gaps between reader and characters shift purposefully across the narrative, and each shift produces a specific emotional effect.
**Source**: [MasterClass: Dramatic Irony](https://www.masterclass.com/articles/writing-101-what-is-dramatic-irony-literary-device-definition-examples-and-tips-for-employing-dramatic-irony-in-writing), [Open Culture: Hitchcock](https://www.openculture.com/2021/07/alfred-hitchcock-explains-the-difference-between-suspense-surprise.html), [Medium: Asymmetry of Knowledge](https://medium.com/@r4isstatic/the-asymmetry-of-knowledge-70fb22a4dbb1)
**Notes**: Hitchcock's bomb example: four people talking for five minutes, then a bomb explodes = ten seconds of surprise. But tell the audience about the bomb first = five minutes of suspense. The knowledge triangle: storyteller, audience, and characters all have different states of knowledge. Mystery = characters know more than audience (speculation). Suspense = audience knows more than characters (anticipation of revelation).

### Curiosity Engine (Question Architecture)
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative actively generates reader curiosity by raising questions and strategically delaying answers, creating a forward-pulling engine of engagement.
**Assumed goal**: Page-turner and commercial fiction traditions (thrillers, mysteries, genre fiction) where sustained curiosity is the primary engagement mechanism -- contemplative literary fiction may prioritize atmosphere or language over question-driven momentum.
**Score 0**: No active questions pull the reader forward. Information is delivered proactively before the reader has reason to wonder about it. The narrative answers questions before they're asked, or fails to raise them at all. Reader has no sense of wanting to know what happens next.
**Score 5**: The narrative consistently generates curiosity by presenting effects before causes, outcomes before explanations, and partial information that invites completion. Multiple questions operate at different time horizons (scene-level, chapter-level, book-level). Questions are answered at satisfying intervals while new ones emerge. The reader is always in a state of wanting to know something the narrative has not yet revealed.
**Source**: [Jane Friedman: Curiosity and Tension](https://janefriedman.com/the-importance-of-curiosity-and-tension-to-storytelling/), [Pages & Platforms: Exposition and the Reading Brain](https://www.pagesandplatforms.com/blog/too-much-information-exposition-and-the-reading-brain), [Margie Lawson: Withholding Information](https://www.margielawson.com/how-withholding-too-much-information-kills-story-tension/)
**Notes**: Pages & Platforms: "Let characters know information readers don't" and leverage the "Pandora Effect" -- humans compulsively pursue unanswered questions. Margie Lawson distinguishes two mechanisms: creating curiosity (raising questions by providing just enough context) and sustaining uncertainty (showing what characters stand to lose). Too much withholding frustrates; too little eliminates forward pull.

### Narrative Economy (Multi-Function Exposition)
**What it measures**: Whether exposition passages serve multiple narrative functions simultaneously (revealing character, advancing plot, building atmosphere, establishing theme) rather than serving only the single function of conveying information.
**Assumed goal**: Craft-conscious fiction tradition (Le Guin's "avoiding expository lumps," Maass's micro-tension principle) where every sentence should do more than one thing -- maximalist literary fiction (Pynchon, Wallace) may deliberately include information for its own sake as a stylistic choice.
**Score 0**: Exposition passages serve a single function: delivering information. Background sections do not reveal character, advance plot, or create tension. Information delivery interrupts rather than enhances the emotional experience. Passages could be extracted as encyclopedia entries without losing their character.
**Score 5**: Every exposition passage simultaneously performs at least two functions: conveying information while also revealing character voice, advancing a relationship dynamic, creating tension, establishing mood, or developing theme. Background information is filtered through character perspective in ways that reveal as much about the character as about the subject. No passage exists solely to inform.
**Source**: [No Film School: Economy in Storytelling](https://nofilmschool.com/economy-in-storytelling), [F(r)iction: Exposition 101](https://frictionlit.org/the-writers-downfall-exposition-101/), [Donald Maass micro-tension](https://writeswithtools.com/2020/03/02/microtension-what-is-it-and-how-do-we-get-it-onto-the-page/)
**Notes**: Maass's micro-tension in exposition: "true tension in exposition comes not from circular worry or repetitive turmoil; it springs from emotions in conflict and ideas at war." F(r)iction: expositional devices can serve character development, as a journal entry shares exposition while telling us the character is self-reflective. Chekhov's economy principle: if it doesn't serve the story in multiple ways, remove it.

### Psychic Distance Management for Exposition
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative appropriately modulates the reader's perceptual distance from characters when delivering exposition -- using wider distance for context-setting and closer distance for emotionally charged information.
**Assumed goal**: John Gardner's psychic distance framework (from "The Art of Fiction") where the writer consciously controls reader proximity to characters -- omniscient narration traditions may operate at wider default distance.
**Score 0**: Exposition is delivered at a single psychic distance regardless of content. Close-POV scenes are interrupted by distant, omniscient-sounding explanatory passages. Jarring shifts between intimate character experience and textbook-style information delivery. The narrator's exposition voice is indistinguishable from an encyclopedia.
**Score 5**: Psychic distance widens gracefully for scene-setting and context, then narrows for emotional content and character-filtered information. Exposition at close distance is colored by character voice, perception, and emotional state. Transitions between distances are smooth rather than jarring. The narrative voice in expository passages is consistent with the overall POV strategy.
**Source**: [Emma Darwin: Psychic Distance](https://emmadarwin.substack.com/p/psychic-distance-what-it-is-and-how), [Jericho Writers: Narrative Distance](https://jerichowriters.com/narrative-distance-definition-with-examples-for-fiction-writers/), [The History Quill: Narrative Distance](https://thehistoryquill.com/narrative-distance-what-it-is-and-how-to-control-it/)
**Notes**: Gardner describes psychic distance as "the distance that the reader feels between himself and the events of the story." The spectrum ranges from distant overview ("It was a time of great upheaval") through mid-range narration to intimate interior ("God, she thought, what am I doing here?"). The key rule: transitions between levels should move one at a time, not jump abruptly.

### Withholding Discipline (Fair Play Information Control)
**What it measures**: Whether information is withheld for legitimate narrative purposes (creating mystery, building suspense, pacing revelation) or withheld artificially to generate false tension (the POV character knows but the reader is denied access without justification).
**Assumed goal**: Fair-play mystery tradition (Van Dine, Knox) and POV integrity convention where readers should have access to everything the POV character knows -- noir and unreliable narrator traditions may deliberately break this for specific effects.
**Score 0**: The POV character clearly possesses information that is artificially withheld from the reader (e.g., "She knew what that meant, and it changed everything" without revealing what). Characters are kept deliberately vague to preserve twists. The narrative creates false mystery by having characters withhold from each other without authentic motivation. The reader feels manipulated rather than intrigued.
**Score 5**: Information is withheld only when the POV character also lacks it, or when unreliable narration is a deliberate, well-signaled technique. Mystery derives from genuine unknowns in the story situation rather than from the narrator hiding what characters know. Dramatic irony (reader knowing more than characters) is achieved by showing information, not by narratorial withholding. Twists are earned through misdirection rather than information suppression.
**Source**: [CRAFT Literary: Withholding Information](https://www.craftliterary.com/2017/10/30/withholding-information/), [Margie Lawson: Withholding Kills Tension](https://www.margielawson.com/how-withholding-too-much-information-kills-story-tension/), [Gilliam Writers Group: When to Tell and When to Withhold](https://www.gilliamwritersgroup.com/blog/when-to-tell-and-when-to-withhold-mastering-the-release-of-information)
**Notes**: Annie Dillard's principle: "Spend it all, shoot it, play it, lose it, all, right away, every time." CRAFT Literary: the urge to save something good for later signals you should use it now. Margie Lawson: "When you don't give readers a clear sense of what might happen if everything doesn't go the character's way... they can't possibly be invested."

### Cognitive Load Management
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative respects the limits of reader working memory when introducing new information, particularly unfamiliar names, concepts, and world elements.
**Assumed goal**: Cognitive science-informed fiction writing (working memory research, reader comprehension studies) -- maximalist fiction (Tolkien appendices, Malazan Book of the Fallen) may deliberately exceed cognitive thresholds as part of their aesthetic.
**Score 0**: Multiple unfamiliar names, concepts, and world elements are introduced simultaneously. The reader must track more than 4-5 new items at once. Invented terminology is introduced without contextual support. Dense exposition passages require backtracking to comprehend. The reading experience feels like work rather than entertainment.
**Score 5**: New information is introduced one element at a time with contextual scaffolding. Invented terms are spaced apart and surrounded by familiar elements. Complex concepts are chunked into digestible pieces. The narrative provides cognitive breathing room between dense information passages. Readers rarely need to reread for comprehension.
**Source**: [Pages & Platforms: Exposition and the Reading Brain](https://www.pagesandplatforms.com/blog/too-much-information-exposition-and-the-reading-brain), [Mythcreants: How to Teach World Terms](https://mythcreants.com/blog/how-to-teach-world-terms-without-confusing-readers/), [Mythcreants: Seven Common Causes of Reader Confusion](https://mythcreants.com/blog/seven-common-causes-of-reader-confusion/)
**Notes**: Working memory capacity: 4-5 items maximum at once. "Chunking" groups information to reduce cognitive burden. Mythcreants warns against "trying to make readers learn too many names in too short a period" -- listing four new names in one paragraph "makes the story feel like homework." Three-tier clarity system for invented terms: dictionary words, associative terms (e.g., "trolls"), and fully invented terms requiring the most support.

### Emotional Exposition Quality
**What it measures**: Whether characters' emotional states are conveyed through behavior, physical response, action, and dialogue subtext rather than through direct emotional labeling or internal monologue that names feelings.
**Assumed goal**: Deep-POV and close-third-person fiction conventions where reader immersion depends on experiencing emotions alongside the character rather than being told about them -- omniscient narration and some literary fiction may use direct emotional commentary deliberately.
**Score 0**: Characters' emotions are named directly ("She felt angry." "He was devastated."). Internal monologue explicitly labels emotional states. The narrator tells the reader what characters are feeling rather than showing through behavior. Emotional moments are summarized rather than dramatized.
**Score 5**: Emotional states are conveyed through physical sensation, involuntary behavior, altered perception, dialogue tone, action choices, and environmental interaction. Readers infer emotions from evidence rather than being told. Complex emotional states are shown through contradictory signals rather than labeled. The gap between what a character says and what they visibly feel creates productive tension.
**Source**: [Hidden Gems: Show Not Tell with Emotions](https://www.hiddengemsbooks.com/show-not-tell-with-emotions/), [C.S. Lakin: Show Emotions](https://www.livewritethrive.com/2015/06/24/how-fiction-writers-can-show-emotions-in-their-characters-in-effective-ways/), [Helping Writers Become Authors: Info Dumps Pt. 50](https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/common-writing-mistakes-pt-50/)
**Notes**: Weiland identifies "emotional info dumps" as a distinct category: characters explaining their feelings explicitly, on-the-nose dialogue revealing motivations, characters articulating internal conflicts directly. The question: "How often do people dump intimate thoughts?" Reserve explicit emotional revelation for earned climactic moments. Use subtext and what characters don't say.

### Exposition Vehicle Character Design
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative deploys characters with legitimate reasons for not knowing things (newcomers, outsiders, apprentices) as natural exposition vehicles, rather than having established characters explain their own world unnaturally.
**Assumed goal**: Genre fiction convention (fantasy, SF, thriller) where world complexity requires a reader proxy -- literary fiction and contemporary settings may not need exposition vehicle characters at all.
**Score 0**: No character has a legitimate reason to learn about the world, forcing established characters to explain things they already know. Alternatively, the newcomer character is a transparent exposition device with no other narrative function. Characters ask questions solely for reader benefit rather than out of genuine character motivation.
**Score 5**: At least one character has authentic reasons for needing information that coincide with the reader's needs. This character has genuine narrative purpose beyond being an exposition recipient. Their learning process creates its own dramatic tension. Established characters' explanations emerge from teaching, warning, arguing, or other motivated interactions rather than from unprompted lectures.
**Source**: [September C. Fawkes: Info Without Info-Dumping](https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2018/04/how-to-dump-info-without-info-dumping.html), [No Film School: Fish Out of Water](https://nofilmschool.com/fish-out-of-water-trope), [Jessica Brody: Exposition Characters and Window Characters](https://www.jessicabrody.com/2021/03/how-to-fix-info-dumping-with-exposition-characters-and-window-characters/)
**Notes**: The "ignorant character" technique from Inception: introduce a character unfamiliar with the world's mechanics, and as this character learns, readers naturally learn alongside them. Fish-out-of-water characters create "an excuse for a character to notice everything and ask questions." The challenge: deliver explanation "in a way that doesn't stop the story" -- explanations must be "short or broken up" with "something interesting going on around the characters."

### Prologue Exposition Management
**What it measures**: Whether prologues (if present) serve genuine narrative functions beyond frontloading world or character exposition that could be delivered more effectively later.
**Assumed goal**: Contemporary commercial publishing convention (agent/editor preferences) where prologues must justify their existence -- epic fantasy tradition (Jordan, Martin) has established prologues as a genre convention serving different functions.
**Score 0**: The prologue is a history/geography lesson establishing the world through exposition dump. It contains no conflict, character engagement, or dramatic stakes. The information in the prologue could be distributed more effectively throughout the narrative. Readers skip the prologue and miss nothing essential.
**Score 5**: If present, the prologue introduces a specific dramatic situation (different from Chapter 1) that creates a question or establishes stakes the main narrative will address. It contains its own conflict and character engagement. It provides information the reader genuinely needs before the main narrative begins, in a form that would be awkward to deliver later. Most information about the world and its mechanics is still saved for integrated delivery within the main narrative.
**Source**: [Reedsy: Prologue Guide](https://reedsy.com/blog/guide/parts-of-a-book/prologue/), [Jess Lawrence: To Prologue or Not](https://www.jesslawrence.co.uk/post/to-prologue-or-not-to-prologue), [Mythcreants: Seven Prologues and Problems](https://mythcreants.com/blog/seven-prologues-and-the-problems-they-cause/)
**Notes**: "A prologue should not be an information dump: a good prologue enhances your story, rather than explaining it." The temptation in fantasy/SF to "front-load with the prologue -- to start with a chunk of exposition setting out the geography and rules of your world" results in a textbook rather than a story. "More often than not, the information given in exposition prologues can be naturally revealed at other points in the story."

### Unreliable Narrator Information Control
**What it measures**: Whether deliberate narratorial unreliability is well-crafted (clues planted, misdirection purposeful, revelation earned) rather than accidental or clumsy (reader feels cheated, no fair-play clues exist).
**Assumed goal**: Postmodern and psychological fiction traditions (Nabokov, Flynn, Ishiguro) where narrator unreliability serves thematic purposes -- transparent narration traditions may avoid unreliability entirely.
**Score 0**: The narrator withholds or distorts information without fair-play clues for the reader. The revelation of unreliability feels like a cheat because no evidence existed to suspect it. Alternatively, the unreliability is so transparent that no productive tension exists. The purpose of the unreliability is unclear beyond generating a twist.
**Score 5**: Clues, hints, and subtle indications throughout the story signal that the reader is being deceived, even while being thrown off the scent. The revelation retroactively enriches earlier passages. The unreliability serves the story's thematic concerns (self-deception, trauma, power dynamics). Misdirection is achieved through emphasis and framing rather than outright lying. On rereading, the signs are visible.
**Source**: [Jericho Writers: Unreliable Narrator](https://jerichowriters.com/the-unreliable-narrator/), [Writing.ie: Misdirection](https://www.writing.ie/resources/the-unreliable-narrator-the-art-of-misdirection/), [PenUltimate: Truth and Lies in Fiction](https://penultimateword.com/book-marketing/how-to-write-unreliable-narrator/)
**Notes**: "To avoid cheating your reader, you'll need to inject clues, hints, and subtle indications throughout the story." Good misdirection techniques: "include the information in a long list of items, or in a paragraph of over-detailed description." The intentionally deceptive unreliable narrator works "like a magician of storytelling, using misdirection and selective disclosure to shape the reader's perception."

### Author Intrusion Discipline
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative voice remains consistent and in service of the story, or whether the author's own voice, opinions, or knowledge break through to lecture, moralize, or explain beyond what the narrative naturally supports.
**Assumed goal**: Modern fiction convention (post-Victorian) where the author remains behind the story -- metafictional traditions (Vonnegut, Calvino) may deliberately use author intrusion as a technique.
**Score 0**: The narrator breaks from story voice to editorialize, moralize, or explain significance. The author's research is showcased beyond what the story requires. Characters become mouthpieces for the author's views. The narrative tells the reader what to think or feel about events rather than dramatizing them. POV boundaries are violated to deliver information the POV character couldn't possess.
**Score 5**: The narrative voice remains consistent with the established POV and tone. Information is delivered through channels available to the characters and narrative situation. The author's knowledge and opinions are invisible -- the story's themes emerge from dramatized events rather than from narratorial commentary. Research enriches the narrative without being displayed for its own sake.
**Source**: [Writerandthewolf: Authorial Intrusion](https://www.writerandthewolf.com/glossary/authorial-intrusion/), [The Editor's Blog: Author Intrusion](https://theeditorsblog.net/2011/12/13/weed-out-author-intrusion/), [C.S. Lakin: POV Violations](https://www.livewritethrive.com/2024/08/31/common-pov-violations-and-how-to-catch-them/)
**Notes**: "An omniscient narrator who sounds like the writer trying to teach a history lesson or preach a sermon is author intrusion." The Editor's Blog: "If you're writing a novel to get a point across or teach a moral -- author opinions are best saved for personal essays." Key test: "When self-editing, ask yourself whether your character knows the things you're conveying."

### Contextual Distance Calibration
**What it measures**: Whether the amount of explicit exposition is calibrated to the actual gap between the reader's likely knowledge and the story world's reality.
**Assumed goal**: Reader-aware fiction craft where exposition volume is proportional to the audience's need -- secondary world fantasy requires more exposition than contemporary realism by definition; historical fiction falls between.
**Score 0**: The narrative over-explains familiar concepts (contemporary real-world settings described as if alien) or under-explains unfamiliar ones (secondary worlds or deep-future settings with insufficient context). The author does not distinguish between what readers can infer from shared cultural knowledge and what genuinely requires explanation.
**Score 5**: The exposition budget is proportional to the contextual distance between reader and story world. Contemporary settings rely on shared cultural assumptions with minimal exposition. Speculative settings provide enough unfamiliar-world context for comprehension without over-explaining elements readers can infer. Historical settings explain what's genuinely surprising to modern readers while trusting familiarity with human universals.
**Source**: [Andy J. Hodges: Exposition and Contextual Distance](https://www.andyjhodges.com/post/exposition-in-a-story-contextual-distance), [Ann Leckie: Show Don't Tell](https://annleckie.com/2015/05/25/writing-rules-show-dont-tell/), [F(r)iction: Exposition 101](https://frictionlit.org/the-writers-downfall-exposition-101/)
**Notes**: Contextual distance is the gap between the reader and the text. A thriller set in 1990s Manchester for UK readers needs minimal context; a novel set in the 2040s needs detailed explanations. Science fiction and fantasy "regularly include more exposition than other genres" as a genre norm. Leckie: "What the right choices are will depend on what you're aiming at, and who your audience is."

### Micro-Tension in Expository Passages
**What it measures**: Whether exposition passages maintain line-by-line tension through conflicting ideas, emotional undercurrents, or character voice, rather than presenting information flatly.
**Assumed goal**: Donald Maass's "fire in fiction" philosophy where every line must create some form of tension or unease -- literary fiction with contemplative pacing may use deliberately un-tense exposition for atmospheric effect.
**Score 0**: Exposition passages are emotionally flat. Information is presented matter-of-factly with no undercurrent of tension, conflict, or unease. Expository paragraphs could be removed and inserted elsewhere without any loss of emotional context. The reader's emotional engagement drops during exposition.
**Score 5**: Even exposition passages contain emotional tension: conflicting ideas, character anxiety about the information being conveyed, ominous implications of seemingly neutral facts, or voice-driven unease. Information delivery is filtered through character perspective in ways that create micro-tension. Readers remain emotionally engaged even during informational passages.
**Source**: [Writes With Tools: Microtension](https://writeswithtools.com/2020/03/02/microtension-what-is-it-and-how-do-we-get-it-onto-the-page/), [Donald Maass Beachtalk](http://wordswimmer.blogspot.com/2009/04/beachtalk-with-donald-maass-on-creating.html), [Linda S. Clare: Microtension](https://lindasclare.com/2013/07/easy-ways-to-write-microtension/)
**Notes**: Maass's technique: list all POV character emotions and ideas in the passage, discard what's obvious, find emotions that conflict and ideas at war, grab what creates unease/uncertainty/worry/questions/puzzles/dilemmas, and rewrite. "True tension in exposition comes not from circular worry or repetitive turmoil; it springs from emotions in conflict and ideas at war."

### Promise-Progress-Payoff Information Structure
**What it measures**: Whether the novel's information architecture follows a promise-progress-payoff pattern where early information creates expectations, middle sections show development toward those expectations, and later sections deliver satisfying resolution.
**Assumed goal**: Brandon Sanderson's structural framework and commercial storytelling convention where reader expectations must be managed -- deconstructionist and anti-genre fiction may deliberately frustrate this structure.
**Score 0**: The novel establishes expectations it never fulfills (broken promises). Middle sections fail to show progress toward established questions. Payoffs arrive without sufficient setup. The information structure creates no sense of direction or purpose. Readers cannot articulate what the novel is building toward.
**Score 5**: Early chapters establish clear promises about the kind of story being told (genre, tone, central question, character trajectory). Middle sections consistently show progress through new information, complications, and partial revelations. Climactic sections deliver payoffs that are both surprising and inevitable. Each promise-progress-payoff cycle operates at multiple scales (scene, chapter, act, book).
**Source**: [Brandon Sanderson: Guide to Plot](https://www.brandonsanderson.com/blogs/blog/brandon-sandersons-2025-guide-to-plot-lecture-2), [September C. Fawkes: Promise, Progress, Payoff](https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2024/10/promise-progress-payoff-in-stories-acts.html), [Helping Writers Become Authors: Setup and Payoff](https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/setup-and-payoff-the-two-equally-important-halves-of-story-foreshadowing/)
**Notes**: Sanderson's five promise types: genre, tone, plot, character, and change/conflict. The perfect payoff is "a balance between expectation and surprise" -- "not about delivering exactly what readers predict, but about giving them something that feels both unexpected and inevitable." Progress must be visible: if the story is a mystery, show characters finding clues; if about travel, show characters traveling.

## Sentinels Found

### Labeled Emotion Exposition
**Detection**: Characters' emotions are directly named using emotion words ("she felt angry," "he was devastated," "a wave of sadness washed over her") more than twice per chapter, where the context already demonstrates the emotion through action or dialogue.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs default to explicit emotional labeling because naming emotions is a high-probability token sequence in training data. The arXiv study on AI writing found that human editors consistently removed explicit emotion labels in favor of behavioral demonstration. LLMs lack the craft understanding that "showing" emotion through behavior creates deeper reader engagement than "telling" emotion through labeling.
**Design space cost**: You lose reader participation in meaning-making. When emotions are named, readers receive information passively. When emotions are shown through behavior, readers actively infer and become co-creators of emotional meaning, creating deeper engagement.
**Source**: [arXiv: Can AI Writing Be Salvaged?](https://arxiv.org/html/2409.14509v5), [Hidden Gems: Show Not Tell with Emotions](https://www.hiddengemsbooks.com/show-not-tell-with-emotions/)

### Redundant Explanation After Demonstration
**Detection**: A scene or passage demonstrates something through action, dialogue, or behavior, and then a subsequent sentence or paragraph explicitly explains what was just shown (e.g., showing a character slamming a door, then writing "She was clearly furious").
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: The arXiv taxonomy identified 18% of all LLM edits as "Unnecessary/Redundant Exposition" -- text that "restated the obvious or provided details that add little value." LLMs generate redundant explanation because their probability model favors completeness and explicit closure over reader inference. This pattern directly violates the RUE (Resist the Urge to Explain) principle.
**Design space cost**: You lose subtext. Every explicit explanation that follows a demonstration eliminates a gap where the reader could have created meaning. It also patronizes readers, signaling distrust in their intelligence.
**Source**: [arXiv: Can AI Writing Be Salvaged?](https://arxiv.org/html/2409.14509v5), [Linda S. Clare: RUE](https://lindasclare.com/2013/03/rue-resist-the-urge-to-explain/)

### "As You Know, Bob" Dialogue
**Detection**: Two characters discuss information that both would already possess, identifiable by applying the test: can you insert "as you know" before the statement and it remains grammatically sensible? E.g., "As the head of the magic council, you've sworn an oath to protect the realm."
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs struggle with tracking what characters in a fictional world would already know, because the model's training data includes the reader's need for information but lacks the ability to distinguish between reader-need and character-need. The model generates exposition through the easiest available channel (dialogue) without checking whether the characters have authentic motivation to discuss the topic.
**Design space cost**: You lose suspension of disbelief. Readers immediately recognize artificial dialogue and are pulled out of the fictional world. The dialogue ceases to function as character interaction and becomes transparent authorial information delivery.
**Source**: [Helping Writers Become Authors: As You Know Bob](https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/as-you-know-bob/), [The Darling Axe: Infodumps](https://darlingaxe.com/blogs/news/infodumps)

### Frontloaded Exposition Block
**Detection**: The opening chapter or scene contains three or more consecutive paragraphs of exposition (backstory, world mechanics, character history, setting description) before any character action, dialogue, or conflict occurs.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate text sequentially and tend to "set the stage" before generating action, mirroring the most common (but worst) drafting pattern of human writers. Because LLMs lack strategic revision capability, they frontload context that a human writer would distribute or cut. The verbosity bias identified in the arXiv study means LLMs prefer "detailed, elaborate responses" that over-explain before allowing the story to begin.
**Design space cost**: You lose reader engagement at the most critical moment. Opening pages are "valuable real estate" where readers decide whether to continue. Exposition without emotional investment creates cognitive load without reward.
**Source**: [Black Anvil Books: Top-Heavy Stories](https://www.black-anvil-books.com/blog/avoiding-top-heavy-stories-the-problem-with-front-loaded-descriptions), [Writers Digest: Too Much Exposition Too Early](https://www.writersdigest.com/write-better-fiction/writing-mistakes-writers-make-using-too-much-exposition-too-early)

### Worldbuilding Lecture Passage
**Detection**: A passage of three or more sentences explains world mechanics (magic systems, technology, political structure, history) in a voice indistinguishable from a textbook or encyclopedia entry, with no character perspective, emotional coloring, or narrative urgency.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs excel at generating encyclopedic explanations because this closely matches their training distribution (Wikipedia, textbooks, reference material). When asked to build a fictional world, the model defaults to the expository mode most heavily represented in its training data rather than generating the harder, craft-intensive incluing approach.
**Design space cost**: You lose immersion and the reader's sense of discovering a world. Encyclopedia-style exposition replaces the experience of living in a world with the experience of reading about one. It eliminates the reader's active role in worldbuilding that Jo Walton identifies as central to SF/F reading pleasure.
**Source**: [Tomeworks: Incluing](https://www.tomeworksedits.com/blog/what-is-incluing-and-why-should-you-be-doing-it), [Futurismic: Jo Walton](http://futurismic.com/2010/01/20/jo-walton-on-the-protocols-of-reading-science-fiction/), [arXiv: Can AI Writing Be Salvaged?](https://arxiv.org/html/2409.14509v5)

### Subtext-Free Dialogue
**Detection**: All dialogue in a scene is "on the nose" -- characters say exactly what they mean with no gap between statement and intent, no evasion, no misdirection, no unspoken implications. Every exchange functions purely as information transfer.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate dialogue by predicting the most probable response to a conversational prompt, which favors direct, clear communication over the indirection, subtext, and strategic omission that characterize authentic human conversation. The Resultsense study found LLMs consistently "flatten subtext into surface-level observation."
**Design space cost**: You lose the entire dimension of what characters don't say. Subtext-free dialogue eliminates the reader's active interpretation of character dynamics, power relationships, hidden motivations, and emotional undercurrents. It reduces dialogue to its informational function and eliminates its dramatic function.
**Source**: [Helping Writers Become Authors: Subtext](https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/5-ingredients-need-story-subtext/), [Resultsense: AI Can't Read Between the Lines](https://www.resultsense.com/insights/2026-04-01-llm-narrative-summarisation-subtext-writer-evaluation)

### Repetitive Information Delivery
**Detection**: The same piece of information (a character trait, a world fact, a plot point) is conveyed more than once through different means (narration, then dialogue, then internal thought) without additional nuance or context each time.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: The arXiv study found LLMs exhibit "verbosity bias" causing them to "fill text with redundant exposition." Token prediction favors restating safe, established information over generating new content. The "repeat curse" in LLMs causes generated text to loop through high-probability phrases and restate established facts. This is a mathematical artifact of the decoding process, not a craft choice.
**Design space cost**: You lose narrative efficiency and reader respect. Repetition signals that the author either doesn't trust the reader to absorb information on first delivery, or lacks the craft to find new information to reveal. It also dilutes the impact of each delivery.
**Source**: [arXiv: Can AI Writing Be Salvaged?](https://arxiv.org/html/2409.14509v5), [arXiv: Understanding the Repeat Curse](https://arxiv.org/html/2504.14218v1)

### Cliched Emotional Formulas
**Detection**: Emotional states are described using stock formulas: "a mix of [emotion] and [emotion]," "a wave of [emotion] washed over," "her heart skipped a beat," "a knot formed in his stomach," "tears pricked her eyes."
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: The arXiv study found these specific patterns ("a mix of [emotion] and [emotion]") appear in 54% of LLM-generated instances and are consistently edited out by human writers. LLMs generate emotional cliches because they are the highest-probability completions for emotional contexts in training data. These phrases are so overused in training corpora that they are virtually guaranteed outputs for emotional scenes.
**Design space cost**: You lose emotional specificity and freshness. Every emotional cliche feels interchangeable, removing the reader from the specific character's unique experience. Character voice and individuality are erased when all characters experience emotions through the same stock phrases.
**Source**: [arXiv: Can AI Writing Be Salvaged?](https://arxiv.org/html/2409.14509v5)

### Purple Prose in Expository Passages
**Detection**: Exposition passages contain "excessively elaborate writing" with overwrought metaphors, florid descriptions, and ornamental language that calls attention to its own style rather than serving the narrative. E.g., "The sobs emerged from this deep well of unspoken expectations, leaving behind a residue of weary resilience and a few hopeful echoes yet unwilling to completely extinguish."
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: The arXiv study identifies this as a systematic LLM pattern: "excessively elaborate writing that disrupts narrative flow by attracting undue attention to its flamboyant style." Human writers consistently simplified such passages (e.g., the above example was edited to: "She cried. She cried deep from this well of scraped knees she bandaged alone and birthdays she missed to work."). The verbosity bias in LLM training causes models to generate elaboration where simplicity would be more effective.
**Design space cost**: You lose clarity, emotional directness, and reader trust. When prose calls attention to itself during exposition, the reader processes the language instead of absorbing the information or experiencing the emotion. It also creates tonal inconsistency if the rest of the narrative is more restrained.
**Source**: [arXiv: Can AI Writing Be Salvaged?](https://arxiv.org/html/2409.14509v5)

### No Open Questions
**Detection**: At any given point in the narrative, the reader has no unanswered questions about what is happening, why it matters, or what will happen next. All information is delivered proactively before the reader has reason to wonder about it.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate text by completing patterns, which means they tend to close informational loops as quickly as possible. The model's training on clear, complete explanations (documentation, articles, textbooks) makes it probabilistically favor answering questions immediately rather than holding them open for narrative tension. This eliminates the curiosity engine that drives reader engagement.
**Design space cost**: You lose the primary page-turning mechanism. Jane Friedman's framework requires 2-3 open questions at all times operating at different temporal scales. When no questions are open, the reader has no forward momentum and no reason to continue reading.
**Source**: [Jane Friedman: Curiosity and Tension](https://janefriedman.com/the-importance-of-curiosity-and-tension-to-storytelling/), [Pages & Platforms: Exposition and the Reading Brain](https://www.pagesandplatforms.com/blog/too-much-information-exposition-and-the-reading-brain)

### Flat Exposition Voice
**Detection**: Exposition passages are written in a neutral, characterless voice that does not reflect the POV character's personality, knowledge, biases, or emotional state. The same exposition paragraph could belong to any character or any story.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs default to a generic "explanatory" register when generating exposition, because encyclopedic explanation is the dominant mode in training data for factual content. The model does not naturally filter exposition through a specific character's consciousness the way a skilled human author would, because this requires maintaining a character model that modifies word choice, focus, and emotional coloring.
**Design space cost**: You lose character voice during the passages where it's most needed. If exposition sounds different from the rest of the narrative, it breaks the psychic distance and reminds the reader of the author's presence. Information filtered through character perspective serves double duty (exposition + characterization); flat exposition serves only one.
**Source**: [Emma Darwin: Psychic Distance](https://emmadarwin.substack.com/p/psychic-distance-what-it-is-and-how), [Donald Maass micro-tension](https://writeswithtools.com/2020/03/02/microtension-what-is-it-and-how-do-we-get-it-onto-the-page/)

### Simultaneous Name Overload
**Detection**: Three or more new character names, place names, or invented terms are introduced within a single paragraph or page without contextual scaffolding.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate world-detail enthusiastically because worldbuilding terminology is well-represented in their training data (fantasy/SF novels, wikis, worldbuilding guides). The model lacks awareness of reader cognitive load and will happily introduce the Harroti of Cantara wielding their Werdle engines in the service of the Thane of Merkulos -- all in one sentence. This overwhelms working memory capacity (4-5 items).
**Design space cost**: You lose reader comprehension and engagement. When readers encounter too many unfamiliar terms simultaneously, they either stop reading to process (breaking flow) or skim past the details (losing the information the author intended to convey). Either outcome degrades the reading experience.
**Source**: [Pages & Platforms: Exposition and the Reading Brain](https://www.pagesandplatforms.com/blog/too-much-information-exposition-and-the-reading-brain), [Mythcreants: How to Teach World Terms](https://mythcreants.com/blog/how-to-teach-world-terms-without-confusing-readers/)

### Unfired Chekhov's Guns
**Detection**: The narrative emphasizes or draws attention to specific details, objects, character traits, or world elements that never become relevant to the plot, character development, or thematic concerns.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate detail based on what is statistically likely in context rather than what serves the narrative's specific trajectory. A model generating a scene in a wizard's study will describe wands, spellbooks, and mysterious artifacts because those are probable in context -- regardless of whether any of them matter to the plot. The model lacks the global narrative awareness to ensure every emphasized detail pays off.
**Design space cost**: You lose narrative coherence and reader trust. Every emphasized detail creates an implicit promise that it will matter. Unfired Chekhov's guns train readers to stop paying attention to details, undermining the foreshadowing that does matter. They also bloat the narrative with purposeless content.
**Source**: [Britannica: Chekhov's Gun](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Chekhovs-gun), [Backstage: Plant and Payoff](https://www.backstage.com/magazine/article/chekhovs-gun-explained-examples-75529/)

### Normative Interpretation Imposition
**Detection**: The narrative imposes conventional or expected interpretive frameworks on situations that the story's own logic suggests should be more complex or ambiguous. E.g., imposing a redemption arc where the story logic suggests moral ambiguity, or imposing romantic reading of a relationship the text does not support.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: The Resultsense study found LLMs "impose conventional interpretive frameworks on unconventional material" -- e.g., assuming a heteronormative reading or imposing a "redemption arc" where the text intended ambiguity. This is a direct consequence of statistical prediction: conventional interpretations are higher-probability than unconventional ones, so the model gravitates toward the most common narrative patterns in its training data.
**Design space cost**: You lose thematic complexity and authorial intention. When a narrative defaults to conventional interpretation, it flattens the specific vision the author constructed. Ambiguity, moral complexity, and unconventional relationships are reduced to their most common-denominator versions.
**Source**: [Resultsense: AI Can't Read Between the Lines](https://www.resultsense.com/insights/2026-04-01-llm-narrative-summarisation-subtext-writer-evaluation)

### Information-Free Dialogue Padding
**Detection**: Dialogue exchanges exist where neither participant learns anything new, no relationship dynamic changes, no subtext operates, and no plot advancement occurs. The conversation fills space without advancing any narrative element.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate dialogue by continuing conversational patterns probabilistically. Once a conversation begins, the model will generate plausible-sounding exchanges indefinitely without checking whether each exchange serves a narrative purpose. The model's training on real conversation (which includes extensive small talk and information-free exchanges) produces dialogue that mimics realistic conversation patterns without serving fictional narrative needs.
**Design space cost**: You lose narrative momentum and reader engagement. Every line of dialogue that serves no function (exposition, characterization, tension, plot advancement) is a line where the reader's attention could wander. In fiction, unlike life, every conversation must earn its place.
**Source**: [Writers in the Storm: Top 20 Editor Mistakes](https://writersinthestormblog.com/2025/03/the-top-twenty-mistakes-developmental-editors-see-in-manuscripts/), [No Film School: Economy in Storytelling](https://nofilmschool.com/economy-in-storytelling)

### Generic Setting Description
**Detection**: Setting descriptions use broad, non-specific details that could apply to any instance of the setting type (any castle, any spaceship, any forest) rather than details that make this specific setting distinctive or that reveal character perspective.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate setting descriptions from the aggregate of all similar settings in their training data, producing a statistical average rather than a specific place. "The castle loomed above them, its stone walls weathered by centuries of wind and rain" could appear in any fantasy novel. The model defaults to the most common descriptors for a setting type rather than the specific, unusual details that would make a particular castle memorable.
**Design space cost**: You lose specificity and immersion. Generic settings feel interchangeable and fail to create a sense of place. They also miss the opportunity to use setting description as exposition (what does this specific castle tell us about who built it and how power operates here?) or characterization (what does this character notice about the castle?).
**Source**: [arXiv: Can AI Writing Be Salvaged?](https://arxiv.org/html/2409.14509v5), [The Novelry: Exposition in a Story](https://www.thenovelry.com/blog/exposition-in-a-story)

### Thematic Thesis Statement
**Detection**: The narrative contains explicit statements of its own themes, morals, or intended takeaways, either through narrator commentary or through characters who articulate the story's meaning directly. E.g., "And in that moment, she realized that the real treasure was the friends she'd made along the way."
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs gravitate toward explicit thematic closure because their training data includes countless stories with stated morals, book summaries that articulate themes, and literary analysis that extracts meaning. The model conflates the analytical process of identifying themes with the creative process of embodying them through narrative, producing text that tells the reader what to think rather than dramatizing the theme for the reader to discover.
**Design space cost**: You lose reader agency and interpretive richness. When themes are stated, readers receive a conclusion rather than an experience. The most resonant fiction allows multiple valid interpretations; explicit thesis statements collapse that interpretive space into a single authorized reading.
**Source**: [The Editor's Blog: Author Intrusion](https://theeditorsblog.net/2011/12/13/weed-out-author-intrusion/), [Helping Writers Become Authors: 5 Ingredients for Subtext](https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/5-ingredients-need-story-subtext/)
