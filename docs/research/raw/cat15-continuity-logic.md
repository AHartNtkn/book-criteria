## Criteria Found

### Factual Detail Consistency
**What it measures**: Whether concrete factual details about characters, objects, and settings (names, physical descriptions, biographical facts, object properties) remain stable throughout the narrative.
**Assumed goal**: Realist fiction tradition and most genre fiction where the fictional world operates by stable internal rules and characters have fixed physical identities — experimental fiction, shape-shifter narratives, or works exploring identity fluidity (e.g., Woolf's *Orlando*) may deliberately alter details for thematic purposes.
**Score 0**: Physical descriptions contradict themselves (eye color, hair color, height change without explanation), character names are spelled inconsistently, biographical details (age, occupation, marital status, educational background) shift between passages, objects change properties or identities (a shotgun becomes a rifle, a 4x4 becomes a sedan).
**Score 5**: Every factual detail introduced about characters, objects, and settings remains consistent across the entire narrative, or changes only when the narrative explicitly accounts for and dramatizes that change. A reader performing a close audit would find zero unintentional contradictions in physical descriptions, names, or biographical facts.
**Source**: [Continuity (fiction) - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuity_(fiction)), [Character Bibles - Writers and Publishers Network](https://writersandpublishersnetwork.com/character-bibles/), [Continuity Errors in Famous Books - Atkins Bookshelf](https://atkinsbookshelf.wordpress.com/2016/03/10/continuity-errors-in-famous-books/)
**Notes**: The Wikipedia article defines continuity as "the consistency of the characteristics of people, plot, objects, and places seen by the audience over some period of time." The Atkins Bookshelf piece documents famous examples: Watson's wound migrating from shoulder to leg across Conan Doyle novels, Eddie's broken arm switching sides in Stephen King's *It*, Jan's hair changing from dark to blonde in *The Long Walk*. The character bible article identifies four specific error types: physical inconsistencies, educational/professional changes, relationship status shifts, and name spelling variations.

### Timeline and Chronological Coherence
**What it measures**: Whether the sequence, duration, and dating of events form a logically consistent timeline that does not contradict itself.
**Assumed goal**: Narrative fiction in which time passes in a comprehensible, trackable way — nonlinear narratives (Vonnegut's *Slaughterhouse-Five*), time-loop stories, or deliberately fragmented chronologies (Faulkner) may violate linear time but should still maintain internal temporal logic within their own framework.
**Score 0**: Events happen in impossible sequences (a character reacts to something that hasn't occurred yet), seasons or months contradict each other within the same timeframe, characters age inconsistently, travel times are physically impossible given established distances, the day-of-week progression is mathematically impossible (events assigned to days that don't exist in the described timeframe).
**Score 5**: A reader could construct a complete, non-contradictory timeline from the narrative. Seasonal references, day-of-week mentions, character ages, and duration descriptions all cohere. Even nonlinear narratives maintain internal temporal logic such that all temporal fragments can be reassembled into a consistent chronology.
**Source**: [Maintaining Continuity: Tales from the Copy Editor - Penguin Random House](https://authornews.penguinrandomhouse.com/maintaining-continuity-tales-from-the-copy-editor/), [Lost in Stories: Consistency Bugs in LLM Story Generation](https://arxiv.org/html/2603.05890)
**Notes**: The Penguin Random House copy editor identifies chronological lapses as "probably the most common issues" in fiction manuscripts, giving specific examples: characters completing impossible journeys (breakfast in Philadelphia, lunch in Boston), government offices operating on Sundays, characters wearing overcoats for 11 months in New York, and two full moons appearing within a week. The LLM research paper identifies six subtypes of timeline errors: absolute time contradictions, duration issues, simultaneity problems, causeless effects, causal logic violations, and abandoned plot elements.

### Spatial Logic and Geography
**What it measures**: Whether character positions, movements between locations, and the physical geography of the story world remain consistent and physically plausible.
**Assumed goal**: Fiction in which the physical world operates by stable spatial rules — surrealist architecture (Danielewski's *House of Leaves*) or magical spaces that deliberately violate Euclidean geometry do so as a marked, thematized feature, not by accident.
**Score 0**: Characters teleport between locations without accounting for travel time, the layout of buildings or cities contradicts itself between scenes, distances between locations change, landmarks appear and disappear or move, characters are simultaneously described as being in incompatible locations.
**Score 5**: The story's geography is internally consistent. Characters move through space in ways that respect established distances and layouts. Travel times are plausible given stated distances and transportation methods. Room layouts, building structures, and city geography remain stable across scenes. A reader could draw a consistent map from the narrative.
**Source**: [AI Story Consistency Guide - Novarrium](https://novarrium.com/blog/ai-story-consistency-complete-guide), [Maintaining Continuity - Penguin Random House](https://authornews.penguinrandomhouse.com/maintaining-continuity-tales-from-the-copy-editor/)
**Notes**: The Novarrium guide specifically identifies geography problems in AI fiction: "distances between locations shift, landmarks move." The Penguin Random House copy editor notes they use "maps and atlases for making sense of place" and track "layouts of houses or towns" as essential continuity elements. The ProWritingAid article lists "a character moves too quickly between far distances" as a specific plot hole type.

### Character Knowledge Tracking
**What it measures**: Whether characters act on and reference only information they have actually acquired within the story, and whether they retain and use information they should logically possess.
**Assumed goal**: Fiction operating under a coherent epistemology where characters are treated as having bounded knowledge derived from their experiences — omniscient characters, telepaths, or prophetic figures may access information differently but should do so through established mechanisms, not authorial convenience.
**Score 0**: Characters know things they have no way of knowing (never witnessed, were never told, could not have deduced). Characters conveniently forget critical information they definitely possessed. A character in one scene demonstrates knowledge that contradicts their established ignorance in a previous scene. Information learned in one plotline bleeds into another character's awareness without any in-story mechanism.
**Score 5**: Each character's knowledge state is rigorously consistent with their experiences. Characters only act on information they could plausibly possess. When characters lack information the reader has, this creates legitimate dramatic irony rather than appearing as authorial error. Characters remember and build on previous discoveries rather than forgetting them when inconvenient.
**Source**: [What Do They Know? - Fiction University / Janice Hardy](http://blog.janicehardy.com/2012/02/what-do-they-know-keeping-track-of.html), [10 Plot Hole Types - The Novel Factory](https://www.novel-software.com/plot-hole/), [Lost in Stories](https://arxiv.org/html/2603.05890)
**Notes**: Janice Hardy identifies three categories to track: what characters actually know, what they think/believe (which may be wrong), and what they are wrong about. She recommends tracking character knowledge by character and chapter, noting in Bob's row and chapter six's column that he "heard a rumor of a secret lab." The Novel Factory defines character knowledge gaps as occurring "when a character suddenly knows some key piece of information out of the blue, or when they inexplicably or suddenly forget something they discovered earlier." The LLM research paper identifies "memory contradictions" and "knowledge inconsistencies" as specific characterization error subtypes.

### Internal Rule Consistency (Magic Systems, Technology, Social Rules)
**What it measures**: Whether the rules governing the story's speculative or world-specific elements (magic, technology, supernatural abilities, social hierarchies, legal systems, physics) remain consistent once established.
**Assumed goal**: Fiction in the tradition articulated by Tolkien's concept of "subcreation" and Sanderson's Laws of Magic, where the fictional world establishes its own internally coherent physics — works like *Alice in Wonderland* or Italo Calvino's fiction may deliberately employ arbitrary or dream-logic rules, but these function as a conscious artistic choice rather than careless inconsistency.
**Score 0**: Rules established early in the narrative are violated later without acknowledgment or explanation. Magic systems gain new abilities precisely when the plot requires them. Technology works differently in different scenes. Social rules are enforced selectively based on plot convenience. Limitations and costs established for abilities disappear when they would impede the protagonist.
**Score 5**: Every speculative element operates by rules that, once established, remain in force throughout the narrative. When rules appear to be violated, the narrative provides a principled explanation that is consistent with the deeper logic of the system. Limitations are respected even when they create difficulty for the protagonist. The rules have logical second-order effects on the world's society, economy, and culture.
**Source**: [Sanderson's First Law - Brandon Sanderson](https://www.brandonsanderson.com/blogs/blog/sandersons-first-law), [Magic System Logic: 3 Non-Negotiable Rules - EV Editing](https://www.ev-editing.com/blog/is-your-magic-system-logical-the-3-non-negotiable-rules-for-structural-consistency), [The Importance of Internal Consistency - L. G. Estrella](https://lgestrella.wordpress.com/2015/01/21/the-importance-of-internal-consistency/)
**Notes**: Sanderson's First Law states: "An author's ability to solve conflict with magic is directly proportional to how well the reader understands said magic." The EV Editing article identifies three non-negotiable rules for structural consistency: limitations (magic requires quantifiable costs), consequences (magic must logically impact worldbuilding), and adherence (no loopholes that bend previously established rules). L. G. Estrella argues internal consistency enables tension and drama (without established rules, conflict loses meaning), facilitates reader engagement (understanding and prediction require consistent logic), and enhances plot twists (surprises feel earned upon reflection).

### Worldbuilding Implication Consistency
**What it measures**: Whether the second-order effects and societal implications of the story's speculative elements are followed through consistently, rather than existing in isolation from the world they would logically reshape.
**Assumed goal**: Fiction that treats its speculative premise as having real consequences for the world it inhabits — "portal fantasy" or adventure fiction where the fantastical element serves primarily as backdrop may justifiably leave implications less explored, but should not actively contradict them.
**Score 0**: A magic system that creates gold exists alongside a non-inflated economy with no explanation. Healing magic exists but characters die of treatable wounds without comment. Telepathy exists but the justice system still relies on witness testimony. Technology that would transform daily life is treated as having no social impact. The world runs exactly like the real world despite containing elements that would fundamentally alter it.
**Score 5**: The speculative elements have been thought through to their logical consequences. The world's social structures, economy, politics, daily life, and cultural practices reflect the existence of the speculative elements. When implications are not fully explored (which is acceptable in a narrative), they at least are not contradicted. Characters within the world behave as people who have always lived with these elements would behave.
**Source**: [Magic System Logic - EV Editing](https://www.ev-editing.com/blog/is-your-magic-system-logical-the-3-non-negotiable-rules-for-structural-consistency), [Worldbuilding 102 - Michael Tedin](https://michaeltedin.com/worldbuilding-102-magic-and-technology/), [The Four Cs of Fantasy Worldbuilding - Fantasy-Hive](https://fantasy-hive.co.uk/2021/04/the-four-cs-of-fantasy-worldbuilding-worldbuilding-by-the-numbers/)
**Notes**: The EV Editing article asks: "If magic can instantly create gold, why isn't the economy inflated? If a mage can heal any wound, why do people still die in battle?" Michael Tedin emphasizes thinking about "the ripple effects and the second, third, fourth order effects that adding a speculative fiction element to your world would result in." The Fantasy-Hive Four Cs framework notes that "adding more and more details to make the world feel Complete makes it harder to keep all those details Consistent, especially when a series goes on for many years."

### Causal Chain Integrity
**What it measures**: Whether events in the narrative follow from preceding events through a logical chain of cause and effect, with proportional consequences.
**Assumed goal**: Fiction in the E.M. Forster tradition where plot differs from story by virtue of causation ("The king died, and then the queen died of grief" vs "The king died, and then the queen died") — literary fiction may employ subtler, character-internal causation rather than external-event causation, but still requires a coherent causal web.
**Score 0**: Events occur without causal connection to preceding events. Consequences are wildly disproportionate to their causes (a minor slight provokes a war with no escalation). Characters make decisions that are unmotivated by anything that has happened in the story. Plot threads are abandoned without resolution or consequence. Scenes can be removed from the manuscript without any effect on surrounding action.
**Score 5**: Every significant event flows logically from preceding events and character decisions. Consequences are proportionate to causes. Character actions are motivated by established personality, knowledge, and circumstances. The causal chain operates at both the plot level (events logically follow) and the character level (character responses are consistent with who they are). Removing any scene would leave a visible gap in the causal chain.
**Source**: [Plots and Causal Chains - Patricia C. Wrede](https://pcwrede.com/pcw-wp/plots-and-causal-chains/), [Causation and Plot Structure in Literary Fiction - Jane Friedman](https://janefriedman.com/quiet-connections-the-role-of-causation-and-plot-structure-in-literary-fiction/), [Building and Fixing Your Causal Chain - Matthew Rettino](https://matthewrettino.com/2019/08/01/building-and-fixing-your-causal-chain/)
**Notes**: Patricia C. Wrede explains that causal chains function simultaneously on two levels: the plot level (events logically follow from preceding circumstances) and the character level (character actions remain consistent with established personality). She notes that a cowardly character would not believably steal a dragon's egg unless the narrative has provided adequate motivation. Jane Friedman's contributor Harrison Demchick argues that literary fiction employs subtler causation through character transformation rather than external events, but causation still operates: "the character arc is the primary narrative." Rettino recommends: "If you can remove a scene or sequence from the manuscript without notable effect on the surrounding action, it reflects a weakened causal chain."

### Emotional Continuity
**What it measures**: Whether characters carry appropriate emotional states between scenes, with emotions flowing logically from what has happened to them rather than resetting to a neutral baseline at each scene boundary.
**Assumed goal**: Fiction aiming for psychological realism where characters are treated as having persistent inner lives — comedic fiction, picaresque narrative, or episodic forms may legitimately employ lighter emotional tracking, but should not produce jarring emotional contradictions.
**Score 0**: Characters experience traumatic events in one scene and appear completely unaffected in the next. Emotional states reset to neutral at every scene break. Characters who were grieving, furious, or terrified show no residue of those emotions in subsequent scenes. Relationships between characters fluctuate wildly (hostile in one scene, casually friendly in the next) with no transition or explanation.
**Score 5**: Each scene's opening emotional state flows logically from the preceding scene's events. Characters carry emotional residue — grief lingers, anger simmers, joy colors subsequent interactions. Emotional shifts between scenes are either explicitly dramatized or occur over plausible timeframes. A character's emotional state at the beginning of a scene is informed by what happened "the moment before."
**Source**: [Emotional Continuity - Fiveable](https://fiveable.me/key-terms/screenwriting-ii/emotional-continuity), [AI Keeps Forgetting My Characters - Noveble](https://www.noveble.com/blogs/character-consistency-management), [The Importance of Emotional Continuity - Almost An Author](https://www.almostanauthor.com/importance-emotional-continuity/)
**Notes**: The Fiveable definition: "Emotional continuity refers to the seamless flow of emotional states and responses in a narrative, ensuring that characters' feelings evolve in a believable manner." It notes that "inconsistent emotional continuity can significantly diminish audience engagement by creating confusion about characters' motivations and feelings." The Almost An Author article introduces the "moment before" concept: when actors enter a scene, they bring an emotional state from the moment before — if we just witnessed a fight between Emma and her mother, "we expect some muttering, dark looks and slamming of silverware" when she enters the next scene. The Noveble article identifies emotional reset as a specific AI failure mode, recommending tracking emotional state chapter by chapter.

### Setup-Payoff Consistency (Chekhov's Gun)
**What it measures**: Whether narrative elements introduced with emphasis or prominence are eventually used in the story, and whether resolutions are grounded in previously established elements.
**Assumed goal**: The Chekhovian tradition of narrative economy where every emphasized element serves the story — literary fiction in the tradition of Chekhov himself (whose *Cherry Orchard* contains unfired rifles) may deliberately subvert this principle, and mystery fiction uses red herrings as an intentional and genre-conventional exception.
**Score 0**: Significant narrative elements are introduced with emphasis but never referenced again. Major conflicts are resolved by elements that were never established or foreshadowed. The narrative contains numerous loose ends that appear accidental rather than intentional. Props, abilities, or characters introduced as important simply vanish from the story.
**Score 5**: Every element given narrative prominence eventually contributes to the story. Resolutions grow organically from previously established elements. Where setups are deliberately left unresolved (thematic ambiguity, sequel hooks), this is clearly intentional and serves a narrative purpose. Red herrings, if present, function as genre-appropriate misdirection rather than forgotten threads.
**Source**: [Chekhov's Gun - Scribophile](https://www.scribophile.com/academy/what-is-chekhovs-gun), [Chekhov's Gun - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chekhov's_gun), [Chekhov's Gun - Chapter Blog](https://blog.chapter.pub/chekhovs-gun/)
**Notes**: The Scribophile article distinguishes three violations: deus ex machina (payoff without setup), red herrings (deliberate setup without payoff, genre-conventional in mysteries), and loose ends (accidental setups without payoffs — marks of sloppy planning). The Chapter Blog makes a genre-dependent argument: in a thriller, an unresolved detail is a flaw; in literary fiction, it can be a feature — "a reminder that not everything in life carries meaning." The Wikipedia article notes even Chekhov violated his own principle: *The Cherry Orchard* contains two loaded firearms that are never fired, tying into the play's theme of incomplete action.

### Deus Ex Machina Avoidance
**What it measures**: Whether major conflicts are resolved through means that have been established within the story's logic, rather than through sudden, unearned interventions.
**Assumed goal**: Fiction in which narrative outcomes feel earned through the story's internal logic and character agency — ancient Greek drama, metafictional works, and certain postmodern narratives may employ deus ex machina as a deliberate structural choice with thematic intent, but these are marked departures from the default expectation.
**Score 0**: The climactic conflict is resolved by a character, ability, object, or event that appears from nowhere with no prior establishment. Characters suddenly reveal previously unmentioned crucial skills at the moment of greatest need. External forces resolve problems that characters should resolve themselves. The resolution requires the reader to accept a massive coincidence.
**Score 5**: Every resolution, especially the climactic one, is grounded in elements the reader has encountered before. Conflict resolution stems from character agency, established abilities, and previously planted story elements. Even unexpected solutions can be traced back through the narrative's causal chain. The protagonist's choices contribute meaningfully to turning points.
**Source**: [What is Deus Ex Machina - September C. Fawkes](https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2026/01/what-is-a-deus-ex-machina-meaning-writing.html), [Deus Ex Machina - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deus_ex_machina), [Is Your Protagonist Wearing Too Much Plot Armor - Inkdrop Lit](https://www.inkdroplit.com/blog/2019/5/1/is-your-protagonist-wearing-too-much-plot-armor)
**Notes**: September C. Fawkes identifies four problems with deus ex machina: it breaks cause-and-effect logic ("strong plots work off cause and effect, not coincidence"), violates knowledge requirements (readers need advance understanding of conflict-resolving elements), lacks foreshadowing (leaving audiences manipulated), and undermines protagonist agency ("the protagonist's choice should be contributing to the major turning points"). The Inkdrop Lit article connects this to Chekhov's gun: "if something saves the day in Act 3, readers should have encountered hints in Act 1."

### Plot Armor / Consequence Consistency
**What it measures**: Whether characters face realistic and consistent consequences for dangerous situations, rather than surviving implausibly due to their narrative importance.
**Assumed goal**: Fiction that maintains genuine stakes through logical consequences — superhero fiction, mythic narrative, and action-adventure genres may feature more resilient characters, but should establish the basis for that resilience (superpowers, training, equipment) rather than relying on authorial fiat.
**Score 0**: The protagonist survives situations that should be lethal based on the story's own established rules. Injuries that would incapacitate anyone else are shrugged off by main characters. The rules of danger apply asymmetrically — villains and minor characters die from threats that leave the protagonist unscathed with no in-story explanation. Readers can predict character survival based entirely on narrative importance rather than story logic.
**Score 5**: Characters face consequences proportional to the dangers they encounter, consistent with the story's own rules. When characters survive dangerous situations, there is a logical explanation grounded in established abilities, preparation, or circumstances. Stakes feel genuine because the narrative enforces its own rules equally on all characters.
**Source**: [Plot Armor - TV Tropes](https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/PlotArmor), [Is Your Protagonist Wearing Too Much Plot Armor - Inkdrop Lit](https://www.inkdroplit.com/blog/2019/5/1/is-your-protagonist-wearing-too-much-plot-armor)
**Notes**: TV Tropes distinguishes plot armor from genre-appropriate character resilience: "When Superman takes a bullet to the eye and survives, that's his superhuman nature. If Indiana Jones survives the same thing, that's Plot Armor." The Inkdrop Lit article identifies three consequences of excessive plot armor: it challenges suspension of disbelief, lowers stakes, and undermines character achievement by attributing success to luck rather than skill.

### POV Knowledge Boundary Consistency
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative respects the knowledge boundaries inherent in its chosen point of view, avoiding "head-hopping" or information leaks across POV boundaries.
**Assumed goal**: Fiction that commits to its chosen narrative perspective — omniscient narration deliberately crosses POV boundaries as a feature of the form, but even omniscient narration should do so consistently and intentionally rather than accidentally.
**Score 0**: A limited-third-person or first-person narrative reveals information the POV character could not know. The narrative slips into another character's thoughts mid-scene without a clear break or POV shift signal. In multiple-POV narratives, the knowledge from one POV character bleeds into scenes from another POV character's perspective. Characters narrate their own unconscious motivations they would not realistically articulate.
**Score 5**: The narrative strictly respects its chosen POV constraints. In limited perspectives, the reader sees only what the POV character can see, know, and interpret. POV switches are clearly signaled (chapter breaks, section breaks, formatting cues). Information asymmetry between characters is maintained and exploited for dramatic tension. Each POV voice is distinguishable and consistent.
**Source**: [Most Common Writing Mistakes: Head-Hopping POV - Helping Writers Become Authors](https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/common-writing-mistakes-goody-goody-characters-2/), [POV Problems - Helping Writers Become Authors](https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/pov-problems/), [POV Consistency - AutoCrit](https://www.autocrit.com/editing/support/pov-consistency/)
**Notes**: The Helping Writers Become Authors article defines head-hopping as "when you suddenly jump from one narrator's point of view to another, with no warning to the reader." The rule of thumb is that "only one character should own a particular scene." The AutoCrit article notes that "it's easiest to spot POV slips in first-person stories, where the narrator describes something they couldn't possibly know or experience — another person's intentions, or an event they aren't present at."

### In-Scene Object and Action Continuity
**What it measures**: Whether objects, character positions, and physical actions remain consistent within individual scenes — whether characters are holding things they picked up, standing when they should be sitting, and located where preceding actions placed them.
**Assumed goal**: Fiction that maintains a coherent physical reality within each scene — even stylized or non-realist fiction maintains scene-level spatial coherence unless deliberately breaking it for effect.
**Score 0**: Characters hold objects they never picked up, or objects they were holding vanish without being put down. Characters stand up without having sat down, or are suddenly in different positions. Actions within a scene contradict each other (a character exits a room and then continues conversing as if still present). Physical actions are described in impossible sequences.
**Score 5**: Every object, character position, and physical action within a scene is accounted for. If a character picks up a weapon, the narrative tracks it until it is put down, dropped, or otherwise disposed of. Characters' physical positions flow logically from their described movements. No action contradicts preceding actions within the same scene.
**Source**: [Catching Continuity Errors - Writer's Digest](https://www.writersdigest.com/catching-continuity-errors-in-3-easy-steps), [Continuity (fiction) - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuity_(fiction))
**Notes**: The Writer's Digest article's central example involves an editor asking "Is he still holding the gun?" during a fight scene, revealing the author hadn't tracked the weapon's status throughout the confrontation. The Wikipedia article gives the example from Chaplin's *The Property Man* where "the Tramp loses his hat in one room, but it is instantly back on his head as he enters the next room."

### Anachronism Avoidance
**What it measures**: Whether elements of the narrative (technology, language, customs, attitudes, objects) are appropriate to the time period in which the story is set.
**Assumed goal**: Fiction set in a specific historical period or a secondary world with a defined technological level — steampunk, alternate history, and deliberately anachronistic comedy (Monty Python, *A Connecticut Yankee in King Arthur's Court*) employ anachronism as a conscious genre feature.
**Score 0**: Characters use technology, language, or reference cultural phenomena that did not exist in the story's time period. Modern attitudes and values are projected onto historical characters without authorial awareness. Objects or concepts appear before their historical invention or existence. The story's treatment of period details reveals the author's lack of research.
**Score 5**: Every element is appropriate to its stated time period, or anachronisms are clearly intentional and serve a narrative or thematic purpose. Language, technology, customs, and attitudes reflect the story's era. When the author takes deliberate liberties with historical accuracy, the departures serve the story rather than resulting from ignorance.
**Source**: [How to Avoid Anachronisms in Historical Fiction - The History Quill](https://thehistoryquill.com/out-of-time-how-to-avoid-anachronisms-in-historical-fiction/), [Anachronism - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anachronism)
**Notes**: Wikipedia identifies four types: parachronism (element possible but unlikely for the period), prochronism (element that did not yet exist), cultural anachronism (attitude or custom from wrong era), and false anachronism (element that seems anachronistic but is historically accurate). The History Quill article emphasizes that "maintaining readers' trust is crucial in historical fiction" and that "accurate details matter because they help readers suspend their scepticism."

### Suspension of Disbelief Maintenance
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative successfully maintains the reader's willing immersion by honoring the internal logic contract established between author and audience.
**Assumed goal**: Fiction operating within the framework Tolkien called "secondary belief" — where the reader accepts the world's rules as true while inside it — as opposed to deliberately Brechtian or metafictional works that intentionally break the fourth wall to prevent immersion.
**Score 0**: The narrative repeatedly forces the reader to stop and question whether the author made a mistake. Inconsistencies, plot conveniences, and logical failures accumulate to the point where the reader can no longer trust the narrative. Characters, events, and world-rules feel arbitrary rather than governed by consistent principles.
**Score 5**: The reader can remain immersed in the narrative without being ejected by inconsistencies or logical failures. The internal logic contract is honored — the story's rules feel real while the reader is inside them. Even when the story contains fantastical elements, they operate by internally consistent principles that sustain belief. As Tolkien described: "what he relates is 'true': it accords with the laws of that world."
**Source**: [How Authors Use Internal Consistency to Create Suspension of Disbelief - Nicole York](https://nicoleyork.com/author-blog/create-suspension-of-disbelief-with-internal-consistency), [Suspension of Disbelief - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suspension_of_disbelief), [Tolkien Secondary Belief - Valarguild](https://valarguild.org/tolkien/encyc/papers/dreamlord/stages/secondary_belief.htm)
**Notes**: Nicole York identifies three essential steps: establish the rule, demonstrate characters believe it, maintain consistency when breaking rules (with foreshadowing and logical explanation). She notes that when consistency fails, it "tears readers out of the story and forces them to question the author." Tolkien argued that suspension of disbelief is actually inadequate — the goal is "secondary belief" where the reader genuinely believes in the world's truth while inside it, which requires "the inner consistency of reality." The Fantasy-Hive survey found internal inconsistencies ranked highest (20 points above second place) for breaking immersion.

### Aggregate Consistency Across Length
**What it measures**: Whether consistency is maintained not just locally but across the full span of a novel-length or series-length work, where details introduced early must remain coherent with events hundreds of pages later.
**Assumed goal**: Any fiction of novel length or longer that expects the reader to treat early and late portions as part of the same coherent narrative — serialized or episodic fiction (certain web novels, soap operas) may operate with looser aggregate consistency by convention.
**Score 0**: Details introduced in early chapters are contradicted in later chapters. The character who was established as left-handed is suddenly right-handed. Relationships described early are forgotten or contradicted. The world's rules shift as the narrative progresses. The narrative reads as if the author did not review earlier sections while writing later ones.
**Score 5**: The narrative maintains perfect consistency between its earliest and latest pages. Details planted in chapter one remain accurate in chapter thirty. Character arcs begun early are carried through to completion. The world's rules established in the opening are still in force at the climax. The work reads as if the author maintained a comprehensive reference document and verified consistency throughout.
**Source**: [The Story Bible - Jane Friedman](https://janefriedman.com/the-story-bible/), [Creating a Story Bible - The Novel Smithy](https://thenovelsmithy.com/create-a-story-bible/), [The Four Cs of Fantasy Worldbuilding - Fantasy-Hive](https://fantasy-hive.co.uk/2021/04/the-four-cs-of-fantasy-worldbuilding-worldbuilding-by-the-numbers/)
**Notes**: The Novel Smithy describes a story bible as "one extra line of defense to keep your story coherent and cohesive," particularly valuable during rewrites where "changes to your worldbuilding or other details" can slip through. The Fantasy-Hive article distinguishes internal inconsistency (within a single story) from aggregate inconsistency (across a series) and notes the tension: "adding more and more details to make the world feel Complete makes it harder to keep all those details Consistent." Interestingly, the article notes Harry Potter contains 200+ catalogued inconsistencies yet remains beloved, "suggesting compelling content can overcome such flaws."

### Retroactive Continuity (Retcon) Avoidance
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative avoids retroactively altering or contradicting previously established facts to solve new plot problems.
**Assumed goal**: Fiction that treats its own established canon as binding — serialized superhero comics and long-running TV shows have developed genre-specific conventions around retcons, but standalone novels and novel series are generally expected to honor their own prior continuity.
**Score 0**: Previously established facts are silently contradicted to enable new plot developments. Character histories are rewritten without acknowledgment. Events that were established as having happened are treated as if they didn't, or vice versa. The narrative gives the impression that the author forgot what they wrote earlier.
**Score 5**: The narrative never silently contradicts its own established facts. When new information recontextualizes earlier events, it does so through legitimate revelation that is consistent with (rather than contradictory to) what came before. Readers who re-read the story find that later revelations enhance rather than undermine earlier passages.
**Source**: [Retroactive Continuity - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Retroactive_continuity), [Retroactive Continuity - TCK Publishing](https://www.tckpublishing.com/retroactive-continuity/)
**Notes**: The TCK Publishing article notes that retconning "often smells of poor writing — either to correct mistakes that should have been avoided, or in an attempt to create an interesting twist" and is seen as "writers being unable to commit to something." The Wikipedia article notes the problem of "story bloat" where continual additions may reach a point where "the original work's themes, message, tone, aesthetic, and/or characters are no longer relevant to or consistent with its latest iteration."

### Commonsense Physical Plausibility
**What it measures**: Whether events in the non-speculative aspects of the story conform to basic physical and commonsense expectations — characters need to eat, sleep, and rest; injuries have consequences; weather affects activity; time passes at a normal rate for non-magical activities.
**Assumed goal**: Fiction that grounds its non-speculative elements in recognizable physical reality — the Four Cs framework calls this "terra de facto," where audiences judge non-magical elements against actual reality. Even fantasy and science fiction are expected to get the non-fantastical elements right.
**Score 0**: Characters go days without eating, sleeping, or attending to basic physical needs with no explanation. Injuries sustained in one scene have no physical consequences in the next. Characters perform feats that would be physically impossible for non-enhanced humans. Real-world elements (how guns work, how long it takes to travel by horse, how weather affects visibility) are depicted inaccurately.
**Score 5**: Non-speculative elements operate by real-world physics and common sense. Characters have physical needs that the narrative acknowledges. Injuries have realistic consequences. Real-world elements are depicted with accuracy appropriate to the genre. The reader's knowledge of how the world actually works is respected for all non-fantastical elements.
**Source**: [The Four Cs of Fantasy Worldbuilding - Fantasy-Hive](https://fantasy-hive.co.uk/2021/04/the-four-cs-of-fantasy-worldbuilding-worldbuilding-by-the-numbers/), [ProWritingAid - Plot Holes](https://prowritingaid.com/art/1603/plot-holes-and-how-to-fix-them.aspx)
**Notes**: The Fantasy-Hive article introduces the "terra de facto" principle: "audiences consider themselves experts of the real world and judge created worlds against actual reality for non-magical elements." ProWritingAid lists "impossible events — like a character who becomes a professional dancer overnight to win a big competition, or a person who can hold their breath underwater for 20 minutes" as a specific plot hole type.

### Unresolved Storyline Tracking
**What it measures**: Whether all significant subplot threads introduced in the narrative are either resolved or clearly left open for intentional reasons.
**Assumed goal**: Fiction that operates under the implicit contract that introduced narrative threads will be addressed — literary fiction may deliberately leave threads ambiguous for thematic purposes, and serialized fiction may carry threads across volumes, but standalone novels are expected to resolve their major plot threads.
**Score 0**: Multiple subplots are introduced and then forgotten entirely. Secondary characters are given their own storylines that simply stop. Mysteries are raised but never answered. Plot threads that were given significant narrative attention simply vanish. The reader finishes the book with a list of unanswered questions that appear to be authorial oversights rather than intentional ambiguity.
**Score 5**: Every significant subplot reaches a satisfying resolution, is deliberately left ambiguous for a clear thematic reason, or is explicitly acknowledged as unfinished (in a series). Minor threads may fade naturally, but no thread given substantial narrative investment is abandoned. The reader feels that the author was in control of all their narrative threads throughout.
**Source**: [How to Find and Fix Plot Holes - Well-Storied](https://www.well-storied.com/blog/plot-holes), [Chekhov's Gun - Scribophile](https://www.scribophile.com/academy/what-is-chekhovs-gun)
**Notes**: Well-Storied defines unresolved storylines as "secondary plots introduced but abandoned" where "a secondary character is given their own subplot, which is later forgotten." The Scribophile article classifies loose ends as "accidental setups without payoffs — marks of sloppy planning that frustrate readers," distinguishing them from intentional red herrings and deliberately ambiguous endings.

### Character Behavioral Consistency
**What it measures**: Whether characters act in ways consistent with their established personality, motivations, skills, and limitations — separate from but related to emotional continuity.
**Assumed goal**: Fiction in the realist character tradition where characters are treated as having stable psychological cores that change only through dramatized development — picaresque, absurdist, or allegorical fiction may employ more schematic or mutable characters, but this should be a consistent feature of the mode, not an accidental inconsistency.
**Score 0**: Characters act "out of character" without any narrative justification. A character established as fearful of heights heroically scales a building with no preceding development arc. Skills and competencies fluctuate — a character is expert in one scene and incompetent in the next. Personality traits appear and disappear based on plot needs. Characters make decisions that serve the plot but contradict their established nature.
**Score 5**: Every character action flows from established personality, motivations, and capabilities. When characters act against their established patterns, this is treated as significant and dramatically meaningful, with clear precipitating causes. Character growth is gradual and grounded in experience. Readers can predict (at least in hindsight) why characters made the choices they did based on who they have been established to be.
**Source**: [The Importance of Internal Consistency - L. G. Estrella](https://lgestrella.wordpress.com/2015/01/21/the-importance-of-internal-consistency/), [Grading Rubric for Student Fiction - Patrick Mooney](https://patrickbrianmooney.nfshost.com/~patrick/ta/m13/eng192/fiction-rubric.html), [Lost in Stories](https://arxiv.org/html/2603.05890)
**Notes**: L. G. Estrella warns that if "a character has behaved rashly for 99% of a story" it shouldn't abruptly reverse without explanation. The academic fiction rubric requires "believable and engaging characters" at the A level and notes that "unbelievable characters" can drop a story to C or D range. The LLM research paper identifies specific characterization error subtypes: "skill fluctuations" and "forgotten abilities."

### Narrative Voice and Tonal Consistency
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative voice, register, and tone remain consistent with the established mode throughout the work, rather than shifting arbitrarily between styles.
**Assumed goal**: Fiction that establishes and maintains a coherent narrative voice — novels with multiple narrators may have distinct voices per narrator, and works that deliberately shift register (Ulysses's chapter-by-chapter style changes) do so as a structural principle, not by accident.
**Score 0**: The narrative voice shifts randomly between formal and informal, literary and colloquial, serious and comedic with no structural justification. A dark literary novel suddenly reads like a young adult adventure. Humor appears in serious scenes without tonal justification. The writing style changes noticeably between chapters or sections in ways that feel uncontrolled.
**Score 5**: The narrative voice is consistent and intentional throughout. Tonal shifts, when they occur, are clearly deliberate and serve narrative purposes (comic relief in a tragedy, gravity in a comedy). Each narrator in a multi-voice work has a distinct but internally consistent voice. The prose style feels like the product of a single controlling intelligence.
**Source**: [AI Story Consistency Guide - Novarrium](https://novarrium.com/blog/ai-story-consistency-complete-guide), [Lost in Stories](https://arxiv.org/html/2603.05890)
**Notes**: The Novarrium guide identifies tonal shifts as a specific consistency failure: "a dark, literary novel suddenly reads like a young adult adventure" with "humor appears in serious scenes." The LLM research paper includes "perspective shifts, tone inconsistencies, style variations" as a distinct error category (Narrative & Style) with three subtypes.

## Sentinels Found

### Physical Description Contradiction
**Detection**: A character's physical attributes (eye color, hair color, height, handedness, distinguishing marks) are described differently in two or more passages with no narrative explanation for the change.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs lose track of specific physical details as context windows fill. The "Lost in Stories" research found that "Factual & Detail Consistency" errors (including "appearance mismatches") are one of the two dominant failure modes in LLM fiction. The model generates plausible-sounding descriptions from its training distribution rather than maintaining fidelity to previously established details.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates fiction where characters literally transform (metamorphosis narratives, shapeshifters) and fiction where physical description is deliberately unreliable (narrator describing from faulty memory). However, these are identifiable by the presence of narrative framing around the change.
**Source**: [Lost in Stories](https://arxiv.org/html/2603.05890), [Character Bibles - Writers and Publishers Network](https://writersandpublishersnetwork.com/character-bibles/)

### Character Name Inconsistency
**Detection**: A character is referred to by two or more incompatible names (not including established nicknames, aliases, or titles) within the same work.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs frequently confuse entity references, especially in longer texts. The Novarrium guide notes that AI-generated fiction exhibits "nomenclature confusion" and the character bible article specifically identifies "Jim on page 3, Jimi on page 36, back to Jim on page 79" as a tracked error type. This results from the model sampling from a probability distribution over tokens rather than maintaining a stable entity registry.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates fiction where characters deliberately conceal or change their identity (spy fiction, identity narratives), or where names are fluid by cultural design. Requires distinguishing between names that the narrative treats as referring to the same person vs. genuine confusion.
**Source**: [AI Story Consistency Guide - Novarrium](https://novarrium.com/blog/ai-story-consistency-complete-guide), [Character Bibles - Writers and Publishers Network](https://writersandpublishersnetwork.com/character-bibles/)

### Impossible Information Access
**Detection**: A character demonstrably acts on information they have no in-story mechanism to have acquired — they were not present when it was discussed, no character told them, they have no means of deduction, and no supernatural ability explains the knowledge.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs have no model of individual character knowledge states. The model knows all information in the context window and must actively suppress that knowledge when writing from a limited perspective. The "Lost in Stories" taxonomy specifically identifies "knowledge inconsistencies" as a characterization error subtype. User complaints about AI storytelling apps specifically cite characters who "suddenly know things they shouldn't."
**Design space cost**: Eliminates fiction with genuinely telepathic or prescient characters who access information through in-world mechanisms. Also conflicts with omniscient narration where the narrative voice (not the character) reveals others' thoughts. Detection requires distinguishing character knowledge from narrator knowledge.
**Source**: [Lost in Stories](https://arxiv.org/html/2603.05890), [What Do They Know? - Janice Hardy](http://blog.janicehardy.com/2012/02/what-do-they-know-keeping-track-of.html)

### Convenient Amnesia
**Detection**: A character who previously demonstrated knowledge of a fact or event behaves as if they do not know it, specifically at a moment when that knowledge would resolve a conflict or eliminate a source of dramatic tension.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs lose information from early in the context as the window fills — the "lost in the middle" effect causes information in the middle 40-60% of the context to be recalled with significantly lower accuracy. When generating a scene that requires tension, the model may generate tension-maintaining behavior without checking whether the character already possesses tension-resolving knowledge. This is the inverse of impossible information access but equally characteristic.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates fiction where characters have genuinely unreliable memories (trauma, aging, magical curse) or where forgetting is itself a thematic element. Also penalizes fiction where characters have plausible reasons not to connect disparate facts.
**Source**: [The Memory Problem - ByteByteGo](https://blog.bytebytego.com/p/the-memory-problem-why-llms-sometimes), [10 Plot Hole Types - The Novel Factory](https://www.novel-software.com/plot-hole/)

### Emotional State Reset
**Detection**: A character who experienced a significant emotional event (death of a loved one, betrayal, major victory, trauma) in one scene shows zero emotional residue from that event at the opening of the next scene, with no time skip or transitional explanation.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs process each generation request with limited attention to emotional arc continuity. The Noveble article specifically identifies emotional reset as an AI failure mode, noting "two characters' relationship behaves inconsistently — they're hostile in one scene and casually friendly in the next, with no transition." The SCORE framework found that AI achieves only 89.7% emotional consistency even with dedicated tracking systems, indicating this is a persistent LLM weakness.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates fiction where time skips between scenes naturally allow emotional processing, and fiction where emotional suppression is itself a characterized trait. Also may penalize episodic fiction forms (picaresque) that conventionally reset emotional states.
**Source**: [AI Keeps Forgetting My Characters - Noveble](https://www.noveble.com/blogs/character-consistency-management), [SCORE Framework](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.23512v1)

### Rule-Violating Convenience
**Detection**: An established rule of the story's world (magical limitation, physical law, social norm, technological constraint) is violated at a moment that conveniently resolves a plot problem, without the violation being treated as significant or extraordinary by the narrative.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs lack a formal world-rule registry and generate resolutions based on pattern-matching to "problem solved" training examples rather than checking whether the solution is consistent with established constraints. The EV Editing article calls these "loopholes" — "bending previously established rules to resolve impossible situations." Sanderson's First Law articulates why this is unsatisfying: the reader's understanding of the resolution mechanism is proportional to their satisfaction with it.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates fiction where rule-breaking is itself the point of the scene (a character discovers the rules are different than believed), and fiction that deliberately employs soft magic where rules are never precisely defined. Requires having established rules against which to check.
**Source**: [Magic System Logic - EV Editing](https://www.ev-editing.com/blog/is-your-magic-system-logical-the-3-non-negotiable-rules-for-structural-consistency), [Sanderson's First Law](https://www.brandonsanderson.com/blogs/blog/sandersons-first-law)

### Timeline Impossibility
**Detection**: Two events that are both described as occurring at specific times or in a specific sequence could not both be true — a character is in two places at once, a duration between events is physically impossible, or calendar/seasonal references are self-contradicting.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs do not maintain an internal timeline model. The "Lost in Stories" research found timeline errors are one of the two dominant failure modes in LLM fiction, with six subtypes (absolute time contradictions, duration issues, simultaneity problems, etc.). The Penguin Random House copy editor specifically identifies impossible journeys (breakfast in Philadelphia, lunch in Boston) and lunar cycle errors (two full moons within a week) as the types of errors that require dedicated tracking.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates fiction with deliberately impossible chronologies (time travel, temporal loops, unreliable narrators describing events out of order). The check is for contradictions the narrative does not acknowledge, not for intentional temporal play.
**Source**: [Lost in Stories](https://arxiv.org/html/2603.05890), [Maintaining Continuity - Penguin Random House](https://authornews.penguinrandomhouse.com/maintaining-continuity-tales-from-the-copy-editor/)

### Object Teleportation
**Detection**: An object that was described as being in a specific location, or in a character's possession, appears in a different location or in a different character's possession with no described transfer.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs do not track object state (held, dropped, placed, destroyed) across paragraphs. The SCORE framework specifically addresses this with "Dynamic State Tracking" that monitors "object and character states (active, lost, destroyed)" because unexplained reactivation of objects is a common AI error. The Writer's Digest example ("Is he still holding the gun?") illustrates the scene-level version; the LLM version extends across chapters.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates fiction where objects have magical properties (teleporting artifacts, summoned items) and scenes where implicit actions are conventionally understood (a character is described entering a car, and is later at the destination — the drive is implied). The check is for objects that change state with no possible implicit explanation.
**Source**: [SCORE Framework](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.23512v1), [Catching Continuity Errors - Writer's Digest](https://www.writersdigest.com/catching-continuity-errors-in-3-easy-steps)

### Repetitive Looping
**Detection**: The narrative repeats the same information, event description, or character introduction verbatim or near-verbatim within a span of text where the repetition serves no rhetorical, structural, or thematic purpose.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Self-reinforcing repetition is a well-documented failure mode of neural text generation. Apple ML Research found that "models have a preference to repeat the previous sentence, and sentence-level repetitions have a self-reinforcement effect where the more times a sentence is repeated in context, the higher the probability of continuing to generate that sentence." User complaints about AI Dungeon specifically cite that "when hitting continue it tends to repeat literally everything from the story."
**Design space cost**: Eliminates fiction that employs deliberate repetition for rhetorical effect (refrains, leitmotifs, the repetition structures of oral storytelling). The check is for repetition that serves no identifiable purpose, not for all repetition.
**Source**: [Learning to Break the Loop - Apple ML Research](https://machinelearning.apple.com/research/analyzing-mitigating-repetitions), [Negative Feedback on LLM Storytelling - Cuckoo Network](https://cuckoo.network/blog/2025/04/17/negative-feedback-on-llm-powered-storytelling-and-roleplay-apps)

### Personality Trait Reversal Without Arc
**Detection**: A character exhibits a personality trait (brave, cowardly, kind, cruel, cautious, reckless) that is the opposite of a previously established trait, with no intervening events that could plausibly explain the change.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate character behavior based on local context and "character archetype" patterns in training data, not from a persistent model of the specific character. The "Lost in Stories" taxonomy identifies "skill fluctuations" and "forgotten abilities" as characterization subtypes. The Novarrium guide notes that in AI fiction, "personality traits shift without development." The model may generate a brave action because the scene pattern (climax, danger) triggers brave-character patterns, regardless of whether this character was established as timid.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates fiction where personality change is a sudden, traumatic event (psychological break, possession, magical transformation) and fiction where apparent inconsistency is later revealed to be a clue (the character was an impostor). Requires that the trait reversal be genuinely unaccounted for.
**Source**: [Lost in Stories](https://arxiv.org/html/2603.05890), [AI Story Consistency Guide - Novarrium](https://novarrium.com/blog/ai-story-consistency-complete-guide)

### Unearned Resolution (Deus Ex Machina)
**Detection**: The climactic or major conflict is resolved by an element (character, ability, object, event) that was not established or foreshadowed in the preceding narrative and whose appearance requires the reader to accept it arrived by authorial fiat.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate resolutions by pattern-matching to "conflict resolved" training examples without checking whether the resolution elements were previously established. September C. Fawkes identifies this as breaking four principles that require long-context tracking: cause-and-effect logic, knowledge requirements, foreshadowing, and protagonist agency. The model cannot retroactively verify that its generated resolution references previously planted elements because it lacks a structured model of what has been established.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates fiction where divine intervention or cosmic randomness is itself the thematic point (Greek tragedy, absurdist fiction, cosmic horror). Also conflicts with fiction where surprise is valued over predictability. The check is specifically for resolutions that contradict the story's own implicit contract with the reader.
**Source**: [What is Deus Ex Machina - September C. Fawkes](https://www.septembercfawkes.com/2026/01/what-is-a-deus-ex-machina-meaning-writing.html), [Deus Ex Machina - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deus_ex_machina)

### Head-Hopping Within Scene
**Detection**: The narrative shifts from one character's internal perspective to another character's internal perspective within a single scene, without a clear break or transitional signal, in a work that otherwise uses limited POV.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs do not maintain a POV-character variable that constrains which internal states they can describe. The model generates whichever character's thoughts or feelings are most contextually relevant to the current paragraph, without checking whether the current scene is "owned" by a different character. This produces text where the reader is "floating unmoored through the story." The LLM research paper's "perspective shifts" error subtype captures this.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates omniscient narration (which deliberately moves between perspectives), free indirect discourse that blends narrator and character voice, and deliberately experimental POV structures. The check applies only to works that establish limited-POV conventions and then violate them inconsistently.
**Source**: [Head-Hopping POV - Helping Writers Become Authors](https://www.helpingwritersbecomeauthors.com/common-writing-mistakes-goody-goody-characters-2/), [Lost in Stories](https://arxiv.org/html/2603.05890)

### Mid-Narrative Detail Drift
**Detection**: Factual details that are established early in the work (within the first 20% of the text) contradict details that appear late in the work (in the final 30%), with the contradictions concentrated in the kinds of specific details (names, numbers, physical attributes, geography) that require exact recall rather than general understanding.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: This pattern directly maps to the "lost in the middle" and context degradation effects documented in LLM research. The "Lost in Stories" paper found that "errors accumulate approximately linearly with output length" and "concentrate in narrative middle (40-60% range)." Context degradation research shows "effective capacity is usually 60 to 70% of the advertised maximum." Human writers working from notes or memory have different error distributions — their errors are more random, while LLM errors systematically increase with distance from introduction.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates fiction where early details are deliberately unreliable (unreliable narrator eventually revealed), and fiction where details change because the world itself changes (apocalyptic fiction, reality-warping narratives). The check is for unacknowledged, unintentional drift.
**Source**: [Lost in Stories](https://arxiv.org/html/2603.05890), [How Long Contexts Fail - Drew Breunig](https://www.dbreunig.com/2025/06/22/how-contexts-fail-and-how-to-fix-them.html)

### Relationship State Incoherence
**Detection**: Two characters' relationship (romantic, hostile, familial, professional) is portrayed inconsistently between scenes — enemies become friends or vice versa between chapters with no intervening events or transition.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate character interactions based on local context and archetypal relationship patterns rather than a persistent relationship model. The Novarrium guide specifically identifies this: "enemies become friends (or vice versa) between chapters with no transition" and "family structures become confused." The Noveble article documents this as a common user complaint: "relationship continuity where characters who broke up act like they are together."
**Design space cost**: Eliminates fiction where relationships are genuinely unstable by design (toxic relationships with cycles of hostility and reconciliation), fiction where different POV characters perceive the same relationship differently, and fiction with time skips where offscreen development is implied.
**Source**: [AI Story Consistency Guide - Novarrium](https://novarrium.com/blog/ai-story-consistency-complete-guide), [AI Keeps Forgetting My Characters - Noveble](https://www.noveble.com/blogs/character-consistency-management)

### Geographic Contradiction
**Detection**: The spatial relationships between locations in the story world are described inconsistently — a place described as north of another is later described as south, or distances between locations change between passages.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: The "Lost in Stories" research found that "geographic contradictions show largest average gaps (31.0%)" of all error types, meaning they span the greatest textual distance between the establishing detail and the contradiction. LLMs do not maintain spatial models and generate geographic descriptions from local context rather than a persistent map. This makes geography particularly vulnerable to long-range inconsistency.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates fiction where geography is deliberately unstable (magical landscapes, dream sequences, Borgesian labyrinths) and fiction with unreliable narrators who misremember geography.
**Source**: [Lost in Stories](https://arxiv.org/html/2603.05890), [AI Story Consistency Guide - Novarrium](https://novarrium.com/blog/ai-story-consistency-complete-guide)

### Abandoned Subplot
**Detection**: A subplot is given at least two scenes of dedicated narrative attention and then receives no further mention in the remaining text, with no resolution, deliberate ambiguity signal, or narrative closure.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs generate scene-to-scene without a structural model of outstanding plot threads. As the context window fills with newer plot developments, earlier subplots lose salience in the attention mechanism and are simply not generated again. This is distinct from a human author's deliberate choice to leave threads open — the AI version shows no signals of intentional ambiguity, the thread simply stops.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates fiction that deliberately abandons subplots as a structural device (Pynchon), serial fiction that intentionally carries threads across volumes, and fiction where the absence of resolution is itself thematically meaningful. The check is for threads that appear forgotten rather than deliberately suspended.
**Source**: [How to Find and Fix Plot Holes - Well-Storied](https://www.well-storied.com/blog/plot-holes), [Lost in Stories](https://arxiv.org/html/2603.05890)

### Formulaic Structural Repetition
**Detection**: The narrative employs the same structural pattern (paragraph shape, scene beat sequence, sentence construction formula) repeatedly across multiple scenes or chapters in a way that suggests template application rather than deliberate stylistic choice.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Research found that approximately 76% of syntactic templates detected in LLM-generated text are present in pre-training datasets, compared to only 35% in human-authored texts. The Record Crash article identifies "oscillating writing modes" — alternation between "long, heavy comparisons, then short lines that sounded like stock phrases" — as a tell. Human writers vary structure instinctively; LLMs produce text that follows high-probability structural templates from training data.
**Design space cost**: Eliminates fiction that deliberately employs repetitive structures (fairy tales, oral tradition patterns, certain literary experiments like Perec), and genre fiction that follows tight structural conventions (romance beat sheets, mystery structures). The check is for structural repetition that appears mechanical rather than intentional.
**Source**: [Uncovering Repetition: Syntactic Templates in AI-Generated Text - Complex Discovery](https://complexdiscovery.com/uncovering-repetition-how-syntactic-templates-reveal-patterns-in-ai-generated-text/), [How to Identify AI-Written Web Fiction - Record Crash](https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction)
