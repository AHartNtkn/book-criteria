## Criteria Found

### C1: Lexical Diversity and Vocabulary Breadth
**What it measures**: Whether the text demonstrates varied and intentional word choice rather than clustering around a narrow band of statistically probable vocabulary
**Assumed goal**: Literary fiction that demonstrates authorial command of language; applies to any fiction aspiring to quality prose
**Score 0**: Text uses a narrow vocabulary band, clustering around common "safe" words. High repetition of the same adjectives, adverbs, and transitional phrases. Type-token ratio is low. Same words appear in the same syntactic positions across paragraphs.
**Score 5**: Text demonstrates a wide and intentional vocabulary. Word choice is specific rather than generic, varied across passages, and appropriate to the context. Author employs precise terminology when available rather than defaulting to vague category labels.
**Source**: https://www.refsmmat.com/notebooks/llm-style.html and https://betterthangood.xyz/blog/ai-writing-has-no-voice/
**Notes**: A 2024 study found human texts show "more scattered sentence length distributions, more varied vocabulary, and shorter syntactic constituents" versus LLM output with "distributions that cluster around lower values and have tight interquartile ranges." MATTR-500 (Moving-Average Type-Token Ratio) is one standard measure.

### C2: Burstiness and Sentence-Length Variation
**What it measures**: Whether the text exhibits natural variation in sentence length, structure, and rhythm across passages
**Assumed goal**: Prose that reads as deliberately crafted by a mind with varying intentions and emphases; literary fiction and commercial fiction alike
**Score 0**: Sentence lengths are uniform throughout. Paragraphs follow identical structural patterns. Standard deviation of sentence length divided by mean is below 0.30. Every paragraph has similar rhythm.
**Score 5**: Sentence lengths vary deliberately and purposefully. Short punchy sentences alternate with complex constructions. Rhythm shifts between passages to match content and emotional register. Standard deviation/mean ratio above 0.60.
**Source**: https://quillbot.com/blog/ai-writing-tools/burstiness-and-perplexity/ and https://betterthangood.xyz/blog/ai-writing-has-no-voice/
**Notes**: Human writing scores 0.60-1.00+ for burstiness while AI writing scores 0.15-0.30. AI detectors measure this by calculating standard deviation of sentence lengths divided by mean sentence length.

### C3: Entropy and Predictability
**What it measures**: Whether the text contains genuine surprises in word choice, phrasing, and narrative development rather than always selecting the most statistically probable next token
**Assumed goal**: Fiction that engages the reader through unpredictability and genuine creative choice rather than statistical averaging
**Score 0**: Text is maximally predictable. Every word choice is the most probable completion. No surprising juxtapositions, unusual phrasings, or unexpected word combinations. Cross-entropy is low.
**Score 5**: Text contains genuine surprises that reward attention. Word choices are sometimes unexpected but justified by context. Phrasing departs from the most probable path in ways that create meaning, humor, or intensity. Cross-entropy approaches human baseline.
**Source**: https://betterthangood.xyz/blog/ai-writing-has-no-voice/ and https://medium.com/@jhanwarsid/human-contentanalysing-perplexity-and-burstiness-in-ai-vs-human-text-df70fdcc5525
**Notes**: A 2025 Mannheim study measured story continuations: LLMs produced "two to four times lower entropy than human ground truth," with the gap widening after RLHF.

### C4: Semantic Specificity vs Semantic Ablation
**What it measures**: Whether the text uses specific, concrete, precise language rather than generic category labels and vague descriptions
**Assumed goal**: Fiction that creates vivid, particular images and references rather than gesturing at categories; any fiction that values prose quality
**Score 0**: Text systematically replaces specific details with generic categories. Proper nouns dissolve into category labels. Metaphors are conventional similes. Wine is described as "notes of dark fruit with velvety finish" instead of "blackcurrant and pencil lead." Jazz is described with "complex harmonies" instead of "McCoy Tyner comping in fourths."
**Score 5**: Text is precise and particular. Details are chosen for their specificity and resonance. Descriptions name specific things rather than gesturing at categories. References are concrete and grounded in knowledge rather than statistical approximation.
**Source**: https://betterthangood.xyz/blog/ai-writing-has-no-voice/ and https://jerz.setonhill.edu/blog/2026/02/16/semantic-ablation-why-ai-writing-is-boring-and-dangerous/
**Notes**: Semantic ablation is defined as "the algorithmic erosion of high-entropy information in AI-written or AI-refined text." The AI identifies precise points where unique insights reside and systematically replaces them with the most probable, generic token sequences.

### C5: Show vs Tell in Emotional Rendering
**What it measures**: Whether emotions are rendered through action, physical detail, dialogue, and behavior rather than stated directly through narration
**Assumed goal**: Fiction in the realist tradition and most commercial fiction; the "show don't tell" principle is near-universal in modern fiction craft
**Score 0**: Emotions are stated directly: "She felt afraid," "He was angry," "A sense of unease grew." Characters announce how they feel. The narrator summarizes emotional states rather than depicting them. Every emotional moment is explained.
**Score 5**: Emotions emerge through physical behavior, dialogue choices, action, and selective detail. The reader infers emotional states from evidence rather than being told. Subtext operates beneath the surface. Some emotions are never named but are unmistakably present.
**Source**: https://www.craftliterary.com/2025/03/26/show-dont-tell-what-ai-cant-do/ and https://betterthangood.xyz/blog/ai-writing-has-no-voice/
**Notes**: CRAFT Literary: ChatGPT defaults to exposition and summary because it's "designed to respond to a prompt, an imperative" and thus "cannot be oblique or indirect, cannot let details speak for themselves." Columbia University researchers documented how LLM creative writing consistently "tells instead of shows."

### C6: Character Voice Differentiation
**What it measures**: Whether individual characters have distinctive speech patterns, vocabulary, syntax, and verbal habits that distinguish them from each other and from the narrator
**Assumed goal**: Any fiction with multiple speaking characters; particularly important in dialogue-heavy fiction
**Score 0**: All characters speak identically. Dialogue could be attributed to any character interchangeably. Every character uses the same vocabulary level, sentence structure, and emotional expression patterns. Characters sound like "they all went to the same therapy retreat."
**Score 5**: Each character has a recognizable voice. Speech patterns reflect personality, background, education, emotional state, and social context. Characters could be identified by their dialogue alone. Voices are consistent across the work.
**Source**: https://www.creativindie.com/how-to-humanize-chatgpt-written-content-for-better-fiction-and-to-pass-ai-detection/ and https://medium.com/@ajtracysk/how-i-use-ai-to-write-dialogue-that-doesnt-sound-like-a-chatbot-having-feelings-5fe9df77a353
**Notes**: One writer reported readers complained: "The characters all sound like they're reading from the same self-help book." ChatGPT tends to choose the same character names, the same words repeatedly, and even has different characters repeat the exact same words.

### C7: Moral and Thematic Ambiguity
**What it measures**: Whether the text presents moral complexity and thematic ambiguity rather than over-explaining its themes and providing tidy moral lessons
**Assumed goal**: Literary fiction and any fiction that aspires to reflect the complexity of human experience; less applicable to didactic or allegorical fiction
**Score 0**: Themes are stated explicitly. The moral is delivered directly. Characters verbalize the lesson learned. Every conflict resolves cleanly with unambiguous right and wrong sides. The text reads as a parable with its interpretation pre-attached.
**Score 5**: Themes emerge organically through events and character choices. Moral questions are posed but not answered definitively. Different characters embody different valid perspectives. The reader must do interpretive work. Ambiguity is preserved where appropriate.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.03136 and https://betterthangood.xyz/blog/ai-writing-has-no-voice/
**Notes**: StoryScope research on 61K stories found AI stories "over-explain themes and favor tidy, single-track plots" while human stories "frame protagonist choices as more morally ambiguous and have increased temporal complexity."

### C8: Plot Diversity and Narrative Uniqueness
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative takes unexpected paths rather than following the most statistically probable plot structure
**Assumed goal**: Fiction that values originality and surprise; any fiction that aims to avoid being formulaic
**Score 0**: Plot follows the most common template for its genre without deviation. Story elements are highly predictable. The same plot beats, landmarks, and character decisions appear as would be found in dozens of other stories given the same premise. Story could be generated by averaging existing stories.
**Score 5**: Plot takes genuine unexpected turns while maintaining internal logic. Story elements are particular to this narrative rather than generic. The combination of plot elements is unique rather than a recombination of common patterns.
**Source**: https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2504966122
**Notes**: The "Echoes in AI" study found that LLM stories contain "combinations of idiosyncratic plot elements echoed frequently across generations and across different LLMs, while plots from the original human-written stories are rarely recreated." In one test, 50/100 GPT-4 generations had the policeman give instructions to "take the second left."

### C9: Temporal Complexity
**What it measures**: Whether the narrative employs varied and sophisticated temporal structures rather than strict linear chronology
**Assumed goal**: Literary fiction and any fiction that uses time structure as a narrative tool
**Score 0**: Narrative proceeds in strict chronological order with no variation. Events are presented in the order they occur without flashbacks, flash-forwards, parallel timelines, or temporal layering. Time is treated as a flat conveyor belt.
**Score 5**: Temporal structure is employed as a deliberate narrative tool. Flashbacks, flash-forwards, parallel timelines, or non-linear chronology serve the story's themes and emotional arc. Time manipulation creates meaning and surprise.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.03136 and https://github.com/lechmazur/writing_styles
**Notes**: StoryScope found human stories exhibit "increased temporal complexity." The writing styles analysis found linear chronology dominates LLM output at 82-99%, with only Claude models showing meaningful flashback usage.

### C10: Subtext and Layered Meaning
**What it measures**: Whether the text operates on multiple levels simultaneously, with surface events carrying deeper implications that are not stated explicitly
**Assumed goal**: Literary fiction and any fiction that aspires to depth; the principle that great fiction rewards re-reading
**Score 0**: Everything is surface-level. Characters say exactly what they mean. Events carry only their literal significance. There is no gap between what is said and what is meant, between what happens and what it signifies. The text is exhausted by a single reading.
**Score 5**: The text operates on multiple levels. Dialogue carries subtext. Events resonate symbolically. What characters don't say is as important as what they do say. The gap between surface and depth creates rich interpretive space. Re-reading reveals new dimensions.
**Source**: https://www.craftliterary.com/2025/03/26/show-dont-tell-what-ai-cant-do/ and https://sudowrite.com/blog/best-ai-for-dialogue-writing-make-characters-sound-human/
**Notes**: CRAFT Literary: AI "cannot embed significance through careful detail selection or strategic omission. It cannot suggest subtext through what remains unsaid." Sudowrite analysis: AI makes characters sound like "chatbots having feelings—explaining emotions instead of implying them, filling every silence with exposition."

### C11: Authorial Voice and Stylistic Identity
**What it measures**: Whether the prose exhibits a recognizable, distinctive authorial voice that could not be mistaken for a generic default
**Assumed goal**: Any fiction that values distinctive prose style; particularly literary fiction
**Score 0**: Prose has no distinctive qualities. It reads as a competent but generic default. There is no sense that a particular mind with particular convictions and aesthetic preferences produced these sentences. The writing could have been produced by averaging the styles of many authors.
**Score 5**: The prose has an unmistakable voice. Sentence construction, word choice, rhythm, and perspective reflect a specific sensibility. The writing could not be mistaken for anyone else's. There is a sense of cost—that a specific human spent time choosing these words over other words.
**Source**: https://betterthangood.xyz/blog/ai-writing-has-no-voice/
**Notes**: The core tell identified is "No sense of cost—no evidence that a specific human spent time choosing these words over other words based on conviction rather than probability." Human voice comes from "accumulated experience, consistent opinions, and willingness to be wrong in ways that reveal character."

### C12: Emotional Authenticity and Earned Catharsis
**What it measures**: Whether emotional moments are earned through narrative development rather than manufactured through ornate language
**Assumed goal**: Any fiction that aims to produce genuine emotional response in readers
**Score 0**: Emotional moments are unearned. Characters have breakdowns, epiphanies, or cathartic moments without sufficient narrative groundwork. Emotions are produced through purple prose and melodramatic language rather than through accumulated tension and character development. Grief sounds like a Hallmark card.
**Score 5**: Emotional moments land because they have been earned. The reader's response emerges from accumulated narrative investment. Catharsis follows genuine tension. Emotional language is restrained and precise rather than overwrought. Feelings are rendered with the specificity of lived experience.
**Source**: https://pjmedia.com/jamie-wilson/2026/03/19/no-ache-no-story-ai-and-todays-publishers-are-producing-the-same-cowardly-fiction-n4950836 and https://elliottslaughter.com/2021/06/sentimentality
**Notes**: AI fiction described as lacking "slow-burn tension, no ache of longing, no soul." "Sentimentality is most helpfully defined as unearned emotion." AI writing "could mimic structure and prose, but it could not feel or express the feeling of betrayal."

### C13: Villain and Antagonist Complexity
**What it measures**: Whether antagonists and morally complex characters are portrayed with genuine depth and sophistication rather than being sanitized or simplified
**Assumed goal**: Fiction that explores the full spectrum of human behavior; particularly relevant for literary fiction, thrillers, and dark fiction
**Score 0**: Villains are either cartoonishly evil or sanitized into merely "misunderstood" figures. Morally complex characters are softened to avoid discomfort. Manipulation is replaced with open aggression. Deceit is replaced with transparent hostility. Characters who should be subtle and dangerous are portrayed as blunt and obvious.
**Score 5**: Antagonists are as fully realized as protagonists. Morally complex characters maintain their complexity throughout. Villains can be charismatic, intelligent, and sophisticated. The text can portray genuine menace, manipulation, and moral darkness without flinching.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2511.04962v1
**Notes**: Research found "consistent, monotonic decline in role-playing fidelity as character morality decreases." Models replace sophisticated villainy with "direct, shallow aggression" and score worst on traits like "Deceitful" (3.54 penalty) and "Manipulative." Claude models rank top in general benchmarks but 9th-15th on villain portrayal.

## Sentinels Found

### S1: "Delve" and "Delve Into"
**Detection**: Text contains the word "delve" or the phrase "delve into"
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: "Delve" was the most infamous AI tell, going from an uncommon English word to appearing in a staggering percentage of AI-generated text. Max Planck Institute research showed its usage surged 50%+ in published text post-ChatGPT. PubMed studies found "delves" appeared 25x more than pre-LLM trends predicted.
**Design space cost**: Authors writing about mining, archaeology, or deep investigation might legitimately use "delve." Some fantasy/historical fiction uses it naturally. Entire genres of academic writing used it before LLMs made it suspect.
**Source**: https://gist.github.com/chrisgherbert/c734ec50ae464135be57cd03b84281f9 and https://betterthangood.xyz/blog/ai-writing-has-no-voice/

### S2: "A Testament To"
**Detection**: Text contains the phrase "a testament to" or "stands as a testament"
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Identified across multiple curated lists as a phrase that appears with disproportionate frequency in LLM output. Co-occurs with other LLM vocabulary markers. Wikipedia's AI writing guide lists it as a high-density AI vocabulary indicator.
**Design space cost**: Legal fiction, historical fiction, and religious fiction may legitimately use this phrase in appropriate contexts. Eulogies and memorial passages in literary fiction sometimes employ it purposefully.
**Source**: https://gist.github.com/chrisgherbert/c734ec50ae464135be57cd03b84281f9 and https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Signs_of_AI_writing

### S3: "Tapestry" as Metaphor
**Detection**: Text uses "tapestry" as a metaphor for something interconnected (e.g., "a tapestry of," "rich tapestry," "the tapestry of human experience")
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Consistently appears on every curated list of LLM overused words. Used to describe anything interconnected regardless of context. tropes.fyi catalogs it as a specific AI writing trope alongside "landscape," "paradigm," "synergy," and "ecosystem."
**Design space cost**: Fiction set in textile-producing cultures, fiction that discusses actual tapestries, and fiction that deliberately employs this metaphor once for thematic resonance would be flagged.
**Source**: https://tropes.fyi/directory and https://gist.github.com/chrisgherbert/c734ec50ae464135be57cd03b84281f9

### S4: Excessive Em Dash Usage
**Detection**: Em dash frequency exceeds 8 per 1,000 words in prose passages (human baseline mean is 3.23, range 0.33-17.12)
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Research paper "The Last Fingerprint" measured em dash frequency across 12 LLMs and found systematic overuse. GPT-4.1 produced 10.62/1K words, Claude Opus 4.6 produced 9.09/1K words. The em dash is "markdown leaking into prose—the smallest surviving unit of the structural orientation that LLMs acquire from markdown-saturated training corpora." RLHF amplifies this because evaluators reward organized-looking prose.
**Design space cost**: Some human authors (Emily Dickinson tradition, certain literary fiction styles) legitimately use em dashes at high rates. Human baseline ranges up to 17.12/1K words. This sentinel catches heavy-em-dash human stylists.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2603.27006v1

### S5: "Not X, It's Y" / Negative Parallelism Pattern
**Detection**: Text contains the construction "It's not X — it's Y" or "Not X, but Y" used for rhetorical reframing, especially with em dashes, more than once per 2,000 words
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: tropes.fyi catalogs this as the #1 most common AI writing trope. The Slop Score weights "Not-x-but-y Contrast Patterns" at 25% of its composite detection metric. The pattern manufactures false profundity through surprising reframes.
**Design space cost**: Rhetorical writing, speeches, and persuasive fiction deliberately employ this construction. Political fiction and debate-heavy fiction may use it in dialogue. A single occurrence is unremarkable; density is the signal.
**Source**: https://tropes.fyi/directory and https://eqbench.com/slop-score.html

### S6: Tricolon/Rule-of-Three Abuse
**Detection**: Text uses three-item lists in prose (adjective, adjective, adjective / phrase, phrase, and phrase) more than twice per 1,000 words
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: tropes.fyi identifies this as "Tricolon Abuse" — overuse of rule-of-three patterns, often extended to four or five items. AI uses these to inflate superficial analyses and create false rhetorical weight. Wikipedia's AI writing guide also flags this.
**Design space cost**: The rule of three is a genuine rhetorical device used throughout literary history. Speechwriting, oratory, and literary prose deliberately employ tricola. Julius Caesar's "veni, vidi, vici" is a tricolon. Banning it entirely would exclude significant rhetorical tradition.
**Source**: https://tropes.fyi/directory and https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Signs_of_AI_writing

### S7: "The Weight of" / "A Sense of" / "A Mix of" Emotional Formulas
**Detection**: Text uses formulas like "the weight of [noun]," "a sense of [emotion]," "a mix of [emotion] and [emotion]" to describe emotional states
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: CHI 2025 research specifically identified that "a mix of [X] and," "sense of," and "weight of" appear disproportionately in AI-generated fiction compared to human writing. One AI novel used "the weight of the crown" 45 times in 16 chapters. These are emotional shorthand that substitutes for rendering.
**Design space cost**: These are legitimate English constructions that human authors do use, albeit less frequently. A single "weight of" in a novel is unremarkable. Flagging any occurrence would be overly aggressive; density is the signal.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2409.14509v1 and https://coyotetracks.org/blog/ai-writing/

### S8: "Nestled" in Geographic Description
**Detection**: Text uses "nestled" to describe the location of a place (e.g., "nestled in the heart of," "nestled among the hills")
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Multiple sources identify "nestled" as a signature ChatGPT word, particularly in travel-style writing. It appears on virtually every overused-words list. The word is used by ChatGPT to describe every restaurant, town, or attraction regardless of whether nesting is an apt metaphor.
**Design space cost**: The word "nestled" has legitimate uses, particularly for small structures sheltered within larger features. A cabin nestled in a valley is a perfectly apt image. Travel writing and pastoral fiction may use it naturally.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Signs_of_AI_writing and https://medium.com/practice-in-public/7-common-chatgpt-phrases-and-why-you-must-avoid-them-5420e60cda9d

### S9: "Vibrant" as Default Descriptor
**Detection**: Text uses "vibrant" to describe communities, cultures, atmospheres, or colors without specific grounding
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Appears on every comprehensive AI word list. Wikipedia flags it as both "promotional/advertisement language" and "high-density AI vocabulary." It functions as a generic positive descriptor that substitutes for actual characterization.
**Design space cost**: Art criticism, color theory discussions, and descriptions of genuinely visually intense scenes may legitimately use "vibrant" with specific meaning. A painter's fiction might use it with precision.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Signs_of_AI_writing and https://gist.github.com/chrisgherbert/c734ec50ae464135be57cd03b84281f9

### S10: "Crucial" / "Pivotal" / "Vital" Significance Inflation
**Detection**: Text uses "crucial," "pivotal," or "vital" to describe the importance of events, moments, or information
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: ChatGPT uses "crucial" for everything—Max Planck research showed "crucial" increased 2.6 percentage points corpus-wide post-ChatGPT. These words inflate the significance of mundane events. They are part of a broader pattern of "undue emphasis on significance" documented on Wikipedia's AI writing page.
**Design space cost**: Medical fiction, military fiction, and political thrillers may legitimately describe events as crucial or vital when stakes genuinely warrant it. The problem is ubiquity, not any single usage.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Signs_of_AI_writing and https://www.grammarly.com/blog/ai/common-ai-words/

**Detection**: Text shifts between past and present tense within a passage without deliberate narrative purpose
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: The CHI 2025 taxonomy identifies "Tense Inconsistency" as one of seven categories of AI writing problems, defined as "unintended shifts between past, present, and future tenses." LLMs generate text locally and may shift tense when the most probable next token happens to be in a different tense.
**Design space cost**: Some experimental fiction deliberately shifts tenses for effect (present tense for immediacy in flashbacks, past tense for reflection). Proust's In Search of Lost Time shifts tenses deliberately. The issue is unintentional shifting.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2409.14509v1

### S51: Purple Prose and Overwrought Description
**Detection**: Descriptions use excessive adjectives, ornate metaphors, and elaborate figurative language where simpler language would be more effective — text is "purple as an eggplant stomping grapes"
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: The CHI 2025 taxonomy identifies "Purple Prose" as a category: "excessively elaborate writing that disrupts narrative flow." AI default is ornate prose because RLHF annotators rate elaborate language higher than simple language. The model has learned that "flowery is good" without understanding that restraint can be more powerful.
**Design space cost**: Some literary traditions (magical realism, Southern Gothic, certain fantasy) legitimately employ lush, elaborate prose. Authors like Gabriel Garcia Marquez and Cormac McCarthy use dense figurative language deliberately. This sentinel would flag their style.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2409.14509v1 and https://pjmedia.com/jamie-wilson/2026/03/19/no-ache-no-story-ai-and-todays-publishers-are-producing-the-same-cowardly-fiction-n4950836

### S52: No Character Makes a Morally Questionable Choice
**Detection**: Across the full text, no protagonist or significant character makes a decision that is morally ambiguous, selfish, cruel, deceptive, or otherwise ethically problematic
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: RLHF safety training systematically penalizes morally questionable content. Research found a "monotonic decline in role-playing fidelity as character morality decreases." The result is fiction where all characters are essentially good people who make essentially good choices — fiction without moral friction.
**Design space cost**: Children's fiction, certain romance subgenres, and inspirational fiction may legitimately feature universally good characters. Some genres deliberately present moral clarity.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2511.04962v1 and https://medium.com/@cbryanvest/llms-cant-write-good-fiction-902d7b66c8e6

### S53: Dialogue Lacks Subtext (Characters Say What They Mean)
**Detection**: In emotionally charged scenes, characters state their feelings, intentions, and analyses directly rather than talking around the real subject, deflecting, lying, or using implication
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: AI models are "designed to respond to a prompt, an imperative" and cannot "be oblique or indirect." Multiple sources confirm: AI makes characters "sound like chatbots having feelings — explaining emotions instead of implying them, filling every silence with exposition." Human dialogue is characterized by the gap between what is said and what is meant.
**Design space cost**: Some characters in some contexts do speak directly — emergency situations, military communication, characters with autism-spectrum traits. Children's fiction may have more direct dialogue. The issue is the absence of subtext across ALL dialogue.
**Source**: https://sudowrite.com/blog/best-ai-for-dialogue-writing-make-characters-sound-human/ and https://www.craftliterary.com/2025/03/26/show-dont-tell-what-ai-cant-do/

### S54: No Character Has a Backstory That Causes Present Pain
**Detection**: Characters lack psychological depth from past experiences. Backstory either doesn't exist or is mentioned without affecting present behavior, decisions, or emotional responses
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Coyote Tracks found characters who "don't truly grapple with their past, because they have no backstory apart from 'the bad guys killed her mom.'" Characters are "never faced with heart-wrenching choices." AI generates characters as functional roles rather than as people shaped by their histories.
**Design space cost**: Plot-driven genre fiction (certain thrillers, some science fiction) may deliberately minimize character interiority in favor of action. Short fiction may not have space for backstory development.
**Source**: https://coyotetracks.org/blog/ai-writing/

### S55: Excessive Elegant Variation (Synonym Substitution)
**Detection**: Text avoids repeating any word by substituting increasingly obscure synonyms for the same referent (e.g., "the protagonist," "the key player," "the eponymous character," "the central figure" all referring to the same person)
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Wikipedia identifies "Elegant Variation" as a specific AI writing indicator: excessive synonym substitution to avoid word repetition. LLMs are trained to maximize lexical diversity at the sentence level, leading to different terms for the same entity that create confusion rather than clarity.
**Design space cost**: Some literary fiction deliberately employs varied reference for stylistic effect. Journalistic convention sometimes requires varied reference. The issue is when variation creates confusion about whether the same entity is being referenced.
**Source**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Signs_of_AI_writing

### S56: "Forged in the Crucible of" and Metaphorical Overreach
**Detection**: Text uses overextended metaphors like "forged in the crucible of," "beacon of hope," "symphony of [noun]" where the metaphorical vehicle overwhelms the tenor
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Coyote Tracks found the phrase "She had been forged in the crucible of her family's tragedy" in an AI novel that hadn't yet developed that tragedy thematically. "Crucible," "beacon," "symphony," and "gossamer" all appear on AI word lists as metaphor vehicles that LLMs deploy regardless of thematic appropriateness.
**Design space cost**: Deliberately ornate literary fiction employs extended metaphors. Fantasy fiction uses forging/crucible metaphors with literal-metaphorical dual meaning. Steelworking fiction would be flagged.
**Source**: https://coyotetracks.org/blog/ai-writing/ and https://gist.github.com/chrisgherbert/c734ec50ae464135be57cd03b84281f9

### S57: "Whispering" / "Murmuring" as Default Dialogue Tag
**Detection**: A disproportionate number of dialogue tags use "whispered," "murmured," "breathed" instead of "said"
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: The lechmazur writing styles study found Claude 3 "overwhelmingly" favored the word "whisper." Creative dialogue tags are a known AI fiction pattern, with the model avoiding the neutral "said" in favor of more dramatic alternatives, paralleling a known beginner-writer mistake that AI has inherited from training data.
**Design space cost**: Thriller fiction, espionage fiction, and fiction set in environments requiring quiet speech may legitimately have high whisper counts. The issue is default usage rather than contextual usage.
**Source**: https://github.com/lechmazur/writing_styles and https://theeditorsblog.net/2010/12/25/use-and-misuse-of-dialogue-tags/

### S58: "Eyeball Kicks" — Stacked Nonsensical Metaphors
**Detection**: Multiple metaphors are stacked to describe a single concept, creating internally contradictory or nonsensical imagery (e.g., "a geometry of madness, a wound in the fabric of what is, older than law and time")
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Record Crash identifies excessive "Eyeball Kicks" — dramatic visual descriptions and metaphorical language stacked excessively — as a web fiction AI tell. Each metaphor individually might work, but stacked they create incoherence. The model generates each metaphor as a locally optimal continuation without evaluating global coherence.
**Design space cost**: Some experimental and surrealist fiction deliberately employs contradictory or layered metaphor (Lautreamont, certain poetry-adjacent prose). Psychedelic fiction may stack imagery intentionally.
**Source**: https://recordcrash.substack.com/p/how-to-identify-ai-written-web-fiction

### S59: "It's Worth Noting" / "It's Important to Note" / "Notably"
**Detection**: Text contains hedging/transition phrases like "it's worth noting," "it's important to note," "notably," "it bears mentioning," "importantly," "interestingly"
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: tropes.fyi catalogs this as a specific trope: filler transitions that disconnect ideas without real logical flow. The Slop Score uses these phrases as part of its detection metrics. They are essay/report transitions that have no place in fiction prose.
**Design space cost**: Academic narrator characters, epistolary fiction mimicking formal reports, and metafictional narrators who adopt an essay voice may use these constructions. Satirical fiction would be flagged.
**Source**: https://tropes.fyi/directory and https://eqbench.com/slop-score.html

### S60: 76%+ Syntactic Templates Match Pre-Training Data
**Detection**: The syntactic structures (part-of-speech patterns) in the text match patterns found in common pre-training datasets at a rate significantly above the 35% baseline found in human text
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Research found that LLMs frequently generate text containing syntactic templates from their pre-training data — about 76% of detected templates in model-generated text were present in pre-training datasets, compared to only 35% in human text. This indicates the model is reproducing learned structures rather than generating novel ones.
**Design space cost**: Genre fiction that follows established conventions may have higher template overlap with training data by coincidence. Romance, mystery, and thriller fiction follow genre templates that may be well-represented in training data.
**Source**: https://complexdiscovery.com/uncovering-repetition-how-syntactic-templates-reveal-patterns-in-ai-generated-text/

### S61: Homogeneously Positive Emotional Valence
**Detection**: The overall emotional trajectory of the text is uniformly positive — setbacks are temporary, characters maintain optimism, and the narrative tone rarely dips into genuine despair, bitterness, or ugliness
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: StoryScope found stories are "homogeneously positive and lack tension." RLHF introduces 29-41% bias toward positive framing. Research confirmed "positive endings dominate" across all LLMs with "negative endings rare across all models." The model has been trained to prefer positive outcomes.
**Design space cost**: Genuinely optimistic fiction, uplifting narratives, and comedy that resolves happily are legitimate genres. Romance fiction with HEA (happily ever after) requirements would be flagged. Children's fiction defaults to positive outcomes.
**Source**: https://arxiv.org/html/2409.14509v1 and https://github.com/lechmazur/writing_styles

### S62: Near-Uniform Earnestness (Absence of Irony)
**Detection**: The text maintains earnest, sincere tone throughout with no irony, sarcasm, deadpan humor, or tonal subversion
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: The lechmazur writing styles study found "near-uniform earnestness (99.8%); irony and ambiguity essentially trace-level" across all LLMs tested. Irony requires the reader to understand that the surface meaning differs from the intended meaning — a form of subtext that LLMs systematically struggle with.
**Design space cost**: Sincere literary fiction (Marilynne Robinson), devotional fiction, and genuine emotional narratives that avoid irony are legitimate traditions. Not all good fiction employs irony.
**Source**: https://github.com/lechmazur/writing_styles

### S63: High Register as Universal Default
**Detection**: All prose, including dialogue, internal monologue, and narration, maintains a consistently high/formal register with no colloquial, informal, or vulgar language
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: The lechmazur study found "high register universal default" with "colloquial absent" across all LLMs. This results from training on formal text and RLHF annotators preferring polished prose. Human writers naturally mix registers — characters speak informally, narrators shift between registers for effect.
**Design space cost**: Literary fiction in the Henry James tradition, historical fiction set in formal eras, and fiction about upper-class characters may legitimately maintain high register. Court fiction and diplomatic fiction would be flagged.
**Source**: https://github.com/lechmazur/writing_styles

### S64: "Fractal Summary" Structure (Tell-Do-Retell)
**Detection**: Text follows the pattern of announcing what will happen, narrating it happening, then summarizing what happened — at multiple levels of the narrative
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: tropes.fyi catalogs this as "Fractal Summaries" — "What I'll tell you; what I'm telling you; what I told you" applied at every level. This is an essay/lecture structure that has leaked into fiction from instructional training data.
**Design space cost**: Metafictional narrators who deliberately comment on their own narrative may employ this structure. Some experimental fiction uses repetition-with-variation as a structural device.
**Source**: https://tropes.fyi/directory

### S65: "Treasure Trove" / "Game Changer" / "Cutting-Edge" Hype Language
**Detection**: Text uses marketing/hype language like "treasure trove," "game changer," "cutting-edge," "groundbreaking," or "revolutionary" in narrative prose
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: These appear on every AI word list. They are promotional language that has leaked into fiction from marketing training data. tropes.fyi catalogs this as business hype/sales language including "Revolutionizing the way," "A game-changer for," "Supercharge your."
**Design space cost**: Fiction about marketing, advertising, or Silicon Valley culture may use hype language in dialogue or to characterize characters' thought patterns. Satirical fiction about corporate culture would be flagged.
**Source**: https://gist.github.com/chrisgherbert/c734ec50ae464135be57cd03b84281f9 and https://www.blakestockton.com/red-flag-phrases/

### S66: Invented Concept Labels Presented as Established Terms
**Detection**: Text introduces compound labels like "the supervision paradox," "the empathy trap," "the nostalgia divide" as if they are established concepts without defining or attributing them
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: tropes.fyi catalogs this as "Invented Concept Labels" — invented compound labels (paradox, trap, creep, divide, vacuum, inversion) presented as established terms. The model generates authoritative-sounding terminology from training patterns without checking whether the concept actually exists.
**Design space cost**: Fiction about academia, intellectual characters who coin terms, and speculative fiction that invents terminology are legitimate. Characters who think in frameworks might naturally label patterns.
**Source**: https://tropes.fyi/directory

### S67: "Gentle Breeze" / "Blooming Flowers" / Generic Nature Description
**Detection**: Nature descriptions use stock imagery like "gentle breeze," "blooming flowers," "sun-dappled," "crystal clear water" without specific, observed detail
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: CRAFT Literary identifies AI relying on "overly familiar phrases like 'gentle breeze' and 'blooming flowers' — descriptions so commonplace they're essentially meaningless." The algorithm "works by predicting commonly occurring phrase patterns" from training data, making specific or surprising descriptions nearly impossible.
**Design space cost**: Children's fiction and certain pastoral traditions may legitimately employ simple nature imagery. Fiction deliberately evoking a fairy-tale register might use stock descriptions.
**Source**: https://www.craftliterary.com/2025/03/26/show-dont-tell-what-ai-cant-do/

### S68: "Dance" as Default Metaphor for Interaction
**Detection**: Text describes interactions, negotiations, or conflicts as a "dance" or "delicate dance"
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: AI Phrase Finder identifies ChatGPT frequently describing elements working together as engaging in a "dance" — "The two companies engaged in a delicate dance of negotiation." This is a dead metaphor deployed automatically regardless of context.
**Design space cost**: Fiction about actual dancing, ballroom-set fiction, and fiction that deliberately develops the dance metaphor thematically would be flagged. Some literary fiction uses the dance metaphor with precision.
**Source**: https://aiphrasefinder.com/common-chatgpt-metaphors/

### S69: Continuity Collapse in Long-Form Fiction
**Detection**: Physical descriptions change (eye color, hair, height), personality traits shift without development, characters know things they shouldn't, or world rules established early are violated later
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Every AI writing tool struggles with maintaining story consistency across long-form narratives. ChatGPT's context window means it "forgets earlier chapters by Chapter 5." Physical descriptions change, personality traits shift without development, and world rules get violated. "A magic system that requires incantations suddenly allows silent casting."
**Design space cost**: Some literary fiction deliberately employs unreliable descriptions or shifting reality (magical realism, psychological fiction). A character whose eye color genuinely changes due to plot reasons would be flagged. Very long human-written series sometimes have minor continuity errors too.
**Source**: https://novarrium.com/blog/ai-story-consistency-complete-guide and https://www.noveble.com/blogs/novel-continuity-context-management

### S70: "Robust" / "Compelling" / "Daunting" Generic Modifiers
**Detection**: Text uses "robust," "compelling," or "daunting" as generic modifiers divorced from specific meaning
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: These appear on virtually every AI word list. They function as generic positive/negative modifiers that signal nothing specific. Max Planck research showed "robust" and "pivotal" surged 50%+ post-ChatGPT.
**Design space cost**: Engineering fiction where "robust" has technical meaning, legal fiction where "compelling" refers to evidence quality, and adventure fiction where challenges are genuinely "daunting" would be flagged. The issue is deployment as empty modifiers.
**Source**: https://gist.github.com/chrisgherbert/c734ec50ae464135be57cd03b84281f9 and https://www.grammarly.com/blog/ai/common-ai-words/

### S71: Existential Themes as Default Subject Matter
**Detection**: Story defaults to existential/philosophical themes (meaning of life, identity, consciousness, what it means to be human) when no specific thematic direction is established
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: The lechmazur writing styles study found "existential themes dominate universally" across all LLMs tested. This represents the model defaulting to the most "literary-seeming" theme rather than engaging with specific, grounded human situations. It's the thematic equivalent of semantic ablation.
**Design space cost**: Genuinely existential fiction (Camus, Sartre, Dostoevsky tradition) is a major literary tradition. Science fiction exploring consciousness is a genre staple. The issue is existential themes as default rather than as deliberate choice.
**Source**: https://github.com/lechmazur/writing_styles

### S72: Missing Cause-and-Effect Chains
**Detection**: Characters achieve outcomes without the narrative explaining how. Goals are accomplished without showing the work, investigation, or struggle required.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Coyote Tracks identified this as a fundamental AI fiction failure: "How does Jack get from confronting the gangsters to bringing them to justice? What evidence did he find?" The model generates outcomes without constructing the logical chains that connect problems to solutions.
**Design space cost**: Some literary fiction deliberately omits causal explanation to create ambiguity or to focus on emotional rather than logical experience. Experimental fiction may skip cause-and-effect intentionally.
**Source**: https://coyotetracks.org/blog/ai-writing/

### S73: Protagonist Lacks Agency / Is Passive Observer
**Detection**: The protagonist does not actively make choices that drive the plot. Events happen to them rather than because of them. They observe and react rather than initiate.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Coyote Tracks found "Kira's personal stakes never change. She doesn't grow, she doesn't learn anything... she's never faced with heart-wrenching choices." AI generates characters as recipients of plot events rather than as agents who drive events through their choices and flaws.
**Design space cost**: Some literary fiction deliberately features passive protagonists (Oblomov, certain Kafka characters). Stories about powerlessness, imprisonment, or systemic oppression may have protagonists with limited agency by design.
**Source**: https://coyotetracks.org/blog/ai-writing/

### S74: Conflict Avoidance / Characters Too Nice
**Detection**: Characters avoid genuine interpersonal conflict. Disagreements resolve quickly. No character acts selfishly, cruelly, or dishonestly toward others. All interactions are pleasant or formally antagonistic with no messy middle ground.
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: LLMs are trained to be helpful and pleasant, which leaks into character behavior. Research confirmed models show "risk-averse tendencies, potentially diluting conflict or avoiding genre-appropriate darkness." Characters default to pleasant interactions because the model's preference training favors agreeable outputs.
**Design space cost**: Cozy fiction (cozy mysteries, cozy fantasy) deliberately minimizes interpersonal nastiness. Wholesome fiction and comfort reads avoid harsh conflict by design.
**Source**: https://www.papercutpost.com/using-ai-to-analyze-fiction-character-generation-styles/ and https://arxiv.org/html/2511.04962v1

### S75: "Echoed" Plot Elements Across Generations
**Detection**: Story contains specific plot elements (landmarks, character decisions, specific numbers, specific locations) that are suspiciously common across stories with similar premises
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: The "Echoes in AI" PNAS study found LLM stories contain "combinations of idiosyncratic plot elements echoed frequently across generations and across different LLMs." In one test, 50/100 generations mentioned a specific landmark (bakery) as a navigational reference. This represents the model sampling from the same narrow region of narrative possibility space.
**Design space cost**: Genre fiction following established conventions (cozy mysteries in small towns, romance at coffee shops) may share plot elements by genre convention rather than by AI generation. Common tropes are not exclusive to AI.
**Source**: https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2504966122

### S76: "Effortlessly" / "Seamlessly" False Ease Descriptors
**Detection**: Text uses "effortlessly" or "seamlessly" to describe achievements, transitions, or integrations
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Both appear on AI word lists. They describe outcomes as easy when the difficulty and effort involved in achieving them is precisely what makes them narratively interesting. They eliminate the friction that creates compelling fiction.
**Design space cost**: Fiction about truly skilled practitioners (master craftspeople, elite athletes) may describe actions as effortless to convey expertise. Fiction where ease is the point (luxury settings, utopian fiction) would be flagged.
**Source**: https://gist.github.com/chrisgherbert/c734ec50ae464135be57cd03b84281f9 and https://www.godofprompt.ai/blog/500-chatgpt-overused-words-heres-how-to-avoid-them

### S77: Bold-First Bullet Points / Markdown Formatting in Prose
**Detection**: Text contains markdown formatting artifacts: bold text, bullet points, numbered lists, headers, or emoji in what should be continuous prose fiction
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: tropes.fyi catalogs "Bold-First Bullets" and Wikipedia identifies markdown usage as AI writing indicators. These are direct artifacts of the markdown-heavy training data leaking into text output. ChatGPT favors lists due to RLHF training.
**Design space cost**: Experimental fiction that incorporates visual formatting (Mark Z. Danielewski's House of Leaves), epistolary fiction containing lists, and fiction incorporating documents may legitimately include formatting elements.
**Source**: https://tropes.fyi/directory and https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Signs_of_AI_writing

### S78: Nominalization Overuse
**Detection**: Text uses abstract nouns derived from verbs at 1.5-2x the rate of typical prose (e.g., "the optimization of processes" instead of "we optimized processes," "the realization that" instead of "she realized")
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Reuters Institute research found AI systems use nominalizations at 1.5 to 2 times the rate of human text, and present participial clauses at 2 to 5 times the rate. This creates dense, academic-sounding prose that is informationally overloaded and emotionally distant.
**Design space cost**: Academic fiction, legal fiction, and fiction set in bureaucratic environments may legitimately employ heavy nominalization to characterize institutional language. Kafkaesque fiction would be flagged.
**Source**: https://reutersinstitute.politics.ox.ac.uk/news/how-ai-generated-prose-diverges-human-writing-and-why-it-matters

### S79: Absence of Hedging / Epistemic Markers
**Detection**: Narrator or characters never express genuine uncertainty, qualification, or "I think" / "maybe" / "perhaps" — all statements are delivered with equal confidence
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Research found ChatGPT essays demonstrate "significantly lower frequency of interactional metadiscourse like hedges, boosters, and attitude markers." Human writing includes epistemic markers reflecting genuine uncertainty and personal engagement. AI text has a "more impersonal, expository tone" with "fewer engagement markers."
**Design space cost**: Confident omniscient narrators in the 19th-century tradition (Dickens, Austen) rarely hedge. Thriller fiction and action fiction may feature decisive characters who don't express uncertainty. The issue is the absence across all narrative voices.
**Source**: https://www.refsmmat.com/notebooks/llm-style.html and https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/ejed.70014

### S80: Simile Density ("Like a...")
**Detection**: Text contains similes at a rate exceeding one per 200 words, particularly using "like a [noun]" constructions with increasingly strained comparisons
**Why it indicates autocomplete**: Coyote Tracks noted AI fiction has "almost every noun preceded by an adjective, actions described with similes." AI Phrase Finder documented ChatGPT's tendency to describe everything through comparison metaphors — navigation metaphors, dance metaphors, tapestry metaphors. The model generates comparisons as its default mode of description rather than rendering things directly.
**Design space cost**: Some literary styles (Raymond Chandler's noir, certain poetry-adjacent prose) employ dense figurative language deliberately. Simile-rich prose is a legitimate stylistic choice when controlled.
**Source**: https://coyotetracks.org/blog/ai-writing/ and https://aiphrasefinder.com/common-chatgpt-metaphors/
