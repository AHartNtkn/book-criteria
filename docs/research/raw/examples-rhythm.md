# Sentence Variety and Rhythm: Prose Exemplars

Three passages from published fiction demonstrating dramatic variation in sentence length and structure to create musical rhythm, emotional pacing, or emphasis.

---

## 1. Toni Morrison, *Beloved* (1987)

> 124 WAS SPITEFUL. Full of a baby's venom. The women in the house knew it and so did the children. For years each put up with the spite in his own way, but by 1873 Sethe and her daughter Denver were its only victims. The grandmother, Baby Suggs, was dead, and the sons, Howard and Buglar, had run away by the time they were thirteen years old -- as soon as merely looking in a mirror shattered it (that was the signal for Buglar); as soon as two tiny hand prints appeared in the cake (that was it for Howard).

**What makes the rhythm work:** The opening is a detonation -- three words, then a five-word fragment, each a blunt declaration that personifies a house number as malevolent. These compressed hammer-blows establish authority through sheer brevity. Then Morrison gradually lengthens: a plain compound sentence, then a sentence with a date and dependent clause, then a sentence that sprawls across parenthetical asides and accumulating detail. The passage accelerates from staccato certainty into the sprawling, layered syntax of memory, mirroring the way trauma surfaces -- first as a bare fact, then as an uncontrollable flood of particulars.

Source: Toni Morrison, *Beloved* (Knopf, 1987), Chapter 1 opening. Passage confirmed via [BookBrowse excerpt](https://www.bookbrowse.com/excerpts/index.cfm/book_number/1908/beloved) and [Dickinson College seminar text](https://housedivided.dickinson.edu/sites/teagle/texts/toni-morrison-beloved-1987/).

## 2. Cormac McCarthy, *The Road* (2006)

> When he woke in the woods in the dark and the cold of the night he'd reach out to touch the child sleeping beside him. Nights dark beyond darkness and the days more gray each one than what had gone before. Like the onset of some cold glaucoma dimming away the world. His hand rose and fell softly with each precious breath. He pushed away the plastic tarpaulin and raised himself in the stinking robes and blankets and looked toward the east for any light but there was none.

**What makes the rhythm work:** The first sentence is long and polysyndetic -- five "and" conjunctions strung together without commas, creating a breathless, groping quality that mimics reaching through darkness. Then two sentence fragments land: no subject, no verb, just noun phrases floating in desolation. "His hand rose and fell softly with each precious breath" -- a short, tender sentence that contracts the rhythm to the scale of a single inhale. The final sentence expands again into a long chain of coordinated actions ("pushed... raised... looked") that ends with the blunt negation "but there was none." McCarthy alternates between polysyndetic sprawl and stark fragments to create a rhythm of reaching and finding nothing -- the novel's emotional core compressed into five sentences.

Source: Cormac McCarthy, *The Road* (Knopf, 2006), opening paragraph. Passage confirmed via [BookBrowse excerpt](https://www.bookbrowse.com/excerpts/index.cfm/book_number/1964/the-road), [Penguin Random House Canada excerpt](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.ca/books/110490/the-road-by-cormac-mccarthy/9780307387899/excerpt), and [Sandra Danby first paragraph analysis](https://sandradanby.com/2020/08/05/great-opening-paragraph-127-the-road-amwriting-firstpara/).

## 3. Virginia Woolf, *Mrs Dalloway* (1925)

> Mrs. Dalloway said she would buy the flowers herself. For Lucy had her work cut out for her. The doors would be taken off their hinges; Rumpelmayer's men were coming. And then, thought Clarissa Dalloway, what a morning -- fresh as if issued to children on a beach.
>
> What a lark! What a plunge! For so it had always seemed to her, when, with a little squeak of the hinges, which she could hear now, she had burst open the French windows and plunged at Bourton into the open air.

**What makes the rhythm work:** The passage opens with four plain, businesslike sentences -- declarative, practical, each under fifteen words. Domestic logistics: flowers, hinges, caterers. Then "What a lark! What a plunge!" -- two three-word exclamations that shatter the measured prose like a gasp of delight. The final sentence immediately plunges (enacting its own verb) into a long, flowing, subordinate-clause-laden construction that carries Clarissa from the present moment backward into memory at Bourton. The rhythm physically performs the transition from the quotidian present to ecstatic recollection: short practical sentences, ecstatic fragments, then one long sentence that refuses to stop, mimicking the way a sensory trigger unlocks an extended memory. The entire emotional arc of the novel -- duty versus aliveness -- is encoded in the sentence lengths.

Source: Virginia Woolf, *Mrs Dalloway* (Hogarth Press, 1925), opening paragraphs. Full text available at [Project Gutenberg Australia](http://gutenberg.net.au/ebooks02/0200991h.html).
